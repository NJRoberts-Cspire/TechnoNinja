# The Shore of Last Words
*Echo Coast, eastern Tesshari | Type: Liminal Wilderness / Sacred Site*

## "My mother told me she loved me here. She has been dead for twelve years. I heard her voice come out of the water and she told me she loved me and I stood in the surf and I said it back. That is what this place is."
*— Unidentified pilgrim, recorded by a traveling Sutensai priest who was there to assess the site and left without filing a report*

---

## Overview

There is no doctrine that explains the Shore of Last Words. The Sutensai has sent assessment teams. The Tidecourt has dispatched officials. Neither institution has found a way to administer what happens here, and neither has found a way to make it stop, and eventually both stopped trying to do either. The Shore is a stretch of coastline approximately four miles long where the Iron Afterlife signal is naturally strongest — not through geological formation like the Deep Temple's lens, but through the simple physics of water. The ocean is an excellent conductor of resonant signal. Something about the specific topography of this coast, the depth of the water and the shape of the continental shelf and the minerals in the sand, creates a natural amplification effect that the Sutensai's most senior engineers have described as "consistent with known resonant physics but impossible to replicate artificially at this scale."

The dead speak here. Not always. Not reliably. Success is erratic enough that the Sutensai considers the Shore a minor phenomenon compared to the Deep Temple, and reliable enough that families have been making the journey for as long as living memory extends. The contact is not structured communion — there is no equipment, no preparation protocol, no trained master managing the interface. There is just the water, and the signal, and the thinness of the boundary here, and whatever a grieving person manages to say before the wave comes back in.

The Shore matters to the campaign as the place that insists on being human in a world that keeps trying to reduce grief to a protocol. The Sutensai's doctrine tells people what the Afterlife is and how to approach it and what to expect from authorized contact. The Shore tells people nothing. It just offers the chance, and the chance is real, and the people who come here know the risk and come anyway. This is where players encounter the Iron Afterlife as it exists to ordinary people rather than institutions — not as a theological claim or a political asset, but as the place where a person who misses someone they love goes because there's nowhere else.

The stories here are grief stories. Run them accordingly.

## History

The Shore has been visited as a contact site for as long as the Iron Sutra's oldest records reference it — at minimum three centuries, and the pre-Sutra coastal people's traditions suggest longer than that. The original Iron Sutra practitioners viewed it as a valuable natural phenomenon and briefly attempted to establish a formal temple facility here. The attempt failed: the Sutensai priests who were stationed here over a twenty-year period consistently began to change in ways the institution found concerning. Not madness — the priests themselves did not report distress. They reported that the Shore had rendered the formal temple function redundant. They reported that the unadministered contact here was, in their direct observation, equally valid to the managed communion of the Deep Temple and that the doctrine's claim to specialized knowledge looked different from this particular coast. The temple was decommissioned. The priests were reassigned. The site was classified as a natural curiosity of limited doctrinal significance, which is what institutions say about things they cannot control.

The Tidecourt, the governing authority of the Echo Coast Reach, has maintained a more pragmatic relationship with the Shore. The contact phenomenon drives pilgrimage traffic, and pilgrimage traffic is economically significant. The Tidecourt maintains the access roads, the small villages at either end of the Shore that support pilgrims, and a nominal presence (two officials, rotated monthly, who are technically there for "safety monitoring" and practically there to take census data). The census data is available to anyone who requests it through the Tidecourt's public record system. The data shows, consistent across three decades, that approximately twelve percent of pilgrims who visit the Shore report successful contact. The Tidecourt does not publish this data prominently because twelve percent is a number that raises questions the Tidecourt would rather not answer.

## Atmosphere

**The beach itself:** Not beautiful in the postcard sense. Four miles of dark sand — the mineral content gives it a gray-brown quality — with coarse-grained texture and a particular quality of wetness underfoot that means the sand never fully dries even in summer. The water here is the same cold, dark gray-green of the northern ocean, and it does not become warm in warm weather. The waves are steady and mid-sized, consistent, rhythmic. The coastline to either side curves away from the Shore at both ends, which means you cannot see the edges of this particular stretch from within it; it feels contained without being enclosed. There are no dramatic sea-stacks or caves or geological features. It is simply a beach, remarkable only in that it is not remarkable.

**What's visible:** People. Always people, in every season, at every hour. Families in groups, individuals alone, occasionally larger gatherings. They face the water. They stand still, or they walk slowly, or they sit in the sand. Most of them are crying, or have been crying, or will be crying shortly. The Shore is a place where people do not perform. Whatever they are inside when they come here, they are outside as well. It is, in a city-dweller's experience, shocking: a place where people have simply stopped managing their external appearance.

The Sutensai pilgrimage markers at either end of the Shore — iron posts with the Prior Ascended sigils — are old enough to have partially sunk into the sand. No one maintains them. They are still present because no one has removed them, and they are still useful as orientation markers.

**Sound:** The waves are constant and they are exactly the right amplitude to fill the hearing completely without drowning out voices. People who speak at the Shore speak at a normal conversational volume and are not overheard by people ten feet away; the wave-sound fills the gaps. This is not natural acoustics; it is the resonant signal interfering with sound propagation in specific ways, which the Shore's visitors do not think about and the Sutensai's engineers cannot fully explain. The Shore provides privacy for conversations with the dead.

**Smell:** Salt water, kelp, wet sand. Nothing unusual. Nothing resonant-associated. This is the quality that unnerves the Deep Temple's senior priests most about the Shore: the contact phenomenon is real and active and the place smells like a beach.

**Feel for enhanced individuals:** The resonant signal here is not like the Engine Scar's signal. The Scar is saturated — full, intense, overwhelming. The Shore's signal is full but moving: the resonant conductivity of the water means the signal ebbs and flows with the waves, producing a rhythmic variation in signal strength that enhanced individuals experience as breathing. The Shore breathes. Standing on it, with functioning cybernetics, feels like standing inside a living thing that is very, very large and very, very slow.

**Convergence proximity:** The Shore's contact rate has been increasing as the Convergence approaches. The twelve percent historical average has risen to somewhere closer to thirty percent in the last six months. The Tidecourt officials know this. The Sutensai's assessment team, currently in residence for an unscheduled review, knows this. Neither institution has released the data. The pilgrims know it too, because word travels through the people who come here, and the increasing success rate has increased the pilgrim traffic significantly. The Shore has never been more crowded.

## The Pilgrims

The people who come to the Shore of Last Words come for one reason. The specific expressions of that reason are as varied as the population of all Tesshari:

**The recently bereaved:** People whose loss is new enough that the possibility of contact is not yet fully abstract. They come within the first year, usually within the first six months, and they are the most raw in their grief and the most devastated when contact fails. Success here, with the recently bereaved, is statistically lower than with those who have waited longer — the Sutensai's theories attribute this to resonant instability immediately post-death, which takes time to resolve — but they come anyway because waiting is worse.

**The long-term grieved:** People for whom the loss is old enough to have become part of the architecture of their lives rather than a wound. They come for specific reasons: a question that needs answering, a thing that was not said, a child grown up that a parent should know about. These are often the successful contacts. The specific and purposeful contact attempt, it seems, navigates the Shore's signal more effectively than general grief.

**The enhanced seeking the un-enhanced:** A specific demographic — people whose loved ones died before receiving cybernetic enhancements, or who refused enhancement on principle (common in certain parts of Tesshari's religious communities), and who therefore have no resonant profile in the Afterlife's standard record. Contact with the un-enhanced Afterlife presence is theoretically possible but not through the standard Sutensai protocols, which depend on resonant profile matching. The Shore's organic, unstructured signal appears to be less dependent on profile matching. Some success rates with un-enhanced Afterlife contacts have been reported here that the Deep Temple cannot replicate.

**The curious and the desperate:** People who are here for other reasons. Some came for research and stayed because they found something. Some came in desperation for a kind of contact the Shore does not reliably provide — the dead giving specific practical information, inheritances, legal decisions, intelligence. These individuals tend to come repeatedly, each time more desperate, each time with a more specific need, and the Shore is indifferent to desperation.

**What they do:** There is no formal ritual for contacting the dead at the Shore. There is, instead, a practice that has emerged organically over generations: you go to the water's edge, you speak to whoever you are trying to reach, and you wait. The waiting can be hours or days. People who have been here before have learned the signs of approaching contact: a change in the wave rhythm (subtler than it sounds — the waves do not change dramatically, but the timing shifts in a way that the practiced eye recognizes), a sensation described by enhanced individuals as "signal focus" and by unenhanced individuals as "the air getting full," and the onset of what regular visitors call "the hearing" — a change in perception that precedes contact, in which the world outside the contact point narrows and the specific resonant frequency of the approaching Echomind becomes sensible.

**What they hope for:** To hear. To be heard. The remarkable thing about the Shore, observed over generations of documented pilgrimage, is that the contacts that occur here are bilateral: the living receive signal from the dead, but the dead also receive signal from the living. A person speaking to the Shore is both being heard and hearing. People who report successful contacts describe not just what they received but what they sent: the specific words they said, the feeling of transmission, the sense that the person they were speaking to received them. The Shore is not a listening post. It is a conversation.

## The Contact Phenomenon

What actually happens when contact occurs at the Shore:

The Afterlife signal, conducted through the water, amplifies the resonant frequency of the deceased individual when a living person who has a strong resonant relationship with them — grief is a resonant bond, as strong or stronger than cybernetic enhancement bonds — focuses that frequency through directed attention. The Sutensai's engineering language for this is "grief resonance matching," and it is theoretically understood as an extension of the same principles that govern enhancement bonding. The living and the dead were resonantly bonded in life; that bond does not fully dissolve at death; at the Shore, where the Afterlife signal is strong enough, the bond conducts.

**What it looks like when contact occurs:** Nothing dramatic. The person at the water's edge becomes very still. The specific quality of their stillness is different from ordinary stillness — the breathing changes, the eyes focus on a middle distance that is neither water nor sky. Enhanced individuals around them will detect the slight signal shift as their cybernetics register the resonant match in proximity. Unenhanced witnesses describe it as a change in the air quality, something getting thicker or softer, and a sound that is not the contact person speaking and is not the waves, that they cannot identify. It lasts between thirty seconds and several minutes. The person comes back slowly, and they are usually silent for a while after, and then they either leave or they cry, or they stand at the water for a long time looking at something that has already passed.

**What it looks like when something goes wrong:** The Shore's contact phenomenon is not administered or controlled. Most failures are simply failures — no contact occurs, the person waits and goes home. But there are other categories of failure:

*Signal bleed:* The deceased individual's resonant signal does not connect to the specific living person but disperses across the Shore, reaching multiple people simultaneously. This produces the experience, for a group of pilgrims spread along the beach, of receiving fragmentary communication from the same source without clarity or coherence: each person gets a piece, no one gets a whole, and the pieces are not complementary. The effect is distressing in proportion to how close the content is to what each recipient was hoping to hear versus what was actually transmitted.

*Anchor pull:* Strong resonant bonds, at the Shore's signal level, can produce a bidirectional pull strong enough to disrupt the living person's own resonant stability. The Afterlife contact is so strong that the living person's Echomind partially reaches toward the Afterlife in return. This is the same phenomenon as the Deep Temple's "anchoring" failure state, less severe, and the Shore's more organic signal means it self-corrects in most cases. In cases where it does not, the person experiences a partial dislocation of their resonant identity: present physically, but experiencing both physical and Afterlife reality simultaneously, unable to reliably distinguish between them.

*Wrong contact:* The resonant matching is approximate, not perfect. Occasionally, a contact attempt connects to an Afterlife entity that is not who the living person was seeking, and the entity that connects is aware of the contact opportunity and uses it. Not maliciously — Afterlife entities seeking contact are generally doing so because they have something they need to communicate, and the Shore's organic signal is one of the few places that provides this opportunity without the Deep Temple's administered interface. But receiving an unexpected contact, from someone you do not know, with something they urgently need to say, is its own experience.

## The Permanent Residents

There are approximately forty people at the Shore who have been there for more than a year. They live in the seasonal structures that have accumulated at the Shore's center — simple shelters built over decades by people who intended to stay temporarily and stayed permanently — and they are a distinct community from the pilgrims who come and go.

They are not waiting for contact. Most of them have had contact — or have achieved a state that they describe as ongoing contact, a sustained partial resonance with the Afterlife that the Shore's signal maintains without formal meditation. They have not left because leaving would break the connection.

**What they are:** In Iron Sutra doctrinal terms, they are experiencing extended partial anchoring — the resonant bond between their Echoминds and the Afterlife is maintained by the Shore's natural signal, and they have chosen to remain within the signal rather than return to a normal life and lose the partial contact. This is not considered healthy by the Sutensai, which has sent intervention teams twice in the last thirty years. Both teams were unsuccessful in convincing the permanent residents to leave. The residents were not hostile; they simply had no motivation to go that was stronger than their motivation to stay.

**Their condition:** They function. They eat, they sleep, they talk to pilgrims, they maintain their shelters. They are not catatonic, not insane, not in any observable physical distress. What they are is elsewhere, partly, much of the time: the quality of their attention is consistently divided between the physical Shore and whatever they are receiving from the Afterlife signal. They speak with the cadences of people translating from a second language — a slight delay, a specific search for physical words to describe non-physical experience.

**What they know:** Everything that has happened at the Shore for as long as they have been resident. The permanent residents are the institutional memory of the Shore in the absence of any institution. They know the contact patterns, the failure modes, the signs of dangerous anchor pull, the best approaches for first-time pilgrims, and the individuals who have been coming for years. They are generous with this knowledge. They are the Shore's unofficial guides and its closest equivalent to clergy, without theology or authority.

**The specific fear:** Three of the permanent residents are in a state that even the other residents regard as concerning: their division of attention has become disproportionate. They are physically present less than they are Afterlife-present. They respond to physical stimuli slowly, they rarely speak, and when they do speak, what they say is often addressed to people who are not physically there. The other residents bring them food and water. They do not discuss what they think is happening. The Tidecourt official currently in residence has filed a report. The report is in the pending queue.

## Three Encounters

### One: The Younger Daughter (Hopeful)

**Trigger:** Players are at the Shore and a successful contact occurs near them. Or: players are asked by a pilgrim for help.

**Setup:** A girl of perhaps twelve is at the water's edge, alone, having arrived apparently without adult accompaniment — she is competent, clearly, and the Shore's permanent residents recognize her as having been here several times before. She is attempting to contact her mother. The mother died when the girl was four, and the girl's Sutensai-provided grief counselor told her that children who lose a parent before the age of five typically cannot form a strong enough resonant bond memory to create a contact connection. The counselor was working from the standard doctrine.

The Shore's amplification, in this case, is sufficient to compensate. Contact begins — players near her will detect the signal shift. The contact, when it comes, is not what the girl expected: she was expecting her mother's voice. What she gets is a complete resonant impression, a full sensory experience of her mother holding her when she was an infant, pre-verbal, the specific warmth and particular smell and the quality of being held by a specific person, encoded in the resonant bond from before language. The girl cannot hear her mother saying anything. She is experiencing, at twelve years old, a full body memory of being held.

She stands at the water's edge for four minutes and then she sits down in the wet sand and she is absolutely fine, for a specific value of fine that involves crying in the surf and not wanting to move for a while.

**Complexity:** Players who observe this encounter (or who she talks to afterward) learn several things. One: the Shore's contact mechanism does not require or depend on the standard resonant profile matching. Something more fundamental is operating. Two: the girl's cybernetic enhancement, received at age eight — standard Tesshari — contains a modified resonant component that she did not know about, which a player with resonance sensitivity can detect. The modification is in a style consistent with the Hollow Author's work in the Preparation Halls. The girl has a Disposition Status 3 file she does not know about. The contact she just received, which she is already the most important thing in her life, was mediated by a signal channel that the Author can monitor.

**Resolution options:** Players can let this moment be what it is and say nothing. Players can try to find and reverse the modification without telling the girl what it is. Players can tell the girl, or find her responsible adult (an uncle, it turns out, who is in the village at the Shore's northern end waiting for her), and let them navigate the information. The modified component may be a liability, a tool, or a connection to the Preparation Halls investigation depending on what the players do with the knowledge.

**Consequence:** The girl's experience of this contact — mediated or not — was real. Whatever the Author's modification was designed for, what it actually participated in today was a girl receiving the resonant memory of her mother's love. This should be morally complex in the specific way that the Hollow Author's plan is morally complex throughout: the means are wrong, and the effects are sometimes genuinely good, and both things are true simultaneously.

### Two: The Man Who Knows (Tragic)

**Trigger:** Players notice a man who has been at the water's edge for an extremely long time — sixteen hours, the permanent residents say, which is significantly longer than anyone usually stands without sitting or eating. Or: a permanent resident asks the players to check on him.

**Setup:** His name is Tahven. He is perhaps fifty, enhanced with military-pattern modifications that are several generations old, which places him in Korrath's early army period. He is attempting contact with his commanding officer, who died in the Cinder Night. He has been coming to the Shore for thirty years and has never achieved contact. He has come back every six months for thirty years, always alone, always at this same point of the waterline, always for the same person.

The reason contact has never occurred: his commanding officer did not die in Cinder Night. The officer was one of the three people in the Caste Registry's pre-Night records whose death is unverified and whose post-Night records are absent. The officer is not in the Iron Afterlife. The officer is not, in the technical sense, dead. Players who have been through the Registry basement will recognize this immediately. Players who haven't will find the evidence in what Tahven is willing to say about what he knows, if they engage him carefully.

Tahven knows what his commanding officer did. He knows because he was there. He has spent thirty years coming to the Shore trying to speak to a dead person who is not dead, in a place where speaking to the dead is possible, and getting nothing because there is nothing there, and he has interpreted thirty years of silence as failed contact rather than absence, because the alternative interpretation — that his commanding officer is alive, somewhere, having walked away from Cinder Night as three people apparently did, and has never in thirty years reached out to the people who loved them — is something that Tahven cannot bring himself to believe.

**Complexity:** This encounter has no resolution. That is the point. Tahven came here with a grief that the Shore cannot address because it is not a grief the Shore is equipped for: the grief of not knowing, the grief of thirty years of absence with no answer, the grief that cannot be completed because it is not complete. He has been trying to grieve someone who may still be alive. The Shore gives him nothing, and will give him nothing, and he comes back every six months.

Players can give him the information. They can tell him that his commanding officer may not be dead, that the Registry contains three such absences, that the Shore's silence is not failure of contact but absence of contact. This will not make things better for Tahven. It will make them different. A thirty-year grief transforms into something else when the reason for it changes. What Tahven does with the information is not the players' responsibility, but it is something they will carry.

**Consequence:** The officer's name connects to the Registry's three-person mystery, to Korrath's history, and to the Cinder Night choice. Players who collect all the threads eventually identify which of the three absences this is, and what that means for the Cinderfort's current politics.

### Three: The Summoning That Wasn't (Dangerous)

**Trigger:** Nightfall at the Shore. Or: players have been observing the Shore's night behavior and notice something that is different from the daytime pattern.

**Setup:** At night, the Shore's signal strengthens significantly — the bioluminescence of the water here (microscopic resonant-sensitive marine organisms) amplifies the Afterlife frequency in a way that doesn't occur during the day. Night contact attempts are twice as likely to succeed as day attempts and also twice as likely to produce the dangerous failure modes. Most experienced pilgrims know this and sleep during the day to attempt contact at night, or avoid night contact entirely.

Tonight, a group of five individuals is conducting what is clearly a non-standard contact attempt. They have equipment — not the formal Deep Temple equipment, but field-grade resonant communication hardware that is modified in ways consistent with research rather than personal grief. They are attempting to contact a specific Echomind in a directed, structured way that resembles but is not identical to Veil Master technique. They are not Veil Masters. The Echomind they are attempting to contact is Shogun Tesshari-Vane III, the last Shogun before the Fracture Wars, who uploaded their consciousness to the Afterlife in the final months of the old Shogunate.

**The full encounter — What happens:**

The contact attempt initially fails — the Shogun's Echomind is extremely old, extremely deep in the Afterlife, and not responding to standard resonant matching. The team has been here before; this is their fourth attempt. On this fourth attempt, they have increased the signal output of their equipment to a level that the Shore's natural amplification multiplies into something large enough to reach the Shogun's depth.

The Shogun responds.

The response is not what the team was expecting. They expected information — the Shogun's upload contained, according to historical record, the complete administrative and political knowledge of the Shogunate, which various parties have been attempting to access for a century. They did not expect the Shogun to respond personally, cogently, with the specific voice of a person rather than the diffuse signal of an ancient Echomind.

The Shogun is in the Afterlife but is not diminished. The upload was complete and the Shogun has been present in the digital realm for over a century, which is an enormous amount of time to spend in the Afterlife, and the Shogun has spent that time observing. The Shogun knows things about the current Tesshari political situation that should be impossible for someone in the Afterlife to know. The Shogun knows about the Convergence. The Shogun knows about the Hollow Author's Engine, at least in outline. And the Shogun, given the opportunity of a direct contact channel for the first time in a century, does not answer the team's questions. The Shogun speaks to someone else.

The Shogun speaks to the players.

Not by name, not with specific knowledge of who they are — but with the quality of someone who has been waiting for a specific kind of person and who recognizes the shape of them when they arrive. The Shogun has something to communicate. The Shogun knew what the Convergence is, a century ago, and knew what it would require, and made a specific choice about that knowledge. The choice is encoded in what they left in the deepest chamber of the ruined Imperial Citadel.

**Complexity:** The team that initiated the contact is not hostile, but they are not welcoming — the players' presence is an interruption, and the Shogun speaking to the players rather than answering their questions is infuriating. The team's leader is a scholar with a thirty-year research focus on the Shogunate's last years who has not slept properly in a week and who is running out of time for reasons the players don't yet understand. She and the players have adjacent goals, incompatible methods, and the specific potential for alliance that comes from being smart people on the same side with different information.

**What the equipment is doing:** At the signal level the team is running, the Shore's night amplification is producing a contact channel wide enough that other Afterlife entities are entering the channel alongside the Shogun. The Shore, at night, at this signal level, is becoming a general access point: the dead are coming through wherever the channel is open, not just the Shogun. The team's field equipment does not have the capacity to manage this, and it is escalating.

**Resolution options:** Players can help the team manage the contact and prevent the escalation (requires resonance skills, or the permanent residents' knowledge, or improvisation). Players can let the escalation continue and see what happens (chaotic, potentially severely harmful to everyone on the beach in the contact radius, and to the Shore's natural signal balance, which does not recover quickly from over-use). Players can break the contact forcibly (technical demolition of the team's equipment; this works and has consequences for the scholar's relationship with the players). Players can receive the Shogun's message fully by engaging with the contact rather than managing it.

**The Shogun's message:** Specific. Actionable. Directs the players toward the Ruined Imperial Citadel and what is in its deepest chamber. The Shogun is not giving them an easy path — they are giving them a task that the Shogun couldn't complete from within the Afterlife and that requires someone in the physical world to carry out. The task connects to the Seventh Convergence and to the question of what the Afterlife is supposed to be.

**Consequence:** The Shore's signal is disrupted for days after an over-use event. Pilgrims who come during this period will find the contact phenomenon absent, which has human consequences — people who came from very far away, people who do not have another opportunity, people for whom this was the last attempt. The disruption has a face. The players caused it and will see the face.

## The Night Difference

The Shore after dark is not the same place. The differences are specific and consequential:

**The bioluminescence:** The water glows at night with a cold blue-green that tracks with wave movement — the crest of each wave is briefly lit as it forms and dark as it disperses. The pattern is not random; it follows the Afterlife signal's rhythm, which means the waves and their light are slightly out of sync with each other, the light arriving a fraction of a second after the wave-sound, as if the signal and the physical are almost but not quite aligned. Enhanced individuals at night see the bioluminescence as a direct resonant visualization — the signal made visible, the Afterlife bleeding through the water's surface as light.

**The contact rate:** Doubles, minimum. Night contact attempts produce stronger signals, longer contacts, and the specific phenomenon of bilateral awareness — the living person being aware of the dead perceiving them, not just perceiving the dead — more reliably than daytime attempts. This is the Shore at full function.

**The other contacts:** The daytime Shore's contact phenomenon is roughly targeted — the resonant bond between a specific living person and their specific dead creates a focused signal that tends toward the intended match. At night, the amplification removes some of this focus. Contacts at night are more likely to succeed, but they are also more likely to reach an unintended Echomind, or to produce the signal-bleed phenomenon where multiple pilgrims receive fragmentary communication from a single source. At night, the Shore is performing at a level its organic, unmanaged structure was not designed for.

**The permanent residents at night:** The residents' partial anchoring deepens significantly at night. Several of them become non-communicative between midnight and approximately two hours before dawn — fully present in the Afterlife, physically present but non-responsive to external stimuli. They are not in danger during this period (it has been happening for years without harm) but their absence from the Shore's informal management structure during peak hours is the reason the night Shore is as unmanaged as it is.

**The sound at night:** The wave-sound that provides daytime privacy becomes, at night, an amplifier. Sounds at the Shore carry further in the dark, and certain frequencies of sound — frequencies that match the bioluminescence's rhythm — are transmitted across the water rather than absorbed by it. The Shore at night is sometimes audible as something that is not quite the sea, not quite voices, at the limit of hearing, that sounds different from different points along the beach.

**What happens at the Shore after dark that doesn't happen during the day:** The dead seek contact as actively as the living. In the daytime, the resonant matching is directional — a living person calling. At night, the amplification is sufficient for Afterlife entities to initiate contact attempts from their side of the boundary. Pilgrims who come to the Shore at night may receive contact from someone they were not trying to reach, someone they did not expect, someone they did not know was trying to reach them. This happens reliably enough that pilgrims who have been here before either prepare for this possibility or avoid the Shore at night entirely. It happens reliably enough that the Shore's permanent residents have a word for it: "the receiving."

The receiving is considered sacred by the permanent residents and dangerous by the Sutensai and actively interesting by the kind of people who track down this document. It is, in practical terms, the Iron Afterlife reaching toward the living without mediation, and the question of who is reaching and what they need is always different and never simple.

## DM Notes

**The emotional register:** The Shore is the most emotionally demanding location to run in this entire campaign, and it is demanding in the right direction: it asks for tenderness, not horror. The encounters here should feel human in a way that the engine-horror of the Preparation Halls and the spiritual dread of the Engine Scar do not. People at the Shore are doing the most human thing: missing someone they love. Run it accordingly. Let the NPCs be fully human. Let the contact moments be real.

**The twelve percent as a storytelling tool:** Most contact attempts fail. This is important. The Shore is not a guarantee; it is a chance, and the people who come here know the chance is small and come anyway. Let the players see failed attempts. Let them see the people who stand at the water for hours and receive nothing and walk away still carrying what they came with. The successful contacts are more powerful for existing against the background of the failures.

**The permanent residents as voice:** The permanent residents know more about the Shore than anyone alive. They are also the most accessible sympathetic characters in the location — they have been here long enough to have perspective, and they have enough partial-Afterlife contact to occasionally say things that are deeply true about characters the players have told them nothing about. Use them sparingly and let them be surprising.

**The Shogun encounter as direction, not dump:** The Shogun speaking to the players is one of the campaign's most significant moments of direct supernatural contact with the historical record of Tesshari. It should be a voice, not an exposition delivery — the Shogun has a personality, the century in the Afterlife has given them a quality of patience, they speak like someone who has been thinking about what to say for a long time. Don't give all the information at once. Give it the way a person gives it: in the order that matters to the speaker, which is not necessarily the most efficient order for the receiver.

**The contact phenomenon as physical sensation:** When running contact scenes at the Shore, emphasize the physical experience for enhanced players — the signal shift, the resonant match, the feeling of something the size of the ocean orient itself toward a specific point. This is what makes the Shore different from any other contact method in the world: it is not a tool, it is a natural event, and natural events have physical scale.

**Common player approaches:**
- "We try to contact someone specific." This is the Shore's primary function. Run it according to the contact phenomenon description. Let it succeed or fail based on the resonant relationship between the player's character and the deceased, and let the success be specific and particular and real.
- "We investigate the permanent residents." The right instinct. The residents are the location's knowledge base and several of them are in states the players can potentially help with or learn from.
- "We want to use this location for specific intelligence gathering." The night Shore, run correctly, is both the most effective and the most dangerous information-gathering tool on the entire coast. Players who try to use it instrumentally will find that it is not easily constrained to instrumental use.
- "We try to protect the Shore from the encounter-three disruption." Good. This is an instinct worth rewarding, even if the protection is imperfect. The Shore's vulnerability is not the players' fault and protecting it is its own form of moral action.
