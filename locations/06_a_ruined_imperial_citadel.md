# The Shattered Throne of Kohren-Vastai
*Borderlands between the Ironhold and Ashgate Reaches | Type: Ruin / Dungeon / Neutral Ground*

## "Whoever built this place believed that the empire would last forever. You can see it in the construction — they built for permanence. The Fracture Wars took eleven years. The building has been falling for a hundred."
*— Lady-Merchant Ovreni Tesshari-Thane, who uses the ground floor for quarterly trade negotiations and has never gone deeper than the second level*

---

## Overview

The Shattered Throne of Kohren-Vastai — known colloquially as the Broken House, the Treaty Ruin, or simply the Citadel — was one of sixteen administrative citadels the old Shogunate maintained throughout the territories that are now the Seven Reaches. It served as a regional courthouse, census bureau, garrison, and the physical manifestation of imperial authority in a territory that was, in the Shogunate's final decades, increasingly reluctant to be reminded that authority existed. When the Fracture Wars began, Kohren-Vastai was one of the first citadels to be contested, changing hands four times in the first three years of fighting before all parties concluded that the fighting was costing more than the citadel was worth and withdrew, leaving the building in the borderland condition it has occupied since: claimed by no one, used by everyone.

The citadel matters to the campaign as the campaign's primary neutral meeting ground and its most complex dungeon. The surface function — the tradition of neutral use that has developed over a century — is real and valuable and is the reason numerous important meetings happen here. Beneath that function is a dungeon that is genuinely dangerous in ways that are specifically connected to the Fracture Wars, to the Iron Afterlife disruption left by the Shattering, and to what the last Shogun left at the very bottom before their final upload. Players who use the citadel for its surface function and never go below will miss the most significant thing in it. Players who go below will find a dungeon that is dark in the specifically earned way of places where real history happened and the weight of it hasn't dissipated.

The stories here are stories about aftermath: the things that persist when institutions fall, the weight of choices made by people who are now dead, and the question of whether what someone leaves behind can carry their intent intact across a century.

## History

The Shogunate's administrative citadels were built on a standard template with local variation. Kohren-Vastai was modified from the standard design in several significant ways at the direction of Shogun Tesshari-Vane III, who had a particular relationship with this specific territory and who spent more time here than the administrative function required. The modifications included: a deeper basement level than the standard design, connecting to a resonant feature in the local geology that the Shogun's advisors identified; additional resonant metal installations in the deep levels that are more extensive than any other citadel in the Shogunate's record; and a room at the deepest level that does not appear in the standard administrative documentation but that appears in a single sealed appendix to the Shogun's personal correspondence, an appendix that was filed in the Shogunate's central archive and was destroyed during the Fracture Wars when the archive building burned.

The appendix's contents are, therefore, unknown. The room exists. Players who reach it are the first to do so since the Shogun was last here, over a century ago.

**The Fracture Wars at Kohren-Vastai:** The citadel changed hands four times. Each siege left physical evidence: the scorching on the outer wall's eastern face from the second siege's fire weapon; the bracing added to the main entrance after the third siege's structural damage; the kill-marks in the great hall where the fourth siege's final close-quarters fighting happened, which were not cleaned up before the last garrison abandoned the building. The kill-marks are still there. People who use the great hall for trade negotiations walk over them as they set up their booths.

The Iron Afterlife disruption at Kohren-Vastai was caused by the third siege: a resonant weapon was deployed against the citadel's defenders, intended to disrupt their cybernetic enhancements. The weapon did disrupt their enhancements. It also disrupted the resonant architecture of the building's deep levels, causing a partial inversion: the Afterlife's signal, rather than passing through the building in its normal Scar-adjacent flow, was reflected back into the building and has been accumulating there for ninety years. The deep levels of the Kohren-Vastai citadel are saturated with reflected Afterlife signal that has nowhere to go. The disruption is not dangerous at ground level. It becomes dangerous at the second level and below.

## Atmosphere

**The surface:** A ruin with active occupancy. The ground floor and the courtyard have been maintained — not restored, but maintained, in the pragmatic sense of fixing what falls and clearing what obstructs and leaving what is merely damaged. The walls of the great hall have the citadel's original frescos, many incomplete (the warfare stopped the decorating program), and the combination of partial grandeur and practical market set-ups is characteristic of the citadel's current function. The permanent things here — the fresco work, the carved imperial eagles over the main entrance, the resonant metal seals in the floor that were part of the original construction — are old and beautiful in the way of craftsmanship that was made to last. The impermanent things — the booths, the sleeping rolls, the cooking fires in the courtyard — are the accumulation of decades of people treating this as a functional space without much interest in its history.

**The smell:** Old stone, cooking, the cold ash of fires that are not currently burning, and — from the stairwells that descend to the lower levels — something that is not quite any of these things. A stillness-smell, the smell of a space that hasn't had moving air in a very long time, layered over something metallic and faintly organic that experienced resonant workers would recognize as the specific smell of a Afterlife-saturated space.

**Sound:** The upper levels are busy when there are negotiations ongoing — voices, the sounds of commercial activity, the particular quiet of two parties that have not yet agreed on anything. The lower levels absorb sound; the stairwells going down carry the citadel's sounds briefly and then silence them.

**Feel for enhanced individuals:** The Afterlife disruption's reflective accumulation is detectable from the ground floor as a low-grade pressure in the enhancement systems — not the invasive pressure of the Engine Scar, but a consistent awareness of a large resonant mass somewhere below. Enhanced individuals in the upper levels of the citadel sometimes describe a sensation of being watched, which is not fully wrong: the accumulated Afterlife entities in the lower levels are aware of living resonance above them, though they cannot reach it.

## The Neutral Ground Culture

The citadel's function as a neutral meeting site has developed over approximately eighty years, beginning with a series of trade negotiations in the early post-Fracture period when no individual Reach's territory offered a site that all parties would agree to. The borderland location was the selling point: technically in neither the Ironhold nor the Ashgate Reach, legally in a gray zone that both Reaches' administrations have explicitly declined to clarify, practically in a space that neither party wants to be seen claiming because claiming it would invite the other party to contest it.

**The customs that have developed:**
- All weapons above personal defense level are deposited in the weapons vault near the main entrance before entering the negotiation floor. The vault is maintained by the current Keeper (see Key Locations). The deposit is voluntary, but the custom is effectively universal; arriving at a negotiation armed at military scale would be read as a declaration of intent.
- No caste marks are recognized within the citadel. Inside these walls, a Hollow Poor district laborer and a Fourth Ring administrator are formally equal, which means in practice they are equal in the sense of being equally ignored by the people they're trying to negotiate with — the social reality doesn't vanish with the rule, but the rule means it can be used.
- Violence within the citadel's walls is sanctioned for ejection by a consensus of the currently present negotiating parties. This has been invoked three times in recorded history. Twice it worked. Once it did not, and the negotiating parties had to get creative.
- The fires in the courtyard are communal. Food cooked on them is available to everyone. This began as pragmatism and has become the custom with the most emotional weight; multiple traders and negotiators who are otherwise hostile to each other will acknowledge the courtesy of a shared fire as a minimum of human recognition.

**Who comes here:** Every type of political and commercial transaction that cannot happen on anyone's home territory. Reach-to-Reach diplomacy. Merchant houses negotiating across political lines. Individuals seeking neutral ground for personal meetings with people they have reason not to meet in their own territory. The citadel's guest register (kept by the Keeper, genuinely confidential, never disclosed without cause) contains names that would reshape several political situations if made public.

## The Surface Level: Ground Floor and Courtyard

### The Great Hall
The citadel's primary negotiating space, approximately forty meters by twenty, with the kill-marks from the Fracture Wars' fourth siege still visible as dark stains on the stone floor that the current users have learned to navigate around without explicitly acknowledging. The hall's high windows let in enough light to work by. The resonant metal seals in the floor are the citadel's original installation — administrative seals, not combat systems — and they hum very faintly with the Afterlife accumulation from below, a sound that visitors usually dismiss as wind or building settling.

The frescos on the north and south walls are the most complete in the building. The north wall shows the Shogunate at its height: the Prior Ascended, the Shogun's court, the administrative apparatus of a unified Tesshari. The south wall is a map — a pictorial map of all the territories of the unified realm, with the Shogun at the center and the administrative citadels marked, including Kohren-Vastai. The map is accurate to within a century of its making and is one of the best surviving records of what the pre-Shattering territorial arrangement looked like.

### The Keeper's Station
The Keeper of Kohren-Vastai is a position that has been held by members of the same family for four generations — the Irenmori family, coincidentally the same family name as the Veil Master Kovath (whether there is a connection is left deliberately unclear). The current Keeper is Sorren Irenmori, a woman in her fifties who has spent her entire adult life in this building and who knows it better than any other living person, including the lower levels she does not go to.

Sorren Irenmori is the most useful person the players can know in this location. She knows the guest register. She knows the custom and history. She knows what the lower levels contain, approximately, from the accounts of the few people who have come back from them, and she has strong opinions about what the players are likely to encounter. She cannot be bought. She can be respected. The distinction is one she makes clearly and early in every conversation.

### The Courtyard
The interior courtyard still has the original well — dry now, the water table below the citadel having changed in a century, but structurally intact. The well shaft goes down to the first sub-level and was used, during the Fracture Wars, as an emergency access point between floors. Players who investigate the well shaft find it descends to a hatch at the bottom that opens to a room adjacent to Room 3 in the dungeon below.

The communal fire space, the weapons vault, the stable structures that have been repurposed as storage and sleeping space — all are here, functional, occupied whenever parties are in residence.

## The Middle Levels: Second and Third Floors (Above Ground)

The floors above the ground level were the administrative offices of the original citadel — courts, census records, the resident garrison's quarters, the Shogun's personal apartments on the top floor. These are significantly less maintained than the ground level. The second floor is structurally sound but has not been occupied in decades; the third floor has partial structural damage from the Fracture Wars that makes some areas impassable and all areas risky.

**What's here:** The administrative records that survived the Fracture Wars' damage are still in their original filing rooms on the second floor. They cover the last twenty years of the Shogunate's operation of this territory — census data, court decisions, enforcement records. They are in deteriorating condition but largely readable. A player who takes the time to work through them (multi-session investment, or specific research approach) will find that the final five years of the Shogunate's records here are notably different from the earlier ones: the census data shows population anomalies, the court records show an unusual number of cases classified "referred to the Shogun directly," and the enforcement records show a systematic reduction in monitoring of certain resonant activities in this territory that the earlier records flagged as prohibited. Something was changing in the citadel's territory in the last years of the Shogunate, under Shogun Tesshari-Vane III's direction, and the changes were being documented here.

**The third floor:** The Shogun's personal apartments are on the third floor. Partially destroyed, partially intact. The damage pattern from the Fracture Wars is interesting: the apartments were not destroyed by the sieges. They were destroyed by something inside the building, from inside the apartments outward, in a pattern consistent with resonant discharge at significant scale. Whatever happened in the Shogun's apartments during the Fracture Wars was not external attack. The Shogun did this on their way out.

In the remaining intact section of the Shogun's apartments: personal items, a century old, including a writing desk whose contents — correspondence, personal notes — have been mostly consumed by the resonant discharge event but whose one surviving element is a fragment of a letter in the Shogun's hand that ends mid-sentence with "the Citadel holds what I cannot carry. The door is below the well. The answer to the Convergence question is" — and then the fragment ends.

## The Dungeon: Below-Ground Levels

The citadel's below-ground levels are where the Fracture Wars left their permanent mark. The resonant weapon that caused the Afterlife disruption has filled these levels with ninety years of accumulated reflected signal, and the entities that the signal has attracted and anchored have made their own contributions. The below-ground levels are not safe and have not been safe since the third siege. They are, however, traversable for prepared and skilled parties.

The dungeon has three below-ground levels. The structural damage from the Fracture Wars is most severe at Level 1 and becomes less so as you descend — the deeper levels were more protected by the Earth. Level 3 is, structurally, nearly intact.

---

### Level 1: The Siege Archaeology

**Room 1: The Stair-Head**
The landing at the bottom of the main staircase from the ground floor. The resonant disruption is immediately detectable here: a pressure, a low thrum in enhanced systems, and a quality to the darkness ahead that is not merely absence of light. The floor at the base of the stair has a fracture line running east-west, the result of Fracture Wars structural damage; it is cosmetic but looks concerning.

*Environmental storytelling:* The walls of the stair-head still have the tactical markings left by the citadel's final garrison — unit designations, supply cache locations (the caches are long since emptied), arrows indicating defensive positions. This floor was a fighting retreat route.

**Room 2: The Garrison Archive**
A large room that was, in the citadel's operation period, the primary administrative storage for current documents. During the Fracture Wars, it became a field hospital. The beds and equipment are gone — salvaged at some point — but the evidence remains: stains, the specific wear patterns on the floor where beds were arranged, an iron ring bolted into the wall that served as a restraint point for patients whose enhancements were active involuntarily. The resonant disruption here is stronger than at the stair-head. Enhanced individuals feel their cybernetics picking up the ghost-signal of the people who suffered in this room.

*Hazard:* Low-level resonant static from the accumulated signal; enhanced characters must make a Constitution save (DC 12) or take 1 level of Signal Fatigue per hour in this room. The room can be traversed quickly without issue.

*Searchable:* A sealed document case in the room's far corner, buried under debris, contains the field surgeon's log from the third siege's final days. The log describes the resonant weapon's effects in clinical detail, including the moment of deployment and the specific frequency used. This is directly relevant to understanding the disruption and potentially to reversing or navigating it.

**Room 3: The Weapons Cache (Empty)**
The garrison's emergency munitions room, accessed from Room 2 and from the well shaft in the courtyard above. Empty — its contents were either used or salvaged. The connection to the well shaft means that players who investigated the courtyard and who are now in the dungeon can use this room as a fast exit and re-entry point. The room has two notable features: resonant-encoded unit insignia on the wall (identifying the garrison's last posting, which players can cross-reference with Ironhold military records if they know where to look), and a section of floor that rings hollow when tapped. The hollow section covers a natural fissure in the rock below — not a passage, but a connection to the resonant geology of the region that the Level 2 disruption effects bleed upward through.

**Room 4: The Court Chamber**
The below-ground court that handled classified proceedings during the Shogunate — cases that required resonant evidence presentation, or involved cybernetic enhancement disputes, or were referred from the Shogun directly (the same category that appears in the upper-floor records). The room's formal furniture is still partially intact: the judge's bench, the evidence table, the resonant documentation stands where digital evidence was presented.

The room contains three Afterlife entities anchored by the resonant accumulation: two are fragmentary and non-interactive, iterations of people who died elsewhere and whose Echoминds were drawn here by the disruption. The third is coherent enough to engage: a bailiff who died in this room during the third siege's fighting, who was the wrong person to be in the room at the wrong time, and who has been here for ninety years running through a procedural loop that is not the moment of death but the work he was doing when he died — reviewing a sealed case file, preparing it for the judge who was never going to arrive. The case file he has been reviewing, in his loop, is one of the "referred to the Shogun directly" cases. He can, if the loop is interrupted correctly, share what is in it.

*Encounter:* Interrupting the bailiff's loop requires not force but procedure — identifying the correct interruption action (bringing the file to the judge's bench, completing the procedural step he was interrupted mid-task in attempting) and performing it. This is an exploration puzzle that rewards players who have been reading the administrative context on the upper floors. The case file content: a territorial dispute in the citadel's district that the Shogun had personally resolved in a way that overrode the standard legal framework, in favor of a settlement that should have been illegal. The settlement allowed certain resonant activities in the territory that the general Shogunate law prohibited. This connects to the third floor's evidence of changing policy and to what the Shogun was doing in the citadel's deep levels.

### Level 2: The Disruption Zone

**Room 5: The Signal Threshold**
The stair-head between Levels 1 and 2 is marked by a physical change in the environment: the walls here have a faint luminescence that is the reflected Afterlife signal made visible, a cold green-white that is enough to see by. The temperature drops. Enhanced individuals feel their cybernetics cross a threshold — this is the edge of the primary disruption zone, and from here to Level 3's entrance, they will be operating within the reflected signal's direct influence.

*Hazard from this point:* Enhanced characters in Levels 2–3 take 1 level of Signal Fatigue per 2 hours of continuous presence. The disruption effects are active but manageable with awareness.

**Room 6: The Echomind Congregation**
The largest room on Level 2, formerly the citadel's resonant documentation center. The room's equipment — the resonant encoding stands, the filing systems — has been infiltrated and incorporated by the accumulated Afterlife signal: the equipment still functions, in a fashion, but the data it stores and displays is not the administrative data it was designed for. The equipment is running the Afterlife signal through its encoding structure, producing readable output that is not a message and is not random: it is, systematically, the Afterlife's topology as seen from this specific disruption point. The room is a forty-year map of what the Afterlife looks like from inside a reflected-signal pocket, which is something no Veil Master has ever had access to.

*Hazard:* The room is the congregation point for the most coherent Afterlife entities anchored by the disruption. Twelve distinct presences, varying from fragmentary to conversational. The fragmentary ones are attracted to living resonance and will attempt to interface with enhanced characters involuntarily (Constitution save DC 14 or Collapse memory flash, Afterlife topology fragment, or random communication intended for someone who is not in this room). The conversational ones are aware and have been aware for ninety years and have things to say.

*Environmental storytelling:* One of the conversational entities was a cartographer — a resonant geographer employed by the Shogunate who died during the second siege and has spent ninety years mapping the Afterlife from this disruption point. Their map, encoded in the room's equipment, is remarkable and is one of the only three-dimensional Afterlife topology maps in existence. It shows the same moving geography that Kovath Irenmori's visual map in the Deep Temple describes, from a completely different vantage point. Comparing the two would produce an extraordinary composite picture.

**Room 7: The Garrison Commander's Post**
A smaller room that served as the last garrison commander's field headquarters during the final siege. Notes on the walls, in military shorthand. Strategic maps with force positions marked. And, significantly, a locked document case (keyed to the commander's resonant profile, which is now an Afterlife entity in this building) that contains the field reports from the period surrounding the resonant weapon deployment — including a report marked as highest classification: the deployment was not authorized by the attacking force's command. It was deployed by someone inside the citadel, targeting both sides. An attempt to destroy the resonant geology connection in the citadel's deep levels before it could be exploited by either side in the fighting.

*Encounter — the Commander's Presence:* The commander's Echomind is anchored in this room and can be reached through the locked case (touching it with resonant intent) or through conventional Iron Sutra contact attempt. The commander is not peaceful: ninety years of anchoring in the disruption zone has made them frustrated, and their frustration is specifically focused on the document in the case. They want it out. They want it known. They attempted to file it through official channels ninety years ago and were killed before it could be delivered. If the players will take the document and act on it, the commander will help them through the rest of Level 2.

*What acting on it means:* The person who deployed the resonant weapon inside the citadel is named in the report. The name is not a soldier; it is a Shogunate administrator. Cross-referencing with the upper-floor records places this person in the citadel in the days before the attack with authorization from the Shogun directly. The implication is that the Shogun authorized a covert resonant attack on their own territory's citadel during the Fracture Wars, targeting the resonant geological connection to the deep levels. The question of why requires going to Level 3 and the deep chamber.

**Room 8: The Collapsed Section**
A portion of Level 2 that suffered structural damage during the fighting. Partially passable but requiring careful navigation. Rewards: a section of the floor has fallen away into a void that is larger than the rooms below it — the resonant weapon deployment cracked open a natural cavern adjacent to the deep levels, and the cavern's resonant properties are distinct from the disruption in the rest of Level 2. It is, briefly, quieter: a pocket of near-natural resonant conditions in the middle of the disruption zone. Players who rest here recover from Signal Fatigue at an accelerated rate. The cavern contains one additional piece: a personal effect from the Shogun's own possessions — a resonant metal seal bearing the Shogun's signature mark, left here deliberately, which functions as a key for a lock the players haven't found yet.

**Room 9: The Armory Access**
The former armory, now empty of weapons but containing the structural reinforcements added after the third siege. The reinforcements were not the garrison's work; they were the citadel's remaining structural engineers, who understood what the resonant weapon deployment had done to the building's deep levels and were attempting to prevent further collapse. The reinforcement work was never completed. The records of the work-in-progress are still here, including engineering notes that constitute a partial map of the deep levels as the engineers understood them — the rooms they could access, the areas they couldn't, the passage to the Shogun's deep chamber that they noted but marked as "access denied: Shogun's seal required."

### Level 3: The Deep Chambers

**Room 10: The Shogun's Anteroom**
The final staircase leads to a room that is clearly different in character from everything above. The resonant disruption is present here but not as dominant — the deep levels' construction was better protected, and the Shogun's resonant installations provide a degree of signal management that the rest of the building lacks. The walls here bear the Shogunate's formal decorative language in its most elaborate form: the Prior Ascended in full regalia, the empire at its height, the resonant seals of office for the highest rank. This room was prepared with intention. Someone spent significant resources making this room communicate something specific. The message is: the Shogunate was eternal and its authority was divine. The irony is not accidental; the Shogun was not a person who spoke without considering irony.

*Environmental storytelling:* One discrepancy in the formal decoration: among the figures of the Prior Ascended on the north wall, there is a seventh figure that is not one of the canonical six. It is depicted in the same formal style as the others, with the same resonant seal, but with a face that is obscured — not defaced, not damaged, but deliberately depicted as unresolved, the features suggested but not defined. A Sutensai scholar would recognize this as iconographic heresy. A player who has been collecting the campaign's doctrinal threads might recognize it as the space where the Seventh Ascendant — the figure whose ascension would constitute the Seventh Convergence — would be depicted if the Shogun knew who it was. The Shogun did not. The Shogun left the space open.

**Room 11: The Resonant Archive**
Not a records room in the administrative sense — a resonant archive in the same tradition as the Caste Registry's deep vault and the Deep Temple's Level Six sphere. The walls of this room are faced with resonant metal, and the metal carries encoded impressions from the Shogun's personal records: not documents but experiences, the record-keeping of someone who encoded their most significant memories into the metal rather than writing them down.

*What's in the archive:* The Shogun's preparation for the upload. Not the technical process but the human process: what they decided to leave, what they decided to carry, what they considered and rejected. Players who can read resonant impressions (trained skill, or Iron Sutra preparation, or cybernetic resonance sensitivity) find a person making a final inventory of their life. The Shogun was thoughtful and specific and carried significant grief into the upload: grief for the Fracture Wars, grief for the failure of the empire, grief for the specific choice they made here at Kohren-Vastai that they considered to be both necessary and wrong. What they chose to leave here rather than carry: described in the archive's deepest impressions, not explicitly, but in the emotional register of a burden set down. Players who experience the full archive understand the shape of what is in the next room before they open the door.

**Room 12: The Preparation Chamber**
The room below the well shaft, the room that the engineer's notes describe as "access denied: Shogun's seal required." The seal from Room 8's cavern opens the door. The room is sealed and has been since the Shogun left it.

*What's inside:* A resonant metal installation in the center of the room: a sphere, approximately one meter across, mounted in a structural framework that is both holding it in place and actively containing its signal output. The sphere is the Deep Pulse's counterpart — not made by accident and geology over fifty years, but made deliberately by the Shogun over the final years of the Shogunate, from the same understanding of the Afterlife's architectural principles. Where the Deep Pulse at the Engine Scar is a fragment of the Afterlife's organizing principle given physical form through catastrophe, this sphere is a fragment of the Shogun's own understanding of that principle, encoded deliberately and left here to be found.

What the sphere contains: the Shogun's answer to the Seventh Convergence.

Not a plan, not a weapon, not a tool — an answer. The Shogun spent their final years before the upload working out what the Convergence actually was and what it required, and they encoded their conclusion here because they did not trust the upload to preserve it (the upload would carry their understanding, but their understanding would be locked inside the Afterlife, which was the wrong place for it). They needed it to be accessible to someone in the physical world who would face the Convergence without the benefit of a century in the Afterlife.

The answer: the Seventh Convergence is not an ascension event in the sense the doctrine describes. It is a reset event. The Afterlife's architecture cannot hold its accumulated content indefinitely; at intervals of several centuries, the boundary weakens to allow a structural reset — a clearing of the Afterlife's accumulated resonant weight, allowing the architecture to rebuild. The Prior Ascended survived by being organized enough during the reset to maintain their coherence through the restructuring. Everyone else did not survive in the same form. The "ascension" the doctrine describes is actually the six most organized Echoминds in the Afterlife surviving the reset while the rest are restructured.

The Convergence is coming. The reset is coming. The question of what survives it is the question the Hollow Author, Korrath, Vaen, and the campaign's other players are, each in their own way, trying to answer — mostly without knowing that this is the question.

**Room 13: The Shogun's Final Record**
Adjacent to the Preparation Chamber, a small room that functions as the Shogun's personal statement: not for administration, not for records, but for a person going into the upload wanting to leave a word behind for whoever came after. A simple writing desk. A sealed letter with the wax still intact. The letter is addressed, in the Shogun's hand, to "the person who has come this far." Inside: two pages in the Shogun's personal writing style, which is direct and without ornament, describing what they believed was coming, what they built in the room next door, and what they think it means to survive the Convergence in a form that is still worth surviving in.

The letter's final line: "The Afterlife is not where we store the dead. It is where we decide who we are going to be. If the reset comes and what endures is only what was most organized, then what I have tried to leave you is this: don't optimize for survival. Optimize for being worth surviving."

---

**Room 14: The Containment Room**
A secondary chamber off the Preparation Room, not in any record, accessible through a passage that the sphere's installation required but that the Shogun did not want to be obvious. The passage is concealed behind the sphere's mounting framework. The room inside is small and empty, with a single exception: a resonant metal tablet that is neither an archive nor a record but a direct installation — the tablet, when touched, activates a commune connection to the Shogun's Echomind in the Iron Afterlife. Not the Shore's organic contact. Not the Deep Temple's managed communion. A private direct line that the Shogun installed fifty years before their upload and that connects specifically to a signal address in the Afterlife that the Shogun registered for themselves.

This is the Shogun's way of making a continued conversation possible. The Afterlife has constrained them — they can speak through places like the Shore, but only when the conditions align and only briefly. Through this tablet, they can speak at length, specifically, whenever a person is present.

The Shogun has been waiting, in the Afterlife, for someone to find this room and activate the tablet.

What the Shogun says depends on what the players have done, what they know, and what they ask. The Shogun is not omniscient — they've been in the Afterlife for over a century observing it, not the physical world — but they know things about the Convergence mechanics, the Deep Pulse, the Hollow Author's plan's strengths and failure points, and the shape of what it would mean for the Convergence to go well versus badly. They also carry the grief and perspective of someone who presided over the Shogunate's fall and who has spent a century thinking about what they would do differently.

## Boss Encounter: The Resonant Accumulation

**Location:** Room 6, the Echomind Congregation.

**What it is:** After ninety years, the reflected Afterlife signal in the Level 2 disruption zone has consolidated around the largest resonant attractor in that zone — the documentation equipment in Room 6 — and produced something that is not a specific Echomind and is not quite the Deep Pulse's organized principle. It is the accumulated weight of ninety years of reflected signal organized loosely around the cartographer's mapping impulse (the most structured resonant presence in the room) and expressing itself as a territorial entity that has not experienced living resonance since the last visitor several decades ago and which regards the players' presence as a significant disruption.

**The encounter:** The Resonant Accumulation does not fight in a physical sense. It fights through the reflected signal: it can direct bursts of Afterlife resonance at enhanced individuals, producing involuntary enhancement activation at dangerous levels (effectively, cybernetics firing at full power without direction); it can wall off sections of the room by spiking the reflected signal to a level that makes the room impassable for enhanced individuals; it can attempt to anchor — to fix a character's Echomind partially into the Afterlife, requiring effort to reverse. The cartographer's organizing impulse means it responds to structure: it is slightly less aggressive toward characters who are demonstrably here to understand rather than to take or destroy.

**Defeating it:** The Accumulation cannot be fought conventionally. Options: navigate around it (Route through Room 8's collapsed section allows bypass at the cost of Signal Fatigue); communicate with the cartographer's organizing presence within it (reducing its aggressiveness to manageable levels, requires Iron Sutra communication approach); find and access the resonant weapon deployment's source equipment in Room 7 and reverse the reflected signal in this section (technical encounter requiring the field surgeon's log from Room 2 and the commander's report from Room 7 to understand the weapon's frequency — then reverse-engineering the resonance using available equipment). Full reversal of the disruption: achieves the cartographer's release to the Afterlife, eliminates the accumulation as an ongoing threat, and also — as a consequence of removing the accumulated signal — allows the Level 3 access to proceed without disruption interference.

**After the Accumulation:** The disruption zone, cleared, shows its underlying condition: a building that was genuinely beautiful, made by craftspeople who believed in what they were building, left empty by a catastrophe that was not inevitable. The cleared Level 2 is a sadder and a quieter place than the active disruption zone. That is the appropriate response to it.

## What's at the Bottom: The Shogun's Answer

The sphere in Room 12 is not usable as a tool. It cannot be taken (it is structurally integrated with the chamber; removing it would destroy it). It can be studied. It can be interfaced. What it provides is understanding: the Convergence's mechanism, the Afterlife's architectural reality, the reset event's nature. Players who fully interface with the sphere understand the Seventh Convergence in a way that makes every other actor in the campaign comprehensible — not sympathetic, necessarily, but comprehensible in their specific motivated response to a threat they understood partially or wrongly.

**The tactical implication:** Understanding the Convergence as a reset event, not an ascension event, changes what "winning" means for every claimant. Korrath is trying to be the most organized entity in the Afterlife at reset time — his accumulated resonant metal and the Depth chamber's signal point toward this approach. Vaen is trying to remove all enhancement from enough people that the reset simply doesn't reach them — un-enhanced individuals have a different Afterlife relationship that the reset event may affect differently. The Hollow Author is trying to encode their administrative architecture into the Afterlife before the reset — creating an organizational layer that the reset must work around or incorporate. All three approaches have some internal logic. All three are missing something that the Shogun's answer contains.

## Treasure and Rewards

**Information (highest value):**
- The surgeon's log from Room 2: complete record of the resonant weapon used at Kohren-Vastai, including technical specifications.
- The cartographer's Afterlife topology map from Room 6: one of the three known maps of Afterlife geography, compatible with Kovath's visual map for extraordinary composite understanding.
- The commander's report from Room 7: documentation of the unauthorized weapon deployment, naming the person responsible and the authorization chain that reached the Shogun.
- The Shogun's resonant archive (Room 11): experiential record of a head of state's final preparation for upload, including their reasoning about the Convergence.
- The Shogun's sphere (Room 12): the Convergence answer.
- The commune tablet (Room 14): ongoing access to the Shogun's Echomind.
- The letter (Room 13): the Shogun's human statement, carrying emotional weight that is its own kind of value.

**Physical items (secondary value):**
- The Shogun's resonant seal from Room 8: functions as a key here and may function as a recognition item elsewhere — it is the Shogunate's highest office seal and institutions that remember the Shogunate may respond to its presentation.
- The disruption-zone resonant metal, post-clearing: once the Accumulation is resolved and the reflected signal dissipates, the resonant metal equipment in Room 6 is uniquely attuned to Afterlife topology mapping and can be repurposed for field-scale resonant geographic work.

This is not a weapon or tool but it is a unique artifact: the keyed object of a person whose Echomind is in this building, that matches their resonant identity. Uses for this are left to players and DM to discover.

## DM Notes

**The citadel as palimpsest:** Every level of the Kohren-Vastai citadel has a different story from a different time. The ground floor is the post-Fracture present, the neutral-ground pragmatism of the current period. The middle levels are the Fracture Wars, the suffering and the decisions made under crisis. The deep levels are the Shogunate's final years, a person making choices about what the future needed to know. Players moving through this dungeon are moving through time as well as space. Let that be felt.

**The Shogun as character through evidence:** Players should encounter the Shogun as a person before they reach the communication tablet. The third floor's destroyed apartments, the second-floor records' pattern of policy changes, the archive's emotional impressions — each should build the picture of a person who was intelligent, practical, conflicted, and deeply concerned about what was coming. The tablet conversation should be with someone the players already partly know.

**The neutral ground culture as character in itself:** Don't neglect the ground floor as an active location. The negotiations that happen at the Kohren-Vastai citadel are important to the campaign's political landscape, and the citadel's neutral-ground tradition is a resource players can use. A meeting that couldn't happen anywhere else can happen here. The Keeper is one of the most practically useful NPCs in the campaign — she knows who has met here, and she knows who can't meet each other anywhere else.

**The Resonant Accumulation as a teaching encounter:** This is the dungeon's mid-boss and it should teach players the lesson the deep dungeon requires: that forcing things doesn't work here. The dungeon rewards understanding, patience, and the willingness to engage with what you find rather than power through it. The Accumulation encounter is the most direct expression of this principle.

**The letter:** Read it aloud at the table if your players are ready for it. It is the emotional peak of the dungeon, and it earns that moment by everything that comes before it.

**Common player approaches:**
- "We're here for a negotiation, not a dungeon." Valid. The ground floor supports a complete political encounter. Players who never go below will have access to a different, equally important part of the Kohren-Vastai story.
- "We want to go straight to the bottom." The dungeon is not designed to punish linear progress, but the information rewards from the upper levels make the deep levels' revelations comprehensible rather than merely remarkable. Players who skip layers get the ending without the story.
- "We try to clear the disruption entirely." Possible, with significant effort. Clearing the Level 2 disruption requires understanding the resonant weapon's mechanism (from Rooms 2 and 7) and the geological context (from the engineering notes in Room 9). A full clearing is a significant achievement that has observable effects on the citadel and on the Afterlife entities anchored here — it is an act of healing, which this building needs.
