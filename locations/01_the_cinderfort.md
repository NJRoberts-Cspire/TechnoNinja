# The Cinderfort
*Ironhold Reach | Type: Fortress-City, Political Capital*

## "You don't see the Cinderfort until you've been inside it. You only see the wall."
*— Merchant Teiva Orun, on her first approach to the Ironhold capital*

---

## Overview

The Cinderfort is the oldest continuously occupied military installation in Tesshari, a city that was never designed to be a city and never forgot it. It began as a fortification, became a garrison, became a settlement, became a capital — and at every stage of that growth, it added another wall. Four centuries of concentric defensive rings have produced something that functions less like a city than like a series of nested sieges, each inner ring fortified against the outer, each district a potential fallback position should the one before it fall. Two million people live inside those walls. Almost none of them can tell you exactly how many walls there are.

This matters to the campaign because the Cinderfort is Korrath's claim made physical. Everything he represents — permanence through force, hierarchy through tested strength, the idea that suffering and survival are the same thing — is encoded in the architecture before you ever meet a single soldier. The city is an argument, and it has been making that argument for four hundred years. Players who come here will feel it: the weight of the place, the way every street angles toward a killing ground, the way the inner districts grow quieter and more controlled the closer you get to the Command Spire. The city is not hostile to visitors. It is merely indifferent to them in a way that feels exactly like hostility.

Stories that happen in the Cinderfort are stories about power: accessing it, surviving it, the cost of living under it without becoming it. The city rewards the patient and punishes the conspicuous. There are people here who want to help the players. There are people here who will sell them to Korrath for a caste advancement. Both groups live in the same neighborhoods, eat at the same food stalls, and you cannot tell them apart on sight. That is the Cinderfort's real defense: not the walls, but the citizens who have learned to believe in them.

## History

The Cinderfort was built by Warlord-Regent Ahemdi Korrath-Progenitor in the second century of the Fracture Wars, on the site of a previous fortification that had been destroyed four times. The Progenitor's innovation was the doctrine of the layered wall: rather than building one strong wall, build many walls each of which could serve as a rally point. The city grew inward as much as outward. When the first outer ring was completed, a second inner ring was begun. When the second was complete, a third. This is why the oldest districts are at the center and the newest at the perimeter — Cinderfort builds from the inside out, always, always protecting the core. The military and administrative elite live at the center. The newly arrived, the poor, the un-enhanced, and the foreign live at the walls.

The name comes from an event now called the Cinder Night, sixty years before the current campaign. A coordinated attack by a coalition of three Reaches breached the outer wall and set the market districts on fire. The fire burned for eleven days. When it was finally extinguished, the then-Warlord (Korrath's predecessor, whose name is no longer spoken in the Cinderfort) ordered the ruins rebuilt exactly as they had stood — same street layouts, same building footprints, same market stalls in the same positions. The idea was continuity, defiance, the demonstration that the city could not be changed by outside force. What it actually produced was districts built on ash foundations that still, in wet seasons, smell faintly of old smoke. The current Warlord Korrath was a junior commander during Cinder Night. He was seventeen years old. He does not speak of it, but the Wall of Names in the Second Ring was his commission.

## Atmosphere

**Sight:** The Cinderfort is gray. Not architecturally — individual buildings are painted, signed, decorated — but in aggregate. The stone used for the walls and the major structures is a local iron-bearing granite that oxidizes to a dark gray-red, and there is so much of it that it bleeds into everything. The streets are narrow in the older districts, widened by demolition and rebuilding in the outer rings. Banners hang from almost every building: Ironhold Reach's black and iron-red, but also unit insignia, caste marks, trade guild flags. The visual noise is high. The sky above the inner rings is often obscured by the steam and smoke of the forge-works and by the resonant-metal transmission lines that run between towers like a second city built on cables.

**Sound:** The Cinderfort sounds like work. Hammers, always — the forge districts run day and night. Military drills at dawn and dusk, the commands carrying over the wall-tops even into civilian districts. The resonant-metal lines hum at frequencies that vary with the weather; experienced residents use this as a rough weather forecast. The markets are loud with the practiced volume of vendors who have learned to project over ambient metal-song. In the deep night, the inner rings go genuinely silent — a silence that feels imposed rather than natural.

**Smell:** Iron filings and hot metal from the forges. Cooking oil from the street food stalls in the market rings. Old smoke in the Cinder Districts — faint in summer, stronger when the ground is wet. The organic smell of two million people living in a space designed for military efficiency, not human comfort, punctuated by the antiseptic smell of the enhancement clinics with their resonance-bonding fluids.

**Feel:** The Cinderfort feels like being watched, even when you aren't. This is partially the architecture — sight lines are designed for observation — and partially the truth of the place: in the inner rings, you probably are being watched. The enhanced citizens' cybernetics pick up the hum of the resonant lines and some report a constant low-grade pressure in the bones, as if the city itself exerts weight. First-time visitors often report feeling shorter inside the walls. Veterans of the city report feeling taller.

**Convergence proximity:** As the Seventh Convergence approaches, the resonant lines have begun to occasionally pulse with visible light — dim and reddish, like cooling iron. This happens without warning and lasts between ten seconds and two minutes. No official explanation has been issued. The citizens have begun to time their days around the pulses. Children play games involving them.

## Layout and Districts

The Cinderfort is organized in five concentric rings, each with a distinct character, population, and function. The outermost ring is the newest; the innermost (the Spire Ring) is the oldest. Travel between rings requires passing through the gate-houses built into the inner walls, and each inner wall's gates are more controlled than the last.

---

### Ring One: The Threshold Markets (Outer Ring)

The newest construction in the Cinderfort, the Threshold is also the most chaotic. The outer wall here is still being extended — Korrath's construction projects push it outward every decade — and the districts immediately inside it have that unfinished quality of places that haven't yet decided what they are. Population is highest here: immigrants seeking Ironhold citizenship, merchants waiting for caste clearance, laborers who work the forge-works but can't afford inner housing, soldiers on their first posting. The streets are wide enough for supply wagons, which they were designed for, and lined with the kind of commerce that serves people who don't know how long they'll be staying: cheap food, short-let housing, enhancement clinics that promise quick bonding without the safety margins the better clinics maintain. The enhancement clinics in the Threshold have a higher complication rate than anywhere else in Tesshari. Business is never slow.

The Hollow Poor live here (see Key Locations), pressed against the outer wall in districts that were planned as temporary accommodation and have been temporarily accommodated for sixty years. The Threshold is not lawless — the Ironhold enforcement is present and efficient — but the law here is primarily concerned with preventing things that would threaten the rings inward. Violence that stays in the Threshold tends to resolve itself.

**Key feature:** The Salvage Markets — the largest open-air trading floor for resonant metal components in Tesshari, running along the inside face of the outer wall.

---

### Ring Two: The Cinder Districts

The districts rebuilt after Cinder Night, recognizable by their slightly-too-regular street grids and buildings that sit on stone courses that don't quite match the mortar above them. These are the working-class districts: the forge-workers, the mid-rank soldiers, the craftsmen who support the military-industrial core of the Ironhold. The streets here are narrower than the Threshold by design — the rebuilt layout was optimized for defendability, not flow — and the building codes enforced here require that every structure include defensible upper floors and a ground-level iron shutter system.

The Wall of Names is here (see Key Locations). It is the emotional center of this ring. People come to it at all hours.

The Cinder Districts have a distinct community identity built on the shared mythology of the fire. "Cinder-born" is a term used by families who were here during Cinder Night and rebuilt, and it carries significant social weight even though most of the people using it were born after the event. The Sutensai temple here is the most attended in the city — the dead of Cinder Night are commemorated three times per year, and attendance is effectively mandatory for anyone with social ambitions in the ring.

---

### Ring Three: The Forge Ring

Not primarily residential — this is where the industrial work of the Ironhold happens at scale. Resonant metal smelting, cybernetic component fabrication, weapons production, maintenance of the city's infrastructure systems. The heat here is constant and oppressive; the ring is noticeably warmer than those adjacent to it. The workers who live in the Forge Ring are primarily the senior technicians and bonding-smiths who need proximity to the works, living in housing built into or above the factory structures.

The Forge Ring is administratively controlled separately from the other rings. It is managed by the Bonding Guild, which has a semi-autonomous relationship with Korrath's command — they have the expertise he cannot replace, and they know it. The Bonding Guild Hall in the Forge Ring is the third most powerful institution in the city after the Command Spire itself and the Caste Registry.

The resonant lines are denser here and the hum is louder. Workers with auditory cybernetics often have the sensitivity reduced to cope with it. Enhanced visitors will feel the resonance pressure throughout their stay in this ring. Those sensitive to the Iron Afterlife frequencies report that the Forge Ring has a different quality of ghost-presence than the rest of the city — not more ghosts, but older ones, anchored to the metal of the works.

---

### Ring Four: The Administrative Ring

A distinct transition in character. The streets widen again, the buildings are stone-fronted and maintained, the signage becomes formal. This is where the Ironhold's bureaucratic and military command functions live: the Caste Registry, the military barracks for senior units, the official residence compounds for high-ranking commanders and administrators. The population here is smaller than any of the outer rings — perhaps eighty thousand — but more uniformly enhanced, more uniformly armed, and more uniformly alert.

Access to the Fourth Ring requires either a registered caste mark of the fourth rank or higher, a military pass, or an escort from a registered resident. The gate-houses check these seriously. They also check for concealed weapons above the level of personal defense — this close to the center, the tolerance for ambiguity is low.

The Fourth Ring is notably quiet. The forge-sound doesn't carry this far at conversational volumes. The streets, while maintained, carry relatively little foot traffic. There is a quality of held breath here, a sense of things being preserved rather than lived in.

---

### Ring Five: The Spire Ring (Inner Ring)

The oldest part of the Cinderfort, and the most like what it originally was: a fortress. The buildings here are the original construction, or close to it — four hundred years of maintenance rather than replacement. The stone is darker than the outer rings, worn to smoothness. The streets are the narrowest in the city. The population is perhaps ten thousand: Korrath's personal guard, his household staff, the senior strategists and advisors who require absolute proximity, and a small number of highly placed individuals who have been granted residence as mark of ultimate favor or ultimate control.

The Command Spire rises from the Spire Ring's center. It is visible from anywhere in the city.

Access to the Spire Ring requires a personal authorization that must be renewed weekly, biometric matching to a registered enhancement profile, and an escort from the Command Spire's staff. In practice, if you are in the Spire Ring without belonging there, someone already knows.

## Key Locations

### The Command Spire

The oldest intact structure in the Cinderfort, originally a watchtower, now a command complex rising eleven stories above the inner ring. The lower seven floors are administrative and logistical: the war room, the strategic planning floors, the officer quarters for the senior command staff. The upper four floors are Korrath's.

The exterior is entirely undecorated. This is intentional and was made intentional sixty years ago when Korrath ordered all carved ornamentation removed from the original stonework. The command of the Ironhold requires no symbols, the reasoning goes: the structure is the symbol. The only external marking is a single vertical iron band running from foundation to parapet, twice the width of a man's hand, kept polished to mirror brightness by staff whose sole function this is. It reflects the city back at itself.

The interior of the lower floors is functional military construction: wide corridors, high ceilings designed for large tactical displays, resonant metal throughout for signal transmission. The upper floors — those players are extremely unlikely to access without an invitation or an exceptional plan — are different. Korrath has lived here for decades. The rooms reflect a person who sleeps less than he needs to and reads more than anyone suspects. Maps everywhere, annotated in a hand so small it requires enhancement to read comfortably. The walls of the top floor have been cut open at irregular intervals to create narrow windows that each frame a specific vista of the city below, carefully chosen: the Wall of Names is visible from one, the outer wall from another, the Forge Ring smoke from a third.

Korrath himself: present three out of five days, absent on strategic tours the other two. When present, he maintains a public access hour — any citizen may petition him directly, without intermediary, for one minute. The line is never shorter than four hours.

**Interactive elements:** The war room on the third floor contains a resonant metal tactical display of all seven Reaches — a living map that updates in near-real-time via the signal network. It is both a remarkable intelligence asset and an active security target. Players with the right skills can read far more from this map than Korrath intends to share.

### The Wall of Names

A section of the Second Ring's inner wall, roughly three hundred meters in length, entirely covered in engraved names. The names of everyone confirmed dead in Cinder Night — 31,847 of them, verified against the Caste Registry records before and after. The engraving took nine years. The smallest names, in the highest register, require climbing the maintenance scaffolding to read.

The Wall is maintained by the city — the engraving is deepened every decade to counter weathering — and the space in front of it is kept clear of commerce or obstruction. Vendors set up on the street behind the visitor's path, not facing the Wall.

The ritual practice that has grown up around the Wall is not officially sanctioned and not officially discouraged. People come to find specific names and leave offerings at the base of the wall: candles, food, small objects. The offerings are cleared each dawn by maintenance staff. People come back and leave them again. The base of the Wall has a particular smell — wax, and something sweet that no one has formally identified — that everyone who lives near it associates with grief without knowing why.

There is a name on the Wall that should not be there. Third column from the left, 67th row from the base: the name "Ahemdi Korrath-Progenitor," the founder of the city, dead two centuries before Cinder Night. Someone had it added during the nine years of engraving, and it was not caught before the section was sealed. The maintenance records from that section's engraving are missing. Korrath knows about it. He has made no move to have it changed.

**Environmental storytelling:** The Wall can tell players a great deal about the social dynamics of the Cinder Districts if they look for it. The names in certain sections are clustered by family — whole families gone, entire extended households — while other sections are scattered individuals. The Hollow Poor districts are represented, disproportionately.

### The Salvage Markets

The economic engine of the Threshold, and one of the most important trade sites in the Ironhold. The Salvage Markets run along nearly a kilometer of the outer wall's inner face, partially sheltered by permanent canvas and iron structures, partially open to sky. Everything resonant-metal related that is not produced by the Forge Ring comes through here: salvaged components from the Ashlands, decommissioned military enhancement rigs, illegal imports from other Reaches, and the full gray-market range of enhancement work that the Bonding Guild hasn't officially certified.

The Markets operate from before dawn to after midnight in three distinct shifts, each with different goods and different clientele. The dawn shift is legitimate salvage — Ashlands runners selling to certified buyers. The day shift is the general market, the busiest, with the widest variety. The night shift is where the interesting things change hands, and the enforcement presence is carefully, deliberately insufficient.

Vendors are identified by their stall numbers and their specialties, both of which are public record, but the actual goods on offer at any given time are not. A vendor registered as selling "structural resonant metal, Grade C-D salvage" may have in their back stock a functioning military-grade enhancement rig stripped from a dead officer, a Convergence-frequency tuner whose origin no one is asking about, or a resonant metal component with a dead person's Echomind still partially encoded in it. The Markets deal in objects; what the objects contain is the buyer's problem.

**Encounter potential:** The night shift of the Salvage Markets is one of the best places in the Cinderfort to gather intelligence, acquire unusual equipment, or make contacts who don't want to be found. It is also a place where other people are doing those same things, and the vendor stalls have eyes.

### The Caste Registry

The administrative record of every person living in the Ironhold Reach. Not just name and caste rank — enhancement records, military service, family connections, debt status, criminal record, and the continuously updated "resonance profile" that tracks the nature and condition of every registered cybernetic enhancement. The Registry is housed in a building that takes up an entire city block of the Administrative Ring, and it employs twelve hundred people full-time.

The physical building is a labyrinth of purpose-built record rooms, most of them climate-controlled against humidity to preserve the older paper records that predate resonant metal encoding. The deeper rooms contain records going back to the city's founding. The oldest records are in a preservation vault in the building's basement that has not been opened in forty years — the resonant metal preservation system for that section was upgraded and the access protocols were, through bureaucratic accident, never updated to reflect the new system's requirements.

What the Caste Registry means for players: it contains information about almost everyone in the Ironhold. It also contains a systematic record of the social engineering Korrath has been quietly conducting for decades — the patterns of who gets promoted, who gets denied enhancement access, who disappears from the records entirely. A player who knows what to look for, and has access, can read the outline of Korrath's actual governance philosophy in the Registry's data. It is different from the philosophy he articulates publicly.

**The basement vault:** The preservation vault that hasn't been opened in forty years contains the original pre-Cinder Night records — including the enhancement profiles of everyone who died in the fire. And, notably, records of at least three people who appear in those profiles as present in the Cinderfort before Cinder Night, whose names do not appear on the Wall of Names, and who do not appear in any post-Night records. They are neither confirmed dead nor confirmed surviving. The Registry staff don't know this discrepancy exists. Korrath does.

### The Military Barracks (Fourth Ring Garrison)

The primary garrison for the Ironhold's active-duty forces is a compound of twelve interconnected buildings in the Administrative Ring, housing approximately eight thousand soldiers in rotating postings. The compound has its own forge-works, enhancement clinic, drill yards, and the unofficial institution of every military garrison: the mess hall that doubles as the social hub where everything gets said that doesn't go in reports.

The barracks' physical environment is intentionally standardized — uniformity as value — but the soldiers inhabiting it are not. The Ironhold draws from every social class, and the barracks mix them in the same bunks and the same mess lines. The social dynamics that result are complex, often volatile, and enormously revealing about the actual state of Korrath's military. Senior officers have their own quarters in a separate wing; the junior ranks are where the real information lives.

The mess hall operates from before dawn to after midnight. Players who can pass as soldiers or contractors, or who have contacts in the garrison, can access the mess hall and hear things: troop movement discussions, complaints about supply problems, rumors about what happened on the last patrol into the Ashlands, the underground betting pool on which of the three Ascension claimants will still be standing in six months. The betting pool has Vaen winning at 3:1 odds. The mess hall has learned not to discuss why.

### The Sutensai Temple of the Forge Ring

The largest temple in the Cinderfort and one of the most complex in the Ironhold, the Forge Ring temple sits against the inner wall of Ring Three at a location that was apparently chosen because the resonant line density there is highest — a decision that makes the temple an active amplifier for Iron Afterlife signals. The priests here do not enter a trance state to commune with the dead; they don't need to. The Afterlife is close enough to bleed through.

The temple is three-tiered. The public ground floor holds the standard devotional functions: the Echo-Altars where people leave offerings for their dead, the resonant pools where enhanced individuals can attempt (inconsistently, imperfectly) to feel for the presence of lost loved ones, the Iron Sutra readings that happen seven times daily. The second tier is for priests and initiated laypeople: deeper communion chambers, the enhancement clinics where the temple performs its own bonding ceremonies, the archive of Iron Sutra texts. The third tier is never open to the public, and the priests deflect questions about it with practiced ease.

The third tier is where the priests who've received what they call "complete contact" with the Iron Afterlife are housed. They receive complete contact and they do not come back the way they left. They are not dead. They are not insane. They sit in their chambers and they hold a quality of absolute stillness that other people find deeply unsettling, and every few days one of them says something that is directly applicable to a situation happening elsewhere in the city that they have no way of knowing about. The senior priests regard this as holy. The junior priests regard it as frightening. Both groups are right.

---

### The Hollow Poor Districts

Against the outer wall, in the gap between the Threshold's commercial districts and the wall itself, are the districts that have never been formally named — they appear in the Registry as "Outer Settlement Zones A through F" but residents call them the Hollow Poor, a term that refers simultaneously to poverty and to the absence of cybernetic enhancement. In Ironhold ideology, enhancement is the means of advancement; those without enhancements are those who have not yet advanced, or who have failed to advance, or who have been assessed as ineligible for the advancement program. The Hollow Poor districts are where that ideology's failures live.

The population here is perhaps three hundred thousand in permanent residence, with a floating transient population that can double that number. They are primarily un-enhanced, or enhanced with failed or degraded systems that provide liability without benefit. The housing is built into and against the outer wall itself — the wall's interior face provides the back wall of row upon row of stacked living units, so the city's primary defense structure is literally the backdrop of the poorest people's daily lives. The symbolism is not lost on the residents.

The Hollow Poor districts are not lawless. They are governed by a combination of the official enforcement apparatus (which is genuinely present, though less thorough than in inner rings) and a parallel system of local authority that the official apparatus deals with through strategic ambiguity. The local authority figures are a mix of community leaders, criminal organizers, and former military who washed out of the advancement system and landed here. They know everyone. They are the reason the official apparatus doesn't have to work very hard in some areas, and the reason it works very hard in others.

**The wall itself as environmental storytelling:** The outer wall bears, on its inner face, the handprints of residents going back decades. This is technically illegal, technically removed during maintenance cycles, and technically never actually removed because the maintenance crews who do the work are from the Hollow Poor districts and do not remove the handprints. Some are painted, some are actual handprints in mortar or clay, some appear to be burned in at temperatures the residents' equipment shouldn't be able to produce. The maintenance records describe this section as "in progress."

### The Depth (Secret Location)

Beneath the Spire Ring, accessed through a door in the Command Spire's basement that does not appear in any architectural record of the building and is not reachable via the official basement layout, there is a chamber that the Cinderfort was built around. Not after the fact — the chamber predates the Cinderfort's founding by at least a century, possibly much more. The original fortification was built at this location because the Progenitor's resonant-sensitive advisors told him this was where the signal was strongest: a natural amplification point, a place where the boundary between physical reality and the Iron Afterlife is structurally thinner.

The chamber is roughly twenty meters across and entirely natural — no cut stone, no constructed elements except a single resonant metal column that was installed by someone and at some time that the records don't capture. The column is old enough to predate the enhancement bonding techniques that are standard practice; it was installed using a method that current bonding-smiths recognize as theoretically valid but practically impossible without equipment that no longer exists. The column resonates constantly. The Iron Afterlife signal in the chamber is not "strong" — it is immediate. The dead are here in ways they are not elsewhere.

Korrath visits the Depth alone, irregularly, and tells no one when he goes. The Command Spire staff know about the visits the way households know about things they have agreed not to discuss. What he does there, what he hears, what the dead have told him — if anything — is unknown. The chamber's walls bear the evidence of very long use: faded markings in at least six different hands across what appears to be several centuries, the meanings of most of which have been lost, and one set of marks that appears recent and is in a notation system that resembles — but is not identical to — the resonant encoding used by current Veil Masters.

## Encounters

### The Petition Line

**Trigger:** Players are in the Spire Ring or have business with Korrath's administration. Or they observe the daily public access hour from outside.

**Setup:** Players arrive at the Command Spire's public entrance to find the petition line already formed — several hundred people stretching back from the entrance, moving with the glacial patience of those who have been here before and know the pace. The line is a cross-section of the Cinderfort: Cinder District families with inheritance disputes, Forge Ring bonding-smiths with Guild complaints, a cluster of Threshold residents with a collective petition about enhancement clinic safety standards, and, significantly, a woman in Fourth Ring dress standing three places behind a man whose entire left side of his face is subsurface resonant metal — the mark of a high-level combat enhancement — who is methodically memorizing everyone in the line.

**Complexity:** The woman is watching the enhanced man. The enhanced man is watching the entrance. He is not here to petition. Players who examine him closely see that his resonant metal is running hot in ways consistent with pre-activation states — he is armed, in the cybernetic sense, and he is waiting for something. The woman is Korrath's security, and she has already clocked him, and she is waiting for him to commit to something she can act on. The petition line is about to become an incident.

**Resolution options:**
- Players intervene to stop the enhanced man before he acts: Korrath's security is grateful but suspicious — who are these people, and how did they know? This opens a door to a guarded conversation with the security apparatus.
- Players warn the woman about the man in a way that tips her to act: she has him removed without incident. The petition line reforms. No one says anything. Korrath learns about this; players receive an invitation to the non-public petition process.
- Players do nothing and let events develop: the man makes his move. He is a failed assassination attempt — not talented enough, clearly out of his depth, and his failure is messy and public. He survives. The petition line is canceled. Korrath is not present but his absence during the incident is noted in ways that reach the players later.
- Players try to extract information from the man before/during/after: he is from the Hollow Poor districts, he is enhanced with a salvage rig from the Markets that is not properly bonded and is slowly killing him, and his petition would have been about his daughter's denial from the enhancement advancement program. He is not a trained assassin. He is a person who decided this was the only thing he had left. He did not expect to survive it.

**Consequence:** How the players handle this shapes their relationship with Korrath's security apparatus throughout the campaign. The man himself, if he survives and can be found, knows things about the Hollow Poor districts' underground economy that are useful. The woman who was Korrath's security is named Vethis Ahemdi-Crane, a distant Progenitor line descendant who has served the Spire for twenty years, and she does not forget.

### The Night Market Find

**Trigger:** Players visit the Salvage Markets during the night shift.

**Setup:** A vendor the players have been directed toward — by someone who described them as reliable — is not at their stall. The stall is empty but not abandoned; the locking mechanism is disengaged, the goods are still present, and there is a small resonant metal component sitting in the center of the display table that is vibrating at a frequency the players' enhanced members can feel in their bones. It is encoding something. The something it is encoding is a fragment of an Echomind — a partial digital spirit — that is trying to communicate, and it is using the component as the nearest available medium because it has no other options.

**Complexity:** The Echomind is fragmentary and distressed. It knows two things: a name (a senior official in the Caste Registry) and a location (a number that resolves to a record vault). It has been trying to encode these things into this component for what fragments of its temporal awareness suggest is weeks. The vendor who was supposed to receive this is dead — players can find evidence of this if they look, in the form of a body in the maintenance passages behind the stall wall, two days old. The vendor was killed before he could receive the Echomind's message. By whom is a question the Echomind cannot answer. The resonant component will not survive being moved very far — the encoding is unstable. Players have a few hours to act on the information or lose it.

**Resolution options:** What the Registry record contains (if players reach it) is a systematic record of Caste Registry falsification — deliberate misclassification of Hollow Poor residents to keep them ineligible for the advancement program, signed off by the named official who is still alive and still working, under orders from a command-chain that players can trace if they are careful and lucky. The information is significant. The question is what they do with it.

**Consequence:** Sitting on the information does nothing. Bringing it to Korrath directly has unpredictable results — he may already know, in which case the players have learned something important about him. Bringing it to the Hollow Poor district leadership creates an obligation. Releasing it through the Market's informal information channels causes a political incident within the Registry that takes weeks to resolve and permanently changes the Registry official's situation.

### The Temple Third Tier

**Trigger:** Players are investigating the Sutensai temple, have a reason to access the restricted third tier, or have attracted the attention of one of the complete-contact priests.

**Setup:** One of the third-tier residents — a woman named Ahori who entered the temple eighteen years ago as a junior priest and completed contact within her first year, and has not left the third tier since — has begun leaving the same message in different locations for different people: a fragment of Iron Sutra notation that translates, roughly, as "the counting is wrong." She leaves this for the temple's senior priests, for Forge Ring bonding-smiths who come for blessing services, and, now, for the players, delivered through whatever channel is most immediate — a folded paper in a player's pocket, the phrase spoken by a child who has no idea what it means, carved into the surface of a table at a place the players are eating.

**Complexity:** Ahori is not in a state where she can be interviewed conventionally. She exists in the complete-contact stillness, but her communications are becoming more urgent and more directed. The senior priests are alarmed. They interpret her communications as a religious matter, which it may be. Players who can find a way to communicate with Ahori — resonance commune, careful questioning of the third-tier care staff, accessing the temple's communication records — find a person who is receiving Iron Afterlife data in a way that has been overwhelming her for eighteen years, and who has just received something new and specific: the Iron Afterlife's population count is increasing faster than the death rate justifies. Something is adding Echoминds to the Afterlife that are not being generated by deaths. Ahori doesn't know what this means. She knows the numbers don't work.

**Resolution options:** Players can ignore Ahori, can find ways to communicate with her and receive the information, can report her increasing activity to the senior priests, or can try to bring a Veil Master's expertise to bear on her situation. Each path reveals a different aspect of the Convergence problem.

**Consequence:** The information about Afterlife population inflation is a major lore piece for the Seventh Convergence. It connects directly to the Hollow Author's work in the Preparation Halls.

### The Registry Basement

**Trigger:** Players have reason to access the Caste Registry's old records, or are investigating the discrepancies indicated by the night market encounter.

**Setup:** Getting into the basement vault requires either bureaucratic access (a slow process that will take days and may flag the investigation) or a more direct approach (the Registry building's maintenance passages connect to the basement through access points that are not in the public architectural record, because they were added by the resonant preservation system's installation crew twenty years ago and the documentation was filed incorrectly — it exists, it's just in the wrong place). Once in the vault, players find the preservation system in a state of partial failure: the resonant metal field that maintains the records is running at reduced capacity because of a power draw issue that has not been diagnosed. The records are mostly intact. The system's reduced operation has, however, created small gaps in the field — areas of the vault where the resonant containment lapses.

**Complexity:** In those gaps, the oldest records — the pre-Cinder Night materials — have been accumulating something. Not decay. The opposite: the records in the lapsed-field areas have been, slowly and consistently, updating themselves. The handwriting is not from the original scribes. The additions are being made by something that has access to the Registry's information through the Iron Afterlife interface that the resonant field, at full power, would suppress. The dead are filing amendments. Some of the amendments correct factual errors in original records. Some add information that the original scribes couldn't have had. One section — the section covering the three people who should be dead but aren't in the records — has been heavily amended, and the amendments are in a notation system the players may recognize from the Depth chamber beneath the Spire.

**Resolution options:** The players can read the amendments, can disrupt or restore the preservation field, can try to communicate with whatever entity is making the amendments, or can report the situation upward (which will result in the lapse being sealed and the amendments being classified, which is itself informative). The amendments, taken together, describe something that happened on Cinder Night that is not in any official record: there was a choice made that night that caused the fire to spread in a specific direction, and the choice was made by a person who is still alive.

**Consequence:** This is the path to one of the campaign's deeper truths about Korrath's rise. The players are unlikely to get the full picture from one visit; this is a thread that rewards returning.

### The Wall at Dawn

**Trigger:** Players are in the Cinder Districts before dawn, or have been directed to the Wall by a contact.

**Setup:** There is a man at the Wall who is there every dawn before the maintenance crew arrives. He is old — seventy or more — with enhancement work that dates from before several of the current standard techniques, which makes it identifiable as approximately fifty-year-old work. He removes one offering from the base of the Wall each morning: a specific type of small iron disc that is always there when he arrives and that he always pockets and never places back. When the maintenance crew arrives, they work around him without speaking to him. He has clearly been doing this for a very long time.

**Complexity:** The iron discs are being left by people who are trying to make contact through the Wall's resonant proximity to the Iron Afterlife — not with the Cinder Night dead, but with a specific person who died in the Forge Ring forty-eight years ago. The discs are a simplified encoding of the Iron Sutra contact request. The man removes them each morning because he placed the name on the Wall himself, and receiving contact requests for that person causes him considerable distress, and he will not explain why until players have earned significant trust. The name on the Wall the man visits is not one of the 31,847. It is a name that was added by someone during the engraving period and that was not caught — a second such name, not the Progenitor's, in a different part of the Wall.

**Resolution options:** Building trust with the man (his name is Oris Tenmaki, a former Forge Ring bonding-smith) over multiple interactions can extract, piece by piece, a story about Cinder Night that no one else alive will tell: he was there, he saw who made the choice that directed the fire, and the disc-leaving is people trying to reach the person who died preventing the full extent of the damage from being worse. He has been protecting this information for forty-eight years because the person who made the choice is in the Command Spire.

**Consequence:** Another thread in the Korrath history. Oris Tenmaki is also a bonding-smith of considerable skill who has been in voluntary retirement for decades, and players who earn his trust earn access to enhancement modification capabilities that are not available through official channels.

## Dungeons and Adventure Sites

### The Under-Forge (Beneath Ring Three)

The Forge Ring's industrial works extend below ground as well as above: the deep forges, the coolant systems, the slag processing chambers. The working levels are occupied and regularly maintained. Below the working levels, however, are the original forge chambers from the first century of the Cinderfort's construction — decommissioned when the above-ground works were expanded, sealed rather than demolished because demolition would have destabilized the working levels above them.

The Under-Forge is not officially forgotten — it appears in the Registry as "decommissioned industrial space, access restricted, maintenance class D" — but it has been effectively unmaintained for a century. The resonant metal in the old forges has been saturating the space with passive signal for that same century, which means the Under-Forge has the densest concentration of anchored Echoминds in the Cinderfort: the ghosts of forge workers who died in the pre-modern era, before the Iron Afterlife doctrine was formalized, who did not pass through properly and who have been accumulating here since. They are not aggressive. They are confused, iterating on the last tasks they remember, and the tasks they remember are industrial processes that no longer exist in forms current visitors can interpret.

**The dungeon:** Five levels, each hotter and more resonance-saturated than the last. Level one is exploration and orientation: the players find the entry points, map the accessible passages, encounter the first Echoминds (non-hostile, merely disorienting). Level two introduces the physical hazards: ancient machinery that still cycles on dormant timers, resonant signal gaps where the field has gone inert and Afterlife entities can fully manifest. Level three is where the forges still run — no living hand maintains them, but the resonant accumulation has given them autonomous cycle, producing nothing, completing no function, simply performing the motions of work without purpose. Level four has one Echomind who is not confused: a bonding-smith who died in the original forges' most productive period and who maintains enough coherence to communicate, to bargain, and to offer a form of access to the Under-Forge's deepest level that the players cannot reach any other way. Level five: the slag pits, where the resonant metal of a century's waste has consolidated into something that is not quite ore and not quite an Echomind and that the Iron Sutra has no formal category for.

**Reward:** The deepest level contains a resonant metal alloy of unusual properties that the bonding-smiths above would recognize as theoretically possible but practically unproduceable with current methods. It is the product of a century of passive resonance consolidation, and it is precisely the material that certain enhancement modifications described in the Iron Sutra's older texts require. It also contains the bonding-smith Echomind's last completed work — a design for an enhancement that was never built because the smith died before finishing it, encoded in the resonant material of the deepest slag pit.

### The Caste Registry Vault Extension

The basement vault the players may have already found has a section that does not appear on any available map: a passage behind the oldest record shelving, concealed behind shelves that are bolted to the wall in such a way that the bolt pattern forms an Iron Sutra notation for "continuation." The passage leads to a chamber that predates the Registry building itself — it was here before the building was constructed, and the building was built around it.

The chamber is a resonant metal archive of a type that current practice does not replicate: not a digital encoding but a structural resonance — the memories and records are in the metal itself, which resonates in patterns that translate, for someone with the right sensitivity or training, into direct experience rather than text. The records here go back further than the Cinderfort's founding. They include something that the founding's official history does not: the reason the Progenitor was told to build here, by his resonant-sensitive advisors, was not just that the signal was strong. It was that something was already here, in the ground. Something had been accumulating for centuries before the city. What it was — whether it was a natural phenomenon or something built or something that arrived — is encoded in the resonant archive but requires someone with Veil Master-level training to interpret cleanly. Players with partial training or high resonance sensitivity will get fragments: impressions of scale, of age, of intent, and one image that recurs in every fragment — the same column that is now in the Depth chamber beneath the Spire.

**Dungeon structure:** The passage and chamber are not long, but they are extremely hazardous to enhanced individuals. The resonance in the passage is high enough that cybernetic enhancements begin to respond to it involuntarily — not failing, not being damaged, but operating in ways their bearers are not directing, as if the resonant archive is attempting to interface with every enhanced visitor simultaneously. The non-enhanced have less difficulty but cannot access the archive meaningfully. The challenge is not combat but navigation: enhanced players must make active choices about suppressing their enhancements or allowing the interface, each option presenting different risks and different information.

### The Founder's Passage

The hidden chamber beneath the Spire (the Depth) connects to more than just the Spire's basement. The resonant column's field extends through the rock in three directions, and two of those directions lead to passages that were created, not found: cut stone, very old, with the same pre-modern toolmarks as the original Spire construction. The passages were sealed from the Spire end but can be accessed from where they terminate elsewhere.

One passage terminates in the second basement of the Sutensai temple. The other terminates beneath the Wall of Names.

These are not coincidental. The original builders of the Cinderfort's core structures were working according to a plan that involved these three points as a connected system. The three points — Command Spire, Sutensai Temple, Wall of Names — form a resonant triangle that, when all three resonant nodes are active simultaneously, produces an amplification effect that the current Iron Sutra doctrine describes as the precondition for direct Ascension. This is not in any accessible text. It is encoded in the column itself, and in the architecture of the passage system, and the only person alive who has read enough to recognize it is currently occupying the upper floors of the Command Spire and has been very deliberately not activating all three nodes, for reasons the players will need to determine through careful, dangerous inquiry.

**Dungeon structure:** The passages require both the ability to find them and the ability to navigate them — they are not traps, but they are not designed for uninvited guests. The resonance in the passages varies: near the column, very high; at distance, lower; at the termination points, spiking again. Players navigating the passages encounter embedded resonant memory fragments from the original builders — not Echoминds, but impressions, feelings, the architectural equivalent of being able to hear what the builders were thinking as they worked. The fragments tell a story about the Cinderfort's true purpose that contradicts the official founding history in every significant particular.

## Secrets

- The Depth chamber and its pre-Cinderfort origins. The column predates every other structure in the Cinderfort.
- The three-site resonant system connecting the Spire, the Temple, and the Wall of Names.
- The Registry amendments being made by Afterlife entities, including the notation-system connection to the Depth.
- The name "Ahemdi Korrath-Progenitor" on the Wall, and why Korrath has left it.
- The choice made during Cinder Night, who made it, and why it spread the fire the way it did.
- The three people in the pre-Night Registry who are neither confirmed dead nor alive and who appear in no subsequent records.
- What Korrath has been told by whatever he speaks to in the Depth chamber.
- The Under-Forge's lowest level and the unconsolidated resonant alloy.
- The Bonding Guild's actual degree of independence from Korrath, which is much greater than publicly understood — they have been making autonomous decisions about who receives certain enhancement levels, and their criteria are not the Ironhold's official criteria.

## DM Notes

**The experience this city should produce:** The Cinderfort should feel like being inside an argument that has been winning for four hundred years. Players should feel the weight of it — the competence of it, actually, because Korrath is not stupid and his city is not broken, it functions extremely well — and the suffocation of it. This is a city that works as designed. That is the problem with it.

**Korrath as a presence before a character:** Players will feel Korrath's influence long before they meet him, if they ever do. The city should introduce him through architecture, through the systems he built, through the people who live under him. By the time players reach a point where they might encounter Korrath personally, they should have a complex sense of him that goes beyond "the warlord antagonist."

**The Hollow Poor as moral weight:** The players will be tempted to focus on the powerful locations — the Spire, the Registry, the temple. The richest environmental storytelling is in the Hollow Poor districts. Run scenes there. Let the players see what the system costs at the bottom. The Wall of Names encounter (Oris Tenmaki) is a way in, but the districts themselves, fully rendered, do more work than any single encounter.

**Caste system navigation:** Players who are visibly low-caste or un-enhanced will be treated differently in every ring. This should be consistent and consequential. The inner rings should feel like physical pressure on characters who don't belong there. The outer rings should feel like abandonment.

**The resonant pulse:** As the Convergence approaches, the resonant lines pulse with visible light more frequently. Use this as a pacing tool. Early in the campaign, it happens once per session at most, briefly. By the time players are deep in the campaign arc, it's happening hourly, lasting longer. The citizens are scared. They're not saying they're scared. This is the Cinderfort: you endure.

**The Depth as revelation:** Don't give the players access to the Depth until they've earned it through multiple investigations. The chamber is the key to understanding Korrath's deepest motivations, and early access removes the tension. Let it be the thing they're working toward without knowing they're working toward it.

**Common player approaches:**
- "We go to the Command Spire and ask for an audience." This works — the public petition hour is real, Korrath actually hears them — but the one-minute window and the subsequent security scrutiny are real constraints. Let this approach work but have consequences.
- "We infiltrate the Registry." The maintenance passage approach is viable for players who do their research in the Threshold. The resonant chamber is the complication — players who go looking for records find more than they expected.
- "We make contact with the Hollow Poor." The players' best allies and most accurate intelligence in this city are in the Threshold and the Hollow Poor districts. Reward this instinct fully.
