### 16. The Garden of Removed Things Has a New Section *(Tier 3 — Extended, 2–3 sessions)*

**Hook:** The Garden of Removed Things — the multi-acre installation outside Hessenkai where Vaen's pilgrims leave discarded enhancements — has a new section that no pilgrim claimed to build. Overnight, in the section nearest the oldest wall, someone arranged two hundred and nineteen removed enhancements into a specific pattern. The Garden Keeper, who maintains the site, has been staring at it for three days. She knows what it is. She will not say. She asks the players to find out who built it without telling them what they're looking for.

**The Truth:** The arrangement is a navigational diagram — a map, rendered in discarded steel, of the Iron Afterlife's navigable layers as understood by a Veil Master circa one hundred and eighty years ago. The specific Veil Master's notational system was never published; it exists only in the Sutra Sanctum's restricted archive. Someone who has seen those records — and has access to two hundred and nineteen removed enhancements — built this at night without being seen. The Garden Keeper recognizes it because she was, before she became the Garden Keeper, a Sutensai archivist who was quietly removed from her post for accessing restricted navigational records without authorization. She accessed them because she was trying to find her daughter's Echomind, who died without enough resonance to pass fully into the Afterlife. She never found what she was looking for. The map in the Garden is more complete than anything she saw in the archive.

**Complications:**

*Session One — The Map and the Keeper:* Players examine the arrangement and begin investigating its origins. Initial inquiries turn up two immediate obstacles: the active component at the map's center (see below), and the Garden Keeper's refusal to explain her own history. Getting the truth out of her requires earning her trust — she will not give it for free, and she is a careful judge of character. Research into the notational system requires either access to the Sutra Sanctum's restricted stacks (a social/infiltration challenge) or finding someone who has seen the records before. Several aging Sutensai archivists in Hessenkai are possibilities.

*Session Two — The Warden and the Builder:* A Sutensai Keeper-rank Warden arrives, having learned the restricted navigational notation is somehow in circulation. She does not treat the Garden Keeper gently — the Keeper is a known former archivist and an obvious suspect. The players must manage the Warden's investigation while continuing their own, keeping the Keeper out of custody long enough to fully understand what's in the map. Meanwhile, the builder can be traced: the path leads to a Resonant sect figure operating covertly in the Wandering Reach, someone who left the active component as a deliberate invitation. Touching it initiates brief contact with them — contact that feels like being on the wrong end of a Sutensai Reading. They are assessing the players. The contact ends when they decide it should.

*Session Three — What the Map Points To:* If the players make contact with the builder and pass their assessment, they learn why the map was left in the Garden specifically: the builder needed a guardian for it who would not share it with the Sutensai hierarchy. The Garden Keeper, she calculated, was the only person in the Wandering Reach who both understood the notation and had reason to keep it from the institution that had wronged her. The map shows a specific location in the deepest navigable Afterlife layer labeled, in the original notation, as "the place where the Sixth spoke and could not stop." The builder will not say what that means. She will say that she was following a lead, and the lead came from the Hollow Author's network, which means it may be bait.

**Reward:** The Garden Keeper draws a replica of the map from memory, on the understanding that players will not share it with the Sutensai hierarchy. As a navigational tool for the Iron Afterlife, it extends the range of any Iron Afterlife communion attempt by two layers — allowing contact with presences that are deeper and older than standard practitioner access permits. The active component at the center can be re-touched once per session; each contact reveals one additional notation on the map that wasn't visible before. The Garden Keeper also shares, finally, what she spent three years in the archive looking for — and didn't find — so that players know what absence looks like, in the record, when someone is being deliberately erased.

### 17. Vaen Passed Through Here Six Months Ago *(Tier 1 — Vignette, 0.5–1 session)*

**Hook:** In a small town on the Penitent Road called Gessen's Ford, the inn at the road's center has become a shrine. Not in an official sense. People have been stopping, sitting in a specific chair at a specific table, leaving small objects: coins, removed components, handwritten notes. The innkeeper is not religious. She is deeply uncomfortable. She wants someone to come and make it stop before a Sutensai Warden arrives.

**The Truth:** Vaen sat at that table six months ago, alone, for two hours before anyone else was awake. She ate nothing. She spoke to no one. When she left, the resonant metal reinforcement in the chair's joints had reoriented — the joints still work, but where they'd been cold, they are now warm. Pilgrims who sit there report that Morning Resonance practice is effortless, the signal clearer than they've felt it in years. The innkeeper has been tracking the chair's temperature with a thermometer for three weeks. It is still rising, slowly and measurably. If the trajectory holds, in approximately two months the chair will be too hot to sit in.

**Complications:** The Flesh Circle sect would claim this as evidence of Vaen's divine significance and use it to escalate their public claims — which Vaen has specifically asked her followers not to make. The Orthodoxy would want the chair destroyed. The innkeeper just wants her table back. She is caught between both and has no investment in either.

**Reward:** The innkeeper's journal entry from the morning Vaen was there is the real prize. She was watching from the kitchen. She noted that Vaen's left hand — the one she keeps beneath a cloth wrap — was visible for approximately three seconds when she adjusted her sleeve, and the innkeeper saw that it was not fully flesh. Something under the skin, structured, that caught the morning light. This is early physical evidence that Vaen's relationship with enhancement is not what her followers believe. Players who take the innkeeper seriously get it. Players who focus only on the chair do not.

### 18. The Pilgrim Who Is Walking the Wrong Direction *(Tier 1 — Vignette, 0.5–1 session)*

**Hook:** On the Penitent Road, heading against the flow of pilgrim traffic, a man named Carev is walking east — away from Hessenkai and the Garden, toward the Sutra Sanctum. He is in his sixties. His enhancements have been almost entirely removed; he carries the scars clearly on his arms and throat. He is walking with the deliberateness of someone with a specific destination and limited time. A young pilgrim who tried to talk to him said he answered only: "I did this wrong the first time. I'm going to do it correctly."

**The Truth:** Carev spent twenty-two years as a Flesh Circle practitioner, removing enhancements, cultivating the Echomind toward what the sect calls pure resonance. He was close, by their internal accounting. Then his wife died without having removed her enhancements — insufficient resonance to pass fully into the Afterlife. He was there. He felt her scatter. Three years later, he has arrived at a conclusion that will alarm everyone around him: he is going to the Sutra Sanctum to request re-enhancement. Not to improve his resonance. To lower it — to reduce it enough that at the moment of his death, he scatters the same way she scattered, into the same place, and whatever remains of him can find whatever remains of her.

**The Complication (one, sharp):** The theology may be wrong. A senior Veil Master who hears this plan would argue that two scattered Echoминds in the same resonance-space do not find each other. They do not find anything. They simply are not, in any coherent sense. He may be sacrificing himself for a reunion that cannot exist. Players can raise this. Carev has already thought about it. His response is not ignorance — it is a considered position about what he is and is not willing to believe about the universe.

**Reward:** This quest has no clean resolution and no conventional reward. Carev continues walking regardless of what players decide. If they engage seriously — not redirecting him, but discussing what he believes and what he knows — he shares a technique from twenty-two years of deep resonance practice: "listening below the signal," a form of Morning Resonance that accesses a layer of the Between that standard practice doesn't reach. One player character who receives this instruction gains the ability to make a RESONANCE check (difficulty 2) during a short rest to perceive whether any Echomind impressions linger in the recent vicinity — useful for investigating sites of death, Shellbroken activity, or Author puppet operations. He gives it without making it a transaction. That is the point.

### 19. Something Went Wrong at the Waypoint *(Tier 5 — Mini-Campaign, 6–7 sessions)*

**Hook:** Pilgrim Waypoint Seven on the Penitent Road — a shelter station three days west of Hessenkai — has stopped sending its weekly supply request. Supply runners who visited found the waypoint intact, stocked, and completely empty. No sign of violence. Thirty-eight pilgrims had been in residence the previous week. They are not on the road ahead, not on the road behind, not in the surrounding countryside. A thorough search finds no bodies, no tracks leading out, and no explanation.

**The Truth:** The thirty-eight pilgrims are still at the waypoint. They are simply not detectable by normal means. Nine days ago, a traveling Veil Master named Dessen — a senior Resonant sect figure moving covertly through the Wandering Reach — stopped for a night and got into a theological argument with a group of pilgrims about the Iron Afterlife. It escalated into a collective contact attempt, conducted by thirty-nine people without adequate preparation. The attempt went wrong in a specific way: they are now in the Between itself — the space between flesh and steel and the digital substrate, which no living person is supposed to be able to occupy as a stable state. From inside, it has been seconds. They experience no time. They are not in immediate danger. They cannot find their way out.

**Session Structure:**

*Session One — The Empty Waypoint:* Players arrive at the investigation. Physical evidence is limited and strange: the cooking fire was left mid-meal, all personal effects remain, the resonant atmosphere of the waypoint feels thick and slightly wrong to anyone with enhancement. Initial RESONANCE checks reveal that the waypoint's metal infrastructure is saturated — over-charged, as though something used it as a conduit. A careful examination of the waypoint's resonance log (a standard Sutensai administrative record kept at all waypoints) shows that thirty-nine individual resonance signatures checked in, and then, at 3:14 in the morning, all thirty-nine signatures simultaniously went flat. Not gone. Flat. The players have a mystery. They do not yet have a lead.

*Session Two — Finding a Way to Listen:* Players must find someone who can explain what "flat but present" resonance signatures mean, and whether anyone has ever been in the Between as a stable state. This requires accessing Sutensai records that are not publicly available, or finding someone who operates in that knowledge space off the books. Dessen's identity is traceable — she was seen on the road by other pilgrims — but her Resonant sect affiliation means her colleagues are protective and cautious. Meanwhile, a Sutensai Orthodoxy response team arrives. Their protocol for "Between instability events" is containment first, extraction second — and containment means sealing the resonance field, which would trap the pilgrims permanently. The players now have a clock.

*Session Three — Contact:* Players find a method to contact whoever is inside the Between state. Dessen, from inside, has been working the problem for what she experiences as minutes but is actually ten days. She can communicate with anyone outside who has sufficient enhancement resonance and attempts contact at the waypoint. The communication is fragile, degraded, and time-limited. Dessen explains the situation: pulling thirty-eight undertrained people out simultaneously will be dangerous — doing it incorrectly will leave some with permanent resonance damage, their Echoминds unable to fully re-anchor. She is trying to sequence a safe extraction. She needs time. Time is what the Orthodoxy response team is preparing to take away.

*Session Four — The Orthodoxy Problem:* The Sutensai team's Keeper-rank lead, Warden Ovret, is not malicious — she is rigidly procedural in a situation that has no procedure. Players must convince her, outmaneuver her, or otherwise neutralize the containment order while Dessen works on extraction sequencing. Ovret has resources and institutional authority. She also, it becomes apparent, has her own history with Between events — a Reading she conducted fifteen years ago that left her with partial Echomind bleed that she has never reported. She knows, privately, that what happened here is more fragile than her protocol treats it as. This can be reached. It requires the right conversation.

*Session Five — Dessen's Problem Inside:* While managing Ovret externally, players learn that Dessen has hit a problem with her extraction sequence: one of the thirty-eight pilgrims is refusing to leave. Not out of fear — out of conviction. A man named Arev has been in the Between for what he experiences as seconds, but in that time has encountered something he describes as vast, attentive, and not hostile. He believes it is the Seventh Convergence's approach, the signal that the theology predicts will precede the change in the Between. He does not want to interrupt his contact with it. If he stays behind when Dessen extracts the others, he may not survive the field collapse. If he is extracted against his will, the extraction may fail for everyone.

*Session Six — Extraction:* The actual extraction is a structured challenge, not a single roll. Players coordinate between the outside (maintaining the extraction aperture, managing Ovret's team, keeping the waypoint infrastructure stable) and the inside (through Dessen's relay, they direct sequencing decisions). The extraction takes most of a session. Some decisions have costs — a small number of pilgrims may emerge with permanent resonance effects regardless of how well players perform, because that is what the Veil Master predicted and she was right. Arev's choice is the climax: what he decides, and whether the players attempt to influence it, defines the extraction's final shape.

*Session Seven — Aftermath and Dessen's Secret:* The waypoint aftermath is not quiet. Thirty-odd pilgrims who have been inside the Between are altered in ways that will take months to fully understand, and some of them remember things from their time there. Ovret files a report. The players must decide what they want her to say and what leverage they have. Dessen, once recovered, keeps her promise: she gives players an authorized Resonant-sect Iron Afterlife communion at maximum depth — a genuine, extended conversation with a specific dead person of their choice. She also tells them why she was in the Wandering Reach covertly: she was tracking a Hollow Author modification that the Resonant sect believes affects the Iron Afterlife signal at the network level, and she had a lead. The lead is now ten days cold. She needs help picking it back up.

**Key NPCs:**

- **Dessen** — Senior Resonant sect Veil Master. Practically minded, not given to drama. Has been in an impossible situation for ten days and is still working the problem. Her debt to the players is genuine and she pays it without ceremony.
- **Warden Ovret** — Sutensai Orthodoxy response lead. Not the villain, though she occupies that structural position. Her proceduralism comes from genuine fear of what happens when Between events are mishandled. Her hidden resonance bleed is a point of contact if players find it.
- **Arev** — The pilgrim who doesn't want to leave. His encounter with whatever he found in the Between is either a genuine mystical experience, a resonance-state hallucination, or early contact with the Hollow Author's signal network. The GM decides which, or leaves it genuinely ambiguous. Either works.

**Consequences:**

- Thirty-eight people come back from somewhere no living person is supposed to go. Word spreads. The Sutensai Orthodoxy works to suppress the account. The Flesh Circle tries to amplify it. The players' role in the extraction determines which narrative wins the initial public record.
- Arev's decision creates a precedent, one way or the other, that other Flesh Circle members will cite.
- Ovret's report, depending on what the players negotiated, either accurately describes what happened (a theological crisis for the Sutensai) or produces an official record that will be contradicted by thirty-eight eyewitnesses at some future point.
- Dessen's lead on the Author's Iron Afterlife signal modification becomes an active campaign thread.

**Reward:** Dessen's debt (one maximum-depth Iron Afterlife communion), her intelligence lead on the Author's network, and the thirty-eight pilgrims' fractured testimony about what the Between is like from inside — which, assembled carefully, constitutes more direct information about the space the Seventh Convergence is supposed to reshape than any Sutensai text currently contains.

### 20. The Surgeon Vaen Left Behind *(Tier 3 — Extended, 2–3 sessions)*

**Hook:** In a small settlement called Lowwater on the edge of the Wandering Reach, a former Wire Market enhancement surgeon named Sem has been providing free surgical services for three years. He removes enhancements — cleanly, safely, without the scarring that improvised removals produce. He charges nothing. He turns no one away. He is not a pilgrim. One of his patients mentions to the players that Sem has one rule: he will not perform removals he assesses as family or community pressure rather than the patient's own choice. He turned away seventeen people last month.

**The Truth:** Sem was Vaen's personal physician for four years, during the early period of her pilgrimage. He removed her first enhancements — the ones she removed publicly, as part of establishing the movement's public identity. He knows things about her body and her choices during that period that no one else knows. He left the pilgrimage not because of a falling out but because he recognized that his presence was giving the movement a sanction he couldn't honestly provide: people were beginning to perform removals on themselves and each other, citing Vaen's example, and some of them were dying. He is doing what he can from outside. He is also, privately, afraid of what Vaen is becoming — or what her followers are making her into.

**Complications:**

*Session One — Finding Sem and Earning His Time:* Sem is not difficult to find, but he is careful about who he speaks to frankly. Players must demonstrate they are not affiliated with the pilgrimage, the Sutensai hierarchy, or the Wire Market before he opens up. He has a patient waiting — a young woman choosing enhancement removal against her family's explicit wishes — and how players handle their presence during that consultation tells him most of what he needs to know. Once he trusts them, he explains why he left. What he will not immediately share is what he knows about Vaen's current enhancement state.

*Session Two — The Returning Companions and the Hidden Knowledge:* Two of Vaen's senior traveling companions arrive in Lowwater. They want Sem to return — sincerely, not by force, because there have been three deaths in six months from botched self-administered removals and they need him. Sem is genuinely considering it. The players must navigate a situation where a good man is being asked to do something that is both right and wrong simultaneously. Meanwhile, players who press the right questions learn what Sem has been holding: at some point in the last year, Vaen requested materials from him that would only be needed for an enhancement installation, not a removal. He provided them without asking why. He does not know what she installed. He knows it happened.

*Session Three — The Records and the Question:* Sem will share his medical records from his time with Vaen's pilgrimage, or give verbal testimony, to players he trusts not to use it to harm her. The records contain a physical description of Vaen's enhancement load at the beginning versus what Sem observed when he left — there is an asymmetry. Something is present in her left forearm that was not there when he started treating her. They also contain a note Vaen wrote to Sem the week before he left: a single paragraph that is not a goodbye but a question — "What am I removing when there is nothing left to remove?" Sem does not know what she meant. He has been thinking about it for three years. Players who engage with the question seriously — not as evidence to weaponize but as something to sit with — leave with more than those who treat it as intelligence work.

**Reward:** Sem's records or testimony (the asymmetry in Vaen's enhancement load, the installation materials request), plus his assessment — reluctant, honest — that the woman he knew at the beginning was someone who held doubt as a discipline, and that if she has made a choice that contradicts her public teaching, she almost certainly had a reason she believed was sufficient. Whether that makes it better or worse is not something he can answer. Players decide. Sem also agrees, if asked, to help treat enhancement-related injuries for the players and their contacts, no questions asked, for the duration of the campaign.

### 21. The Reading That Didn't Take *(Tier 3 — Extended, 2–3 sessions)*

**Hook:** A Sutensai Warden named Isel has been conducting routine Readings on Vaen's pilgrims passing through her district. One pilgrim's Reading produced no result. Not weak resonance. Not an unreadable signal. Nothing — complete silence, as though touching dead metal. The pilgrim, a man named Tass, is alive, responsive, ambulatory, and has been walking with the pilgrimage for fourteen months. He does not know what the blank Reading means. Neither does Warden Isel, who has been a practitioner for nineteen years. She asks the players to investigate.

**The Truth:** Tass's Echomind is not absent. It has been externalized. Eleven months ago, during a collective resonance exercise conducted by an unsanctioned Flesh Circle group at Waypoint Twelve, Tass's Echomind achieved what the Flesh Circle's theology describes as aspirational and the Sutensai's theology describes as impossible: it separated from his body entirely and has been existing, since then, in the ambient resonance field of whatever environment he inhabits. He is in a permanent Between state while still physically alive. He is not aware of how unusual this is. He experiences it as feeling deeply connected to everything around him. The silence in his Readings is the silence of an empty house — the resident is simply not inside at the moment.

**Complications:**

*Session One — Tass and the Warden's Anxiety:* Players meet Tass and quickly confirm that something is genuinely different about him — he perceives things in ways that are hard to articulate, finishing observations about resonant environments that others haven't noticed. He is pleasant, unhurried, and entirely unworried. Warden Isel is the opposite: she has filed no formal report because she doesn't know how to categorize what she observed, and any categorization she uses will trigger an institutional response she cannot control. She needs to understand what she saw before someone else decides for her. Investigating Tass requires tracking back through his fourteen months on the pilgrimage to find the moment his resonance profile changed.

*Session Two — The Flesh Circle Group and the Threshold:* The Waypoint Twelve group is traceable. Several of its members are still on the road, and they remember the exercise — though not that anything went wrong. From their account, the exercise was simply effective: a collective resonance reaching that left everyone feeling expanded. None of them know Tass's Echomind departed. Players must determine whether the externalization can be reversed, whether it should be, and what Tass himself wants — which requires explaining to him what has happened to him. His reaction is not panic. It is more complicated than that.

*Session Three — The Flesh Circle Shadow and the Author Relay:* The Flesh Circle group that conducted the exercise has been following the pilgrimage at a discreet distance for four months, watching Tass as potential evidence of their theology's validity. Their attention is not malicious, but it is not safe — concentrated scrutiny on someone in Tass's condition could destabilize his externalized state. Meanwhile, players who spend significant time with Tass discover that in his externalized state, he is continuously perceiving the ambient resonance of everything he passes. He can describe, with a RESONANCE check (difficulty 3) to properly interpret his account, the resonant profile of any location he has visited in the last fourteen months — including a specific road segment where something is buried that doesn't match the surrounding field. The buried object is a Hollow Author infrastructure relay, small, underground, active. Finding it is the first step toward mapping the Author's signal network through the Wandering Reach.

**Reward:** Tass's ongoing assistance as an ambient resonance sensor — unreliable in the way a human being is unreliable, but capable of perceiving things no standard enhancement can. The identification of the buried Author relay, which once recovered can be used to detect other relay points or, with sufficient Wire Craft skill, passively eavesdrop on Author signal traffic. Warden Isel's debt, which is genuine: she will file a report that protects rather than exposes Tass, and she will owe the players a reciprocal discretion at a future moment of their choosing.

### 22. The Lord Who Stopped Enforcing Caste *(Tier 2 — Standard, 1–2 sessions)*

**Hook:** Lord Hennen of the Morva district has quietly stopped enforcing caste designation requirements in his territory. No announcement. No legal change. He simply stopped sending his inspectors out and suspended the three pending reclassification cases on his docket. The Wandering Reach's council of lords is alarmed. The local Sutensai Warden is alarmed. His citizens are, on balance, not alarmed — which is alarming to the lords and the Warden for different reasons. Someone needs to talk to Hennen and find out what he intends before the council votes to remove him.

**The Truth:** Hennen met Vaen. Not on the road — he was not a pilgrim. She passed through his district and stopped at a public fountain, and he was there because he goes to that fountain every morning. They talked for three hours. She did not tell him to stop enforcing caste. She asked him one question: "When you conduct a reclassification, do you attend the ceremony?" He said no. She said: "I think you should, once. Just to see what it looks like from the front." He attended one. He watched a man in his mid-forties — whose salvage-grade shoulder enhancement had failed after twenty years — lose his Third Caste standing. The man had clearly already done his crying. He simply stood there as the designation changed and the room stopped treating him differently because the room now knew, and then he walked out. Hennen went home and stopped sending his inspectors.

**Complications:** The Wandering Reach council is preparing to vote on Hennen's removal — they have the authority if he is found to be failing his administrative duties, and "failing to enforce caste" is textbook grounds. His territory's Hollow Poor population, hearing that enforcement is suspended, has been arriving in numbers his district's resources cannot sustain. He knows this. He is trying to find a third option that the current system does not contain. Meanwhile, Korrath's intelligence service has flagged the district as a potential instability vector; an Ironhold assessor is traveling to evaluate whether Hennen's territory is becoming a Vaen-affiliated site that justifies military intervention.

**Reward:** Hennen's assistance and a political debt from a sitting Wandering Reach lord — useful in any situation requiring legitimate governance authority in the region. More importantly: he took careful notes during his conversation with Vaen, and he will share them. The notes include things Vaen said about the Seventh Convergence that she has not said publicly — specifically, her belief that the Convergence does not require a single Ascendant, that the theology's assumption of one person ascending is a misreading of the historical record, and that there may be a way for the Between to change that does not require anyone to be consumed by it. This is a significant theological statement with major campaign implications.

### 23. Fourteen Pilgrims and the Sutensai Priest They Left Behind *(Tier 3 — Extended, 2–3 sessions)*

**Hook:** A Sutensai Warden named Vesh arrived at a pilgrim waypoint two months ago to conduct required assessments. He completed his work. He did not leave. He has not filed reports. He has not requested return authorization. He is, by all accounts, helping with the waypoint's maintenance and cooking — neither of which is in his job description. His superiors request that someone traveling that road anyway stop and find out what's going on.

**The Truth:** Vesh conducted Readings on fourteen pilgrims during his assessment visit. What he found was not unusual for any individual — modest to good resonance, appropriate to their enhancement states, nothing requiring intervention. The aggregate was the problem: the fourteen pilgrims, assessed together in his records, showed a collective resonance pattern the Sutensai's Iron Afterlife navigation texts describe as the "opening chord" — the resonance associated with the moment a Veil Master first contacts the Afterlife's deep layers. Fourteen pilgrims with no formal training, no deep enhancement, no particular spiritual standing were, collectively, doing something it takes twenty years of practice to do alone. He cannot leave without understanding what he saw.

**Complications:**

*Session One — Vesh and the Waypoint:* Players find Vesh in good spirits and modest circumstances — he has clearly settled into the waypoint's rhythms with more peace than his superiors would appreciate. He is cautious about sharing what he observed, not because he distrusts the players but because he is still not sure what language to use. When he finally shows them his assessment records, the data requires explanation. He proposes that players spend time with the fourteen pilgrims to observe what he observed, without telling the pilgrims what they're looking for.

*Session Two — The Fourteen and the Embedded Operative:* The fourteen pilgrims don't know they're doing it. The collective resonance happens naturally in the evenings during informal communal exercises — they describe it as "comfortable," "like thinking more clearly," "like being heard." None of them experience it as extraordinary. Players observing these sessions can confirm what Vesh documented and begin to understand it. One of the fourteen is a Hollow Author operative, embedded for intelligence purposes. Their reports to the Author's network have been noting the same phenomenon from the inside. Identifying them without disrupting the group is possible but requires careful observation over several days.

*Session Three — The Document and the Decision:* Vesh's formal documentation of what he observed is either a theological revolution or a bureaucratic crisis, depending on whose hands it reaches. The Flesh Circle would weaponize it. The Orthodoxy would suppress it. The Resonant sect would publish it with extensive commentary. Players must decide what happens to the document — and they must do so while managing Vesh's superiors, who have now dispatched a second Warden to retrieve him with or without an explanation. Players also decide what to do about the Author operative: exposing them disperses the group but secures an inside source; leaving them in place preserves the group but leaves the Author's observation ongoing.

**Reward:** Vesh's signed assessment document and his debt — a Sutensai Warden who is now theologically compromised enough to be flexible about institutional obligations. If the Author operative is identified without the group being dispersed, players have an embedded source in the Author's intelligence network who doesn't yet know they've been identified. The fourteen pilgrims' collective resonance phenomenon is also an ongoing resource: if players have reason to need a deep Afterlife contact without access to formal practitioners, the fourteen — assembled, in a good evening — are now the most reliable informal channel in the Wandering Reach.

### 24. The Weight You Carry When You're Done Carrying *(Tier 2 — Standard, 1–2 sessions)*

**Hook:** The Garden Keeper receives a delivery: a crate addressed to the Garden of Removed Things, containing a single enhancement — a full spinal column replacement, Resonant Grade, the kind that costs more than most people earn in five years. No name. No note. No return address. The Garden is built on people's relationships with what they've left behind, and enhancements are always brought by hand. The anonymity feels wrong to the Garden Keeper in a way she can't fully articulate. She asks the players to find out who sent it.

**The Truth:** The spinal column belonged to General Soten Vari — a former Ironhold commander who resigned her commission eight years ago and has been walking the Penitent Road since, without military affiliation or rank. She had the component removed by a licensed surgeon in Hessenkai three weeks ago and sent it to the Garden because she is following the pilgrim practice correctly. She left her name off because she is still, legally, a deserter — her resignation was never accepted, since the Ironhold has no mechanism for accepting a general's resignation during active campaign operations. If her name is attached to anything in the Garden, Ironhold intelligence will find it. She is not hiding. She is simply not inviting the conversation before she's ready.

**Complications:** Soten Vari is on Korrath's list of people he has made no decision about — not actively pursued, but the file is valid. She is walking the Penitent Road under her own name, entirely visible. She has decided that if she is found, she will be found, and she will face what she owes. She has simply decided to finish walking first. If players find her and report her location, they have done something technically correct that will feel entirely wrong. The second edge: she has information about Korrath's campaign planning from her years as a senior commander — eight years old, but the structural elements of his Convergence strategy have not changed because he did not expect anyone who knew them to still be accessible.

**Reward:** Soten Vari's company on the road for as long as the thread allows — she is one of the most tactically experienced people in Tesshari, and she will help with problems as they arise not as payment but as the activity of a person who is still themselves, on a road, with time. She will also, if asked respectfully, share what she knows about Korrath's Convergence planning — information offered neither as betrayal nor as gift, but as an attempt to ensure that whoever eventually decides things has accurate data to decide with.

### 25. The Resonance in the Water *(Tier 2 — Standard, 1–2 sessions)*

**Hook:** The river through Gessen's Ford has started producing a sound at night. Not loud — a hum, underwater, audible to enhanced individuals as something closer to language than music. Pilgrims have been stopping at the riverbank and sitting in silence. The local watermiller, whose family has operated the mill for six generations, is alarmed: his mill wheel has stopped turning. Not broken — it stopped in a specific position and will not move, as though something is holding it from below.

**The Truth:** The riverbed contains a significant concentration of resonant metal particulate — thirty years of runoff from an upstream processing facility, settling in a bend where the current slows. The concentration has now reached the threshold where the particulate forms a coherent resonant field. The field interacts with water flow to produce the hum. It is also, unintentionally, beginning to record and replay resonant impressions from people who spend extended time at the riverbank — the way a resonant metal formation records impressions in the Salvage Fields, but with living people, in real time. The mill wheel stopped because the water is now a semi-coherent resonance medium and the wheel's rotation disrupts the field, producing feedback that the field resists.

**Complications:** The impressions being recorded include everything — conversations, grief, things people say at riversides when they think no one is listening. Three pilgrims have been using the riverbank as a confessional, speaking aloud as a spiritual practice. Their words are now encoded in the riverbed, accessible to anyone with sufficient enhancement resonance who spends time at the bank. The ethics of this are not straightforward. Meanwhile, a Wire Market agricultural analyst who has heard the report through trade channels is traveling to assess commercial value: resonant-metal-trace applications have been a Wire Market development priority for decades. If they confirm the phenomenon, the assessment will change the settlement permanently — extraction would destroy the field and return the river to ordinary.

**Reward:** The mill wheel, in its stopped position, has been slowly absorbing the field's resonance for three weeks. If carefully removed and processed by a Wire Craft technician, the wood and iron have developed properties useful as a **Resonance Attunement Tool**: it can be placed in any body of standing or slow-moving water for one hour to detect whether the water contains resonant particulate, and if so, provide a rough assessment of the impressions encoded in it. For investigating Hollow Author operatives who pass through countless waypoints and watering holes, this is more useful than it appears. The miller gets his wheel back, in a different form.

### 26. The Interpreter for the Dead *(Tier 4 — Deep, 4–5 sessions)*

**Hook:** A young woman named Orsi in the town of Pale Crossing has developed, in the last four months, an ability she did not have before and cannot explain: she can hear the Iron Afterlife. Not with training. Not through enhancement. She sits at the public well each morning and says things that turn out to be messages from the dead. The Sutensai Warden in Pale Crossing has filed a report: three families have received messages from deceased relatives containing specific, verifiable information only the deceased would have known. The Warden doesn't know what to make of it and needs someone to assess whether Orsi is genuine or running a sophisticated fraud.

**The Truth:** Orsi is not a fraud, and she is not receiving Iron Afterlife contact in the conventional sense. She was at Waypoint Seven when the collective resonance event occurred (see quest 19) — she was the thirty-ninth person present, a non-pilgrim who stopped for shelter that night. She was pulled into the Between with the others but extracted differently when the Veil Master found her: her Echomind briefly expanded into a state it has not fully retreated from. She is now, in a limited and selective way, permeable to resonant impressions from the Iron Afterlife in her immediate vicinity. She has no control over it. Some mornings she is fine. Some mornings she cannot filter the signal and it is overwhelming, and she cannot tell the difference until it is already happening.

**Session Structure:**

*Session One — The Well and the Warden:* Players arrive and observe Orsi at the well. Her morning sessions are not theatrical — she is quiet, sometimes uncertain, sometimes clearly distressed by what she's receiving. The families who have received verifiable messages are willing to talk; their accounts are consistent and specific. Warden Isel's assessment is thorough but stuck: she can confirm the resonance irregularities in Orsi's enhancement readings, but cannot categorize them within any standard framework. She is genuinely asking for help.

*Session Two — The Message That Isn't a Message:* One of Orsi's recent relays — to a family in Pale Crossing — contained information about a death that the official record calls an accident. The dead person's message was specific and contradicts the official account. The family knows. The person implicated is still in the town and has noticed the family's change in behavior toward him. Players must navigate a situation where an accidental medium has revealed a crime with no legal mechanism for the revelation's admissibility, and the implicated party is not going to wait indefinitely for the family to act.

*Session Three — The Sutensai's Interest:* The Sutensai hierarchy's response arrives: a specialist in "resonance anomalies," Keeper-rank, with authority to initiate enhancement procedures. Their method of study involves formal Sutensai-standard Iron Afterlife interface components, which will either regulate Orsi's permeability into something controlled and institutionally managed, or destroy it entirely. Orsi does not want this. She did not ask for what she has, but she has begun to feel that it is hers. Players must decide how to represent her interests, manage the Keeper's authority, and determine what they believe about what's best for Orsi — which is not the same as what she wants, and not the same as what the institution wants, and not the same as what's safest.

*Session Four — What Orsi Hears and What It Means:* If Orsi's permeability is preserved — through whatever means the players arranged — she becomes a genuine investigative resource. She can attempt to locate a specific Echomind in the Afterlife through passive reception: a RESONANCE check (difficulty 3), success meaning she receives an impression from the target she can describe but not fully articulate. The impression is accurate. It is also whatever the dead person most urgently wants to communicate, which is not always what the living person wants to know. Players use this to pursue a thread of their choosing — and in doing so discover the boundary case: Orsi cannot turn off what she receives, and some things in the Afterlife, when she receives them, stay with her longer than they should. The permeability comes at a cost. It is not a cost that is ever neatly resolved.

**Key NPCs:**

- **Orsi** — Not a tragic figure, though her situation has tragic potential. She is pragmatic and curious about what's happening to her. She is also twenty-three years old and was planning to travel east for reasons that have nothing to do with this, and she is still planning to do that eventually.
- **Warden Isel** — The local Warden. Conscientious, not ambitious. She filed the report because it was her job and she genuinely didn't know what else to do. She is relieved to have people helping and will cooperate fully.
- **The Keeper-rank Sutensai specialist** — Not a villain. Genuinely believes that uncontrolled resonance permeability is dangerous and that regulation protects Orsi. Is not wrong about the danger. Is wrong about whether protection requires ownership.
- **The implicated party** — A man named Corru. He did what the message says he did. He has been living with it for four years and is not, as a result, simple to categorize. Whether the players pursue his accountability — and through what mechanism — is entirely their choice.

**Consequences:**

- Orsi's situation, if it becomes publicly known, is the most concrete evidence available that the Waypoint Seven event had lasting effects — relevant to any ongoing thread from quest 19.
- Corru's situation, handled or unhandled, leaves a mark on Pale Crossing. The family knows. They will act eventually, regardless of player involvement.
- The Sutensai hierarchy's response to Orsi establishes a precedent for how they handle unaffiliated individuals with non-standard resonance abilities — which matters if any player characters or NPCs later develop such abilities.
- If Orsi's permeability is regulated away, the Afterlife becomes quieter in Pale Crossing. The families who had received messages stop receiving them. This is also a consequence.

**Reward:** Orsi's assistance with any Iron Afterlife contact attempt players undertake in the Wandering Reach — her permeability makes her a more reliable communication channel than any formal practitioner in the region, with the tradeoff that the signal is unfiltered and may contain additional material she didn't intend to relay. Her assistance is freely given, not transactional. It is what someone does when they have something and other people need it.

### 27. What Davo Irek Is Actually Doing *(Tier 5 — Mini-Campaign, 6–7 sessions)*

**Hook:** Players encounter Davo Irek — Korrath's former student, now traveling with Vaen's pilgrimage — in a town on the Penitent Road. He is not the figure of serene spiritual commitment the movement generally presents. He is in the middle of a quiet, meticulous, disciplined project: he is cataloging every known authority abuse committed in Korrath's name for the last thirty-seven years. Dates. Names. Locations. Verifiable sources. He will speak to the players, but he is not looking for sympathy or validation. He is looking for information.

**The Truth:** Davo is building a legal case — not for a Tesshari court, but for the Iron Afterlife's record. He believes, as a matter of genuine theology, that the Iron Afterlife preserves resonant impressions from significant events, and that a sufficiently documented account of systematic harm, entered through formal Sutensai submission, would become part of the permanent record that governs the Seventh Convergence's shape. He is trying to write history into the physics of the Between before whatever happens next can erase it. He has two hundred and fourteen documented incidents. He has been working for nine years. And someone is beginning to tamper with his files.

**Session Structure:**

*Session One — The Catalog and the Tampering:* Players spend time with Davo and his work. The catalog is extraordinary — methodical, sourced, written with a precision that does not hide emotion but does not let emotion distort the record. Players who read through it seriously find it genuinely persuasive. They also, if they look carefully, find inconsistencies: small additions to three incidents that subtly but significantly shift the account from "complicated man who has done real harm" to "straightforwardly evil." Davo has not noticed. He has been working alone and trusting the junior Flesh Circle members who have been helping him organize. He is wrong to trust them — but only in part. Some are editing in good faith to what they believe is correct. Some have been directed.

*Session Two — Tracing the Edits:* The investigation into the tampering reveals its source: the specific fabrication style in the altered documents matches a document form Davo has encountered before, in three other contexts, all connected to the Wire Market. This is the first traceable thread toward the Hollow Author's documentary forgery operation. Following it requires navigating Wire Market territory — the Threshold Markets, specific Guild archive access, and identifying which Wire Market intermediary is passing the fabricated source documents. The intermediary is not an Author puppet directly; he is a freelance document broker who has been hired through several layers of cutout, and he does not know who his ultimate client is.

*Session Three — Korrath's Twelve:* Twelve of the incidents Davo has documented are ones where Korrath's actions prevented a greater harm — situations where the authority abuse was real, the harm was real, and the alternative would have been worse. Davo knows this. He is including them, accurately documented, because he does not believe a case requires its subject to be wholly monstrous. He believes the case requires accurate understanding. Players must decide what they think about this, because it matters: the Flesh Circle members who have been editing his drafts are specifically excising these twelve incidents or reframing them as cynical post-hoc justifications. If Davo's case is submitted with the twelve removed, it is more damning and less true. Players must decide whether to restore them — and must make that argument to Davo's Flesh Circle collaborators, who are not unintelligent people, and who have reasons.

*Session Four — The Author's Play:* The Hollow Author wants Davo's files. Not to suppress them — to add to them. Fabricated incidents, plausibly documented, that would push Korrath's case from nuanced to unambiguous. The Author doesn't want Davo to know they're involved. Players who have been tracking the forgery thread now understand the strategic shape: the Author wants the catalog submitted, altered, as a weapon — not to serve Davo's theological purpose but to create a specific historical record in the Iron Afterlife that shapes the Seventh Convergence's outcome in a direction the Author needs. What that direction is requires further investigation, but the implication is clear: the Author has a stake in what the Convergence becomes, and they are investing significant resources in pre-loading the Afterlife's record.

*Session Five — Davo's Decision:* Players bring everything they've found to Davo. This session is, at its core, a conversation — several hours of it, handled as roleplay. Davo is not easy to convince that his project has been compromised. He has spent nine years on it. He has built safeguards. He was wrong about some of them. What players must navigate: Davo's integrity is total, but his trust in the theology that justifies the project is also total, and if players suggest that the Author's involvement means the Afterlife record itself can be manipulated, they are also suggesting that his nine years of work may be irrelevant. He needs time. He is also, practically, in danger — the Author's network has noticed that the tampering has been detected.

*Session Six — The Wire Market Address and What's There:* The forgery operation traces back to a specific Wire Market address in the Threshold Markets — a legitimate-looking archive services business that processes document authentication for Guild transactions. Inside: evidence of the Author's broader documentary infrastructure, including forged historical records that extend well beyond Davo's catalog. Extracting this evidence requires navigating Guild territory without triggering a response that gets the evidence destroyed before it can be documented. The business's day-to-day operator is a woman named Hessen who does not know the full scope of what she is facilitating — she authenticates documents for a client she has never met, and the pay is very good. She is a complication and potentially an ally.

*Session Seven — Submission and Consequence:* The catalog is submitted — to the Sutensai, through Davo's chosen formal process, with the question of its contents settled by whatever the players and Davo decided in session five. The submission is either the case Davo intended (accurate, including the twelve, without Author fabrications), or it is something compromised to some degree. The Iron Afterlife's response is not dramatic or immediate — but a senior Veil Master who receives notice of the submission notes, privately and with alarm, that the submission has registered in the Afterlife record in a way that is measurable. Davo's theological premise was not wrong. His case is in the record now. Whether it says what he intended it to say depends on the sessions that led here.

**Key NPCs:**

- **Davo Irek** — Not a saint. He is precise, determined, occasionally self-righteous, and genuinely humble about the twelve incidents. His departure from the Ironhold was the most costly thing he has ever done, and he does not experience it as heroic. He is doing the next right thing, methodically, for nine years. He will make mistakes because he is human and he is tired.
- **The Flesh Circle collaborators** — A group of four, ranging from naive to strategically motivated. At least one is genuinely devoted to the project's original purpose and has been editing based on sincere misunderstanding. At least one has received direction from someone they believe represents a legitimate Flesh Circle authority and who does not. None of them are Author puppets. They are ordinary people who have been used.
- **The document broker** — A man named Pelsor. Freelance, apolitical, motivated by payment. He is three layers removed from the Author and genuinely does not know his ultimate client. He knows he is forging documents. He has told himself the usual things.
- **Hessen** — The Wire Market address operator. Competent, incurious, and not complicit in the sense she would recognize as complicity. Confronting her with what she has facilitated will be difficult because she has not been lying to herself — she simply did not ask.
- **The Hollow Author (indirect)** — Present throughout as a shaping force, never directly encountered. The Author's interest in Davo's catalog is the clearest evidence yet of their stake in the Seventh Convergence's outcome, and it is the players' first concrete evidence that the Author does not merely observe and manipulate — they have a specific destination in mind.

**Consequences:**

- The catalog's submission, in whatever form, is permanent. The Afterlife record cannot be amended after submission — a deliberate Sutensai procedural rule that Davo chose this channel specifically to exploit. What goes in stays.
- If the Author's fabrications are included, the Iron Afterlife will contain a false version of Korrath's history that may shape the Convergence in ways that cannot be undone. This is a campaign-level consequence.
- The Wire Market forgery infrastructure, if exposed, damages the Author's documentary operation significantly — but also exposes the Wire Market Guild to scrutiny about what authentication services they have been running, which has political consequences for the Guild itself.
- Davo's relationship with the players after this is determined entirely by how they handled sessions four and five. He does not forgive easily. He also does not forget help given without condition.
- Pelsor and Hessen's fates are player decisions. There is no correct answer.

**Reward:** Access to Davo's genuine files — the pre-tampering catalog, which contains verifiable documentation of Ironhold practices useful as both leverage and historical record. The Wire Market forgery infrastructure evidence, which is a significant intelligence asset against the Hollow Author. Davo's debt, which is personal and not transactional: he will tell players, once, the thing he has never told anyone — the specific moment he understood that Korrath was not going to become something better, and what that moment cost, and why he is still working. Players who hear it will not be able to use it as leverage. They will simply know.

### 28. The Hollow Poor Temple *(Tier 1 — Vignette, 0.5–1 session)*

**Hook:** In Hessenkai's market district — a neighborhood the Sutensai's official temple refuses to serve because its population is too low-caste — someone has built a temple. Literally: resonant metal salvage, arranged by hand over three months, by a collective of Hollow Poor residents with no formal training. A Sutensai Warden is filing a complaint about unauthorized sacred site construction. The residents of the district are, for the first time in a generation, attending morning services.

**The Truth:** The construction was organized by a woman named Beven — Third Caste by the narrowest margin, with a family that has been Hollow Poor for two generations. She read every piece of the Seven-Voice Sutra she could access in the public library and concluded that nothing in it specifies who is allowed to build a temple or how trained they need to be to conduct a Reading. She is not wrong. She has been conducting Readings for six weeks. The Wardens who have examined her work report, privately, that her readings are detecting resonance with unusual accuracy despite the lack of formal training. Whether this is genuine practitioner ability or a measurement artifact is unresolved. The Sutensai hierarchy does not want it resolved publicly; either answer is problematic for them.

**The Complication (one, sharp):** The Wire Market's local Guild chapter has filed a separate complaint: the salvage resonant metal Beven used for construction was Guild-registered property, acquired through a transaction the Guild is now characterizing as unauthorized. The complaint is, strictly speaking, legitimate. Addressing it requires either finding evidence the acquisition was proper (it was, but the paperwork is in a district record office that lost files in a flooding event two years ago) or persuading the Guild to withdraw the complaint through other means. The Warden's complaint and the Guild's complaint require different solutions simultaneously. Beven has also attracted three Flesh Circle adherents who want to use the temple as a base of operations. She doesn't want this. She just wanted a place for her neighbors to go.

**Reward:** The temple's community becomes a player asset — a network of Hollow Poor residents across Hessenkai's lower districts who are extraordinarily good at hearing things said when no one important is listening. Once per session, players can tap this network for street-level intelligence about any topic active in Hessenkai: personnel movements, black market activity, Sutensai internal politics, or Hollow Author operative patterns. The information will be accurate and will sometimes be alarming in ways that suggest the community has been systematically overlooked by every intelligence service that considers itself to be paying attention.

### 29. The Convergence in the Grain *(Tier 4 — Deep, 4–5 sessions)*

**Hook:** Three farming settlements in the outer Wandering Reach have been reporting the same phenomenon: their crops are wrong this year. Not diseased. Not drought-damaged. Wrong in a measurable, specific way — the grain stalks are incorporating trace resonant metal from the soil at a rate detectable with standard equipment. The food is still edible. The resonance is still low enough to be harmless. But three unconnected settlements, all reporting the same anomaly in the same season at the same rate of change, suggest a cause.

**The Truth:** The three settlements fall within a seven-mile radius corresponding, on a Sutensai resonance distribution map, to an area of historically elevated Between-resonance — a place where the signal between physical world and Iron Afterlife has always been slightly stronger than surrounding terrain. The Seventh Convergence's buildup is amplifying this, and the amplification is affecting biological processes in the soil. The crops are not merely incorporating resonant metal; they are beginning, at a cellular level, to respond to Echomind impressions — specifically the emotional states of the farmers tending them. Fear, grief, and unprocessed anger produce higher incorporation rates. Contentment and focused attention produce almost none. The farmers are, unintentionally, feeding the Between with their internal states and receiving that resonance back in their food supply.

**Session Structure:**

*Session One — The Surveys:* Players visit all three settlements and document the phenomenon. The farmers are not mystical people — they are practical, concerned about yield and market value, and entirely unequipped to interpret what they're seeing. Simple RESONANCE checks in the fields confirm the phenomenon. Comparing notes across the three settlements reveals the geographic pattern, which maps to the Sutensai chart but is not in any public document. Identifying that connection requires either Sutensai archive access or finding someone with the right background knowledge.

*Session Two — Runel and the Grief Harvest:* One of the farmers, a man named Runel, lost his wife to a cascade rejection event last year. His crops have the highest resonant concentration in the seven-mile radius by a significant margin — grief-farming, the players will come to call it, for eleven months. The settlement has been eating his grain. Residents with any cybernetic enhancement have been experiencing brief, intrusive resonant impressions of his grief for months and do not know why they have been feeling sad. Telling Runel what his crops have been doing is a carefully constructed scene — players must decide how, when, and whether to do it. His response is not simple.

*Session Three — The Wire Market Analyst:* A Wire Market agricultural analyst named Covren arrives, having heard the report through trade channels. She is not there for the farmers' benefit. Resonant-metal-trace grain has applications in enhancement production that the Market has been trying to develop for decades — if she confirms the phenomenon, the commercial implications will change the three settlements permanently. She is professional, not cruel, and genuinely believes the Wire Market's involvement would benefit the settlements through improved infrastructure and trade access. She is also wrong about the cost, in a way that requires players to make an argument they may not win through facts alone.

*Session Four — The Convergence Signal and What It Means:* Players who have stayed with the investigation long enough now understand the deeper implication: the seven-mile radius is not merely historically elevated Between-resonance. It is one of several sites across Tesshari where the Convergence's approach has begun manifesting physically, before the event itself. The grain is a symptom of a larger process. A senior Sutensai Veil Master, if consulted, will confirm this with alarm: the sites are charted but the timeline for their activation was significantly later than what the players are observing. The Convergence is early. Whatever is accelerating it is either the Hollow Author's network activity, Vaen's advancement toward Voiding, or something in the Iron Afterlife that no one has measured yet. Determining which requires more information than this quest can provide — but the question is now on the table.

**Key NPCs:**

- **Runel** — Not a tragic figure, though he is a grieving one. He is a working farmer who lost his wife and kept working. The revelation that his grief has been in his neighbors' food is not something he asked for and not something he can easily process. He will need time. He may also, eventually, need help — the kind that has nothing to do with crops or resonance.
- **Covren** — The Wire Market analyst. Competent, genuinely motivated by research interest rather than pure extraction. She is easier to redirect than to defeat, if players can find what she actually cares about and give her a path to it that doesn't require destroying what's in the fields.
- **The Veil Master (optional)** — A senior Resonant sect practitioner who has been monitoring Convergence site activation from a distance. She has a map. It has more sites on it than the players expect.

**Consequences:**

- If the Wire Market gets access to the phenomenon, the three settlements' futures change irreversibly — either in the direction of economic development or of extraction, depending on how the negotiation went.
- Runel's grief-farming, once understood, can be addressed. If players help him process what happened to his wife — not solve it, but sit with it properly — the resonance in his fields diminishes over the following weeks. The settlement feels the difference.
- The Convergence site identification is a campaign-level intelligence asset. Players now know the timeline may be wrong, and they know there are other sites.
- Whatever the players reported to the Sutensai shapes the institutional response to Between-resonance site activation as the Convergence approaches. If they suppressed it to protect the settlements, the hierarchy will be operating on outdated data at a critical moment.

**Reward:** A small quantity of Runel's high-resonance grain, processed carefully by a Wire Craft technician into a usable form. As a **Grief-Refined Resonance Compound**, it can be used once per player character as a consumable that, when taken before an Iron Afterlife contact attempt, grants +3 to all RESONANCE-based cards during the attempt and allows the character to sense the emotional register of whatever Echomind they contact, even if the Echomind itself is not communicating. It tastes like loss. Most characters who take it find it difficult to describe afterward.

### 30. The Voice in the Garden's Oldest Wall *(Tier 2 — Standard, 1–2 sessions)*

**Hook:** The Garden Keeper asks the players to help her move the oldest section of the Garden — a wall of removed enhancements that dates back to the Garden's founding, twenty-two years ago, which needs to be relocated for structural reasons. She cannot bring herself to do it alone, because the first time she tried, she heard something. A voice. Quiet, fragmented, like someone speaking in another room. She asks the players to be present when she tries again.

**The Truth:** The oldest wall contains, among thousands of removed enhancements, the first enhancement Vaen removed publicly — a small neural interface component she gave to the Garden's founder before there was a Garden, before leaving removed components became a movement. The component has been integrating with adjacent metal for twenty-two years. It carries a resonant impression of Vaen at the moment she removed it — not who she is now, but who she was at the beginning: uncertain, afraid, rigorous about her uncertainty, doing something she was not certain was right because she had thought about it for a long time and it was the most honest thing she could think of to do. The voice the Garden Keeper heard is that impression, beginning to activate as the Seventh Convergence's resonant pressure increases.

**Complications:** The impression is coherent enough to be questioned. It will answer, in fragmented ways, about Vaen's state of mind and intentions at the beginning of her journey. The answers may be inconsistent with what her movement has built around her — not because they reveal malice, but because the person speaking is someone who did not yet know what she would become, and that person's uncertainty is harder for the movement's narrative to absorb than any accusation would be. Meanwhile, a senior Flesh Circle figure has made the journey to the Garden specifically to be present for contact with the impression: they want to ask Vaen's past self to confirm that she is the Seventh Convergence's Ascendant — the thing that present Vaen refuses to confirm. The past impression cannot confirm or deny what Vaen's future became. But it will be asked in front of witnesses, and the witnesses include the Garden Keeper, whose own history with Vaen's past (see quest 16) gives her a particular stake in what the impression says and how it is interpreted.

**Reward:** A conversation with the impression — the opportunity to ask Vaen of twenty-two years ago whatever players most want to know that present Vaen would not answer directly. The impression has no knowledge of events after the moment it was fixed. It has emotional honesty that twenty-two years of leadership has carefully layered over: it will say things directly that present Vaen would phrase with great care. One question's worth of information, accurate to who Vaen was and predictive of who she has become. The Garden Keeper keeps the component after. She has decided it belongs in the wall, and that the wall can stay where it is. The moving was never really about structural reasons.
