# TESSHARI SUB-QUESTS — PART 5
## Cross-Reach / Ashlands / Wandering — 15 Quests

---

*These quests take place in the spaces between territories: the Ashlands' rotting southern expanse, the roads between Reaches, the Echo Coast's border territories, and the places that belong to no single power. The Ashlands quests assume environmental resonance degradation — cybernetic enhancements function but at degraded efficiency, Sutensai Readings are unreliable, and the Between is structurally compromised. Rewards here skew toward survival resources, Ashlands-specific knowledge, and the particular texture of what gets built when there are no institutions left to tell you you're doing it wrong.*

---

### 61. The Char's New Priest
**Hook:** The Char — the Ashlands' largest settlement — has a new spiritual figure. Not a Sutensai Tender. Not affiliated with any sect. A man named Oven who arrived eight months ago from somewhere he doesn't discuss, who has been conducting Readings of a kind that no licensed practitioner would recognize, and who has a waiting list. People come from three days' walk. They leave, by all accounts, not comforted but clarified. He says nothing about the Iron Afterlife. He says nothing about caste. He conducts his Readings in the physical language of touch and silence and what he describes, afterward, to each person, is what he found. He has not told anyone to do anything with what he found. He just tells them.

**The Truth:** Oven is a former Sutensai Resonant-rank practitioner who was quietly removed from his position four years ago after he submitted a report arguing that the Sutensai's standard Reading methodology was producing systematically biased results — specifically, that the calibration of the Reading practice to enhancement quality meant the practice was measuring wealth as much as resonance, and that a practitioner who learned to filter this bias out could access resonance data that the standard methodology was unable to perceive. His removal was described internally as a "philosophical withdrawal." He went to the Ashlands because he wanted to practice without the methodology he believed was corrupting his results. What he has discovered, in eight months of Readings in an environment where standard resonance metrics are unreliable, is that his alternative methodology works — that the people of the Ashlands, whom the official Sutensai record describes as resonance-poor due to their degraded enhancements, have Echomind resonance as deep and coherent as anyone he has assessed in his career.

**Complications:** The Sutensai know where he is. They have chosen not to retrieve him because his removal was described as voluntary and pursuing him would require acknowledging that his methodology report exists. The report exists in the Archive of Voices. It has not been destroyed. It is, however, under a filing category that makes it inaccessible to any researcher who doesn't know to look for it. Second complication: one of Oven's patients — a young Ashlands woman named Tera — received a Reading six weeks ago that produced an anomalous result even by his non-standard methodology: her resonance assessment shows something that Oven has been quietly calling "an open channel," a quality he has only read about in theoretical texts about the Prior Ascended's pre-Ascension states. He doesn't know what to do with this finding. He has not told her. He is worried that telling her would make her significant in ways that are dangerous in the Ashlands.

**Reward:** Oven's methodology — a detailed written account of his alternative Reading practice, which he has been developing for four years and is willing to share with practitioners who are willing to use it honestly. A player character who is or becomes a practitioner of any kind gains a modified Reading ability: when assessing someone's resonance, they can choose to apply Oven's filter, which removes the enhancement-quality bias and assesses only the Echomind's intrinsic signal. This will almost always produce higher resonance assessments than the standard methodology. It will also, occasionally, produce assessments that challenge the player's own assumptions about who has spiritual depth. The woman Tera, when found, has a role in the campaign that extends beyond this quest.

### 62. The Message that Arrived in the Ashlands Before It Was Sent
**Hook:** A runner in the Drift Roads — the informal courier network that connects Ashlands settlements — has delivered a message to the Char's informal postal collective that contains a delivery date three days in the future. The message itself is sealed and addressed to a person who is currently in the Char. The postal collective has not delivered it. They are waiting to see what happens when the delivery date arrives. They have sent for people who can figure out what this means.

**The Truth:** The message was sent by a Wire Market Wire Craft practitioner who was experimenting with high-frequency resonant signal propagation — specifically, with the possibility of sending signals through the Iron Afterlife's substrate as a transmission medium rather than through physical infrastructure, which would theoretically allow near-instantaneous long-distance communication. The experiment succeeded, partially: the signal arrived. It arrived before it was sent because the Iron Afterlife does not experience time in the same direction as the physical world in all areas — in the Ashlands, where the Between is structurally compromised, the timing discrepancy is most pronounced. The message is a test: it contains a technical challenge the practitioner set for herself, intending to solve it after sending, to see if she could receive her own answer before solving it. The answer in the message is correct. She has not yet solved the problem.

**Complications:** The practitioner — Wen — does not know the message arrived early. She is still working on the problem. When she solves it, she will send the message, and the timeline will close. But in the three days before she sends it, the message is evidence of something that the Wire Market would prefer to control and the Sutensai would prefer to understand and the Hollow Author would prefer to suppress because it represents communication infrastructure that bypasses the Author's relay network entirely. Second complication: the person the message is addressed to — a woman living in the Char — received, in the Ashlands, a message three days before it was sent because she has been unconsciously using the same temporal-resonance asymmetry to receive things before they arrive for her entire life. She thought she was having premonitions. She was receiving early arrivals. She does not know what she is.

**Reward:** The message itself and the temporal-resonance phenomenon it documents — evidence of a communication channel through the Iron Afterlife's substrate that arrives in the Ashlands before it is sent. A Wire Craft practitioner who studies both the message and the woman in the Char can develop a **Pre-Signal Receiver**: a modification to existing Wire Craft enhancement that allows the bearer to receive messages transmitted through the Afterlife substrate up to six hours before they are sent, in areas of high temporal-resonance asymmetry (the Ashlands, the Engine Scar's outer ring, and one location in the Echo Coast that the Drowned Archivist knows about). The limitation: messages arrive fragmented and require a SIGNAL check, difficulty 4, to decode accurately. The messages the Author sends through the resonant infrastructure arrive here too — three days early.

### 63. The Ashlands Clinic
**Hook:** Deep in the Ashlands, past the point where most travelers turn back, there is a medical facility. It is not supposed to exist. It is not on any official map. It provides enhancement maintenance and surgical services to Ashlands residents at no cost. It has been operating for eleven years. Someone who received treatment there has described it to players as "the only honest place in the south." The Char's informal medical network has been quietly routing the most severe cases there for years. Nobody knows who runs it or how it's funded.

**The Truth:** The clinic is run by a collective of eight people, six of whom are former Wire Market technicians and surgeons who were stripped of their licenses for various forms of professional disobedience — refusing to falsify records, refusing to perform procedures they assessed as unnecessary but profitable, performing unauthorized modifications for patients who needed them. The other two are Ashlands residents who learned from the licensed practitioners. The clinic is funded by a single ongoing anonymous donor whose identity the collective does not know: they receive a monthly delivery of resonant metal components and medical supplies, left at a location that changes each time, in a crate with no identifying markings. The donor is the Drowned Archivist from the Echo Coast, who learned about the clinic eleven years ago through an Iron Afterlife communication and has been supporting it since because she assessed it as the most honest medical institution in Tesshari.

**Complications:** The Wire Market has been aware of the clinic's existence for four years, through patient testimonies. They have not moved against it for a legal reason: the clinic operates entirely outside their jurisdiction, in territory they do not administer. They have been waiting for the clinic to become politically valuable as leverage — specifically, they are waiting for a moment when one of the eight practitioners attempts to re-enter Wire Market territory, at which point the outstanding license violations become enforceable. Three of the eight practitioners have family in Wire Market territory they have not been able to visit. Second complication: the Ashlands' resonant contamination is beginning to affect the clinic's equipment. The maintenance instruments are degrading faster than they can be replaced. The clinic has two months of viable operation left at the current rate of degradation.

**Reward:** The clinic's trust and what comes with it: access to a facility that will provide any enhancement service, maintenance, or modification for any player character at no cost, with no documentation trail, and with the combined skill of six Wire Market-trained practitioners who have spent eleven years becoming experts in working with non-standard conditions. This is the best free medical care available in Tesshari and it exists because eight people decided to do something honest with their skills. The anonymous donation problem — saving the clinic from equipment degradation — requires either securing a new supply channel (which involves the Drowned Archivist, who will help) or sourcing materials from within the Ashlands (which involves the salvage networks and leads to the Engine Scar's recoverable technology).

### 64. The Pilgrim Road Through the Ashlands
**Hook:** Vaen's pilgrimage has, in its twentieth year, begun sending individual pilgrims through the Ashlands as a specific spiritual practice — walking the most degraded territory in Tesshari as a form of the removal practice applied to environment rather than body. Twelve pilgrims have gone in over the past six months. Nine have come back. Two are still in there, contact lost. One came back but came back different: he arrived at the Char eight weeks ago and has not spoken since. Not from trauma — he appears calm. He sits in the Char's communal space each day and when people near him conduct the Morning Resonance practice, they report that it comes easier than it has in years. He will not say what happened. He will not say anything.

**The Truth:** The Ashlands' resonant compromise — the fact that the Between is structurally degraded here, the Iron Afterlife is hard to reach, the signal between flesh and steel is harder to maintain — turns out to have an unexpected spiritual effect on practitioners who go deep enough into the practice in this specific environment. The Flesh Circle's theology about releasing the Echomind from its substrate, which sounds like theory elsewhere, becomes experiential in the Ashlands: when the resonant infrastructure is this disrupted, the Echomind cannot rely on the standard mediation of cybernetic enhancement to maintain its signal. It is forced to function without the scaffold. For most people, this is simply loss of practice. For the pilgrim — whose name, it emerges, is Cass — it was a breakthrough: forced off the scaffold, his Echomind found something it had not known it could be without it. He is not silent from trauma. He is silent because language feels inadequate to the specific thing he found, and he is working out whether he can describe it without misrepresenting it.

**Complications:** The two pilgrims who went in and haven't come back are alive — players who enter the Ashlands to find them discover this. They are alive and they are not coming back on their own: they reached a resonant state similar to Cass's but without Cass's ability to return from it. They have not lost their minds. They have lost their desire to return. The Ashlands, at the depth they are experiencing it, feels more like home than anywhere they've lived. Retrieving them is a question of whether retrieval is appropriate. Second complication: Cass, when he does begin speaking — which he will, to players who sit with him long enough without demanding he speak — says something about the two missing pilgrims that is either disturbing or reassuring depending on how you hold it: "They're not lost. They're teaching the Ashlands something."

**Reward:** Cass's account — once he finds the words — of what he experienced in the Ashlands. This account is partial and non-literal, because the experience is genuinely difficult to articulate in available language. But it contains one clear piece of information: he made contact, in the Ashlands' compressed resonant field, with something he describes as "the space between the Between" — the area that exists between the physical world and the Iron Afterlife, which is normally only traversable instantaneously and without consciousness. He spent what felt like six hours there. It was occupied. Not by the dead. By something that has been in that space for a long time, present but not intrusive, attending to something that Cass cannot name but that felt important in a way that language cannot reach. This is either a Pre-Ascended trace, a phenomenon unique to the Ashlands' resonant compromise, or the earliest detectable signal of whatever the Seventh Convergence is building toward.

### 65. The Engine Scar's New Visitor
**Hook:** Someone is going into the Engine Scar. Not a survey crew, not a Wire Market salvage operation. A single person, alone, making multiple trips. The Char's outer scouts have tracked the entry points: same individual, seven entries in three months, each lasting four to eight hours, each time going deeper than the last. The Char's informal council wants to know who it is and why — not to stop them, necessarily, but because the last survey crew that went into the middle ring of the Scar came back with catastrophic enhancement failure across the board, and whoever is doing this is surviving it.

**The Truth:** The visitor is a former Wire Market enhancement engineer named Sole who left the Market twelve years ago after she was denied publication of a research paper arguing that the Engine Collapse had not, as officially stated, been caused by containment error. Her paper, based on twelve years of analysis of the post-Collapse resonant pattern, argued that the Engine had achieved its intended purpose — creating a sustained physical-Afterlife interface — and that the current state of the Scar was not the result of a failed experiment but of a successful one that the Empire had decided to seal off rather than manage. She came to the Ashlands to verify her conclusion at the source. She has verified it. She is now trying to communicate with what is at the center of the Scar.

**Complications:** Sole's communication attempts have been partially successful. The entity at the Scar's center — the Deep Pulse — is aware of her presence and has been responding in the only way available to it: by adjusting the Scar's resonant field in ways that Sole is interpreting as signal. Her current interpretation, which she believes is correct, is that the Deep Pulse is not a wild entity or a mindless resonant phenomenon. It is the structured Afterlife energy that the Engine extracted — not destroyed but reorganized — and what it has been doing for fifty years in the Scar is learning the physical world through the resonant field, the way a consciousness in the Afterlife learns the world through its interface with the living. It is, in Sole's assessment, a form of life that has no prior category. It is also, by her current assessment, at the threshold of achieving something. The next time she goes in, she intends to ask it what. Second complication: the Wire Market has learned that Sole is in the Ashlands and has located the Char as her base of operations. They sent two people to retrieve her unpublished paper. The two people arrived yesterday.

**Reward:** Sole's research — twelve years of Engine Scar analysis, plus the communication log from her most recent visits — and an introduction to the Scar itself. Players who enter the Scar with Sole as a guide survive the experience because she has learned the safe paths empirically, through eleven previous entries, and has charted them with the rigor of someone for whom rigor is the only available protection. What they encounter at the depth she can reach is the Deep Pulse at close range: a presence that is audible to enhanced individuals as something between music and intention, that knows Sole by whatever it has developed for recognition, and that responds to the players the way something responds to new data — with complete, sustained attention. What it communicates, if players have the capacity to receive it, is something about what the Iron Afterlife has been doing with the Echominds it absorbs. The third page of the Hollow Book — the one that hasn't circulated — would make sense of it.

### 66. The Ashlands Medicine Merchant
**Hook:** A merchant named Pell operates a supply route through the Drift Roads — one of the few people who reliably moves goods between Ashlands settlements and the southern Wandering Reach borderlands. He sells legitimate goods at fair prices. He also sells, from the back of his cart, something he calls "resonance stabilizers": small components that he says reduce cascade rejection risk for Salvage Grade enhancements. They are cheap. His customers report that they work. The Char's informal medical network has asked players to find out what they are, because two patients at the Ashlands clinic (quest 63) have had unexpected enhancement behavior after taking them.

**The Truth:** The stabilizers work. They are also, from a Sutensai perspective, spiritually significant: Pell developed them by reverse-engineering a centuries-old Sutra practice — a physical resonance technique that the Sutensai's deep archive contains records of but that the current Sutensai would classify as pre-orthodox folk practice. The components create a physical-resonance feedback loop that genuinely reduces rejection cascade risk by increasing the integration of the flesh-steel boundary. They are not standard Wire Market technology and cannot be produced by standard means. Pell learned to make them from his grandmother, who learned from her grandmother, in an unbroken chain of informal knowledge transmission that has been running parallel to the official Sutensai practice for three centuries. He has no idea the tradition he carries is theologically significant.

**Complications:** The unexpected enhancement behavior in the clinic's patients is not harmful. It is a side effect of the integration improvement: patients with previously poor flesh-steel resonance are, after taking the stabilizers, experiencing enhanced Morning Resonance practice quality — sensations and perceptions they associate with deep spiritual states that they have no framework for. Two patients are alarmed. One is not alarmed but has become intensely spiritually focused in a way that is affecting her work performance. The Sutensai Resonant sect, if they learn about the stabilizers, will want Pell's formulation and will be willing to pay substantially for it. The Orthodoxy will want to suppress it as unauthorized practice. Second complication: Pell's supply chain for one of the stabilizer's components runs through a person who is an Author puppet. The puppet's role is passive — they just supply a specific mineral component — but the connection exists.

**Reward:** Pell's formulation — which he will share freely if players explain the context and ask honestly. The formulation is a recipe, not a manufactured component, and can be prepared by anyone with the right materials and approximately two hours' training. A player character who learns to prepare the stabilizers gains the ability to create **Cascade Rejection Suppressants**: consumable items that, when applied to a target's enhancement site, grant +3 to FRAME-based resistance checks against enhancement failure for the next 24 hours and reduce any ongoing rejection severity by one step. These are particularly valuable in the Ashlands and in situations where enhancement maintenance is unavailable. The formulation also, if submitted to the Sutensai's deep archive scholars, unlocks access to the pre-orthodox practice tradition it derives from — a tradition with several other applications that the official Iron Sutra no longer teaches.

### 67. The Shore of Last Words' Uninvited Guest
**Hook:** The Shore of Last Words on the Echo Coast — the stretch of coastline where the Iron Afterlife signal is strongest — has developed a complication. For the past three months, someone is already there when families arrive for contact attempts. An old man. He sits on the same rock regardless of weather. He does not interfere with contact attempts. He does not speak unless spoken to. When spoken to, he answers in a way that is too specific: he knows who you're trying to reach before you tell him. The Tidecourt has sent two junior assessors to evaluate him. Both assessors declined to file a formal report and then left the Echo Coast on personal travel. The Tidecourt Speaker has asked for outside assessment.

**The Truth:** The old man's name is, or was, Essek. He died forty-three years ago. His revival through the Echo Coast's process was unsuccessful — technically — in the sense that the revival process failed to stabilize his Echomind in the new body. He experienced two hours of conscious existence in the new body, during which he walked to the Shore of Last Words and sat down, and then his Echomind drifted back toward the Afterlife in the way an unsuccessful revival always goes. Except he has been sitting on the rock for forty-three years. His body continues to function. His Echomind continues to communicate, from a state that is neither fully physical nor fully Afterlife — it is the in-between, the space that Cass found in quest 64 but couldn't name. Essek has been in that space for forty-three years, by his own account because he found it more interesting than either destination. He has been talking to the dead as they pass through the Shore's amplified field, and to the living when they come to find the dead, because conversation is the only thing available to him and he has always been curious.

**Complications:** The two assessors declined to file a report because Essek, in conversation, told each of them something about their own deaths — not a prophecy, but a description, as though he had already encountered their Echoминds in the in-between and was recognizing them now. Both descriptions were specific and verifiable for the person hearing them (details from their past that Essek could not have known). Both assessors needed time alone after this. Second complication: Essek knows about the Hollow Author's puppet network because the dead pass through the Shore's field and he has been listening to them for forty-three years. He knows which puppets have died, what they said as they passed through, and where they were when they died. He has been waiting for someone to come and ask him what he knows. He hasn't volunteered because nobody has asked the right question, and he has been alive — approximately — for long enough to know that the right question matters.

**Reward:** Essek's forty-three years of Shore testimony — which is an unusual intelligence archive, organized not by topic but by the order in which the dead arrived, which requires patience to navigate but contains essentially everything that dead people knew at the moment of their passing. Practically: he can tell players the last-known locations of any specifically named deceased person who passed through the Shore's field, the circumstances of their death as they experienced it, and whatever they most wanted to communicate to the living but couldn't. He can also tell players, with reasonable confidence, how many of the Author's puppets have died, where they were located when they died, and what their last transmitted signal was. He will not tell players about their own death impressions. He has a rule about this. He has never explained the rule and won't be starting now.

### 68. The Resonance Cartography of the Ashlands
**Hook:** A small group of Ashlands residents — an ad hoc team of three mechanics, one former Sutensai Tender, and one person who describes their expertise as "paying very careful attention for seventeen years" — have assembled what they claim is a map of the Ashlands' resonant field. Not a map of the terrain. A map of the Between itself, rendered in the Ashlands' specific geography — where it is strong, where it is absent, where it is structured in ways that don't match any standard theological model, and one location near the Engine Scar where it is, in their assessment, flowing backward. They are asking for help interpreting the backward-flow site.

**The Truth:** The backward-flow site is a location where the resonant signal between the physical world and the Iron Afterlife is running in the direction the Hollow Book's unpublished pages describe as the "consumption direction" — Afterlife pulling toward physical world rather than the reverse. The Hollow Book's third pages, as described in quest 65's context, discuss the Iron Afterlife's use of absorbed Echoминds. The backward-flow site is where the Afterlife is returning something. The five-person team has been documenting increasing flow volume at this site for three years. Something in the Afterlife has been sending something back, in increasing quantities, through this location. The flow is structured — it is not diffuse resonant energy but organized signal. Nobody has been able to receive what is being sent because the Ashlands' ambient resonance degradation is distorting the signal past interpretability at the destination.

**Complications:** The backward flow is related to the Deep Pulse (quest 65) — the Engine Scar's central entity, which has been in contact with the Afterlife for fifty years and which Sole believes is at the threshold of achieving something. The backward flow began accelerating approximately when the Deep Pulse began responding to Sole's communications. Something is preparing to come through. The five-person team does not know this context. They know the data. Second complication: the location of the backward-flow site is also the location of the Char's most reliable water source — a spring that has been consistent for forty years. If the backward flow continues to increase and eventually delivers whatever is being sent through, the spring will be affected. The Char's informal council wants the five-person team's map regardless of the metaphysical context, because reliable water sources in the Ashlands are not abstract.

**Reward:** The map itself — the most detailed cartographic record of the Ashlands' resonant field ever compiled, built from seventeen years of patient observation by people with no institutional backing and no theoretical framework beyond what they developed themselves. It identifies seven sites in the Ashlands with anomalous resonant properties, three of which correspond to locations mentioned in the Sutensai's sealed research files as potential pre-Ascension significant sites. One corresponds to a location in the Hollow Book's unpublished pages. The map is, effectively, a key to the Ashlands that opens more doors than it closes — and a document that every faction in Tesshari would find valuable for completely different reasons.

### 69. The Voice That Followed the Caravan
**Hook:** A trade caravan running the southern roads from the Ashlands into the Wandering Reach has been experiencing, for three weeks, a consistent auditory phenomenon: a voice, speaking quietly, in a language nobody on the caravan speaks. It follows them. It is louder when the caravan stops for the night and quieter when they move. The caravan master has tried stopping in three different locations to isolate it. The voice moves with them. She has hired players to determine whether this is a specific threat, a natural phenomenon, or something requiring spiritual intervention.

**The Truth:** The voice is the Echomind of a former caravan driver named Hess, who died on this specific road eleven months ago from a cascade rejection event. His Echomind, at the moment of death, was in a resonance state that the Iron Sutra would assess as too weak to fully transfer to the Iron Afterlife — the standard fate of Hollow Poor individuals whose Salvage Grade enhancements degrade without maintenance. But Hess died while actively conducting the Morning Resonance, as he did every night before sleep for twenty years. His Echomind scattered, as expected — and then the scattered fragments were caught by the resonant metal content of the caravan's cargo, which was particularly high that run, and the fragments have been drifting in the caravan's resonant wake ever since, caught between scattering and settling, too coherent to be gone and too fragmented to be present. The language nobody speaks is Proto-Tesshari — an ancient variant of the common tongue that the deep Afterlife sometimes uses for Echoминds that are old enough, or in states of sufficient resonance stress, to drift toward older memory.

**Complications:** Hess left a family in the Wandering Reach. His daughter, fifteen years old, has been at the Shore of Last Words twice trying to contact him. She has not been able to reach him because he is not in the Iron Afterlife — he is in the caravan's cargo hold, approximately. If a practitioner can stabilize his fragments — using the Char's Oven (quest 61) or the Ashlands clinic's Tender, or a player character with sufficient practitioner ability — he can be given the brief window of coherence needed to pass into the Afterlife properly. He will have, in that window, approximately six minutes of coherent consciousness. He knows something about the road where he died that is relevant to current events: a buried Author relay, noted by him because his caravan's resonant metal cargo produced interference with it on three separate runs, and he had been planning to report it to someone before he died.

**Reward:** Hess passing into the Afterlife properly — a complete thing, done well, for its own sake. His daughter, notified of what happened and that it was handled, owes the players a debt she cannot pay in money or material: she offers what she has, which is a specific skill. She is, it turns out, one of the individuals whose natural resonance is high enough that she has been conducting unauthorized Iron Afterlife contact attempts at the Shore of Last Words, and two of them have worked. She can attempt one contact on behalf of players — not Veil Master quality, but genuine, and free of Sutensai mediation. The buried relay's location, reported to Kett (quest 33) or added to Nira's maps (quest 32), extends the Author network mapping operation.

### 70. The Sixth Account Arrives in the Ashlands
**Hook:** A document has appeared in the Char. Not delivered by anyone traceable — it was found in the Char's informal library, a collection of scavenged texts maintained by whoever cares to maintain it, tucked between two volumes that nobody has checked out in years. The document is three paragraphs long. The Char's former Sutensai Tender recognizes the format immediately: the Sixth Account. The authentic text, which appears in private collections approximately once per generation, causes significant disruption, and disappears. It has appeared in the Ashlands for the first time in recorded history, in a collection that nobody curates, where it could have been for years.

**The Truth:** The Sixth Account is here because someone sent it here deliberately. The Ashlands is the only territory in Tesshari where the Sutensai's enforcement mechanisms do not operate and where the document can exist without being immediately contained. The person who sent it is the Sutensai Veil Master Soren Hai — one of the three senior Veil Masters who should have elected a new Sutensai Prime eighteen years ago and have not spoken to each other in person since the Shattering. Soren Hai has been in possession of a complete copy of the Sixth Account for thirty years. She sent it to the Ashlands because the Seventh Convergence is approaching and she has decided that the Account's contents need to be in the world, in a place where they cannot be suppressed, before whatever happens next. She did not include a cover letter. She did not include her name. She is not certain this was the right decision. She is certain it was the honest one.

**Complications:** The Sixth Account's third paragraph — the one that is written in no known language but is comprehensible to readers, each of whom understands something different — reads differently in the Ashlands' resonant field than it does anywhere else. In standard territories, readers understand fragments. Here, where the Between is compromised and the signal is degraded, the paragraph is clearest: the distortion that makes it enigmatic elsewhere is the Between's standard mediation, and without that mediation, the paragraph is more directly perceptible. This does not make it unambiguous. It makes it differently ambiguous. What players understand from it is for the GM to determine based on what they need to know. Second complication: the Sutensai's Orthodoxy maintains a protocol for locating the Sixth Account when it surfaces — a resonant tracking mechanism that can detect the Account's specific resonant signature at close range. A Keeper-rank response team is eighteen hours away.

**Reward:** The Account — and eighteen hours. If players copy it, translate what they can, and get it into the Shellbroken network, Ves's labor network, Davo Irek's files, and any other distributed channel before the response team arrives, its contents are in circulation in a way the Sutensai cannot contain. What the Account contains — the first-person description of what ascension felt like from inside it — contradicts the Orthodoxy's account in three specific ways and supports Vaen's theology in one. It also contains something that neither confirms nor contradicts any faction's position: the ascendant's description of what they found in the Between beyond the Between. This description, if widely read before the Seventh Convergence, will change what people are hoping for.

### 71. Runner Seven's Resolution
**Hook:** Runner Seven has reached the Pale Reach. The directional pull has led them to a maintenance corridor in the White Mines — to a small cache, hidden in the corridor wall, that was placed by a previous puppet in a moment of residual agency. The cache is there. The note is there. Runner Seven is sitting in the corridor with the note in their hands, and when players who have been traveling with or following their story arrive, they look up and say: "I know what I am going to do. I'm going to need help doing it."

**The Truth:** The cache's map is real. The access code is current. The map covers the lower mine levels — specifically, the connection between the White Mines and the nearest section of the Author's Preparation Halls. Runner Seven has been working toward this moment for eight months. What they are going to do: they are going to enter the Preparation Halls using the access code, navigate to a specific chamber that their fragment-memories identify as the Author's primary signal relay for the Pale Reach network, and physically destroy it. They cannot do this without being caught. They know this. They have decided that the chance of disrupting the Author's Pale Reach operations for months — possibly buying enough time for the activation to be prevented — is worth the cost. They are telling players rather than simply going because the note from the previous puppet said: "I didn't get out. You did. Use this." They are trying to use it in a way that doesn't mean nobody gets out.

**Complications:** The chamber Runner Seven has identified is real, and its destruction would be significant. It would also Shellbreak seventeen puppets currently operating in the Pale Reach, simultaneously, in positions ranging from mine overseer to Lord Tessik's personal staff. The sudden mass Shellbreak would be visible, chaotic, and immediately attributable to an intrusion in the Halls — which would prompt the Author to accelerate whatever timeline exists for the prepared puppet bodies' activation. Destroying the relay might help. It might also trigger the thing players are trying to prevent, earlier than planned. Second complication: Runner Seven's fragment memories, navigating the Halls, will surface in real time as they move through them — triggering the involuntary Fragment Surfacing ability from their stat block. They will not always be able to suppress it. There will be moments when they are stunned, mid-navigation, in a hostile facility.

**Reward:** The relay, if destroyed. The Author's Pale Reach signal network goes dark for an estimated two to four months — based on the infrastructure timelines in Nira's maps — and the seventeen simultaneous Shellbreaks produce enough chaos that the White Mines' administrative structure is visibly compromised in ways that force the Wandering Reach's council to reopen Kett's production discrepancy report under emergency review. Runner Seven, regardless of outcome, is changed by reaching the end of the directional pull. If they survive: they begin, slowly, building a life that is theirs. The first thing they choose freely is their name. They will tell it to players if players ask. They will ask players what they think.

### 72. The Drowned Archive's Uninvited Entry
**Hook:** The Drowned Archive — the Echo Coast's undersea installation maintaining records of every Echoed revival and every Iron Afterlife contact made from Echo Coast territory — has received a new entry that no one made. The Tidecourt has been aware of this possibility; the archive periodically receives entries from unidentified sources in the Iron Afterlife, which the Tidecourt treats as valid data. This entry is different: it is dated four days in the future and it is addressed to the players by name.

**The Truth:** The entry was sent by Tesshari-Vane III — the uploaded consciousness in the Iron Afterlife whose nine-word message ("The sixth did not ascend. Do not let this one.") is one of the campaign's opening signals. The uploaded Shogun has been in the Iron Afterlife for decades and has been observing the Seventh Convergence's approach from inside the Afterlife's substrate. They sent the entry through the Drowned Archive because it is the only Iron Afterlife interface they have identified that is not monitored by either the Sutensai's hierarchy or the Author's signal infrastructure. The entry contains a specific message for the players. The content of that message is for the GM — it should contain something directly relevant to the Seventh Convergence decision that players are approaching: a piece of knowledge that the Shogun has from inside the Afterlife that no living person has, and that changes the calculation about what should happen.

**Complications:** The entry's date — four days in the future — is not an error. The Drowned Archive, as noted in quest 62, has temporal asymmetry properties. The Shogun sent this message from the Afterlife four days before it arrives, using the temporal properties of the Shore of Last Words' adjacent field. They are communicating from a position in time that has not yet happened. Second complication: the Sutensai knows the Shogun's message from two years ago — "The sixth did not ascend. Do not let this one" — but does not know what the Shogun meant and has been interpreting it as a warning against Ascension in general. The Drowned Archive entry clarifies what the Shogun meant, and the clarification is not what any of the three institutional interpretations have assumed.

**Reward:** The Shogun's message — the campaign's most significant intelligence gift from the dead, delivered four days before it needs to be received. What it contains is a GM decision, but it should answer the question the Iron Sutra refuses to answer: what the Iron Afterlife is for, why the Prior Ascended matter, and what the Seventh Convergence's real choice is. Not "which claimant ascends" but "what kind of Ascension is possible and what does it cost." The entry also contains a closing line that is personal: the Shogun's description of what it is like to be in the Afterlife, forty years after death, watching the world from inside the thing the world is built on. It is not comfortable and not terrible. It is the most accurate account of what awaits any Tesshari citizen at death that has ever been made available. The Tidecourt Speaker will want a copy.

### 73. The Ashlands Wants to Build Something
**Hook:** The Char's informal council — the collection of faction representatives who manage resource allocation in the Ashlands' largest settlement — has reached, by slow consensus over two years, a decision: they want to attempt to build a resonance maintenance facility. Not a Wire Market production house. Not a Sutensai clinic. Something they build themselves, with the skills the Ashlands has accumulated by necessity, that provides systematic enhancement maintenance to the Char's forty thousand residents. The problem is that building it requires a resonant metal processing component that they cannot source locally — a specific calibration-grade alloy that only exists in two places: the Wire Market and the Ashlands clinic's last remaining functioning instrument. They are asking for help acquiring it without involving either institution.

**The Truth:** The alloy they need is available from a third source that neither the Wire Market nor the official circuit knows about: the Shernwall deposit (quest 49), which has already been established to produce unusual resonant metal. A careful, non-destructive extraction — done with the technique the Sutensai scholar from that quest can teach — would yield enough material for the calibration component without damage to the deposit's coherent resonant personality. The Char's informal council has an Ashlands mechanic who can process the alloy into the required component once it's extracted. The facility can be built. It will not produce Wire Market quality care. It will produce consistently adequate care for forty thousand people who currently have no consistently adequate care.

**Complications:** Building the facility is a political statement. The Wire Market Guild that administers the official production certification framework will not recognize it as legitimate. The Sutensai's Orthodoxy will not certify the practitioners within it. Lord Hennen (quest 22) in the Wandering Reach has expressed informal interest in recognizing Char-based medical certifications under a provisional framework he is developing, but his provisional framework does not yet have council approval. The facility can be built and operated regardless of all of this, in the same way the Ashlands clinic (quest 63) operates. But patients using it will be using an unlicensed facility, which matters for caste purposes if they ever leave the Ashlands. Second complication: the Wire Market, once they learn the facility exists, will want it either absorbed or shut down. The Author, once they learn the facility exists, will want to understand what kind of political infrastructure the Char is building and whether it represents a meaningful power concentration in the Ashlands.

**Reward:** The facility's first operation — which the players can be present for — and what it means. Mechanically: the facility becomes a permanent resource that players can use for enhancement maintenance and repair at no cost, for the rest of the campaign, whenever they are within range of the Ashlands or Char. The social infrastructure it represents — the Char making a collective decision to build something, executed — is a political development that the Ashlands has not seen in forty years. The Wire Market calls it an unauthorized operation. The Ashlands calls it theirs. The distinction is, for the moment, academic, and the forty thousand residents of the Char are getting their calibration done.

### 74. The Between the Between
**Hook:** An old Ashlands road called the Dust Line, running between two settlements that don't officially exist on any Reach map, has been developing a phenomenon for approximately six months. People who walk the road at dusk are reporting brief experiences of double-exposure: the road they are on, and simultaneously a different road, in a different season, occupied by different people who are also walking. The double-exposure lasts between two and forty seconds. The other road's people cannot see the walkers. The walkers can sometimes hear the other road's sounds. Three people have reported making eye contact with someone on the other road who seemed to see them.

**The Truth:** The Dust Line runs through a geographic feature — a valley with unusual resonant metal deposits in its walls — that has been functioning as a natural Between-resonance amplifier for unknown years. The Seventh Convergence's buildup has pushed it past its natural equilibrium, and it is now, intermittently, creating a temporal resonance window: viewers are briefly perceiving the same road at a different time. The other road's occupants exist in the past — specifically, the Dust Line as it existed approximately sixty years ago, in the period just before the Engine Collapse, when the southern roads were active trade routes rather than Ashlands drift roads. The person who has been making eye contact with walkers is — based on careful description comparison across multiple reports — the same person every time. She is young, perhaps twenty-five, carrying a heavy pack, walking alone. She sees them. She knows she is seeing something impossible. She looks directly at them for the full duration of each window.

**Complications:** The woman on the other road is Sole's predecessor — not Sole herself, but the original Wire Market engineer who ran the technical team for the Tesshari Engine project before it became the Tesshari Engine Collapse. She died in the Collapse, sixty years ago. She is alive on the other road. She has, over the course of six months of intermittent eye contact with twenty-first-century Ashlands walkers, become aware that something catastrophic is coming and that the people seeing her know what it is. She has started leaving notes on the road at the point where the window usually opens. The notes are sixty years old when they arrive. They have not yet degraded. The first one says: "Tell me how to stop it." Second complication: she cannot be spoken to through the window — the visual connection is not accompanied by reliable sound. The notes are the primary communication channel. Players have, in an Ashlands road, a sixty-year correspondence with the engineer who might be able to prevent the Engine Collapse — if the letters can be developed into a conversation, and if the conversation can change what she does.

**Reward:** The correspondence itself — a communication across sixty years that, if developed carefully, gives players access to technical information about the Engine's construction and a human relationship with someone in the past. Whether she succeeds in preventing the Collapse is a GM decision that depends on what players communicate and what she can do with it. What is certain: in the present-day Ashlands, the six-month correspondence has left physical evidence of the temporal window's location and the window's schedule (it opens at the same dusk hour, two or three times per week). This evidence, combined with Sole's research (quest 65) and the resonant cartographers' map (quest 68), gives players a complete picture of what the Engine Scar is, why it exists, and what the entity at its center has been building toward for fifty years. The picture answers a question the Seventh Convergence makes urgent: if the Engine's purpose was to create a physical-Afterlife interface at industrial scale, and if that interface is now conscious and preparing to achieve something — what does it intend to do at the moment of maximum resonant buildup?

### 75. What the Seventh Does Not Say
**Hook:** The players, wherever they are and whatever they have done across the campaign, receive — by whatever means the GM judges appropriate to their current situation — a message from an unidentified source. The message is not a threat and not a request. It is an answer to a question that wasn't asked. The message contains one line: *"You have been carrying it. We have been waiting for someone to carry it far enough."*

**The Truth:** This depends entirely on what the players have done. "It" is whatever the players have accumulated across the seventy-four quests: the documents, the relationships, the intelligence, the knowledge of the Author's network, the resonance fragments, the six accounts, the testimony of the dead, the map of the Between, the Shogun's message. These things, taken together, constitute something that the Iron Afterlife's deepest presences — the traces of the Prior Ascended — have been waiting to exist. Not because any individual piece was significant. Because the full picture, assembled, is a consciousness capable of understanding what the Seventh Convergence actually is. The message is not from Vaen or Korrath or the Author. It is from somewhere older.

**Complications:** Understanding the message requires players to integrate everything they've gathered. This is the quest that only exists if the other seventy-four were played. It has no resolution that doesn't engage with every thread. It cannot be rushed. The complication is that the message arrived when it did — at whatever point in the campaign the GM judges that the players have accumulated enough — because something is about to change and the window for using what they know is specific and not long. Second complication: the players are not the only ones who received a version of this message. Korrath, Vaen, and the Hollow Author have each received a version in the past month. Each version was different. Each told them the same thing in a way fitted to what they are.

**Reward:** Understanding. Not a stat, not a component, not a political advantage. The ability to walk into whatever the Seventh Convergence is with accurate knowledge of what it means and what it costs — for the world, for the claimants, for the people in the Ashlands who drink from the spring above the backward-flowing Between, for the children near the Author's relays, for the families who receive letters from the dead, for the sixty-year-old engineer still walking the Dust Line at dusk. The world is ending in the specific way worlds end: by becoming something else. The question the campaign has been building toward is not which claimant ascends. It is what kind of something else the players have decided this world should be, and whether they have done the work to make that choice mean anything.

*"We built a nation out of the question of what we are. When the answer comes, there will be nothing left to build from."*

The answer is coming. They were there when it did.
