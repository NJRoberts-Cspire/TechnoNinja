# TESSHARI SUB-QUESTS — PART 1

## Sunder Reach (Ironhold / Korrath's Territory) — 15 Quests

---

### 1. The Wall Has a New Name *(Tier 2 — Standard, 1–2 sessions)*

**Hook:** A section of the Wall of Names — Korrath's ten-mile monument to his military dead — has been defaced overnight. Not vandalized. Overwritten. Someone has chiseled out a row of names and replaced them with a single name, repeated until the row ends: DAVO IREK DAVO IREK DAVO IREK. The caste inspectors who discovered it at dawn have not reported it yet. They are waiting to see who else noticed first.

**The Truth:** A decorated veteran named Sura Pell, who served under Davo Irek before he left the Ironhold, did this alone with a rented chisel and twelve hours of work. She is not unstable. She is making a precise statement: Davo Irek should be on the Wall. He should be counted among the dead that Korrath's orders produced, because as far as she is concerned, the man she served under died the day Korrath gave the execution order and Irek had to choose between his soul and his commander. Pell is waiting to be arrested. She has already sent a copy of her reasoning to the Ironhold's two most-read broadsheets. The inspectors stalling is an opportunity, but the window is closing — the broadsheets' morning edition runs in roughly four hours.

**Location 1 — The Wall:** The players arrive at the defaced section. The inspectors are uncomfortable witnesses, not adversaries. One will quietly point out that two of the chiseled-out names belonged to the most recent battlefield casualties; their families have been notified of the wall addition and will arrive within the hour. The other family is already present and oddly calm — the players can get a read on which family is furious and which thinks Pell might be right, which tells them something about the political temperature on the street.

**Location 2 — Finding Pell:** She is exactly where she said she'd be in the message she sent the broadsheets: at the Third Ring veterans' house, seated at a table, eating breakfast, waiting. She is not afraid. She has done the math on her Fourth Caste standing and believes summary punishment is legally complicated enough to protect her from anything short of a direct Korrath order. She is right. She will talk to the players with the directness of someone who has made peace with consequences. She does not want rescue. She wants a witness.

**The Choice:** The players can suppress the story (persuade or pay the broadsheets, manage the families, negotiate with Korrath's liaison for a quiet resolution that doesn't make Pell a martyr), let the story run and manage the fallout, or find a third path — such as presenting Pell directly to Korrath's liaison before the broadsheets print, framing her as a petitioner rather than a criminal. The liaison will make a different calculation if the matter arrives on his desk as a petition than if it arrives as a headline.

**Complications:**

1. One of the families whose name was chiseled out arrives at the Wall while players are still there, accompanied by an off-duty soldier who is not interested in waiting for bureaucracy. Situation management required before it becomes physical.
2. The broadsheet contact has already been paid in advance by someone the players don't yet know — someone who wanted this story to run. Suppressing it costs extra, and whoever paid may push back.

**Reward:** If the players resolve this without public escalation, Korrath's administrative liaison grants them a **Caste Endorsement Letter** signed by a Keeper-rank Sutensai — a portable caste upgrade usable once, allowing the players to claim Fourth Caste legal standing in any Ironhold-controlled territory regardless of their actual enhancement profile. It expires in one year. Additionally, Sura Pell — grateful or not depending on how they handled it — becomes a contact with access to Ironhold veteran networks.

### 2. Forty Days to Reclassification *(Tier 3 — Extended, 2–3 sessions)*

**Hook:** A man named Othen Vaar has thirty-one days left before he loses his Third Caste standing. His primary left-arm enhancement — a load-bearing resonant-metal prosthetic received as a battlefield commendation twelve years ago — is failing at the bone anchor point. He cannot afford a repair or the caste appeal process. He can afford to ask the players for help finding the one person who might fix it without documentation: a former Wire Market senior technician named Pol, stripped of her license two years ago, now operating somewhere in the Threshold Markets under a different name.

**The Truth:** Pol is real and findable. She lost her license because she refused to sign off on a batch of Salvage Grade components she'd assessed as having a rejection cascade risk above the legal threshold. The Wire Market Guild disagreed. She was found to have falsified maintenance records (she hadn't — someone else did). She now operates under the name "Carra" with a small waiting list of clients she will not take unless she trusts them. She will fix Othen's arm. The complication: finding her requires navigating the Threshold Market's information economy, and at least two people who know where she is are also informants for the caste inspection bureau. The bureau wants Pol/Carra's testimony — she unknowingly repaired an enhancement belonging to a Hollow Author operative — and the agent handling the case wants to establish that she knowingly aided a conspirator, not that she was an unwitting witness.

**Session 1 — The Search:** The Threshold Market is layered. Players must work through two or three information brokers before they can locate Carra. Each broker has a price or a favor-requirement. One of the brokers is an inspection bureau informant; they may not realize this immediately. Meanwhile, Othen's arm is degrading. If the players are too slow in Session 1 (more than three or four significant delays), run a cascade pre-failure event — the arm locks, discharges a low-level resonant pulse, and Othen ends up in a mediocre street clinic that buys them a day and costs them both money and exposure to the bureau's surveillance net.

**Session 2 — Carra and the Bureau's Offer:** Players find Carra operating out of a converted transit maintenance bay on the Threshold's deep edge. She is competent, guarded, and reads the players quickly. She will work on Othen's arm, but the bureau's agent — a woman named Vin Soru, methodical and not cruel — arrives within the session with her offer: deliver Pol/Carra, and Othen gets his paperwork expedited at no charge. Soru will not arrest Carra today; she is offering players the chance to make a choice, not forcing one. This session should end on the choice, not its consequences.

**Session 3 — Fallout and Fix:** Whatever the players chose, there are consequences to navigate. If they protected Carra: Soru becomes a persistent minor obstacle rather than an enemy, the bureau remains interested, and Carra completes the repair with genuine gratitude. If they traded Carra: Othen is processed, Carra is taken in, and a follow-up hook opens — because Carra knows enough about the Author's Threshold network to make her detention dangerous to her, and she may reach out to the players later from a much worse position. Either path leads to a conclusion, but neither closes cleanly.

**Complications:**

1. Othen's failing arm, if it reaches full cascade rejection during the quest, becomes physically dangerous — the resonant overload discharges unpredictably, and anyone in close proximity during a failure event takes collateral exposure. This creates time pressure and also makes Othen a liability in crowded Threshold spaces.
2. Carra, once she trusts the players, reveals something she hasn't told anyone: the Author operative whose enhancement she repaired asked her, afterward, about a specific Threshold location — a maintenance junction she'd never heard of. She gave no useful answer at the time. The location, if the players investigate it, is an Author relay point, and it connects directly to Quest 9.

**Reward:** Carra owes a significant debt. She will perform one unlicensed enhancement modification for a player character — specifically, she can apply **Ashlands-derived rejection suppression weave** to any existing enhancement, extending its operational life by two enhancement tier grades and granting resistance to forced disabling effects such as resonance spike or Wire Craft sabotage. This is not on any official menu. She will ask for nothing except discretion about her location.

### 3. The Practical Sutra Has a Chapter They Never Published *(Tier 4 — Deep, 4–5 sessions)*

**Hook:** A junior Sutensai Warden named Teish has been quietly circulating pages he claims are from a suppressed early draft of Korrath's *The Practical Sutra* — the military treatise used as training doctrine across three Reaches. The circulating pages contain a chapter titled "On the Management of Grief in Occupied Populations," written not as tactical guidance but as something that barely passes for a confession. Teish wants to sell the original manuscript. He doesn't know who has it. He knows it exists because he conducted a Reading on a dying Ironhold quartermaster who had carried it for thirty years.

**The Truth:** The chapter is real. Korrath wrote it in his fourteenth year of reign, approximately six months after his wife Mira died, and cut it from the published treatise by his own hand before distribution. The quartermaster — Hen Sova — was given the original pages by Mira herself, who had transcribed them from a draft she found in their correspondence. Mira asked Hen to keep them until someone asked the right questions. Hen carried them for twenty-six years without anyone asking. The chapter describes, in precise tactical language that does not quite hide what it actually is, the process of continuing to function after a loss that reorganizes everything. It is the most human thing Korrath has ever committed to paper. It is also, in the wrong hands, the most potent political weapon in Tesshari: proof that the Iron Sovereign can be unmade by a specific kind of grief.

**Key NPCs:**

- **Teish** — Junior Sutensai Warden, in over his head, motivated by money and not malice. He conducted the Reading without authorization and is terrified of institutional exposure. His information is accurate; his judgment is poor. He will cooperate if players protect him from his own order's disciplinary process.
- **Hen Sova's executor** — Hen died with the pages in her possession, and they passed to a named executor she chose: a retired cartographer named Beln, who has no idea what he's holding and keeps them in a lockbox with her other effects. He lives in the Cinderfort's Second Ring and is approachable.
- **Mira's archivist** — A woman named Soos who spent twelve years cataloging Mira's personal correspondence for the Ironhold's historical record and was directed, by Korrath, to seal all correspondence written after the third year of marriage. Soos knows something exists that she wasn't permitted to process. She will not betray Korrath's instruction, but she will tell the players that the seal instruction came from Korrath's own office, which tells them everything.
- **Davo Irek's intermediary** — A pilgrim named Vaen Ash, who carries messages for Irek without knowing their full context. If players encounter her, she delivers a sealed message to no one in particular — she will give it to anyone who looks like they're asking the right questions. The message says Irek wants to read the pages privately and return them. It doesn't explain why, but the implication is clear: he wants to understand the grief of the man who gave the order that ended his career.

**Session Structure:**

- **Session 1 — The Warden and the Rumor:** Players encounter Teish and must assess whether his claim is credible. Investigating the Reading's source leads them to Hen Sova's death record and to Beln, the executor.
- **Session 2 — Three Interested Parties:** As players move toward Beln, three other parties converge: a Wandering Reach pamphleteer who will publish the pages as political attack material; a Hollow Author operative who wants them as leverage over Korrath; and word from Davo Irek's intermediary. Players must navigate competing approaches before reaching the pages.
- **Session 3 — The Pages Themselves:** Players read or handle the chapter. This is a significant moment — run it slowly. The writing is not what anyone expected. It is specific and private and human. Whatever the players do next, they do it having read something Korrath never intended anyone to read.
- **Session 4 — The Decision and its Weight:** Players must act on one of the paths: suppress the pages, deliver them to Irek, use them as leverage, let the pamphleteer publish, turn them over to the Author, or return them to Korrath directly. Each path resolves the quest but changes the campaign. Run the fallout of whichever choice is made and let the consequences settle.

**Complications:**

1. Teish conducted the Reading without authorization. If the Sutensai hierarchy learns he accessed a dying woman's resonance impressions for personal gain, his career ends and he faces criminal exposure. He will sell the players out to protect himself if cornered — unless they've given him a real reason not to.
2. The pamphleteer is not working alone. She is funded by a political faction within the Ash Threshold succession dispute that has an interest in Korrath being seen as emotionally compromised. Players who investigate her backing discover a thread that connects to a faction that will appear again in the main campaign.
3. The Author operative already has a partial transcription — Teish, trying to create a bidding war, already sold him excerpts before approaching the players. The operative will attempt to acquire the full document once players locate it, and he is more resourceful than he appears.

**Consequences:**

- **If players use the pages as leverage against Korrath:** He makes the concession, does not forgive it, and the relationship becomes professionally correct and personally cold. He will remember, permanently.
- **If players return the pages to Korrath unread:** He is still for a long moment. Then he gives them the standing invitation to the Cinderfort. This is the only time in the campaign he will be genuinely, unguardedly grateful. It does not make him soft. It makes him something more complicated.
- **If Irek reads the pages:** He disappears from his known network for a month. When Vaen Ash is next encountered, she says he's "gone quiet." Something changed in him. The players won't know what unless they find him again.
- **If the pamphleteer publishes:** The story runs. The Ironhold's censorship office suppresses it within two days, but not before it circulates in three Reaches. Korrath convenes a closed tribunal. Teish is the most visible target.

**Reward:** The pages themselves, if recovered and kept confidential, give the players a **Leverage Fragment** — a piece of political information about Korrath usable once in negotiation to compel a significant concession (calling off a military action, releasing a prisoner, granting access to restricted records). Using it changes the relationship permanently. Alternatively, returning the pages to Korrath directly without reading them earns something rarer: his genuine, unguarded trust, expressed as a standing invitation to the Cinderfort for any reason, no questions asked.

### 4. Sato Kiren's Left Eye *(Tier 2 — Standard, 1–2 sessions)*

**Hook:** Vessel-Commander Sato Kiren, Korrath's most senior field commander, has put out a discreet call through her personal staff: she needs someone outside the Ironhold chain of command to retrieve a specific item from disputed territory. She will not name it over any communication channel. She will tell the players in person, once. The location is three days into territory currently occupied by a faction that is not the one Korrath is backing.

**The Truth:** The item is an ocular enhancement — a Sanctum Grade left eye, custom-built for Kiren's specific resonance signature — taken from a dead courier eight months ago during a raid. The faction that has it cataloged it as salvage and it is sitting in an equipment depot. Kiren has not disclosed why this specific eye matters beyond function: her current Third Caste Standard replacement has been causing a resonance drift she's hidden from Korrath for months. Without the Sanctum Grade eye, her next Reading will show a caste drop she cannot explain without admitting concealment. She is not corrupt. She is frightened, and she has chosen concealment as the path of least catastrophe.

**Location 1 — The Depot:** Getting there requires navigating three days of disputed-territory travel, which the DM should present as two or three brief tension beats — a checkpoint, a bad stretch of road, a faction patrol — rather than a full overland adventure. The depot is lightly guarded and disorganized. The eye is in a salvage bin mislabeled as "optic salvage, non-resonant." The players can take it clean, or they can arrive during something that complicates the extraction.

**Complication — The Meeting:** The depot is being used as a meeting location by an Ash Threshold faction leader and a Hollow Author representative when the players arrive. They can avoid the meeting, observe it, or inadvertently interrupt it. If they observe it, they hear enough to establish that the Author is making guarantees to this faction in exchange for territorial access — a piece of intelligence with downstream value.

**Complication — Examining the Eye:** A DC 18 Arcana check on the eye before returning it reveals that the calibration upgrade Kiren commissioned is not a standard adjustment. It includes a modification to suppress the natural resonance drift that occurs when the ratio of flesh to steel crosses a specific threshold — a modification that, by strict Sutensai standards, constitutes falsifying a spiritual record. Players now have information about Kiren that she did not intend to share.

**Reward:** Sato Kiren, relieved of a problem that could have ended her career, provides players with a **Vessel-Commander's Seal** — a command authorization token granting passage through any Ironhold military checkpoint without inspection, one use, any point in the campaign. She also becomes a genuine ally within the limits of her loyalty to Korrath's purpose.

### 5. Conscript 4471 *(Tier 5 — Mini-Campaign, 6–7 sessions)*

**Hook:** A woman named Hara, working a labor battalion maintenance shift on the Cinderfort's resonant transmission lines, passes a note to a player through a market contact. The note says: "My brother was reclassified eight weeks ago. He was conscripted. He should have gone to the north grid maintenance battalion. He is not there. I have asked through the right channels. The right channels have given me the same answer three times: Conscript 4471 is assigned to active rotation, location classified. He has never missed a letter. Eight weeks of silence."

**The Truth:** Conscript 4471 — Essik, nineteen years old, reclassified from Second to Fourth Caste after a cascade rejection event left him unable to maintain his shoulder enhancement — was not sent to maintenance. He was assigned, as part of a quiet program six Ironhold administrative officers are running without Korrath's knowledge, to a labor operation in the sub-basement levels of the Cinderfort's oldest ring. The operation strips resonant metal from pre-Ascension structural elements in the fortress's original foundation — elements predating the empire, never examined by the Sutensai, architecturally significant in ways the six officers either don't understand or don't care about. The stripped metal is sold quietly to a Wire Market black-market intermediary. Essik is alive. He has been underground for fifty-three days. He is not well. And the resonant metal being removed from the foundation is structurally load-bearing in the Echomind sense — Korrath's resonance field is partially anchored to it. Stripping it is slowly, imperceptibly weakening the resonance architecture of the Cinderfort itself. No one knows this yet.

**Key NPCs:**

- **Hara** — Essik's sister. Practical, controlled grief. She works Ironhold maintenance and knows enough about the Cinderfort's physical systems to be useful if the players get into the sub-basement levels. She is the players' anchor through the story.
- **Essik** — Nineteen, underground for seven weeks. He is functional but changed: quieter than Hara describes, careful with information, protective of the other conscripts still underground. He has been paying attention. He knows more than the six officers intended.
- **Administrator Veth** — The operational lead of the six officers. Not a monster. She is funding three things through the stripped-metal proceeds: her family's medical debt, a bribe keeping her son out of conscription, and a personal retirement fund in a Reach Korrath doesn't control. She understands what she is doing and has constructed a justification she keeps close.
- **Intermediary "the Keeper"** — The Wire Market black-market contact buying the stripped metal. He is not directly accessible early in the story but becomes relevant in Sessions 4-5. He has sold portions of the metal to three buyers, one of whom is a Sutensai scholar who doesn't know it's stolen and would be appalled to learn the provenance.
- **Chief Rigan** — The Cinderfort's head of structural resonance maintenance, a sixty-year-old engineer who doesn't know about the sub-basement program but has noticed irregularities in the fortress's resonance output readings and filed three reports that were quietly redirected by Veth's office.
- **The Six Officers (supporting)** — Only Veth is developed in detail. The other five are functional — two are true believers in their own justifications, two are being coerced by Veth, and one is already looking for an exit before this collapses.

**Session Structure:**

- **Session 1 — The Missing Conscript:** Players begin with Hara's account and try to locate Essik through official channels (which provide the runaround) and unofficial ones (which require favors and navigation of the Cinderfort's administrative layer). They establish that Conscript 4471 was routed somewhere unusual and that the routing was deliberate. Session ends with the discovery that the sub-basement has an active, unauthorized labor operation.
- **Session 2 — Going Under:** Players find access to the sub-basement levels — likely through Chief Rigan, who has a maintenance key, or through one of the officers who is already looking for an exit. The sub-basement itself is atmospheric: old stone and resonant metal, pre-Ascension carvings that no one has read, and a labor crew of eighteen conscripts working in shifts. Essik is here. His rescue is possible this session but complicated by the fact that leaving takes everyone, not just him, and the infrastructure to disappear eighteen people doesn't exist yet.
- **Session 3 — The Architecture of the Problem:** Players investigate the stripped sites and connect with Chief Rigan, who can confirm (with evidence they bring) that the structural resonance drain is measurable. This session is primarily investigation and alliance-building — players decide whether to involve Rigan, whether to approach any of the six officers, and how to handle the conscripts still underground. The Keeper's identity becomes accessible here if players follow the sold-metal trail.
- **Session 4 — The Intermediary:** Players locate the Keeper and must decide how to approach a well-connected Wire Market black-market operator who has done nothing (from his perspective) except buy salvage. He can be a resource, a target, or a problem depending on their approach. The three buyers he sold to become relevant — particularly the Sutensai scholar, who is now unknowingly in possession of pre-Ascension foundation material and is conducting research with it.
- **Session 5 — Veth's Offer:** Administrator Veth, aware that the players are close, makes contact. Her offer is transactional: she will release all the conscripts, expose the two officers who were most operationally involved (the true believers), and provide enough documented evidence to make the case to Korrath's office, in exchange for the players facilitating her exit from the Cinderfort before the case lands. She has a destination. She has the money. She just needs the players to look the other way for forty-eight hours. Whether her offer is honest, and what she's left out, is for the players to assess.
- **Session 6 — Bringing It to Korrath:** Players must decide who knows what and in what order. Reporting to Korrath directly means a swift and probably violent resolution. Reporting through Chief Rigan first gives them institutional cover. Going to the Sutensai with the structural resonance data changes the frame entirely — it becomes a spiritual emergency as well as a criminal one. The session should involve the conscripts' release and at least one confrontation that doesn't go cleanly.
- **Session 7 — Consequences:** The immediate crisis is resolved, but the world has changed. Run the fallout: the officers' fates, Korrath's response (which may include a quiet fury at having been operated around), the Sutensai's reaction to the pre-Ascension foundation material, and a private moment between Hara and Essik that the players are present for if they want to be.

**Complications:**

1. The resonant metal already stripped and sold has been processed. It cannot be restored. The damage to the Cinderfort's resonance architecture is permanent at the level already taken — though stopping the program now halts further degradation. Korrath's resonance anchor is weakened. This is a meaningful consequence that will matter in late-campaign play.
2. Essik, during his underground time, has overheard conversations that implicate not just the six officers but a connection to an external party — specifically, someone who suggested the sub-basement program to Veth three years ago. This external party is not the Hollow Author. It is a figure in the Ash Threshold succession dispute who wanted the Cinderfort's resonance architecture degraded for reasons connected to the main campaign's larger conflict.
3. One of the other conscripts underground — a young woman named Bres — is Tethered, which Veth's office did not realize when she was conscripted. Her presence in the sub-basement has had an unintended effect: the pre-Ascension resonant metal responds to her proximity differently than it does to enhanced workers. She has been documenting this privately. What she has documented is significant.
4. The Sutensai scholar who bought the stripped metal has incorporated it into a resonance research apparatus. If the apparatus runs before the players intervene, the results will be visible to the Scholar and to anyone in the Archive of Voices who's paying attention — which means the metal's provenance becomes discoverable by multiple parties simultaneously.

**Consequences:**

- **Essik and Hara:** Essik is changed. He will not be the boy Hara was waiting for. What he does next depends on what the players have shown him is possible. If they were careful with the other conscripts, he stays to help organize something. If they weren't, he leaves the Cinderfort entirely.
- **The Cinderfort's Resonance Architecture:** The degradation is real and permanent at the level already taken. Korrath will feel it eventually, though he won't know the cause unless the players tell him. This creates a campaign-level pressure point: the Iron Sovereign's resonant anchor is weakened, and the right kind of attack at the right moment could exploit it.
- **The Six Officers:** Their fates range widely based on player decisions and Veth's trustworthiness. At minimum, two are imprisoned and the operation is formally closed. At maximum, all six face military tribunal. Veth's exit is either clean (if players allowed it) or complicated (if they didn't).
- **Bres and the Pre-Ascension Foundation:** She is a thread that doesn't resolve this story — she connects forward into the campaign's larger questions about what the Cinderfort was built on and why.

**Reward:** A fragment of the pre-Ascension resonant metal from the Cinderfort's foundation — small enough to carry, warm to the touch consistently even in cold environments. It functions as a **Resonance Anchor Fragment**: once per day, the holder can automatically stabilize their Echomind signal, granting +4 to FRAME-based resistance checks against enhancement disruption, shell-state onset, or Echomind-targeting effects. The Sutensai would very much like to examine it. Additionally, Hara becomes a deep, reliable contact inside the Cinderfort's maintenance infrastructure — access to systems that no administrative contact provides.

### 6. The Cinder Priest's Last Reading *(Tier 3 — Extended, 2–3 sessions)*

**Hook:** The Cinder Priest — the Ironhold's most senior local Sutensai, performing Readings in the Cinderfort for thirty-one years — has stopped. He announced this to his congregation last week without explanation. The administrative office has sent three unanswered requests. He is still present for morning services. He has simply stopped touching people.

**The Truth:** The last Reading he performed was on a soldier returning from the northern border — routine re-entry assessment. What the Cinder Priest found was not the soldier's resonance. It was something using the soldier's resonance as a transmission medium: a signal from the Hollow Author's network, encoded in the resonant frequencies of the soldier's enhancements, readable only by a practitioner in active contact during a Reading. He read it accidentally. The message was not meant for him. He now knows something about the Author's northern Ironhold operation that the Author did not intend anyone to know. He has not written it down. He has told no one. He stopped performing Readings because the next one might deliver another message — and he does not know if the Author is aware he received the first.

**Session 1 — Approaching the Priest:** The administrative request is pretext — players are sent for a welfare check, but that framing evaporates quickly. The Cinder Priest is not in distress in any ordinary sense. He is careful and precise and clearly frightened in the way of someone who has thought the problem through and found no safe options. He will not share what he knows during this session. He will assess the players. He will ask them one question: "What would you do with information you received by accident, that was meant for someone else, that could get you killed for having it?" His question is genuine. He wants to know before he trusts.

**Session 2 — The Soldier and the Signal:** Players investigate the soldier — a corporal named Ren, who has no idea what he was carrying. He is in the Cinderfort's barracks, assigned to a standard rotation, good service record, no visible Author contact. The investigation of how a transmission was encoded in his enhancements requires a Wire Market or Sutensai contact and produces a result that is genuinely alarming: the encoding method is sophisticated, passive, and would survive a standard Ironhold re-entry scan. Anyone running this encoding scheme through a military unit could maintain signal relay inside Ironhold's most secure installation indefinitely. The complication arrives: Ren has just been assigned to his next northern border rotation. If he goes, the next encoded message goes with him — and this one might have instructions about a Sutensai who accidentally received the first.

**Session 3 — The Priest Speaks:** Once convinced the players can help — or once Ren's reassignment makes the timeline critical — the Cinder Priest shares what he received. Run this scene slowly. He speaks precisely and without embellishment, in the manner of a man who has rehearsed the words so many times they've become clean. The contents of the message: a code phrase, a contact name, and a geographic coordinate that corresponds to nothing on any Ironhold map. The coordinate is, players may eventually discover, a location in the Salvage Fields' southeastern quadrant — connecting directly to Quest 7. The session should resolve the immediate danger to Ren (whether players prevent his reassignment, warn him, or arrange an alternative) and leave the Priest changed. He returns to performing Readings at the session's end. He offers one for each player character — deep, honest, at the level of a practitioner who has remembered why it matters.

**Complications:**

1. The soldier carrying the first message does not know he was carrying it. If he receives another assignment to the northern border before the players act, the next message arrives encoded in him — and this message may carry instructions about what to do with a Sutensai who accidentally read the first.
2. The Cinder Priest will not share what he read without assurance that doing so won't put him and the soldier in immediate danger. This assurance is genuinely difficult to provide honestly. Players must decide whether to promise something they cannot guarantee, or find a different form of persuasion.

**Reward:** The encoded message contents — a fragment of the Hollow Author's operational communications for the northern Ironhold, including a code phrase, a contact name, and a coordinate connecting to other quest threads. The Cinder Priest, having told someone at last, returns to performing Readings and will do so for each player character free of charge, with the depth and honesty of someone who has been reminded that some things are too important to protect alone.

### 7. What Grows in the Salvage Fields *(Tier 3 — Extended, 2–3 sessions)*

**Hook:** A Salvage Fields crew chief named Messen has been told, officially, that the thing he reported is not unusual and he should return to work. He is now telling anyone who will listen in the Third Ring markets: in the southeastern quadrant of the Salvage Fields, in an area stripped and cataloged eight months ago, something is growing. Not plants. Not mold. Something metallic, crystalline, emerging from the ground in formations between two and eighteen inches tall. It pulses faintly in the dark.

**The Truth:** The formations are resonant metal, growing — which is not supposed to be possible. The southeastern quadrant contains remnants of the largest single-engagement death toll in the Cinderfort's history: a battle four hundred years ago where approximately twelve thousand soldiers died within six hours. The mass death created a resonant density accumulating for four centuries. Something about the Seventh Convergence's approach — building resonant pressure in the Between — has pushed this accumulation past a threshold. The formations are crystallized Echomind residue: the resonant impressions of twelve thousand dead soldiers, slowly taking physical form. Anyone with cybernetic enhancements can accidentally receive fragmented sensory experiences from them — the last moments of whoever died in a given location, experienced as involuntary visions. Some are mundane. Some are not. Players who spend significant time in the area will receive increasingly coherent impressions that include tactical information about a fortification that no longer exists, and a political secret about the founding of the Cinderfort that Korrath's administration has buried.

**Session 1 — First Contact:** Players visit the southeastern quadrant with or without Messen as a guide. The formations are genuinely strange and beautiful in the way that uncomfortable things sometimes are — faintly luminescent, warm, oddly arranged in patterns that the players may recognize as significant after the fact. First-contact impressions are fragmented: a flash of heat, the sound of a command being given in an accent nobody uses anymore, a soldier's face turned toward something out of frame. Players establish that this is real and that the municipal authority's denial is covering something. They learn about the planned harvest.

**Session 2 — The Impressions Deepen:** With more contact and better preparation (wire-craft insulation, resonance practice, or simply time), the impressions become coherent enough to navigate deliberately. Players can seek out specific formations and receive more complete sensory memories. This session is partly exploration, partly the emerging political secret: the founding of the Cinderfort involved a decision that the battle's survivors were not informed of, and the commanding officer of the original battle knew about it and was ordered to silence. ### 9. The Battalion That Came Back Wrong *(Tier 5 — Mini-Campaign, 6–7 sessions)*

**Hook:** Third Reclamation Battalion returned from a six-week border operation two weeks ago. Standard re-entry assessment. Everything normal on paper. But squad leaders who served with them in previous deployments are filing informal complaints through sergeant channels: the battalion is wrong. Nothing specific. They eat, they drill, they respond to orders correctly. They just feel wrong. The complaint has reached a captain who doesn't know what to do with it, and the players have been asked — through whatever channel is most natural for their current position — to look into it before it becomes something official.

**The Truth:** Eleven members of Third Reclamation were captured during the operation's second week and held for four days before escaping or being released. During those four days, all eleven received enhancement modifications from an unknown source — subcutaneous, small, not visible on standard inspection. The modifications are Hollow Author infrastructure: low-level listening and signal-relay components that use the hosts' resonant fields as transmission networks. The eleven soldiers are not aware. They are not puppets; the Author hasn't taken control. They are antennas. Every conversation passing through their resonant field is transmitted, encoded, to an Author relay point somewhere in the Cinderfort's outer ring. The wrongness their colleagues sense is the slight resonant distortion the unauthorized components create — perceptible to experienced soldiers in the way you notice a sound that's a quarter-tone flat without being able to name what's wrong. She is perceptive, loyal, and genuinely frightened of what she might be. She becomes the players' most important internal contact within the battalion.

She wants an explanation she can live with, and she will cooperate with the players provided they keep her informed and don't make her battalion into a scandal. She will become a problem if she decides the players' approach is worse than the condition she started with.
- **The Author's Relay Operator** — An agent operating out of the Cinderfort's outer ring, managing the signal relay from the eleven soldiers. This person has a day job, a cover identity, and a communication protocol that looks like ordinary Wire Market maintenance traffic. Identifying this person is the investigation's core objective and should take two to three sessions.
- **Wire Technician Bova** — A Tier III wire-craft technician hired by the Ironhold, who is the only person in the Cinderfort qualified to remove the components safely. She is not an ally automatically — she requires convincing, compensation, and assurance that the procedure is sanctioned, or she won't work outside official channels.
- **The Captured Faction Leader** — The leader of the faction that captured and held the eleven soldiers. He is now in Ironhold custody on unrelated charges, processed through standard prisoner intake. He can be interviewed. He knows what was done to his prisoners, but his account of it is filtered through his own understanding: he was paid to hold them for four days and instructed not to mark them. He doesn't know who paid him, but his description of the payment method connects to the Author's financial infrastructure.
- **Sato Kiren** — She surfaces in this story in Session 4 or 5, when she realizes the intelligence implications. She is an ally but a complicated one: she will want the information and will have opinions about how it should be handled that may not align with the players'.

**Session Structure:**

- **Session 1 — The Wrongness:** Players interview the squad leaders filing complaints and spend time with the battalion. The feeling is real but undefinable. Essa Tave approaches players on her own in this session — or is steered toward them by Captain Marro — because she has been trying to describe what she's feeling to people who can't help her, and the players look like they might be different. The session ends with players having enough to know this is not a morale problem.
- **Session 2 — Identification:** Players obtain access to a Sutensai Warden willing to conduct Readings on the affected soldiers — finding the modifications requires a specialized approach, as the components are designed to evade standard assessment. The Warden finds something during Essa Tave's Reading (the first Reading, because she volunteered) and the scope of what's been done becomes clear. Eleven soldiers. Three in Korrath's planning sessions. Eight days of transmitted intelligence.
- **Session 3 — The Relay Hunt:** Players begin tracing the signal. This requires Wire Market contacts or Sutensai technical support and involves working backward from what the components emit to where the relay is receiving. The outer ring is large. The investigation involves two or three dead ends and at least one moment where the relay operator nearly burns the relay point and relocates — players must move fast in the session's final beat to prevent full erasure of the trail.
- **Session 4 — The Intelligence Reckoning:** Players must confront how much has already been transmitted. This session is partly procedural — determining what Korrath's planning sessions covered, what the Author now knows — and partly relational. Reporting to Korrath means exposing that his military has been broadcasting his planning sessions to an enemy. Not reporting means carrying that information. Kiren arrives in this session as a pressure point: she has noticed the investigation and wants to know what the players have.
- **Session 5 — Removing the Components:** Bova agrees to perform the extraction, which requires that the players have established either official sanction or sufficient private leverage. The procedure is medically unpleasant and cannot be performed all at once — it must be staged across the session, which means the relay operator knows something is happening and will try to burn the remaining relays before the players finish. The session intercuts between Bova's extractions and players trying to stop the operator from going dark.
- **Session 6 — The Operator:** Players have enough to locate the relay operator. The confrontation is their choice — capture, kill, turn, burn. The operator is not a fanatic; she is a professional. She has an exit protocol. She also has, in her workspace, significant documentation of what the Author has received from the eleven soldiers and what the Author intends to do with specific pieces of that intelligence. This documentation is the session's central prize.
- **Session 7 — Aftermath:** The immediate threat is resolved, but the consequences are extensive. Run the soldiers' recovery (Essa Tave's response to the removal is a scene worth playing), Korrath's response to learning the scope of the penetration, the question of what the Author now knows and will do with it, and the players' relationship to Kiren and Marro going forward. The documentation from the relay operator opens the next chapter of the Author's story in the campaign.

**Complications:**

1. Three of the eleven soldiers are on Korrath's close-support staff and have been in his planning sessions for eight days. The Author's relay has received significant intelligence before the players even begin. This means the players' investigation is already against the clock in a way that cannot be undone — they are doing damage control for something that has already happened, and the Author is already acting on what they received.
2. Essa Tave notices a faint wrongness in herself five days before the players arrive and has been trying to figure it out. Her questioning has made the wrong people nervous — specifically, it has drawn attention from the relay operator, who is now monitoring whether she's a threat to the operation. If players don't move carefully with her, the operator may try to handle her directly.
3. Removing the components requires Bova, who requires official sanction or strong private leverage. Getting either without alerting the wrong people in the Ironhold's administrative chain — specifically, without alerting the relay operator's contact inside that chain — is an exercise in careful information control.
4. One of the eleven soldiers refuses extraction. He is not an Author agent; he is simply a man who was told the component is harmless, has felt no ill effects, and resents the implication that something was done to him without his knowledge. He is not wrong to resent it. His refusal is a complication that cannot be solved by force without making the players into the same kind of violators the Author was.

**Consequences:**

- **The Intelligence Breach:** The Author knows things about Korrath's operational planning that cannot be unknown. This manifests in the main campaign as the Author being one step ahead at specific moments — the DM should note what was transmitted and use it deliberately.
- **Essa Tave:** Her experience changes her relationship to her own enhancements and to the Ironhold's institutional trust. If the players were honest with her throughout, she becomes a reliable long-term ally with access to the battalion's informal communication network. If they weren't, she becomes quietly, permanently suspicious of them.
- **Korrath:** Learning that eleven of his soldiers were used as antennas inside his own planning sessions is a specific kind of violation that Korrath will feel personally and will not discuss. The players will see it in him — in a new, controlled coldness in his manner — whether or not he says anything.
- **Captain Marro:** Her reputation survives the incident or doesn't depending on how publicly the players handled the investigation. If she gets credit for raising the alarm, she advances. If she gets blamed for not catching it sooner, she doesn't.

**Reward:** The extracted modification components — too damaged to reinstall, intact enough to analyze. Physical evidence of Author-network infrastructure inside Ironhold's military, which Korrath's intelligence apparatus has been trying to obtain for years. Presenting it to Korrath directly or through Sato Kiren earns a **Standing Intelligence Contract**: a recurring payment arrangement and priority access to Ironhold intelligence files on the Author's known activities. The relay operator's documentation, if recovered, is worth more than any single contract — it is a roadmap of what the Author knows and what they plan to do with it, and it belongs in whatever hands can act on it fastest.

Neutralizing the flag — through Sova's access management, through Ran's cooperation, or through demonstrating the document's legal irrelevance — requires navigating the Ironhold's bureaucratic systems, which are byzantine by design. Neutralizing Karn requires leverage, and finding it requires an investigation into how he has previously used existing procedures in their most abusive forms. He has done this before. There are people who will talk.
- **Session 4 — The Other Crates:** Whatever the resolution, Sova's offer remains: three more sealed crates of pre-Fracture intelligence documents that have never been opened. Access is granted using a supervisory override she technically has but would be fired for using without authorization. One crate almost certainly contains records of the suppressed ascension attempt from Quest 11. What's in the others is a campaign-level discovery. This session opens those crates and confronts players with what's inside.

**Complications:**

1. Someone else knows the document was found. Veth Karn's flag reached him before players could act. Players are working against a clock they discovered after it started running.
2. One name on the list, marked DANGER, is a name players may recognize from another quest thread — someone currently in a position of trust whose DANGER designation was, two hundred years ago, accurate about what their family line is capable of. The DM should choose this name to create a productive complication: likely Keeper Malen, whose reform efforts and DANGER designation combine into something that is neither villainous nor safe.
3. Genne's infrastructure audit, if she continues it without being warned, will reach the sub-basement maintenance records from Quest 5. If those discrepancies surface through official channels while players are also handling them through unofficial ones, the two threads collide in a way that may help or harm the players depending on their choices.

**Consequences:**

- **Genne and Malen:** If protected, they continue in their positions with an awareness that someone in the intelligence apparatus wanted them gone, and with a debt to the players. Genne's audit continues. Malen's reform work continues. Both matter later in the campaign.
- **Veth Karn:** If stopped, he is not destroyed — he redirects. If the players simply block this particular action without addressing his broader operational methods, he becomes a recurring low-level antagonist within the Ironhold's own administrative structure.
- **The Pre-Fracture Crates:** The contents of the other three crates are campaign-level discoveries to be developed by the DM. At minimum, one contains the records of the suppressed ascension attempt. The players having this knowledge changes what they know about what the Seventh Convergence is actually approaching.

**Reward:** The list, and Sova's trust, and access to three sealed crates of pre-Fracture intelligence documents. What's in those crates is a campaign-level discovery, but one of them almost certainly contains records of the suppressed ascension attempt that Quest 11 points toward. The archivist's ongoing access to the civic records office — granted with appropriate paranoia and care — is a persistent resource for any investigation that requires historical documentation.
