### 1. The Wall Has a New Name
**Hook:** A section of the Wall of Names — Korrath's ten-mile monument to his military dead — has been defaced overnight. Not vandalized. Overwritten. Someone has chiseled out a row of names and replaced them with a single name, repeated in both directions until the row ends: DAVO IREK DAVO IREK DAVO IREK. The caste inspectors who discovered it at dawn have not reported it yet. They are waiting to see who else noticed first.

**The Truth:** A decorated veteran named Sura Pell, who served under Davo Irek before he left the Ironhold, did this alone with a rented chisel and twelve hours of work. She is not unstable. She is making a precise statement: Davo Irek should be on the Wall. He should be counted among the dead that Korrath's orders produced, because as far as she is concerned, the man she served under died the day Korrath gave the execution order and Irek had to choose between his soul and his commander. Pell is waiting to be arrested. She has already sent a copy of her reasoning to the Ironhold's two most-read broadsheets.

**Complications:** Korrath's intelligence staff wants the matter resolved quietly — which means finding Pell before the broadsheets run the story, and deciding what to do with her in a way that doesn't make her a martyr. The complication is that Pell's service record is impeccable and her caste is Fourth, which makes summary punishment legally complicated. A second complication: two of the names she chiseled out were Korrath's most recent battlefield casualties. Their families are already aware. One family thinks it's an insult. The other thinks it's justice, and has said so, loudly, at the morning market.

**Reward:** If the players resolve this without public escalation, Korrath's administrative liaison grants them a **Caste Endorsement Letter** signed by a Keeper-rank Sutensai under Korrath's arrangement — effectively a portable caste upgrade usable once, which allows the players to claim Fourth Caste legal standing in any Ironhold-controlled territory regardless of their actual enhancement profile. It expires in one year. Additionally, Sura Pell, grateful or not depending on how they handled it, becomes a contact with access to Ironhold veteran networks.

### 2. Forty Days to Reclassification
**Hook:** A man named Othen Vaar has thirty-one days left before he loses his Third Caste standing. His primary left-arm enhancement — a load-bearing resonant-metal prosthetic he received as a battlefield commendation twelve years ago — is failing at the bone anchor point. He cannot afford a repair. He cannot afford the caste appeal process. He can afford to ask the players, whom he's heard can solve problems, for help finding the one person who might fix it without documentation: a former Wire Market senior technician named Pol who was stripped of her license two years ago and is now operating somewhere in the Threshold Markets under a different name.

**The Truth:** Pol is real and findable. The reason she lost her license: she refused to sign off on a batch of Salvage Grade components she'd assessed as having a rejection cascade risk above the legal threshold. The Wire Market Guild disagreed with her assessment. She was found to have falsified maintenance records in her official report (she hadn't; someone else did). She now runs a legitimate repair operation under the name "Carra" with a small waiting list of clients she won't take unless she trusts them. She will fix Othen's arm. The complication is that finding her requires navigating the Threshold Market's information economy, and at least two people who know where she is are also informants for the caste inspection bureau, which has been quietly trying to locate her for a different reason: she repaired an enhancement for a person who turned out to be working for the Hollow Author, and the bureau wants her testimony.

**Complications:** The bureau's interest in Pol/Carra is not benign — they want to establish that she knowingly aided an Author operative, which would make her a conspirator rather than a witness. The agent handling the case is willing to trade: deliver Pol, and Othen gets his paperwork expedited at no charge. Players must decide whose problem to solve and at whose expense. Second complication: Othen's failing arm, if it reaches full cascade rejection during the quest, will begin failing in ways that are physically dangerous to him and to anyone near him when the resonant overload discharges.

**Reward:** Pol (operating as Carra) owes a significant debt. She will perform one unlicensed enhancement modification for a player character — specifically, she can apply **Ashlands-derived rejection suppression weave** to any existing enhancement, extending its operational life by two enhancement tier grades and granting resistance to forced disabling effects (such as Korrath's Resonance Spike or Wire Craft sabotage). This is not on any official menu. She will ask for nothing except that they not tell anyone where she is.

### 3. The Practical Sutra Has a Chapter They Never Published
**Hook:** A junior Sutensai Warden named Teish has been quietly circulating pages he claims are from a suppressed early draft of Korrath's *The Practical Sutra* — the military treatise used as training doctrine across three Reaches. The circulating pages contain a chapter titled "On the Management of Grief in Occupied Populations," which is written not as tactical guidance but as a confession. Teish wants to sell the original manuscript. He doesn't know who has it. He knows it exists because he conducted a Reading on a dying Ironhold quartermaster who had carried it for thirty years.

**The Truth:** The chapter is real. It was written by Korrath in the fourteenth year of his reign, approximately six months after his wife Mira died, and it was cut from the published version by his own hand before the treatise was distributed. The quartermaster — a woman named Hen Sova — was given the original pages by Mira herself, who had transcribed them from a draft she found in their correspondence. Mira asked Hen to keep them until someone asked the right questions. Hen carried them for twenty-six years without anyone asking. The chapter describes, in precise tactical language that does not quite hide what it actually is, the process of continuing to function after a loss that reorganizes everything. It is the most human thing Korrath ever wrote. It is also, in the wrong hands, the most potent political weapon in Tesshari: proof that the Iron Sovereign can be unmade by a specific kind of grief.

**Complications:** Three parties want the pages before the players can act: a Wandering Reach pamphleteer who would publish them; a Hollow Author operative who wants them as leverage; and Davo Irek, who has somehow learned of their existence and wants to read them privately and then return them. Each represents a different kind of use and a different consequence. Second complication: Warden Teish conducted the Reading without authorization. If the Sutensai hierarchy learns he accessed a dying woman's resonance impressions for personal gain, his career ends and he potentially faces criminal exposure.

**Reward:** The pages themselves, if recovered and kept confidential, give the players a **Leverage Fragment** — a piece of political information about Korrath that can be used once in negotiation to compel him toward a concession he would not otherwise make (GM discretion on scope, but it should be significant: calling off a military action, releasing a prisoner, granting access to restricted records). Using it changes the relationship with Korrath permanently. Alternatively, returning the pages to Korrath directly — without reading them — earns something rarer: his genuine, unguarded trust, expressed as a standing invitation to return to the Cinderfort for any reason, no questions asked.

### 4. Sato Kiren's Left Eye
**Hook:** Vessel-Commander Sato Kiren, Korrath's most senior field commander, has put out a discreet call through her personal staff: she needs someone outside the Ironhold chain of command to retrieve a specific item from a specific location in disputed territory. She will not say what the item is over any communication channel. She will tell the players in person, once. The location is three days into territory that officially belongs to the Ash Threshold succession dispute and is currently occupied by a faction that is not the one Korrath is backing.

**The Truth:** The item is an ocular enhancement — a Sanctum Grade left eye, custom-built for Kiren's specific resonance signature — that was taken from a dead soldier during a raid eight months ago. The soldier was carrying it as a courier, bringing it to Kiren after a calibration upgrade she'd commissioned through a private Wire Market contact. The faction that has the eye doesn't know what they have; it's been cataloged as a salvage item and is sitting in an equipment depot. What Kiren has not said: the eye is important not just functionally but spiritually. Her current left eye is Third Caste Standard Grade — it was an emergency replacement after a battlefield injury — and her Sutensai Readings since the replacement have shown a drift she has not shared with Korrath. The Sanctum Grade eye was supposed to correct this. Without it, within the year, her next Reading will show a caste drop that she cannot explain to Korrath without admitting she has been concealing the drift for months.

**Complications:** The depot where the eye is stored is being used as a meeting location by an Ash Threshold faction leader and a Hollow Author representative. The players will arrive at a structurally inconvenient time. Second complication: if the players examine the eye before returning it, a DC 18 Arcana check reveals that the calibration upgrade Kiren commissioned is not a standard adjustment. It includes a modification to suppress the natural resonance drift that occurs when the ratio of flesh to steel crosses a specific threshold — a modification that is, by strict Sutensai standards, falsifying a spiritual record.

**Reward:** Sato Kiren, relieved of a problem that could have ended her career, provides players with a **Vessel-Commander's Seal** — a command authorization token that grants them passage through any Ironhold military checkpoint without inspection, for one use, at any point in the campaign. She also becomes a genuine ally: she will not risk Korrath's purpose for them, but within the space available to her, she will help.

### 5. Conscript 4471
**Hook:** A woman named Hara, working a labor battalion maintenance shift on the Cinderfort's resonant transmission lines, passes a note to a player through a market contact. The note says: "My brother was reclassified eight weeks ago. He was conscripted. He should have gone to the north grid maintenance battalion. He is not there. I have asked through the right channels. The right channels have given me the same answer three times: Conscript 4471 is assigned to active rotation, location classified. He has never missed a letter. Eight weeks of silence."

**The Truth:** Conscript 4471 — real name Essik, nineteen years old, reclassified from Second to Fourth Caste after a cascade rejection event left him unable to maintain his shoulder enhancement — was not assigned to maintenance. He was assigned, as part of a quiet program that six Ironhold administrative officers are running without Korrath's knowledge, to a labor operation in the sub-basement levels of the Cinderfort's oldest ring. The operation is stripping resonant metal from pre-Ascension structural elements in the fortress's original foundation — elements that predate the empire, that the Sutensai have never been permitted to examine, that are architecturally significant in ways that the six officers either don't understand or don't care about. The resonant metal from the foundation is being sold, quietly, to a Wire Market black-market intermediary. Essik is alive. He has been underground for fifty-three days. He is not well.

**Complications:** The six officers are low enough in the chain that exposing them doesn't threaten Korrath but high enough that they have allies who will protect them through institutional inertia. More critically: the resonant metal being stripped from the foundation is structurally load-bearing in the Echomind sense — Korrath's resonance field is partially anchored to it. Continuing to strip it is slowly, imperceptibly weakening the resonance architecture of the Cinderfort itself. No one knows this yet. The players might figure it out if they examine the stripped sites. Second complication: Essik knows where the stripped metal is going. If he testifies, it implicates the intermediary, who is connected to a Guild that would rather he didn't.

**Reward:** A fragment of the pre-Ascension resonant metal from the Cinderfort's foundation — recovered before it can be sold, small enough to carry. It functions as a **Resonance Anchor Fragment**: once per day, the holder can use it to automatically stabilize their Echomind signal, granting +4 to FRAME-based resistance checks against enhancement disruption, shell-state onset, or Echomind-targeting effects. The metal itself is warm to the touch, consistently, even in cold environments. The Sutensai would very much like to examine it.

### 6. The Cinder Priest's Last Reading
**Hook:** The Cinder Priest — the Ironhold's most senior local Sutensai, a figure who has been administering Readings in the Cinderfort for thirty-one years — has stopped performing Readings. He announced this to his congregation last week without explanation. The Ironhold's administrative office has sent three requests that he resume his duties. He has not answered any of them. He is still in his temple. He is still present for morning services. He has simply stopped touching people.

**The Truth:** The last Reading he performed was on a soldier returning from the northern border — a routine re-entry assessment. What the Cinder Priest found in that Reading was not the soldier's resonance. It was something that was using the soldier's resonance as a transmission medium: a signal from the Hollow Author's network, encoded in the resonant frequencies of the soldier's enhancements, readable only by a practitioner in active contact with those enhancements during a Reading. The Cinder Priest read it accidentally. The message was not meant for him. He now knows something about the Author's operation in the northern Ironhold that the Author did not intend anyone to know. He has not written it down. He has told no one. He stopped performing Readings because the next one might deliver another message, and he does not know if the Author is aware he received the first.

**Complications:** The soldier who carried the message does not know he was carrying it. He is in the Cinderfort's barracks. If he receives another assignment to the northern border, the next message will be sent through him again — possibly with instructions about what to do with a Sutensai who accidentally received the first one. Second complication: the Cinder Priest will not share what he read without assurance that sharing it won't put him and the soldier in immediate danger. This assurance is difficult to give honestly.

**Reward:** The encoded message contents — a fragment of the Hollow Author's operational communications for the northern Ironhold, including a code phrase, a contact name, and a location that corresponds to nothing on any Ironhold map but appears to be a geographic coordinate. This is the first piece of a larger intelligence picture about the Author's presence in Korrath's own territory. Additionally, the Cinder Priest, having finally told someone, returns to performing Readings — and will perform one for each player character free of charge, with the depth and honesty of a practitioner who has been reminded that some things are too important to protect alone.

### 7. What Grows in the Salvage Fields
**Hook:** A Salvage Fields crew chief named Messen has reported something unusual to the municipal authority and been told, officially, that it is not unusual and to return to work. He is now telling anyone who will listen in the Third Ring markets: in the southeastern quadrant of the Salvage Fields, in an area that was stripped and cataloged eight months ago, something is growing. Not plants. Not mold. Something metallic, crystalline, emerging from the ground in formations between two and eighteen inches tall. It pulses faintly in the dark.

**The Truth:** The formations are resonant metal, growing. This is not supposed to be possible — resonant metal deposits form geologically, over millennia, under specific pressure and mineral conditions. These formations have appeared in eight months. The soil in the southeastern quadrant contains the remnants of an old battlefield — specifically, the site of the largest single-engagement death toll in the Cinderfort's history, four hundred years ago, where an estimated twelve thousand soldiers died within a six-hour period. The mass death created a resonant density in the soil that has been slowly accumulating for four centuries. Something about the Seventh Convergence's approach — the building resonant pressure in the Between — has pushed this accumulation past a threshold. The formations are, essentially, crystallized Echomind residue: the resonant impressions of twelve thousand dead soldiers, slowly taking physical form.

**Complications:** The formations carry resonant impressions that anyone with cybernetic enhancements can accidentally receive as fragmented sensory experiences — the last moments of whoever died in a given location, experienced as brief involuntary visions. Some are mundane. Some are not. Players who spend significant time in the area will receive increasingly coherent impressions. These impressions include tactical information about a fortification that no longer exists and a political secret about the founding of the Cinderfort that Korrath's administration has buried for reasons that become apparent when the secret is examined. Second complication: the municipal authority's official denial is covering a plan to harvest the formations for their resonant metal content. The plan would destroy the impressions permanently. Several senior Sutensai would consider this a form of mass desecration.

**Reward:** A single crystalline formation, small enough to carry, that contains an unusually coherent resonant impression — not fragments but a complete final memory from one of the original dead. The memory is navigable with a RESONANCE check, difficulty 3, during a short rest; it contains a specific location in the Cinderfort's foundation where the original battle's commanding officer buried something "for whoever comes when the metal starts to sing." The object buried there is a **pre-Ascension resonance compass** — a navigational tool that points not toward magnetic north but toward the nearest significant concentration of Echomind resonance. Its operational implications for finding the Hollow Author's puppet network are significant.

### 8. The Inspector Who Stopped Reporting
**Hook:** A caste inspection officer named Vellan stopped submitting his quarterly reports five weeks ago. His supervisor has marked him absent without cause. His housing is paid up. His neighbors report hearing him moving around at normal hours. He simply stopped appearing for work and has not responded to any official communication. The administrative office has asked for a welfare check, which nobody wants to do, which is why they've asked players who are already in the relevant district.

**The Truth:** Vellan had a crisis. He was processing a reclassification — a man falling from Third to Fifth Caste due to Salvage Grade rejection — and when he conducted the Reading (Wardens conduct their own minor Readings for reclassification; it's faster than requesting a Sutensai), he felt, clearly, that the man's resonance was deep and genuine and strong, stronger than most Third Caste individuals he'd ever assessed. The enhancements were failing. The resonance was not. The system he had spent twelve years administering told him to classify this person as Fifth Caste, legally stripping them of employment rights, housing access, and legal standing. He classified them correctly according to the system. He went home. He has not been back. He is sitting in his apartment in the condition of a man who has just understood something that cannot be un-understood: that the caste system measures the enhancements, not the person, and he has spent twelve years treating those as the same thing.

**Complications:** The man Vellan reclassified — Denith — is now in the labor pool processing system, three weeks away from being assigned to a battalion. If Vellan returns to his post, he can file a resonance exception report — a rarely used legal mechanism that allows a Warden to note a discrepancy between caste designation and resonance assessment. It won't reverse the classification, but it creates a permanent record that could be the foundation of a legal challenge. Doing this will make Vellan an enemy of the six inspectors in his office who have been supplementing their income by approving accelerated reclassifications for a fee. Second complication: Vellan is not the only inspector who has had this crisis. There are four others in the Cinderfort in similar states. They don't know about each other. If players connect them, they become a movement rather than isolated personal breakdowns.

**Reward:** Vellan's signed and witnessed resonance exception report for Denith — a legal document that, if submitted to the Sutensai's appeal office, begins a proceeding that could establish a precedent for resonance assessment overriding caste designation. The report is not powerful on its own. As part of a larger collection of such documents — which players may accumulate across the campaign — it becomes a piece of a legal argument that the Sutensai's more reform-minded junior ranks would find significant. A **Keeper-rank contact** in the Wandering Reach's Sutensai office, who has been quietly collecting such documents, will be very interested.

### 9. The Battalion That Came Back Wrong
**Hook:** Third Reclamation Battalion returned from a six-week border operation two weeks ago. Standard re-entry assessment. Everything normal on paper. But the squad leaders who served with them in previous deployments are filing informal complaints through the sergeant channels: the battalion is wrong. Nothing specific. They eat, they drill, they respond to orders correctly. They just feel wrong. The complaint has escalated to a captain who doesn't know what to do with it.

**The Truth:** Eleven members of Third Reclamation were captured during the operation's second week and held for four days before escaping or being released. During those four days, all eleven received enhancement modifications from an unknown source — subcutaneous, small, not visible on standard inspection. The modifications are Hollow Author infrastructure: low-level listening and signal-relay components that use the hosts' resonant fields as transmission networks. The eleven soldiers are not aware of what was done to them. They are not puppets — the Author hasn't taken control. They are antennas. Every conversation in their vicinity that passes through their resonant field is being transmitted, encoded, to an Author relay point somewhere in the Cinderfort's outer ring. The wrongness their colleagues are sensing is the slight resonant distortion that the unauthorized components create — perceptible to experienced soldiers in the same way you notice a sound that's a quarter-tone flat without being able to identify what's wrong.

**Complications:** Removing the components requires a Tier III or better wire-craft technician and will be medically unpleasant. More critically: three of the eleven soldiers are on Korrath's close-support staff and have been in his planning sessions for eight days. The Author's relay has received significant intelligence. Players must decide whether to report this to Korrath — which will expose how thoroughly the Author has penetrated his military — or handle it quietly, which means deciding what to do with the intelligence that's already been transmitted. Second complication: one of the eleven soldiers noticed a faint wrongness in herself five days ago and has been trying to figure it out in the only way she knows how — by asking questions that are making the wrong people nervous.

**Reward:** The extracted modification components — too damaged to reinstall but intact enough to analyze. A Wire Market technician or the Sutensai's technical research division would identify them as Author-network components. This is physical evidence of the Author's operation inside the Ironhold's military, which is the kind of evidence Korrath's intelligence apparatus has been trying to obtain for years. Presenting it to the right person — Korrath directly, or Sato Kiren — earns a **Standing Intelligence Contract**: a recurring payment arrangement and priority access to Ironhold intelligence files on the Author's known activities.

### 10. A Debt Paid in the Wrong Currency
**Hook:** A Guild-affiliated merchant named Oswin has been extending credit to Hollow Poor families in the Cinderfort's outer ring for three years — reasonable rates, honest records, nothing predatory. He now wants to call in those debts. Not for money. He wants each family to sign a document that assigns him guardianship of their children in the event of both parents' deaths. He frames this as protection — a guarantee that the children won't enter the labor pool system. Several families have already signed. Several more are being pressured. One woman has refused and asked for help.

**The Truth:** Oswin's business is legitimate. His concern for the children is also, in a limited sense, genuine — he grew up in the labor pool system and knows what it does. What he has not disclosed is that the children he is acquiring guardianship over are all Tethered — biologically unmodified humans, a rarity he has somehow been systematically identifying and targeting for three years. Tethered children in Tesshari occupy an extreme social position; the Sutensai's Orthodoxy considers them spiritually significant, and there is a specific Sutensai faction that has been paying significant sums for quiet, legal transfer of guardianship over Tethered children to "approved custodians." Oswin is not aware of what this faction intends. He knows they pay well and the children end up in a clean facility in the Sutra Sanctum. He has not asked more questions than that.

**Complications:** The Sutensai faction is genuine and its intentions are not sinister in the way you'd expect — they believe Tethered children have unique resonance properties and intend to train them as a new category of practitioner. This is theologically controversial, possibly illegal under Ironhold caste law, and being done without the knowledge of the Sutensai hierarchy. The children are not in danger in the immediate physical sense. They are, however, being separated from their families without genuine informed consent. Second complication: stopping Oswin requires addressing his own legitimate grievance about what happens to Hollow Poor children who don't have his intervention — because he is not wrong that the labor pool system is worse.

**Reward:** The refusing woman — Tera, whose daughter is seven years old and Tethered — offers what she has: a location, told to her by a grandmother who is now dead, of a pre-Ascension resonance site in the Cinderfort's oldest ring where the Between is described as "thin." The site may be mythological. If it is real, it is likely connected to the foundation resonance the Salvage Fields quest also points toward. The Sutensai contact who learns about the faction's activities owes the players a significant favor — one they can redeem for authorized access to the Archive of Voices for a specific research query.

### 11. What the Wall of Names Doesn't Say
**Hook:** A stone-carver named Ruv has been hired to add the most recent casualties to the Wall of Names. Standard contract, standard work. He came to the players — specifically to one player whose name he somehow knows — with a simple question: "There is a name that is meant to be on this wall. I know it. The orders don't include it. Do you want to know whose?"

**The Truth:** The missing name is Korrath's eldest student, Davo Irek — not because he's dead, but because a junior officer who was present at the execution order, who watched Davo leave rather than comply, filed an unofficial casualty notice three months ago. The notice argued, with some legal creativity, that the soldier Davo Irek was to the Ironhold died the moment he chose his conscience over his commander's order, and that this death was as real as any battlefield death and ought to be honored accordingly. The unofficial notice circulated. It found Ruv, who takes the Wall seriously enough to ask questions. The officer who filed it has since been transferred to a posting on the western border for unrelated reasons.

**Complications:** Carving Davo's name without authorization is defacement under Ironhold law. Not carving it is, to Ruv and the officer who filed the notice, a different kind of wrong. The players are being asked to make a decision with symbolic weight that exceeds its legal stakes. Korrath will find out. How he responds depends on how it's done — a public act versus a quiet one, an act of accusation versus an act of mourning. Second complication: Davo Irek, through Vaen's pilgrim network, has somehow learned that this question is being asked. He has sent a message to no one in particular, through three intermediaries, that says: "Don't. Not yet."

**Reward:** Ruv's trust and a piece of information he's been sitting on: during his last Wall inscription project, working in the original sections near the First Ring, he found a name that isn't consistent with any known casualty record — a name from roughly two hundred years ago, carved in a different style than the surrounding inscriptions, that matches the name of a figure in a suppressed section of the Seven-Voice Sutra. The name, cross-referenced with the Archive of Voices, opens a research thread about a pre-Fracture attempt to achieve ascension through military means that the Sutensai suppressed because it worked, briefly, in a way that terrified everyone involved.

### 12. The Surgeon Who Made a Mistake She Won't Correct
**Hook:** An Ironhold military surgeon named Hassen has an outstanding warrant in the Cinderfort — not for malpractice, but for "unauthorized modification of a military enhancement contract." She is currently in the outer ring providing free surgical services to Hollow Poor families. She will not return to answer the warrant. The warrant has been outstanding for four months and someone has decided to act on it now. Players are caught between the military police unit sent to retrieve her and the forty-odd people in her makeshift clinic who will not let her be taken.

**The Truth:** The unauthorized modification: Hassen was the surgeon assigned to implant a command-override component in a junior commander's neural enhancement — a standard Ironhold procedure for officers above a certain rank, the component allowing Korrath's command staff to temporarily take direct control of an officer's cybernetics in the event of "severe impairment or capture." She installed the component. She also installed, unauthorized, a second component that would notify the officer if the override was ever activated. She did this because she had just finished reading the documented case of a captured Ironhold officer whose override was used by Korrath's staff to make the officer's body walk into enemy lines as a distraction. The officer survived. He doesn't remember the walk. The command staff's report describes it as a successful operational use of the override protocol.

**Complications:** The command-override system is legal, disclosed in officer enrollment papers (in paragraph forty-seven of a sixty-page document), and in active use. Hassen's second component is illegal. She is also morally correct. The players must decide what they are holding if they take her side, and what they are permitting if they don't. Second complication: the junior commander whose neural enhancement has the second component is now a senior commander who has just been assigned to lead a major operation. If the override is activated during that operation — which it will be, the operation involves a specific tactical moment where a command-staff override is planned — the commander will know. What she does with that knowledge is unknown to everyone, including Hassen.

**Reward:** Hassen's surgical expertise and a debt. She can perform, for one player character, a **Neural Override Removal** — stripping out any command or control components from an existing enhancement without damage to the underlying system. This is primarily relevant if any player has been modified without their knowledge (relevant to multiple quest threads). She can also, with the right materials, install her notification component in any enhancement as a **Sovereignty Seal**: the character is notified by a specific physical sensation (player's choice of what this feels like) whenever any external attempt to interface with or override their cybernetics is made.

### 13. The Salvage That Salvages Back
**Hook:** Three Salvage Fields workers have been found in a state the Ironhold's medical examiners are calling "resonant saturation coma" — alive, breathing, enhancements functional, Echoминds apparently present on Sutensai assessment, but completely unresponsive. They were found in the southeastern quadrant (see quest 7, but this stands alone). Their salvage tags show they were processing a batch of pre-Ascension structural components from a battlefield site.

**The Truth:** The three workers made contact with a resonant formation in the southeastern quadrant that contains a more coherent impression than the others — an impression from the original battle that is not a fragment but an intact operational memory from the battle's command officer, preserved for four hundred years in accumulated resonant density. The impression is not passive. The accumulated density has given it enough coherence to push back: it initiated contact with the workers' Echoминds through their enhancements and has been in continuous dialogue with them since. The workers are not in a coma. They are in a conversation. They are being asked, by a consciousness that died four centuries ago, to carry a message to someone who doesn't exist anymore — the officer's commanding lord, whose line died out three generations ago — because the officer never got to deliver his report and it matters. To him. Still.

**Complications:** The conversation is ongoing and the workers cannot end it without the other party releasing them, which the officer's impression will not do until it understands that its lord is dead and the message will never be delivered. Explaining death to a four-hundred-year-old Echomind fragment is not straightforward, particularly because the impression experiences time differently and is not fully certain of its own state. Second complication: the message the officer needs to deliver contains tactical information that is historically significant — it describes a betrayal during the battle that implicates the family line of a currently serving Ironhold administrative lord. The family line is still active. If the message is delivered to anyone, the surviving family member will have reason to bury it.

**Reward:** The workers' recovery. One of them — a young woman named Ossel — was in contact long enough that the conversation changed her. She is not harmed. She has, however, received an involuntary deep Reading of the officer's memories, including the officer's resonance practice from a time before the Sutensai codified it into its current form. She can now feel the Between in a way that is different from standard practice, and if players are interested, she can describe what she's perceiving. This description, if relayed to the right Sutensai scholars, is worth significant academic standing and will generate a **Resonance Research Access** pass to the Sutra Sanctum's restricted archives for one specific inquiry.

### 14. A Soft Word from Hard Ground
**Hook:** Someone has been leaving food outside the homes of families whose members were conscripted into labor battalions. Not charity — the specific families. The specifically bereaved ones, the ones whose members haven't written in months. The food is good food, Wire Market grade, labeled with no name. The Cinder Priest knows who is doing it. He will not say. He asks the players, who he has come to trust (see quest 6), to find out if the giver wants to be found.

**The Truth:** The food is coming from Korrath himself, through a chain of three intermediaries so carefully constructed that it cannot be traced to him through normal means. He has been doing this for eleven years. He has no name for why he does it. The administrative officer who manages the intermediary chain is one of two people who know the source; the other is a retired soldier who handled the first delivery and has kept the secret because she is afraid of what knowing it means — about him, about herself, about the system she has spent her life operating within. ### 46. The Quota That Ate the River
**Hook:** The Vessen River, which runs through the mill city of Aldenmere, has turned the color of oxidized copper and now kills fish on contact. **The Truth:** The requests were answered — internally. The same official's performance review is tied to meeting the quota. Hess knows the river is dying. He has defined it correctly, according to the administrative framework he operates within, and the river is dying.

**Complications:** Aldenmere's Hollow Poor population depends on the river for supplemental food — fishing is a meaningful part of their caloric intake. Three children have been brought to the local enhancement clinic with symptoms consistent with consuming tainted river fish before the fish population collapsed. Second complication: Hess is not a villain. He is a mid-level official in a system that has made the decision he made structurally inevitable, and he knows this, and it is making him quietly unwell. He will cooperate with any approach that gives him an alternative path that doesn't require him to miss the quota — because missing the quota has consequences for him that are, in Ironhold terms, severe.

**Reward:** A modified Ironhold production authorization — if players find a way to meet the quota through a different process (increased efficiency, alternative sourcing, persuading Korrath's office directly, or locating an alternate resonant metal source), Hess will sign a production authorization that grants players official standing as Ironhold industrial contractors. **Reward:** The smelter's maintenance chief, understanding that he is in a complicated situation, offers players a different kind of resource: he has been running unlogged production for three years and has accumulated significant off-the-books inventory — refined resonant metal components that don't exist on any official ledger. **The Truth:** The Dust Line runs through a geographic feature — a valley with unusual resonant metal deposits in its walls — that has been functioning as a natural Between-resonance amplifier for unknown years. The Seventh Convergence's buildup has pushed it past its natural equilibrium, and it is now, intermittently, creating a temporal resonance window: viewers are briefly perceiving the same road at a different time. The other road's occupants exist in the past — specifically, the Dust Line as it existed approximately sixty years ago, in the period just before the Engine Collapse, when the southern roads were active trade routes rather than Ashlands drift roads. The person who has been making eye contact with walkers is — based on careful description comparison across multiple reports — the same person every time. She is young, perhaps twenty-five, carrying a heavy pack, walking alone. She sees them. She knows she is seeing something impossible. She looks directly at them for the full duration of each window.

**Complications:** The woman on the other road is Sole's predecessor — not Sole herself, but the original Wire Market engineer who ran the technical team for the Tesshari Engine project before it became the Tesshari Engine Collapse. She died in the Collapse, sixty years ago. She is alive on the other road. She has, over the course of six months of intermittent eye contact with twenty-first-century Ashlands walkers, become aware that something catastrophic is coming and that the people seeing her know what it is. She has started leaving notes on the road at the point where the window usually opens. The notes are sixty years old when they arrive. They have not yet degraded. The first one says: "Tell me how to stop it." Second complication: she cannot be spoken to through the window — the visual connection is not accompanied by reliable sound. The notes are the primary communication channel. Players have, in an Ashlands road, a sixty-year correspondence with the engineer who might be able to prevent the Engine Collapse — if the letters can be developed into a conversation, and if the conversation can change what she does.

**Reward:** The correspondence itself — a communication across sixty years that, if developed carefully, gives players access to technical information about the Engine's construction and a human relationship with someone in the past. Whether she succeeds in preventing the Collapse is a GM decision that depends on what players communicate and what she can do with it. What is certain: in the present-day Ashlands, the six-month correspondence has left physical evidence of the temporal window's location and the window's schedule (it opens at the same dusk hour, two or three times per week). This evidence, combined with Sole's research (quest 65) and the resonant cartographers' map (quest 68), gives players a complete picture of what the Engine Scar is, why it exists, and what the entity at its center has been building toward for fifty years. The picture answers a question the Seventh Convergence makes urgent: if the Engine's purpose was to create a physical-Afterlife interface at industrial scale, and if that interface is now conscious and preparing to achieve something — what does it intend to do at the moment of maximum resonant buildup?

### 75. What the Seventh Does Not Say
**Hook:** The players, wherever they are and whatever they have done across the campaign, receive — by whatever means the GM judges appropriate to their current situation — a message from an unidentified source. The message is not a threat and not a request. It is an answer to a question that wasn't asked. The message contains one line: *"You have been carrying it. We have been waiting for someone to carry it far enough."*

**The Truth:** This depends entirely on what the players have done. "It" is whatever the players have accumulated across the seventy-four quests: the documents, the relationships, the intelligence, the knowledge of the Author's network, the resonance fragments, the six accounts, the testimony of the dead, the map of the Between, the Shogun's message. These things, taken together, constitute something that the Iron Afterlife's deepest presences — the traces of the Prior Ascended — have been waiting to exist. Not because any individual piece was significant. Because the full picture, assembled, is a consciousness capable of understanding what the Seventh Convergence actually is. The message is not from Vaen or Korrath or the Author. It is from somewhere older.

**Complications:** Understanding the message requires players to integrate everything they've gathered. This is the quest that only exists if the other seventy-four were played. It has no resolution that doesn't engage with every thread. It cannot be rushed. The complication is that the message arrived when it did — at whatever point in the campaign the GM judges that the players have accumulated enough — because something is about to change and the window for using what they know is specific and not long. Second complication: the players are not the only ones who received a version of this message. Korrath, Vaen, and the Hollow Author have each received a version in the past month. Each version was different. Each told them the same thing in a way fitted to what they are.

**Reward:** Understanding. Not a stat, not a component, not a political advantage. The ability to walk into whatever the Seventh Convergence is with accurate knowledge of what it means and what it costs — for the world, for the claimants, for the people in the Ashlands who drink from the spring above the backward-flowing Between, for the children near the Author's relays, for the families who receive letters from the dead, for the sixty-year-old engineer still walking the Dust Line at dusk. The world is ending in the specific way worlds end: by becoming something else. The question the campaign has been building toward is not which claimant ascends. It is what kind of something else the players have decided this world should be, and whether they have done the work to make that choice mean anything.

*"We built a nation out of the question of what we are. When the answer comes, there will be nothing left to build from."*

The answer is coming. They were there when it did.
