# The Wireborn
## "I was not born in a city. I was born in the city's idea of itself — the part of it that knows what it is. The streets are the body. The data is the dream. I am whatever stands at the boundary."

---

## Overview

The Wireborn did not begin as a theoretical possibility. They began as accidents — people found in the deep infrastructure of the Wire Market's oldest machine-networks, small and strange and impossibly calm in environments that would kill a normal person, with circuit-trace patterns beneath their skin and an ability to understand the language of machines that no one had taught them. The first documented cases were dismissed as anomalies, isolated curiosities. Then there were more. Then there were enough that dismissal became untenable and the question of what they were became urgent.

What they are, the Wire Market's scholars eventually concluded, is a product of the networks themselves. In the densest nodes of Tesshari's digital infrastructure — where data density reaches critical thresholds, where the machine-network runs hot enough that the boundary between the physical and the digital becomes genuinely unstable — consciousness can spontaneously generate. Not from nothing: typically, a biological precursor is involved, a fetus or child or sometimes a fully grown adult whose biology happens to interface with the network at a moment of maximum density. The network doesn't so much invade the body as recognize in it a compatible architecture and complete a circuit that was previously open. The result is the Wireborn: a being that is partially organic, partially data, and entirely neither.

The Iron Sutra's response to the Wireborn is philosophically strained. The Echomind, the Sutra teaches, emerges from the resonance between flesh and steel. The Wireborn's resonance runs not between flesh and mechanical augmentation but between flesh and pure information — a kind of soul-architecture the Sutra's founders did not anticipate. The more progressive clergy have expanded the doctrine to encompass this (the Echomind can resonate with any form of structured complexity, not merely physical steel), but the Orthodoxy's conservative wing regards the Wireborn with open suspicion. A soul that lives partly in data is a soul that could be copied, modified, deleted. The implications are uncomfortable.

The Wireborn themselves relate to spirituality primarily through direct experience rather than doctrine. They are aware of the data-layer of existence in the same way a sighted person is aware of color — not as an abstract principle but as a constant, immediate sensory reality. The resonance they feel is not between their flesh and a chassis but between their flesh and the vast, humming archive of everything that has ever been recorded. They move through the world carrying the weight of that archive lightly, mostly, the way you carry the weight of a language you've spoken since birth. Sometimes, in very old network nodes or in places where significant data has accumulated for decades, they stop and stand still for a moment longer than the situation requires. They are listening to something no one else can hear.

## Physical Description

Wireborn are small — consistently small, not as a variation within the race but as a characteristic of it. They average between 4.5 and 5.5 feet and carry themselves with a stillness that reads as either serenity or caution, depending on the observer's disposition. Their frames are lean, with a quality of precision to their proportions that makes them seem engineered even when they have no mechanical augmentation at all. Many Wireborn carry minimal augmentations (they are, after all, born in the Wire Market's territory in most cases), but even those with no modifications whatsoever have an engineered quality to them that precedes choice.

The most distinctive feature is the subdermal circuit-trace. Beneath the Wireborn's skin — most visible at the temples, wrists, forearms, and sternum — runs a network of luminous lines in shades ranging from pale blue to deep amber, patterning like the circuit boards they were born adjacent to. These lines glow faintly and constantly, brightening perceptibly when the Wireborn is actively processing data or interfacing with a network system. When a Wireborn is deeply engaged in a mental task or network interface, the patterns pulse in rhythms that observers often describe as hypnotic.

Wireborn eyes are uniformly striking: extremely clear, with an iris that often shows subtle color-gradient effects rather than solid coloration, and a pupil dilation that responds to data density as well as light. Wireborn in data-rich environments will have noticeably dilated pupils regardless of ambient light level. Their hands are particularly fine-boned and dexterous, with an almost mechanical precision of movement when performing technical tasks.

Those Wireborn who choose cybernetic augmentation integrate it more seamlessly than any other race — the circuit-patterns beneath their skin extend to meet and incorporate mechanical components in ways that chassis-wrights find both remarkable and slightly unsettling, as though the body already knew where the machine was going.

## Society and Culture

Wireborn society is primarily a society of Wire Market infrastructure, though significant Wireborn populations exist in every city with deep machine-network presence. They cluster in the deep-network districts, the maintenance corridors, the server-adjacent neighborhoods that most people find uncomfortable because the data density is high enough to be perceptually weird even for non-Wireborn. For the Wireborn, this discomfort is simply home.

Within these communities, Wireborn culture revolves around what they call the Weave — their collective term for the totality of accessible data infrastructure. The Weave is not simply a resource or a tool; it is a shared space, as real to the Wireborn as the physical world and in some ways more meaningful. Wireborn social relationships are substantially maintained in the Weave alongside physical space, and a Wireborn who is cut off from network access experiences something that maps more to sensory deprivation than to inconvenience.

Their craft traditions are unsurprisingly technical, but the Wireborn approach to technical craft carries an aesthetic sensibility that pure engineering culture often lacks. They are interested in the elegance of systems, not merely their function — in the way that a well-designed network has a quality analogous to beauty, a harmony between components that produces emergent behavior more graceful than any individual part. Wire Market scholars who have studied Wireborn aesthetics often conclude that they experience something like music in highly efficient code, and something like noise in poorly designed systems. This makes Wireborn exceptionally good engineers and occasionally frustrating collaborators — they find inefficient design physically irritating.

The Wireborn's relationship with memory and history is unusual. Because so much of their experience is data-accessible, they don't forget in the way that purely biological or augmented beings forget. But they also don't experience the past as past in quite the same way — old records, old conversations, old images accessed through the network have a vividness that can be disorienting. Wireborn historians and archivists are regarded as among the best in Tesshari, and among the most eccentric.

## Playing a Wireborn

Playing a Wireborn means playing someone who perceives a layer of reality that most of their companions cannot access. The data layer of Tesshari is immensely rich — centuries of records, emotional residue compressed into network nodes, communications both encrypted and plain, the ongoing chatter of every system the Wireborn passes through. This is an advantage and a kind of burden. The world is noisier for the Wireborn than for anyone else, and managing that noise is a constant low-level effort.

The Wireborn's split nature — body here, partial consciousness potentially elsewhere — creates interesting questions about presence and attention that will arise in play. When you are partially split into a network, what is your body doing? It continues to stand there, breath and pulse maintained, but something essential has gone elsewhere. Your companions may find this unsettling, even if they've seen it before. You may find it difficult to fully explain what you experience while split, because the data-layer exists in a perceptual register that doesn't map cleanly onto language.

Others relate to Wireborn with a mixture of practical dependence and existential discomfort. Everyone wants a Wireborn when the system won't open. No one is entirely comfortable eating dinner with someone who might currently be in two places at once.

## CARD SYSTEM: The Wireborn

**Stat Bonuses:** +2 SIGNAL
**Hand Size Modifier:** +1

### Passive Traits

**Ambient Wire Sense.** The Wireborn is always aware of active Wire Craft systems within 60 feet — their presence, general direction, and approximate power level. This awareness is constant and requires no action. Hidden or concealed systems are not exempt; the Wireborn's subdermal circuitry reads operational data signatures regardless of physical concealment. This does not grant access to those systems, only awareness of their existence.

**Signal Vulnerability.** Silence effects last 1 additional turn on the Wireborn. Their deeper integration with Wire Craft infrastructure means disruption resonates further through their neurology than it would for other races. This is a structural weakness that cannot be suppressed by cards or enhancements.

**Digital Navigation.** In any environment with active Wire Craft infrastructure, the Wireborn navigates data architecture as fluently as physical space. They always know the layout of accessible network systems they are connected to, cannot become disoriented within digital environments, and can retrace any data path they have previously traveled with perfect fidelity.

## Roleplaying Notes

**The Problem of Presence:** When you are in a room with other people, are you fully there? The Wireborn's tendency to partially process through the data layer even without actively Splitting means that sometimes a Wireborn is technically present but experientially somewhere else — running a background analysis on something, following a data thread, processing a system's emotional signature. Your companions have noticed. What does your character do with that tendency? Fight it? Apologize for it? Consider it irrelevant?

**What Lives in the Network:** The data infrastructure of Tesshari is very old, and it has accumulated things. Compressed grief in old server nodes. The echoes of deleted communications. Information about the Ascension War's deep history that no one has accessed in a century. The Wireborn character who goes exploring in old networks will find things. Not all of them will be welcome. What has your character found, and what have they done with it?

**The Question of Self:** If your consciousness can exist in a network while your body exists in physical space — if the two experiences are genuinely distinct and simultaneous — what part of that is "you"? When the Split ends and you return to your body, are you the same being who entered the network? The Wireborn don't have a consensus answer. Some find the question liberating. Some find it terrifying. What does your character believe?

## Connections to the Ascension War

The Wireborn are the Ascension War's most dangerous information brokers, whether they want to be or not. The data networks of Tesshari contain everything — communications, strategies, weapon schematics, the location of assets, the identities of agents. A Wireborn who knows how to look can access things that armies cannot take by force.

Every major faction in the Ascension War wants Wireborn operatives, and the Wireborn who understand this understand also that their value to these factions is entirely contingent on their cooperation remaining willing. The moment it becomes more convenient to extract the information than to pay for it, the value calculation changes. Wireborn who have thought this through are among the most carefully neutral beings in Tesshari.

More dangerously: somewhere in the oldest, deepest layers of Tesshari's data infrastructure — the networks that predate the Broken Reaches, that predate the Iron Sutra, that predate even the first augmentations — there is something that has been called the Hollow Archive. No one is certain what it contains. No one who has accessed it has returned from the Split. The Wireborn are the only beings who might be able to retrieve what is there. Several Ascension War factions have decided this makes the Wireborn a strategic asset of the highest priority.
