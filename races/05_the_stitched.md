# The Stitched
## "You want to know what it's like? Imagine waking up and not being certain which pieces of the body you're in today are the same pieces that were there yesterday. Now imagine that not mattering as much as you expected it to."

---

## Overview

There are several paths to becoming Stitched, and none of them are chosen lightly. Some are survivors: people whose bodies were so catastrophically destroyed — by war, by industrial accident, by the particular brutality of the Ashlands' collapsed infrastructure — that conventional medicine and chassis-work could not rebuild them from their own parts alone. The solution, pioneered by necessity in the Ashlands and then systematized by the Echo Coast's more experimental medical wings, was assembly: reconstruct the damaged person using compatible biological and mechanical components sourced from whatever was available. If the right arm was unsalvageable, find one that fits. If the original heart was destroyed, install another. If enough components needed replacement that the body became, functionally, a patchwork of different sources — well. The person is still alive.

Others became Stitched deliberately. The experimental surgery schools of certain Ashlands communities, and the darker research programs that some of Tesshari's less reputable factions fund in secret, have produced individuals whose bodies were intentionally assembled or restructured from multiple sources. The reasons vary: radical enhancement procedures that exceeded what could be done with a single unmodified frame, punishment and torture reshaped into something a patient might survive, ideological commitment to the idea that the body is entirely malleable and that selfhood has nothing to do with the specific components housing it. The results are consistently disturbing and consistently alive.

The Iron Sutra regards the Stitched with a complicated discomfort it has never fully resolved. The Echomind, the Sutra teaches, resonates between flesh and steel — but the Stitched's flesh is not a continuous original tissue. Multiple component sources mean multiple potential resonance origins, and what that does to the soul is a question that Sutra theology has not managed to answer without internal contradiction. Some clergy believe the Stitched have a fractured Echomind — a soul that cannot achieve full resonance because its vessel is too divided. Others argue that the Echomind is more resilient than that, that it asserts itself over whatever vessel it inhabits. The Stitched themselves provide little testimony on the matter. They tend to have opinions about theology the way they have opinions about everything else that is said about them: blunt, unsparing, and based on direct experience rather than doctrine.

What the Stitched are, functionally, is nearly unkillable. Not immortal — they die. But what kills a normal being — the catastrophic failure of a critical system — often just removes a component that can be replaced. Stitched survivors of injuries that should have been fatal are common enough that in some parts of the Ashlands, being Stitched is considered a kind of endurance certification. Their bodies also change, over time, in ways they do not fully control — components drift, biological tissue interfaces with mechanical parts in unpredictable ways, and occasionally something new develops: a secondary biological structure growing where previously there was none, a mechanical component developing organic-adjacent behavior, a synthesis of the two that doesn't map to any prior category. The Stitched call this process the Drift. It is rarely comfortable. It is also, sometimes, extraordinary.

## Physical Description

The Stitched are, to put it simply, disturbing to look at. This is not ugliness in any conventional sense — some Stitched are striking, even beautiful, in the way that very unusual things can be beautiful. But the human (or Forged) brain is pattern-matching machinery, and the Stitched pattern does not match. Different skin tones at different points on the body, following lines of surgical reconstruction. Limbs of subtly different proportion than the torso. Seam lines — not the smooth seams of a Forged chassis integration, but the rougher marks of assembly, of things brought together from different origins and persuaded to coexist.

The mechanical and biological components of a Stitched body are often clearly from different sources: different grades of steel, different installation aesthetics, occasional visible mismatch between a joint's housing and the limb it connects. A Stitched person might have one arm that reads as Ironhold salvage and another that reads as Wire Market precision, and a torso that is mostly original biological tissue interrupted by a mechanical sternum of obvious emergency-room origin. This is not always the case — some Stitched have been carefully reassembled with aesthetic consistency as a priority — but even those bear the fundamental tell of assembly: a quality of deliberate construction that differs from the Forged's organic integration.

The Drift produces further variations. Older Stitched — those who have lived with their assembled bodies for decades — often carry additional modifications that no surgeon installed: biological structures that grew at assembly seams, mechanical components that have developed unusual properties, hybrid tissue that doesn't classify cleanly. One Stitched elder in the Ashlands is documented to have developed a secondary joint in each forearm, a completely unplanned biological adaptation to the stress of the mechanical components adjacent to them. Another is noted for mechanical fingers that have begun generating organic calluses. The Drift is individual, unpredictable, and permanent.

## Society and Culture

Stitched communities are small, intensely bonded, and geographically scattered. They congregate primarily in the Ashlands and in the industrial fringe zones of larger cities — places where their appearance is least likely to cause the kind of sustained social friction that makes ordinary life exhausting, and where the practical skills that assembly survival requires are valued rather than feared.

Within these communities, a particular ethic of body autonomy has developed that is unusual in Tesshari's broadly instrumentalist relationship with the physical self. The Stitched, having had their bodily integrity so thoroughly compromised by the events that made them what they are, have become in many ways the fiercest advocates for the principle that a person's body belongs to them in a way that supersedes all other claims. This is not merely philosophical: in Stitched communities, tampering with another person's components without consent is treated as violence on par with assault. The body, however assembled, is yours. This extends to a deep suspicion of anyone who wants to modify them further against their will — which puts them in regular conflict with the experimental programs that originally created some of them.

Stitched maintain their assembled bodies with an intimacy of knowledge that most other races don't have about their own chassis. They know, in a very precise and sometimes uncanny way, the provenance and status of each component — which parts came from where, which are oldest and most vulnerable, which have changed since installation. This knowledge is partly survival necessity and partly something that functions like identity: when you are assembled, knowing what you're assembled from is knowing who you are.

The Drift is treated within Stitched culture as a spiritual process rather than a medical anomaly. When a new adaptation develops — when the Drift produces something new — it is celebrated, examined, and integrated into the individual's self-understanding. You are not what you were assembled from. You are what you have become.

## Playing a Stitched

Playing a Stitched means playing resilience — not invulnerability, but the specific kind of toughness that comes from having been destroyed once and put back together. The Stitched player character is almost impossible to finish in a single blow, but they are not without vulnerability; their instability can produce both extraordinary capabilities and genuine crises at unexpected moments.

The social dimension of playing a Stitched is significant. People look at you with an unease they often cannot name. Children stare. Devout Iron Sutra practitioners sometimes make warding gestures. Even people who know you and like you can have moments where they look at you and their expression changes for just a second before they recover. Playing a Stitched means deciding how you carry that constant low-grade social abrasion — whether you've developed armor for it, or whether you haven't, or whether the armor has developed cracks over time.

The Drift is an ongoing source of character development. Your body changes. Sometimes it changes in ways that are useful and sometimes in ways that are not, and the DM should work with you to make those changes feel organic to your character's story rather than arbitrary.

## CARD SYSTEM: The Stitched

**Stat Bonuses:** +1 FRAME, +1 to one stat of the player's choice (chosen at character creation, reflects the dominant nature of the Stitched's assembly)
**Hand Size Modifier:** +0

### Passive Traits

**Modular Construction.** Replacing or repairing the Stitched's enhancements costs half the standard time and resources. A repair that would take a full rest for another race takes a short rest for the Stitched. A modification that would require a chassis-wright's full session can be performed in a single scene. This reflects the Stitched's body being built for replacement rather than permanence.

**Inconsistent Resonance Signature.** Abilities and effects that target a specific Echomind — tracking effects, identity-lock status, and abilities that require reading a target's personal resonance — have a 50% chance to misfire against the Stitched. When such an ability targets the Stitched, the GM rolls a die before applying its effect; on an odd result, the ability fails to lock on and is wasted. Standard damage and non-targeting effects are unaffected.

**Stagger Resistance.** The first source of Stagger per combat that would be applied to the Stitched is negated. Their distributed pain response and redundant systems absorb the first disruption before it can cascade. Subsequent Stagger from different sources in the same combat applies normally.

### Race Cards

#### Composite Response
**Tier 2 (2 AP) | Power | Race Card**

**Effect:** The Stitched calls on one of their two dominant components, defined at character creation. Choose which component is active when this card is played.

Component A (player-defined at creation, example: Heavy Frame): Deal 6 + IRON damage to one target in melee range and apply Root for 1 turn. The Stitched gains Guard (4 + FRAME) until the start of their next turn.

Component B (player-defined at creation, example: Precision Assembly): Deal 4 + EDGE damage to one target within 20 feet and apply Expose 2. The Stitched may immediately reposition up to 10 feet without spending movement.

Both options are always available. The player records their two component definitions at character creation with their GM and they do not change.

## Roleplaying Notes

**The Provenance Question:** Every component in your body came from somewhere. Some Stitched know their components' origins in detail; some don't. What does your character know about where they were assembled from? Is there a part of them that belonged to someone who died? How do they feel about that? Does the component feel like theirs now, or does it feel like a borrowing that has outlasted the original owner?

**The Social Cost:** The reaction most people have to the Stitched is not cruelty, usually. It's a specific discomfort that people try to suppress and fail at suppressing, and that your character has watched a thousand times. How do you handle the moment when a new acquaintance's expression changes? Have you built defenses? Do those defenses ever slip? What is underneath them when they do?

**What the Drift Is Teaching You:** If you take the Drift seriously as a character element — if you think of your body's ongoing evolution as a process that has meaning rather than just being random — what is it teaching you? What pattern, if any, do you see in the adaptations you've developed? Is your body trying to become something? If so, what?

## Connections to the Ascension War

The Stitched are not natural political actors in the Ascension War, but they are deeply affected by its prosecution. The experimental programs that created many of them — programs that operated under war-time urgency and wartime ethics — continue in the shadows of the conflict, funded by factions that want better soldiers and are not squeamish about method.

The Stitched have a particular relationship to the Ascension War's question of what Tesshari should become. The Prior Ascended transcended the body entirely — they left the flesh-and-steel vessel behind. The Stitched are, in some sense, the extreme of what happens when you stay: when you insist on persisting in flesh and steel even after the flesh has been destroyed and rebuilt and destroyed again. They are the answer to Ascension that says: no. I am staying. I am staying in this.

Whether that answer is heroic or tragic or simply stubborn is a question the Ascension War has not finished asking.
