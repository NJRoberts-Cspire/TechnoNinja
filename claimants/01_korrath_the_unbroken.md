# Korrath the Unbroken — The Iron Sovereign

## "You tell me gentleness is virtue. I have watched gentleness bury more children than I ever have. Come back when you have held forty years of graves in your hands and still believe that."

---

## Who They Are

### The Surface

Tesshari knows Korrath the way it knows mountains — as something that was simply always there and will outlast everything else. He is the Warlord-Lord of the Ironhold Reach, the largest contiguous military territory in the Broken Reaches, and he has held it for thirty-seven years without surrendering a single claimed border. His forces number somewhere between forty and eighty thousand — nobody outside the Ironhold has an accurate count, and he does not publish one.

He is also almost entirely machine.

The stories vary on what remains of the original flesh. A lung. Part of a jaw. Eyes that are said to still be original, though no one who has looked into them closely has described them as warm. The rest is resonant war steel, bonded so deeply into his Echomind that the Iron Sutra's most conservative scholars refuse to acknowledge him as fully alive. He does not care what the scholars think. He has outlived three generations of them.

His public theology is simple: the Seventh Convergence is approaching, and he will be its instrument. Not because he was chosen. Because he will take it. Because taking is the only honest relationship a soul can have with power. He says this plainly, in open court, to visitors and envoys and to the faces of the other claimants' representatives when they come to negotiate. He is not subtle about what he intends. He considers subtlety a waste of time that belongs to weaker people.

His armies do not rape or burn. This is not mercy — it is efficiency. Populations who are not traumatized into hatred become populations who can be redirected toward his purpose. He has written extensively on this distinction. The treatise is called *The Practical Sutra* and it is used as a military training text in three of the seven Reaches, including some who consider themselves his enemies. They use it because it works.

### The Depth

Korrath was born Kor Shattan, third son of a minor rice-lord in the Pale Reach — a territory that no longer exists under that name, having been absorbed, fragmented, and renamed twice since his birth. He was not always the thing he became. There was a version of him that laughed at bad jokes and kept a garden of dwarf persimmon trees he grew from seed and felt genuine, uncomplicated joy when a student he was teaching finally understood something.

That version of him made a decision sixty-two years ago that he still believes was the right one. He is no longer certain he survives in the consequence of it.

His truth is not performance. He has genuinely watched every alternative fail. He watched the peace treaties of the Second Concordance collapse into the Ash Burnings. He watched the spiritual revolution of the Gentle Reformers produce a decade of poetry and then a famine that killed two million people because no one with enough force had bothered to protect the grain roads. He watched his own teacher, a man of extraordinary wisdom and genuine compassion, negotiate his way into being publicly disassembled — piece by piece, over three days — by a warlord who simply did not share his commitment to non-violence.

What Korrath learned from these things is not that cruelty is good. He learned that cruelty is neutral — a tool, like any other. What matters is the architecture around the tool. He built his architecture over forty years. It is, in some genuine sense, working. The Ironhold Reach has the lowest child mortality rate in Tesshari. Its roads are maintained. It has not had a famine in twenty-three years. These are not accidents. They are the result of a man who has thought very carefully about what peace requires and concluded that peace requires the absolute credible threat of overwhelming force, maintained without exception or sentiment.

He believes this is love. He is not wrong. He is also not entirely right.

What he will not say, what he barely allows himself to think: the process of becoming the thing capable of delivering this has cost him the ability to feel the thing he was trying to protect. He has built a civilization that keeps children alive. He cannot remember what a child feels like when you hold one. The steel took that specific warmth somewhere before the second decade. He notices its absence the way you notice a tooth that has been gone so long you only find the gap by accident.

He is afraid of Vaen. Not because she might kill him. Because when he has observed her from a distance — through reports, through the testimonies of surrendered cities — he recognizes something in her method that looks like what he gave up. He does not know if she has found it genuinely or built a convincing architecture around its absence. He does not know which answer frightens him more.

### The Cost

He has replaced his nervous system four times. Each time, the replacement is better — faster, more precise, capable of registering finer gradations of mechanical input. Each time, the range of what he can actually feel narrows slightly. He can no longer taste. He has not been able to taste since roughly the twenty-second year of his reign. He was not aware this would happen when it happened and he did not choose it explicitly. It was simply what the steel required.

He had a wife. Her name was Mira Shattan. She died not violently but slowly, of a sickness that came for the lungs, in the fourteenth year of his reign. She spent the last two years of her life in a house he had built specifically for her comfort in the mountains above the Ironhold's capital, with paid attendants and a view she had always wanted. He visited seven times. There were seven times he could spare. He does not believe he could have visited more often. He also knows she stopped expecting him to after the fourth visit. The house is still there. No one else lives in it. He does not know why he has not had it repurposed.

His eldest student, a young man named Davo Irek who was the closest thing he had allowed himself to a son, challenged him publicly in the twenty-ninth year over an order to execute the leadership council of a surrendered city. Korrath explained the logic: the council had already once accepted peace terms and then organized resistance. A second chance was an invitation to a third insurrection. Davo argued for mercy. Korrath gave the order. Davo left the Ironhold. He is now one of Vaen's most prominent traveling companions.

Korrath has not sent anyone after him. He has not tried to bring him back. He has not tried to have him killed. He has spent nine years doing nothing about Davo Irek at all. This is the clearest evidence that something in him still functions in a way he cannot name.

## History

He was born in the third month of the Year of the Pale Tide, sixty-four years before the campaign begins, in a village called Shattan-on-the-Slow-River that was notable only for producing modest rice harvests and a small local tradition of resonance-weaving — the folk craft of tuning resonant metal fragments to carry personal histories. His mother was a resonance-weaver. His father administered the rice lord's granary. He was the third son, which meant he had no inheritance and significant freedom, and he spent his early years exploiting both.

He came to martial study under a master named Ohren Vast, a philosopher-soldier of the old school who believed combat was a form of dialogue and that victory was less important than the quality of the conversation. Korrath was a brilliant student who disagreed with the central premise. He and Ohren argued constantly for eleven years. He genuinely loved the man. Ohren's method — engagement, negotiation, finding the humanity in an opponent, creating the conditions for a conflict to resolve without requiring one side's destruction — produced remarkable results in small encounters and intimate disputes. Korrath watched it fail at scale for a decade.

The breaking point was the Pale Reach Dissolution — a political crisis that unraveled over four years and ended in the absorption of the Pale Reach by three neighboring territories. Ohren was the senior advisor to the Pale Reach's lord, who was a genuinely decent administrator and genuinely terrible tactician. Ohren negotiated brilliantly. He secured agreements that should have worked. Each agreement was honored by the other parties for exactly as long as it was inconvenient to break it. When the final collapse came, Ohren offered himself as a hostage for the lord's safe passage. The lord escaped. Ohren was held for three days and then publicly disassembled while a crowd watched. Korrath was not there. He received the news by messenger.

He spent the following three years deciding what to do about everything Ohren had taught him. The answer he arrived at was not to discard it but to recognize that it was technology — a tool for managing human interaction — and that like all technology it required the right deployment environment. Ohren's methods worked in conditions of rough parity and genuine mutual interest. Korrath would create those conditions by force and then the methods would work. He began acquiring the first of his cybernetic enhancements at thirty-one with salvaged war steel from a battlefield he had walked alone after the fighting ended. He picked up the pieces of dead soldiers and considered what each one meant. He made his first replacement — the left hand, lost to a crossbow bolt two months earlier — using a dead man's finger joints and the resonance-weaving techniques his mother had taught him as a boy.

The campaign that built the Ironhold Reach took eleven years and ended not in a final battle but in a negotiation — a long, exhausting, technically brilliant piece of diplomacy that unified eight minor territories through a combination of credible military threat and genuine economic incentives. Korrath had, in fact, learned everything Ohren taught him. He had simply surrounded it with iron.

The middle decades are the decades of governance — of policies implemented, of the treatise written, of borders held, of the slow systematic replacement of his body that accelerated each time a wound required it. He did not plan to become what he is. He simply chose, each time, the repair that made him better capable of continuing. At some point in the thirty-eighth year he realized he had passed the threshold where the flesh remaining was mostly structural necessity rather than identity. He did not grieve this. He noticed that he did not grieve it, which troubled him briefly, and then he moved on.

The Year of the Iron Augur — twelve years before the campaign begins — was when the resonance scholars announced what most serious practitioners had suspected for a generation: the Seventh Convergence was not prophecy in the vague religious sense but a demonstrable structural feature of the Between, measurable in resonance signatures accumulating in the deep bands. Something was building. A seventh Ascension was thermodynamically possible in a way it had not been since the last Prior Ascended achieved the Between two hundred years ago. Korrath had been preparing for this for fifteen years before the announcement. He had not needed the scholars to tell him.

## Goals and Methods

### Immediate Goals (Campaign Opening)

Korrath is engaged in the systematic absorption of the Ash Threshold — the second-largest military territory in Tesshari, which has been destabilized by the death of its previous lord and the four-way succession dispute that followed. He is doing this not through open invasion but through a combination of: backing one of the four succession claimants (a capable young administrator named Hessa Vor who does not know the full terms of his backing), economic strangulation of the other three factions, and positioning his forces on three of the four borders in what he describes publicly as "defensive positioning."

He is simultaneously running a systematic espionage operation against the Hollow Author, who he has identified as the most dangerous threat among the claimants — not because of power but because of invisibility. He knows puppet bodies exist. He does not yet know how many. He has assigned three separate intelligence cells to this problem and they have not yet found each other because he does not want them to know about each other.

He has sent no representatives to contact Vaen's pilgrimage. He is watching it from a distance and doing nothing. This is unusual enough that his closest advisors have noted it.

### Long-term Goals

Korrath's conception of the Seventh Convergence is the conquest of the Between itself. The six Prior Ascended achieved the resonance state but then, according to all available theology, became something else — present everywhere in the deep resonance but no longer directing, no longer choosing, no longer *doing*. Korrath considers this a failure of will. The Between does not get to unmake him. He will enter the Between and remain himself, which means the Between will be reorganized around the fact of him. He has not told anyone this because even his most loyal commanders would find it disturbing.

The end state, in his mind, is not a world ruled by an iron fist. It is a world that no longer needs to be ruled because the weight of will sufficient to organize it has been built directly into its structure. He imagines something like a machine that maintains itself. He imagines not being needed anymore. He has not acknowledged to himself that this is what he wants.

### Methods

Korrath pursues every goal through a consistent architecture: establish overwhelming force capability first, then offer genuine terms that make resistance irrational. He does not lie about his intentions. He does not make promises he cannot keep. He will negotiate in good faith and honor every agreement he makes. He will also make sure, before negotiating, that the structure of the situation makes his preferred outcome the only rational choice.

He is willing to do almost anything if the logic holds. He will execute leaders who have proven they will not honor agreements. He will destroy infrastructure to deny it to enemies, even knowing civilians will suffer. He will use people — including the players — as instruments for goals they may not fully understand.

The one line he holds: he will not kill children for political purposes. This is not mercy in the general sense. He cannot fully articulate why this line exists when other lines do not. His advisors have learned not to suggest crossing it.

## Relationships

### Allies

**Vessel-Commander Sato Kiren** — Korrath's most senior field commander, a woman of fifty with twenty-three years of service who has replaced roughly sixty percent of her body in Korrath's service and believes in his methods with the fervor of a convert. She is not a yes-person — she pushes back on tactical decisions regularly and is usually right — but she has absorbed his fundamental philosophy so completely that she is, in a meaningful sense, an extension of him. She commands the eastern armies. She also keeps the one written record Korrath has allowed of his original history, held in a sealed container she has instructions never to open. She has not opened it. She is curious about it every day.

**The Iron Sutra Order of the Ironhold Reach** — The Sutra's orthodoxy holds that Echomind resonance requires both flesh and steel in balance; Korrath, who is almost entirely steel, should by strict theology be spiritually compromised or dead. The Order in the Ironhold has quietly developed a counter-interpretation over thirty years — that flesh and steel are not the only components of Echomind balance, and that will can serve as the organic element when sufficiently refined. They did not develop this interpretation because Korrath told them to. They developed it because Korrath's Echomind resonance readings are, by any measurable standard, among the strongest ever recorded. Their theology adjusted to fit the data. Korrath finds this both useful and slightly contemptible.

**Hessa Vor** — The succession candidate Korrath is backing in the Ash Threshold. She is twenty-nine, genuinely capable, and does not know the full terms of his support. She believes she is receiving backing in exchange for a favorable trade agreement and a mutual defense pact. Both of those things are in the actual contract. The third term, which she has not seen, transfers administrative authority over the Ash Threshold's deep resonance extraction sites to the Ironhold Reach in the event of her death without a named heir. Korrath considers this a reasonable hedge. He has no current intention of enforcing it.

### Enemies

**The Pale Concordance** — A loose alliance of three smaller territories that exist in the space between Korrath's reach and Vaen's pilgrimage route. They are not strong enough to fight him directly. They are strong enough to be an ongoing logistical complication. Their primary strategy is to maintain themselves as a neutral buffer and hope that Korrath and Vaen destroy each other. He finds this sensible and mildly admirable as strategy. He plans to absorb them in the third year of his Convergence campaign regardless.

**On Vaen:** He does not consider her an enemy in the way he considers armed opponents enemies. She is a problem of a different category. He has not moved against her pilgrim movement despite having the military capability to crush it, and he has given no public explanation for this restraint. Privately: he is uncertain what killing her would do. Her calm is not armor — it appears to be a property of her resonance that operates without her active direction. He does not know what happens to that resonance when she dies. He suspects it does not simply stop. He suspects, based on deep resonance readings his scholars have taken from the areas her pilgrimage has passed through, that the silence she leaves behind in places is structural — permanent changes to the ambient resonance field. He is running calculations.

**On the Hollow Author:** Cold contempt mixed with genuine professional respect. The Author is doing something Korrath recognizes — building infrastructure of control — but doing it in a way that requires hiding. He considers hiding a confession of weakness. He also knows the Author has been invisible for forty years, which means they are either incomparably better at this than they appear or operating on a timeline longer than his own. Either possibility is worth taking seriously. He has told his intelligence cells that the Author is the primary threat and that understanding the Author is more important than engaging Vaen. He has not told them why he considers the Author more dangerous than Vaen. The answer is: because he understands Vaen's method and does not understand the Author's.

### The Players

Korrath will encounter the players through one of two vectors: as potential instruments for a specific problem he cannot solve through military force, or as witnesses to something he wants documented. He does not particularly need friends. He will, however, recognize capability when he sees it, and he is pragmatic enough to offer genuine value to people he is using.

He wants from the players what he wants from everyone: accurate information and reliable action. He will pay extraordinarily well. He will be completely honest about what he is asking them to do and why. He will not ask them to cross lines that would make them useless to him afterward. He is, in a meaningful sense, an excellent employer.

The complication: working for Korrath means being seen working for Korrath. Every other faction in Tesshari will adjust its posture toward the players accordingly. He knows this and will warn them. He considers it their problem to manage.

## The Other Claimants

### On Vaen of the Empty Hand

"She has done something real. I will not take that from her. Whatever she has found — or whatever she has convinced herself she has found — it is not a performance. I have seen performances for sixty years and I know what they look like from the resonance readings. She is not performing.

What I do not know is whether the thing she has found is what she says it is or whether it is a very deep wound that she has learned to experience as peace. The difference matters enormously for the question of what happens when she enters the Between. A genuinely empty vessel achieving the Between is one outcome. A vessel full of suppressed fire achieving the Between while believing it is empty is something else entirely.

I have not moved against her. I should. Every week I do not move against her the geography of her pilgrimage becomes harder to reclaim. My commanders have stopped asking me directly. They have started documenting it instead. I notice this.

I do not know why I have not moved against her. I have seventeen justifications. None of them are the real one. I have not found the real one yet."

*— from a private dictation, Year of the Returning Current, recovered by the players in a possible late-campaign discovery*

### On the Hollow Author

"Whoever is doing this has been doing it for longer than I have been looking for them, which means either they began before the resonance scholars announced the Convergence was measurable — which implies they have independent knowledge sources I don't have access to — or they began at the same time I did and I simply wasn't looking in the right direction. The first possibility is more disturbing. A person who knew before the announcement either has access to deep resonance readings of unusual quality or they experienced something personally that served as data.

The puppets bother me less than the question of the original. Everyone who operates through instruments has a center. Find the center and the instruments become irrelevant. My people have not found the center. In forty years of intelligence work, with the best resonance-tracking capability in Tesshari, I have not found a single candidate who can be definitively placed as the origin. That is not bad luck. That is architecture. Someone built the architecture before I started looking.

I respect the work. I intend to find them. I do not yet know what I will do when I find them."

## Secrets

### What No One Knows

Korrath the Unbroken has, in the deep resonance core of his war-steel body, a single small component that predates everything else — a fragment of resonance-woven steel no larger than a thumb joint, shaped into the form of a fish, which his mother made him when he was seven years old. It is not cybernetic enhancement. It does not serve any mechanical function. It is simply there, nested between two primary structural plates near what was once his sternum, because when he removed the last of his organic chest in the thirty-first year of his reign and his surgeons laid everything out on the table, he looked at the fish and said: "Leave that."

His surgeons did not ask why. They know better than to ask why about certain things.

The fish is the only thing in his body that contains the resonance-weave signature of another person — his mother, who died of a fever when he was nineteen and whom he has not spoken about publicly in forty years. It is the one piece of evidence that Kor Shattan still exists somewhere inside the machine.

If the fish were ever removed — through surgery, through damage, through the Author's interference, through Vaen's strange resonance effect on the area near her — his scholars believe his Echomind coherence would remain stable. It is not load-bearing. It is simply present.

He has never told anyone about it. He has never examined why he kept it. He keeps a small area of his resonance signature deliberately suppressed around that location, which his scholars have noted and interpreted as a minor calibration anomaly. None of them have pushed on it.

### What They Know That Others Don't

Korrath's deep resonance readings have picked up something that the Iron Sutra scholars have not yet published and may not understand: the Between is not simply building toward a seventh resonance event. It is building toward a seventh resonance event *that will require a choice about what kind of event it is*. The six Prior Ascended did not simply achieve the Between — they reshaped it slightly by the nature of their Echomind at the moment of entry. Six different kinds of entry produced the current structure of the Between. A seventh will alter that structure.

He knows — and he suspects Vaen's continuous deep resonance readings from her unaugmented body have also detected — that the shape of the Seventh Convergence is not predetermined. What enters will determine what the Between becomes, permanently, for everyone who relies on resonance thereafter. For every cybernetic enhancement ever made. For every Echomind still in a living body. The Seventh Convergence is not a single person becoming a god. It is a single person rewriting the physics of spiritual existence for every living soul in Tesshari.

He has not shared this because sharing it would give every faction in Tesshari a stake in the outcome that would make his consolidation strategy impossible.

## In Play

### Personality and Voice

Korrath speaks slowly and quietly. This is not affect — it is the natural speech pattern of a man who has learned over sixty years that what he says will be listened to, and therefore does not need to compete for attention. He does not raise his voice. People who have heard him raise his voice have generally not reported it afterward because it did not leave survivors.

He is specific. He never deals in generalities if a specific is available. "Your forces will take the eastern granary by the third day of the following month" rather than "your forces will move quickly." He remembers details — names, dates, specific quantities — with a precision that his interlocutors often find unsettling. He will recall something mentioned in passing six months ago and reference it casually without drawing attention to the fact that he remembered it.

He notices load-bearing structures. In any room he enters, he will involuntarily catalog which walls are structural, where the exits are, what the flooring can support. He does the same thing with conversation — identifying the structural assumption the other person's argument rests on, which is where he will apply pressure if he needs to.

He is not unkind to people he considers genuinely trying. He will explain things. He will correct without condescension. He has a dry, very dark sense of humor that emerges occasionally and that his commanders have learned to recognize as a sign he is actually comfortable, which is rare. He laughed at a soldier's bad joke once and the soldier told the story for fifteen years.

He becomes uncomfortable when asked about his feelings in the first-person present tense. "What do you want?" is more likely to produce a genuine long pause than any tactical question. He will usually answer with what he is *doing* rather than what he wants. His advisors have learned to ask "what is the right outcome here?" instead, which he can answer fluently, even when the answer and the want would be identical.

### Combat Philosophy

Korrath does not enter personal combat unless the calculation demands it. He has armies. He has commanders. He has weapons emplacements and resonance projectors and siege equipment. Personal combat is for situations where a demonstration is specifically required — where the symbolic value of his personal presence, his personal invulnerability, is the operational point.

When he does fight, he fights to end the threat as efficiently as possible, which usually means incapacitation rather than death. Dead opponents can't be absorbed into his purpose. Broken opponents can be redirected. He will spend a full round analyzing a new opponent before engaging if the situation permits, because understanding the specific method an opponent relies on is the first step toward making himself immune to it.

When losing — when a fight is genuinely going against him — Korrath does something that surprises most opponents: he disengages. Not retreats in a panicked sense. He simply stops engaging, takes the hits if necessary, and withdraws with complete deliberateness. He has no ego invested in winning a specific engagement. He has an ego invested in winning everything else. Taking a loss in a single fight to avoid triggering his Unbroken Adaptation before he chooses to is rational. The players should feel unsettled by an enemy who retreats without distress.

His response to being killed in combat is, from the outside, very strange. His body goes inert. His resonance signature does not disappear — it drops to its lowest possible frequency, like a bell that has been struck and is still vibrating but too slow to hear. He will be back at sunrise. He will remember everything.

### Encounter Hooks

**1. The Practical Contract.** The players are hired, employed, or cornered into meeting with Korrath directly in the Ironhold's administrative center. He wants something specific done outside his territory — a retrieval, an assassination, an act of sabotage against the Ash Threshold succession — that he cannot do himself without triggering a formal declaration of war he isn't ready to make yet. He will be completely honest about what it is and why. He will pay extraordinarily well. He will wait while they decide, genuinely, without pressure tactics. His patience in the silence is its own form of pressure.

**2. The Fallen Commander.** A mid-level Ironhold commander has gone missing in territory disputed with the Hollow Author's network. Korrath doesn't send his own forces because doing so would reveal how much he knows about the Author's operation in that area. He wants the players to find the commander — or what the commander became — and bring back whatever they found. He does not tell them that the commander was one of his best intelligence assets and that the Author's people may have spent the last six months getting information out of them.

**3. Witnessing the Unbroken.** The players are present when Korrath is killed — perhaps in an encounter they are tangentially part of, perhaps as witnesses to an assassination attempt. They watch his body go inert. They watch the attackers treat this as a victory. They watch him rise at sunrise. The encounter is not about fighting Korrath — it is about understanding what he is. He will thank them, afterward, for not leaving during the night. He will ask them what they saw. He is genuinely curious how it looked from the outside.

**4. Davo Irek's Choice.** Korrath asks the players to find Davo Irek — his former student, now with Vaen's pilgrimage — and simply ask him if he wants to come home. Not to compel him. Not to bribe him. Just to ask and bring back the answer. This is the most human thing Korrath has asked anyone to do in twenty years and he gives no explanation for it. If the players push him on why he doesn't send a message himself: long pause, then: "Because I want to know what he says when he doesn't know I'm asking."

**5. The Fish Question.** Late in the campaign, if the players have developed significant trust with Korrath — or if they have somehow discovered the resonance anomaly in his chest — he may do something extraordinary: ask them what they would have kept. Not explain what he kept. Ask them. This is not a combat encounter or a negotiation. It is, possibly, the closest thing to a private conversation Korrath the Unbroken has had in thirty years. What the players do with this moment will define the nature of their relationship with him for the rest of the campaign.

## The Claimant's End

### If They Win

The Tesshari that Korrath builds after the Seventh Convergence is genuinely better in specific, measurable ways. Famine becomes structurally impossible — the new physics of the Between, reshaped by his Echomind at the moment of entry, make resonance-based agricultural enhancement available and stable across the entire continent. Child mortality continues to fall. The roads are maintained everywhere. Wars between territories stop, because the infrastructure for war becomes systematically unavailable to anyone who hasn't made a case for it to the governing resonance grid.

None of this requires Korrath to be present or conscious in any traditional sense. He entered the Between as himself and the Between rearranged around the fact of him. He is, by the Iron Sutra's most extreme interpretation, now what the Prior Ascended always were — everywhere and nowhere, a structural feature rather than a person. The difference from the other six Ascended: he intended this. He built the architecture first. The world-machine he enters the Between to become works because he spent sixty-four years designing it.

The cost: the world is also colder. The resonance physics that prevent famine also prevent certain kinds of spontaneous human creativity — the chaotic, inefficient, beautiful waste that comes from people doing things for reasons that don't make strategic sense. The Between now has a preference for efficiency. Art does not disappear. It becomes more purposeful. Children are alive and fed and slightly less likely to do anything surprising.

Whether this is better than the alternative depends entirely on what you think people are for.

### If They Lose

Korrath does not stop. Losing the Convergence — watching one of the other two enter the Between first — does not end his campaign. He recognizes that the physics of the Between have changed, that the Seventh Convergence is complete and whoever won it has reshaped reality at the fundamental level. He assesses what the new physics are. He adapts his methods to the new physics. He begins working toward whatever is possible in the world that now exists.

He is sixty-four years old. He has replaced every piece of his body that can be replaced. He does not age. He will be doing this for a very long time.

If Vaen wins, he will watch what her Convergence produces with the careful attention of a man who has been proved wrong and intends to understand exactly how. He will not admit he was wrong. He will simply incorporate the evidence. He might, in a hundred years, arrive at something that looks like peace. He would not call it that.

If the Hollow Author wins, he will spend every resource he has finding them. Not to undo the Convergence. Because a god that will not show its face is a god he does not trust to maintain what needs maintaining. He will try to compel them to accountability. He will do this even if it kills him, which it might, and then he will rise at the next sunrise and try again.

### If They Die Before the Convergence

Korrath's death before the Convergence releases the resonance compression built up in his Echomind over forty years of enhancement. This is not an explosion. It is a slow dispersal — over the following weeks, across the entire Ironhold Reach and beyond, small pieces of mechanical equipment that had been calibrated to the Ironhold's resonance signature begin to malfunction. Not dramatically. Just slightly. Just enough that maintenance cycles that used to be monthly become weekly. Just enough that the infrastructure he built requires more human attention than it did when he was alive.

His armies do not collapse. Sato Kiren takes command. She is excellent. She is not him. Within five years, the Ironhold Reach is smaller, because she is excellent at what she does but lacks his specific willingness to hold a position no one else thinks is holdable for the decade required to make it make sense. She will do well. His legacy will last. It will also slowly become something other than what he made.

The fish, somewhere in the ruins of his disassembled body, continues to hold his mother's resonance-weave signature. Scholars who examine it will not know what it is or who made it. It is the only piece of him that was never war steel.
