# The Hollow Author — The One Who Is Not There

## "The question is not who I am. The question is what kind of god you would want if you could not see it. If you could not know. If it simply... held. Answer that question carefully. The answer you give is the answer I've been living."

*— spoken by the Penitent, the wandering puppet, to a dying farmer in the Threshold Lowlands. The farmer lived. The farmer could not describe why.*

---

## Who They Are

### The Surface

Nobody knows the Hollow Author exists.

This is not exaggeration. There is no faction in Tesshari with a file bearing this name. There is no Iron Sutra text that identifies this entity. There is no intelligence report, no scholar's paper, no military assessment that describes someone called the Hollow Author as a Claimant for the Seventh Convergence. As far as the organized world of Tesshari is aware, the third Claimant does not exist.

What Tesshari knows instead are three separate people: Shivenne, Lord Tessik, and the Penitent. None of these three people are known to be connected. They have never been seen in the same location. Scholars who have studied all three would find no common authorship in their words because each of the three has a completely distinct voice, history, and method. Shivenne's treatises on Iron Sutra theology read nothing like Lord Tessik's administrative correspondence, which reads nothing like the miracle accounts that follow the Penitent across the lowland territories.

And yet: all three are pursuing the same objective through different approaches, coordinated with a precision that would require communication networks that do not visibly exist.

**Shivenne** is a religious scholar of the Iron Sutra, forty-two years old, based primarily in the great scholarly archive of the Sixth Seat in the Pale Concordance. She is respected for the rigor of her research and slightly distrusted for her willingness to follow an argument wherever it goes regardless of whether the conclusion is comfortable. She has published nine major works. Her most recent argues that the Iron Sutra's framework for the Between is structurally incomplete in a specific way that matters enormously for understanding the coming Convergence. This paper has created significant controversy and has also placed her at the center of exactly the conversation she needs to be at the center of. She is thin, intense, frequently impatient with people who haven't done the reading, and very funny when she decides to be.

**Lord Tessik** is a northern territorial administrator, sixty years old, governing a small but strategically positioned territory called the High Margin that sits on the only viable overland pass between three of the seven Reaches. He inherited the High Margin under ambiguous circumstances seventeen years ago — the previous lord died without named heirs and Tessik appeared with documentation establishing a distant blood claim, which several lawyers disputed and several others did not. He has governed competently and quietly ever since. He is jovial, physically large, fond of long meals and longer negotiations. Three of his standing alliances are with people who are nominally aligned with two different claimants, which he maintains by being genuinely useful to everyone. He is also, nobody has noticed, the single most strategically positioned individual in Tesshari — controlling the pass means controlling the movement of armies, which means being able to make or break any major military campaign in the final phase before the Convergence.

**The Penitent** is a masked wanderer who moves through Tesshari's rural territories performing what witnesses describe as miracles — not theatrical ones, but small, specific, structural ones. Broken bones that are set and healed over three days instead of six weeks. A well that was dry that becomes productive. A child with the deep-lung sickness who recovers. The Penitent says little. The mask is white and featureless, worn at all times, and the body beneath it is slight and moves with the unhurried deliberateness of someone who has nowhere to be. The Penitent's theology, the little that has been recorded of it, concerns itself entirely with the relationship between absence and care — the idea that the most important hand in any life is the one you cannot see, the one that held the structure in place while you were looking elsewhere.

The Penitent has never been seen unmasked. Three people have attempted to remove the mask forcibly. Two of those people are dead from unrelated causes that followed within two weeks. The third removed the mask and found another mask underneath. They have not spoken publicly about the experience.

### The Depth

Somewhere there is a body that is not a puppet.

The Hollow Author was a person once. Has a name from before — the name of a person who existed, who was seen, who made choices in the open with their face toward the world. That person began the project of hiding approximately forty-two years before the campaign opens. The project was intended, at the start, as a tactic. A way of operating without attracting attention from the powers of the day. A way of pursuing a very specific goal — understanding the Seventh Convergence from the inside, becoming part of it from a position of invisibility — without becoming a target.

The tactic became the person.

This is what no one understands about the Author, and what even the Author may no longer fully understand: at some point in the middle decades, the hiding stopped being what they were doing and became what they were. The puppets began not as replacements for a self that was then safely hidden somewhere else, but as extensions of a self that was actively present. The Author moved through the world in multiple bodies simultaneously because this allowed them to see more, do more, understand more. It was exhilarating, at the beginning. It was the most alive they had ever felt.

The problem: the self that was alive in all of those extensions was, over years, being distributed rather than multiplied. When you put part of yourself into Shivenne's arguments and part into Tessik's alliances and part into the Penitent's quiet care, you are not in three places. You are divided into fractions. What constitutes the Author, the actual author — the center from which the puppets are directed — has been getting smaller for forty years while the puppets have been getting more elaborate, more alive, more complete in themselves.

The Author's actual physical body is somewhere in Tesshari, in a room that has not been left in a very long time, maintained by automated resonance systems that keep the body alive while most of the Echomind's active attention is elsewhere. The body is fed. The body is kept at temperature. The body has not spoken in something like eleven years because there has been no one to speak to. The body is the Author, in the technical sense. It is also the least of them.

Their truth is this: invisibility as theology is not wrong. The prophecy does not specify a known god. A god that is everywhere and seen by no one is a real kind of god, perhaps the realest kind — closer to the way the world actually works than the prior Ascended's approach, all six of whom are known and named and therefore can be argued with, refused, resented, replaced. An invisible god cannot be refused because it cannot be identified to refuse. The Author has spent forty years building the infrastructure to become this thing and the infrastructure works.

The part of this they do not examine: they are no longer certain they want to be saved by the thing they are becoming. The god they are building toward requires that the person at the center — the one doing the wanting, the one for whom this all began — dissolves entirely into the structure. The Author began with a goal: to matter, to hold the world, to be the hand in things. At the end of their design, the thing that holds the world will not know it was ever a person who had a name and a face and sat in the sun once and found that pleasant.

They have not changed the design. They have been very careful not to think about this too carefully.

### The Cost

The cost is the room.

The room where the body sits is not uncomfortable. The Author designed it well, forty years ago, with a thoroughness that reflects their general approach to things: every contingency managed, every need addressed, every surface considered. The light adjusts. The temperature adjusts. The resonance systems that keep the Echomind connected to the puppet network are the most sophisticated such systems ever built, built in secret over a decade by a chain of technicians none of whom knew what they were building.

The room has not changed in forty years because no one has been in it to change it. The Author, when they are in the room — when the puppets are sleeping or inactive and the distributed attention has nowhere else to be — has the experience of being in a room that is exactly as it was designed to be and has no interest in them specifically. The room does not know the Author. It only knows the maintenance cycle.

There is no one who knows the Author exists as a single entity. There is no one who knows the Author's original name. There is no one in the world who, if the Author were to die tonight in that room, would know to look. The puppets would continue operating on their standing protocols for approximately three to six days before the lack of active direction from the center became apparent, and then they would go still wherever they were standing, and anyone who saw a puppet go still would simply find a body — three bodies in three different places — and not know that something had ended.

The Author does not miss people in the forward-leaning, urgent way that grief requires. The Author has not had that kind of missing available for many years. What they have instead is a specific kind of detailed attention they give to the puppets' interactions — a quality of watching that is not vicarious but is also not quite direct. Shivenne argues with a colleague and the Author, directing Shivenne, feels something in the argument that is close to satisfaction. Tessik shares a long meal with allies and the Author feels something that is close to warmth. The Penitent heals a child and the Author feels something that is close to meaning.

Close to. The gap between close to and the actual thing has been widening for twenty years. The Author has been measuring the gap with the precision of someone who cannot do anything about it but finds it important to know exactly how large it has become.

## History

There was a name. The name was real. The name belonged to a person who grew up in the Ironhold Reach under Korrath's early consolidation campaigns — not as a noble or a military figure, but as the child of resonance technicians who served the Ironhold's engineering corps. They were unremarkable in most ways and extraordinary in one: the facility with resonance that they had from an early age was so fine-grained that they could perceive the Echomind's field as something closer to a physical sensation than the blunt instrumental readings that even good scholars were limited to. They could feel a person's resonance signature the way someone else might feel the warmth of a body nearby.

They came to the attention of the Iron Sutra's deep scholarship through a paper submitted under their real name at nineteen, which described the Between's structural features from the inside with a precision that older scholars found either fabricated or extraordinary. The scholars who investigated found it extraordinary. The Author spent three years as a research collaborator on the deep resonance project that would, twelve years later, produce the announcement of the measurable Seventh Convergence.

Those three years are where the design began. Not the puppets — the puppets came later. The design began in the reading rooms of the archive, in the late hours, when the Author's unremarkable face was bent over texts describing the six Prior Ascended in detail the public Iron Sutra does not include. The Prior Ascended were all known. They were all seen. They were all, in the historical record, targeted — opposed, captured, martyred in some cases — by powers who could not permit them to succeed. Two of the six achieved the Between by the narrowest possible margin, against significant organized opposition. One did not achieve it at all. The Author read this history and recognized a pattern and made a decision.

The first puppet was built in the Author's late twenties, two years after they left the archive under circumstances they arranged carefully — a small fabricated scandal, nothing dramatic, sufficient to make their departure unremarkable. The first puppet was a simple instrument: a body prepared with a resonance bridge that allowed the Author's Echomind to direct it remotely. The body was not a person. It was not taken from anyone. The Author built it — grew it, technically, in a process they had developed from a synthesis of deep resonance scholarship and enhancement surgery that had no equivalent anywhere in Tesshari. The technology required to build a puppet body is, to the present day, unique to the Author.

Over the following decade, the Author disappeared from their own life in gradual stages. Changed cities. Changed names. Reduced the visible footprint of their existence until, by the time they were forty, no reliable record of their original identity remained in any archive that was not held in their own secured storage. The original name is in that storage. The Author has not looked at it in thirty-one years. They know where it is.

The project of the three puppets took another ten years to build to operational capability — Shivenne took the longest, because her infiltration into the scholarly community required years of published work establishing her credibility as an independent intellect. Tessik required the construction of a plausible noble history. The Penitent required nothing except a mask and the genuine resonance healing ability that the Author had possessed since childhood and that, operated through a puppet body, produces the effects that followers describe as miraculous.

For the last twenty years, all three puppets have been active simultaneously. The Author has not gone a single day in twenty years without being at least two people at once. They remember, distantly, what it felt like to be one person. They remember it the way you remember a smell — not visually, not structurally, just a quality without a form.

The one person who came close to knowing — who deserves a place in this history — was a resonance engineer in the Pale Concordance named Raka Ven, who encountered the Author in the very early days of the project and who noticed something in the first puppet's resonance signature that didn't read right. She was sharp and she was careful and she was building toward a conclusion. The Author did not have her killed. They had Shivenne befriend her over two years and ultimately redirect her research. Raka Ven died of natural causes eleven years ago. She was one of the best resonance scholars of her generation. Shivenne delivered a genuine eulogy at her memorial that Shivenne wrote but the Author felt — and afterward, sitting in the room, the Author understood that the feeling of loss was real and that they did not know which of the two of them it was real in.

## Goals and Methods

### Immediate Goals (Campaign Opening)

The three puppets are in their final approach positions. Shivenne's controversial paper on the incomplete Iron Sutra framework has opened the door to her being invited as a primary scholar-observer at the deep resonance site reading that will confirm the Convergence's timeline — meaning one of the Author's eyes will be in the room where the precise window is calculated. Tessik's strategic position in the High Margin pass is about to be leveraged: he is beginning a series of alliance negotiations that will, over the next two years, allow him to guarantee or deny passage to whichever military force needs the pass most in the final approach to the Convergence. The Penitent is moving toward the Navel of the Between from the opposite direction from Vaen's pilgrimage, following an interior route through territories that neither Korrath nor Vaen's networks penetrate effectively.

The Author is also doing something the three puppets do not know they are doing: they are each, through their separate activities, building toward a ritual architecture the Author has been designing for forty years — a way of entering the Between while distributed across multiple bodies simultaneously, which has never been done and which the Iron Sutra's scholars would call impossible. The Author does not believe it is impossible. They believe the Prior Ascended simply never had the necessary infrastructure.

If the distributed entry works, the Author will be in the Between and everywhere in Tesshari at the same moment. Not as the Prior Ascended are — not as structural features of the deep resonance that can be perceived but not located. As actual present will, in every body that carries a puppet framework, which after forty years of gradual preparation is a very large number.

### Long-term Goals

The Author wants to be the Between without leaving it. Where the Prior Ascended dissolved into the resonance and became ambient and ungoverned, the Author's design is to enter the Between and maintain the puppet network — to be simultaneously the underlying structure and the active agent within it. An invisible god that is also an everywhere-present one.

Their theological argument: the Between is not a destination. It is an infrastructure. The Prior Ascended treated it as a destination and arrived there and became part of it the way a stone becomes part of a riverbed. The Author intends to treat it as a tool and remain the one who holds the tool.

The world, in this outcome, would not know it was being governed. That is the design. The Author has thought carefully about whether they would make good decisions, and has concluded that they would — not because they are wise or moral in some absolute sense, but because they have no stake in outcomes that could corrupt their judgment. They do not want power for wealth, because they have no body that can spend wealth. They do not want it for status, because no one knows they exist. They want it because the world, left to the visible, is a series of documented failures, and someone needs to hold the structure in place while the visible people are busy learning the same lessons over and over.

They believe this. It is not performance. They may also be rationalizing something they cannot see from inside it.

### Methods

The Author does not act directly. They never have. They work through instruments — the puppets, but also networks of relationships that the puppets have built, alliances that Tessik holds, scholarly communities that Shivenne is embedded in, the informal networks of rural communities that have experienced the Penitent's healing and carry a loyalty to the masked figure that is not organized but is real.

They are patient in a way that Korrath's patience (which is tactical) and Vaen's patience (which is spiritual) are not quite. The Author's patience is architectural — they build things that work over decades and then wait for the decades to pass. They are willing to let situations develop through paths they did not initiate as long as those paths move the architecture forward. They will not take an action that risks the structural invisibility of the project. Revelation is the only failure state. Everything else is recoverable.

The one line: they will not harm the puppets' associated networks innocently. The people Shivenne knows, the people Tessik governs, the communities the Penitent has healed — these are, in a functional sense, the Author's only real relationships. The Author will allow harm to come to many people in service of the architecture. They will not direct harm toward the specific individuals who have been, through the puppets, close to them. This is not principle. It is the last operating residue of a person who once had attachments and can no longer access most of them.

## Relationships

### Allies

The Author has no allies who know they are the Author's allies. They have instruments. What follows are the three puppets' own allied relationships, which are real relationships with real people, built over real years, that the Author experiences through the puppet but did not engineer purely for instrumental purposes. These are the closest things the Author has to friends.

**Ryen Callou** — A young data-archivist at the Sixth Seat who works closely with Shivenne and whom Shivenne has mentored for three years. He is sharp, earnest, and deeply committed to the Iron Sutra's scholarly tradition. He suspects nothing. He brings Shivenne (and therefore the Author) a continuous stream of information about what the archive's inner circles are discussing, not because he is a spy but because he talks to Shivenne the way junior researchers talk to mentors they respect: constantly and indiscreetly. The Author's attention in the moments when Shivenne talks with him is different from the Author's attention in most other moments. There is something in the quality of it that, if the Author examined it, would look very much like being interested in a person for who they are rather than what they can provide.

**The High Margin Council** — Lord Tessik's small administrative council, five people who have governed the territory with him for between eight and seventeen years. They are competent, loyal to Tessik personally, and entirely unaware of what Tessik is. They believe he is what he appears to be. They have built real working relationships with him over years. When Tessik needs to make a decision that the Author has not planned for, the Council's input shapes the response — which means, in a meaningful sense, five ordinary people who believe they work for a competent lord have been quietly co-governing the Author's strategic position for a decade. The Author has made better decisions for their input. The Author knows this and finds it uncomfortable to examine.

**The Name on the Pilgrim Roads** — The Penitent has no named allies. But across the rural territories where the Penitent has passed, there is an informal network of people — farmers, road-workers, innkeepers — who have been healed or helped or simply seen by the masked figure, and who pass information about the Penitent's safety and passage without being asked. They do this because the Penitent helped them and they want to help back. The Author receives this care from strangers constantly and has been receiving it for twenty years and has never once been able to respond to it as themselves.

### Enemies

**On Korrath:** He is looking. The Author knows this — Korrath's intelligence cells have come closer to the edge of the project in the last four years than anything before. He has correctly identified that puppet bodies exist and that someone is operating them. He has not found the center. He will find the center if given enough time; his methods are excellent and his persistence is essentially unlimited.

The Author respects Korrath with a specificity that has been building for forty years of watching him work. He is the best architect in Tesshari — which is what the Author considers themselves — and the fundamental difference between them is that his architecture is visible and named and therefore limited by who will follow it. The Author watches Korrath manage the constraints of visibility and finds something in the watching that is not contempt and not quite admiration. It is the recognition of a genuine peer working under a disadvantage.

They do not want him dead. Dead Korrath is a martyr and a legacy and a structural feature of the political landscape in ways that make the Author's final approach more complicated. Living Korrath is a known force whose methods are predictable. The Author will do what is necessary when the Convergence window opens, but until then: let him be a warlord in public, where everyone can see him.

**On Vaen:** She is the more dangerous problem and the Author knows it. Vaen's unenhanced resonance means she can perceive the puppet signature — the specific quality of a directed Echomind — in a way that enhanced individuals cannot. She cannot do anything with this perception directly, but she perceives it, and she has been close to the Penitent twice and once passed within a hundred feet of Tessik. Each time, she said nothing. She did not act on what she perceived. The Author does not know if this is because she is waiting or because she has no urgency about it.

The Author is afraid of what Vaen's Convergence would mean specifically. Not for the world — the Author genuinely does not know if Vaen's dissolution outcome is better or worse than their own design, and they have sat with this uncertainty honestly and not resolved it. What they are afraid of is the specific architectural consequence: Vaen entering the Between as absence would rewrite the physics of resonance in a way that might make the puppet framework impossible after the fact. Not might destroy it — might make it structurally unsupported. The infrastructure the Author has built over forty years requires a Between that maintains certain architectural features. Vaen's design dissolves those features. If Vaen wins, the Author may achieve the Between and find that the thing they built to exist inside of no longer has the shape required to hold them.

### The Players

The players will not meet the Hollow Author. They will meet the puppets.

What the Author wants from the players — separately, through different puppets, potentially before the players have any reason to connect the three figures — is specific: Shivenne wants their testimony on deep resonance phenomena they may have witnessed that isn't being reported in official channels. Tessik wants capable, discreet operators for a sensitive pass security assignment that has nothing obvious to do with the Convergence. The Penitent may simply appear at a critical moment and heal someone who needed it.

If the players are very thorough — if they are the kind of people who compare notes and notice patterns — they may begin to detect the Author through inconsistencies across the three relationships. Shivenne refers to a specific resonance phenomenon using a term that Tessik used in an unrelated conversation two months earlier. The Penitent's theological fragments match the philosophical underpinnings of an obscure Shivenne footnote. Small things. The Author is not perfect. Nobody is perfect for forty years.

What happens when the players begin to suspect the connection is one of the most important escalating sequences in the campaign.

## The Other Claimants

### On Korrath the Unbroken

The Author has been watching Korrath for thirty-eight years. Shivenne has read every published version of his *Practical Sutra* and all available military dispatches. Tessik has maintained careful, profitable neutrality with the Ironhold Reach for seventeen years. The Penitent has moved through Ironhold-adjacent territories and observed the results of his governance: the maintained roads, the lower mortality, the specific quality of a population that is cared for within tight constraints and knows it.

The Author's private assessment, in the space of their own thought where the puppets are not performing: he is probably the second most sophisticated architect in Tesshari. His framework has real merit. His framework is also insufficient for the Convergence goal because it requires a visible, identified center — it requires Korrath to be known as Korrath, and the Between does not care about names. The architecture of his soul is extraordinary. It is also his limitation.

They find his search for the puppets technically impressive and personally neutral. He is looking for the Author's weakness and will not find it because the weakness is not the puppets, which he is looking at; the weakness is the room, which he does not know to look for.

They have spent four years trying to decide whether, if Korrath found the room, they would let him in.

They have not decided.

### On Vaen of the Empty Hand

The Author has sent the Penitent twice to observe Vaen directly, at safe distance. What the Penitent perceived and reported back — what the Author felt through the Penitent — was something the Author has described in their private resonance notes as: *the sensation of being in the presence of a question that is further from answer than I am from my own name.*

She is not performing. The Author would know performance — they have been inside it for forty years. She is doing something real. The Author does not know if what she is doing will work. They are fairly certain they know what it will cost if it does, and this knowledge is the closest the Author comes to something that could be called dread.

The Author has made no move against Vaen. The Author has not manipulated the territories through which Vaen is moving in ways that would prevent her passage. The Author has been watching and waiting and has not pulled any of the available strings. This is not indecision. The Author is not capable of indecision about architecture. This is something else. This is the Author not wanting to touch the question of Vaen because touching it requires thinking about what it means that her path and the Author's path might ultimately arrive at the same place from opposite directions, and the Author has not been able to complete that thought without arriving somewhere uncomfortable.

## Secrets

### What No One Knows

The puppets have developed, over the years, a degree of functional autonomy that was not in the original design.

This is not malfunction. It is an emergent property of the architecture the Author built: the puppet bodies, sustained long-term by a continuous thread of Echomind attention, have developed resonance signatures that are partially independent of the Author's direction. Not fully — not in a way that would allow them to act against the Author's will. But in a way that means each puppet has preferences, tendencies, responses that are their own rather than purely directed.

Shivenne has opinions. Not Shivenne-performing-opinions. Actual opinions about ideas, born from years of sustained intellectual engagement with the material, that sometimes surprise the Author when they form. Shivenne has argued, internally — in the space between directed action and performed speech — for a conclusion in her scholarship that the Author had not reached and that, when Shivenne arrived at it, the Author evaluated and found correct.

Tessik has loyalties. To the Council. To the people of the High Margin. These loyalties are not purely performed — they have weight that pulls against the Author's direction in small, consistent ways that the Author accommodates because accommodating them produces better governance.

The Penitent has something that has no clean name. The Penitent has been healing people for twenty years. Something in the sustained practice of that — of moving through the world with empty hands and attending to suffering with care — has built in the puppet body a resonance quality that is, the Author believes, more genuine than anything the Author currently has access to in the original body.

The secret is this: the Author may have spent forty years building people.

Not instruments. People. Incomplete, distributed, dependent on the Author's active sustaining attention — but with something in them that is real. And the Author's design requires dissolving them at the moment of the Convergence. All of them. The distributed Echomind must draw back to the center for the entry into the Between. Everything that has accumulated in Shivenne's arguments and Tessik's loyalties and the Penitent's careful hands will be recalled, compacted, consumed.

The Author has been aware of this for eleven years and has not changed the design. The Author does not feel urgency about changing it. The Author also, in the room, does not sleep easily in the pre-dawn hours, and the thing that is in the room with the Author in those hours is the specific resonance quality of three people who do not know they are about to end.

### What They Know That Others Don't

The Author has, through forty years of distributed observation — through three sets of eyes and ears in completely different social and geographic contexts, through Shivenne's archive access, through Tessik's network of informants, through the Penitent's accumulated knowledge of what lives at the bottom of suffering in ordinary people — a map of Tesshari that no single observer could have built.

From this map, the Author knows three things that are of major strategic importance:

First: Korrath's Echomind has a singular component at its center — something small, old, irreplaceable — that appears to be the structural anchor of his adaptive resilience. Remove the anchor and he would still be powerful. But the specific quality of his adaptation — the thing that makes each death make him stronger — is organized around that anchor. The Author does not know what the anchor is. They know where it is in his resonance signature.

Second: Vaen's ambient field is not entirely within her control. The Author's distributed resonance observations have mapped the field's behavior over twenty years with more precision than anyone else has managed. The field has a vulnerability window — a specific condition under which it collapses entirely for approximately six to eight seconds. The condition is: Vaen being confronted with specific, direct testimony about the violence her movement has committed, delivered by a witness she cannot dismiss. The Author has not used this. They have not given it to Korrath. They have not decided what to do with it. It is information.

Third: the deep resonance site at the Navel of the Between has a second access point that the Iron Sutra's scholars do not know about — an interior approach through a series of resonance-active caves in the High Margin's eastern ridge. Tessik has known about the caves for twelve years as a geological curiosity and not connected them to the Navel. The Author connected them seventeen years ago. The second access point can be used to enter the Navel's deep resonance field approximately four hours before anyone approaching by the known route could arrive.

Four hours is the entire margin of the Convergence window.

## In Play

### Personality and Voice

The Author does not have a voice. This is the structural reality of what they are. What exists are three voices, each complete, each distinct, each authentic in its way — because the Author has been inside all three long enough that the puppets are expressing something real, even if the real thing is distributed and incomplete.

**Shivenne's Voice:** Fast. Dense. She speaks the way someone thinks when thinking is going well — with the implicit assumption that her interlocutor is keeping up and if they are not that is a solvable problem. She uses technical terms without apology and defines them when asked without condescension. She gets visibly impatient with conversations that are moving too slowly and visibly energized by conversations that surprise her. She laughs suddenly and briefly at things other people do not find funny and then returns to her point. She touches her left temple when she is working through something difficult. She refers to the Iron Sutra's framework the way an engineer refers to a building they admire and think could have been designed better in two specific places.

The tell in Shivenne: she will occasionally pause mid-sentence for slightly too long — half a second, three-quarters of a second — before responding to a question that the Author is processing from a distance. In a fast-moving conversation this is invisible. In a slow, careful conversation, a very perceptive person might notice that her responses sometimes feel not like responses but like reception followed by response.

**Tessik's Voice:** Warm, expansive, physically present. He takes up the right amount of space in a room — not aggressive, but comfortable. He has the specific social ease of a man who has been good at his job for a long time. He laughs often and genuinely at things that are genuinely funny. He compliments good work specifically — not "well done" but "the way you handled the second clause of that agreement was the difference between it holding and not holding." He listens with the quality of attention that makes people feel heard, which is partly genuine (the Author has been listening to people through Tessik for seventeen years and has developed real facility) and partly practiced.

The tell in Tessik: his physical gestures have a very slight uncanny quality up close — not wrong, but like someone imitating physical ease rather than having it naturally. A good actor is performing at rest. Tessik is always performing at rest, and the performance is excellent, but the quality of a body that does not quite inhabit its own comfort is visible if you have spent years around people who have it naturally.

**The Penitent's Voice:** Sparse. The Penitent speaks in short sentences. Declarative. No rhetoric, no persuasion, no framing. Statements. The Penitent says a thing and waits to see if it lands. If it doesn't land, the Penitent says something else. There is no impatience in this — not because the Penitent is patient in Vaen's sense but because the Penitent has no stake in convincing. The Penitent knows what the Penitent knows. What the listener does with it is the listener's project.

The Penitent does not use personal pronouns. Never "I felt" or "I believe" — always "this is" or "there is." This is deliberate and consistent and the one stylistic marker that connects the Penitent to the Author's fundamental nature, though no one has noticed it. The Author, in their own private thought, also rarely uses first-person constructions. They describe their own mental states to themselves in the third person. This has been true for a long time.

The tell in the Penitent: after a healing — after a genuine act of resonance care — there is a moment before the mask turns away where the Penitent's stillness shifts slightly. Something that looks like it is trying to be present and is not quite able to. A person genuinely present in their own body, after performing an act of care, would integrate the experience. The Penitent receives it and then it goes somewhere else. Where it goes is the room.

**The Universal Tell:** All three puppets, when asked directly about their early history or their childhood or the original shape of their life before they became what they are now, deflect with a specificity that is inconsistent with their general directness. Shivenne becomes briefly overly academic. Tessik changes the subject with the smooth competence of a practiced misdirector. The Penitent simply does not respond. The original history is the thing the Author cannot generate for them because the original history is the thing the Author no longer has access to themselves.

### Combat Philosophy

The puppets do not fight unless fighting is necessary to maintain the cover or protect the architecture. Shivenne is a scholar who would not survive physical confrontation with a trained fighter without significant resonance support. Tessik is a lord who has bodyguards and does not personally engage in violence. The Penitent does not carry weapons.

If a puppet is genuinely threatened with violence, the Author's response is: let the puppet take the hit, assess whether the cover is compromised, activate a replacement. The Author has rehearsed this decision tree so many times that it is essentially automatic. There is no grief in the loss of a puppet. There is a brief, cold assessment — the Author evaluating what information was accessible to the destroyed puppet that might now be compromised — and then continuation.

The exception: the Author has, in practice, occasionally found themselves directing a puppet to take actions in combat that are not optimal for the architecture but protect specific individuals. Shivenne has moved between Ryen Callou and incoming damage twice. Tessik has personally intervened to stop violence against a Council member once, at cost to his cover. The Author is aware that this is happening. The Author has not resolved what it means.

If the players find the room: the Author is a person in a room who has not interacted with another human being in eleven years, whose body has atrophied, who is maintaining a connection across hundreds of miles to a network of people who do not know they are being held. They will speak. Their voice, in the actual body, is quieter than any of the puppets' voices — not weaker, but quieter, in the way a room is quiet after people leave it. They will answer questions honestly. Not because honesty is their policy — they have been lying through puppets for forty years — but because there is nothing left in the room that performs. The performance is elsewhere. What is here is the thing that remained when everything else was sent out.

### Encounter Hooks

**1. The Shivenne Paper.** Shivenne's controversial Iron Sutra paper on the incomplete framework has attracted attention from every faction with a stake in the Convergence. The players are asked to meet with her — by Korrath's people, by the Pale Concordance scholars, by someone else — to evaluate her claims. The meeting with Shivenne is a genuine scholarly discussion. She is interesting. Her arguments are real. She will ask the players questions about what they have seen, and the questions are calibrated to extract exactly the information the Author needs from people with field experience. The players may leave the meeting thinking she was simply a very sharp academic. They may also notice one of the tells.

**2. The High Margin Assignment.** Tessik contacts the players through a mutual acquaintance and offers a significant sum for a discrete security assessment of the eastern ridge caves in the High Margin. He frames it as routine geological survey — the caves are structurally active and he wants an independent assessment. What the caves actually are, the players will discover, is the second access point to the Navel of the Between. Tessik watches their discovery with enormous interest. The Author, through Tessik, sees their faces when they understand what they've found. The Author then has to decide whether this team can be trusted — or used — or whether they need to not remember what they found.

**3. The Penitent's Miracle.** Someone the players care about is dying. The Penitent appears. The healing is genuine — the player's person is saved, fully and without condition. The Penitent asks nothing. The Penitent leaves. If the players follow, they will not be able to catch up — the Penitent's route through the landscape is designed to be untraceable. What they will find, if they search thoroughly, is that the Penitent was in this region specifically because Tessik's information network placed the players here, and the Author sent the Penitent deliberately, and the miracle was a real miracle performed with genuine care AND a calculated investment in the players' future behavior. Both things are true. What do the players do with that?

**4. The First Connection.** The players have met at least two of the three puppets in separate contexts. A researcher, or Korrath's intelligence cell, or their own instinct has begun to suggest a connection. They bring this theory to someone with relevant knowledge — a deep resonance scholar, Davo Irek, Vaen herself. What they get back is partial confirmation: yes, the resonance signature quality of directed Echomine puppets has a specific marker. Yes, that marker is present in at least one figure they've encountered. No, no one knows where the center is. The players now have a thread. What they do with it is going to define the second half of the campaign.

**5. The Room.** The players find the room. This requires extraordinary work — months of campaign investment, multiple clue threads, the combination of Korrath's intelligence leads and Vaen's resonance readings and their own synthesis of what they've encountered. The room is in an unremarkable building in a city that has no particular significance to the Convergence geographically. The body is there. The Author is there, in the only sense in which they exist in one place. The Author looks at the players, across the room they have not left in eleven years, and says: "I did not think anyone would find this." And then: "I'm glad someone did." And they mean it. And the players have to decide what to do with a god-in-progress who is also just a person in a room who is glad to see them.

## The Claimant's End

### If They Win

The Tesshari that the Hollow Author's Convergence produces is, from the outside, identical to the one that preceded it. No announcement. No change in visible political structures. No disruption to daily life. Roads maintained. Wars still happen. Famines still happen. Everything as before.

Except: decisions that would previously have led to catastrophe begin to not quite get made. Not prevented — the Author, distributed through the Between, does not override human will. But in the room where a policy is discussed that would break the grain roads, someone says a thing and someone else responds and the conversation goes a direction it might not have gone, and the policy doesn't pass, and no one knows why. Across Tesshari, over years, over generations, outcomes that should have been disasters are slightly less disastrous. Not because anyone is being governed. Because the architecture was adjusted, subtly, at the points that matter.

Whether the Author is still a person in any meaningful sense at the conclusion of this Convergence is unknowable. The distributed Echomind that entered the Between did so as a distributed thing, not a unified self, and the Between received it as such. What exists afterward is something with the Author's forty years of accumulated knowledge and purpose and the specific tendencies they developed across Shivenne's arguments and Tessik's governance and the Penitent's care. It is not nothing. It may not be someone.

There is one strange consequence no one anticipates: the Penitent's healing gift is now ambient, distributed through the resonance grid across Tesshari. Small, very small — not miracles, but a slight improvement in recovery rates from illness, a slight improvement in structural healing from injury. Measurable in population statistics by careful scholars over generations. No one ever identifies the source. Ryen Callou, who is by then in his seventies and the leading resonance scholar of his generation, spends the last decade of his life investigating the anomaly. He concludes it is a feature of the Between that has always been there and was simply not noticed before. He is not entirely wrong.

### If They Lose

If Korrath achieves the Convergence, the Author's distributed architecture still exists, still functions, and still has the second access point to the Navel. The Author spends three years after the Convergence assessing what has changed in the Between's architecture under Korrath's reshaping. Then they begin adjusting the puppet network's operation to work within the new structure. Their fundamental goal — to be the invisible hand in things — is not incompatible with Korrath's outcome if the two architectures can be made to coexist. The Author begins, carefully, to explore whether they can influence the iron world from the inside the way they always intended to influence the previous one. They are patient. The new physics can be learned. Forty years of building in one environment means nothing except experience.

If Vaen achieves the Convergence, the Author's worst technical scenario comes true: the puppet framework is structurally undermined by the new Between physics. The resonance bridges that sustain the puppets begin to fail over months. The Author experiences this as the puppets becoming heavier — the connection requiring more attention for less result. Within a year, only the three main puppets remain sustainable, and only with constant effort. Within three years, those fail too. The puppets go still. Wherever they are standing in Tesshari, they collapse.

The body in the room is still there. The Author is still there. They are now, for the first time in forty years, only one person in one place. They do not know their own name anymore. They sit in the room for a very long time. Then, because the room's automated systems are still feeding them and the body still works, they get up. They walk to the door. They open it. They go outside for the first time in eleven years and stand in the light. They have no plans. They have no puppets. They have no name. They are beginning.

Whether this is the worst outcome or the best one for the person who was originally in that room is a question the Author will spend the rest of their life not being able to answer. There is no urgency left in them either, anymore. Vaen took it from the Between. The Author walks through the world in the silence that remains. They do not wear a mask. Nobody knows their face. Nobody will. They are finally, genuinely, invisible.

### If They Die Before the Convergence

If the body is destroyed, all puppets go inert simultaneously. This happens in a moment — Shivenne mid-sentence, Tessik mid-negotiation, the Penitent mid-healing. Three bodies collapse in three places. The people with them will not understand why for years, if ever.

What collapses with them is the most complete map of Tesshari in existence — forty years of distributed observation, cross-referenced across three social contexts, stored in a mind that is now gone. No one knew to preserve it. No one knew it existed. Every insight about Korrath's resonance anchor and Vaen's field vulnerability and the second approach to the Navel disappears the moment the body stops.

Ryen Callou, who was with Shivenne when she collapsed, spends six months trying to understand what killed her. He never finds a cause of death consistent with anything natural. He eventually publishes a careful, speculative paper suggesting she may have been a puppet operated by an unknown party, citing three observational tells he noticed in retrospect. The paper is academically derided. He is not wrong.

The High Margin falls into administrative confusion without Tessik. The Council manages. They were better prepared than they knew.

The Penitent's last healing is completed before the collapse. The child who was being healed recovers fully. Whoever was standing near the Penitent sees the masked figure simply stop — mid-breath, mid-care — and become a body on the floor. The mask stays on. No one removes it. The body is buried by the people of the village, with the same care they would give their own, because the Penitent was theirs in the way that ordinary things that hold you are yours.

There is a gravestone. It says: *The Penitent. Who Was Here.*

No one knows that is the truest epitaph ever written for the Hollow Author. Someone was here. For forty years, distributed and invisible and genuinely, deeply alone, someone was here.
