# The Hollow
## "You want to know what I gave up. The better question is what I kept. I kept the part that made the choice. Everything else was negotiable."

## Overview

The Iron Sutra is explicit: the Echomind requires both. It is the resonance between flesh and steel. It cannot exist in either alone. The Prior Ascended who developed this theology had reasons — theological, philosophical, and almost certainly political — for drawing the line exactly where they drew it. For centuries, the line held. What has emerged in the era of the Ascension War — as the stakes of every choice have become absolute, as the factions have each pushed toward their extremes — are people who have crossed it.

They went to the edge of one extreme and stepped off. Either they stripped away every enhancement, returning themselves to a state that Tesshari society has not seen in generations, a state it no longer has language for, a state the Caste of Steel classifies as Caste Zero and treats as evidence of either profound spiritual attainment or complete social failure depending on the sect speaking. Or they went the other direction: they removed organic tissue, replaced it, and continued replacing it, pursuing the absolute perfection of the mechanical until what remains of the original biological self is a fraction, a trace, a memory of something that was once the primary substance of their being.

The Hollow class is the class of those who have committed to one of these extremes. It is not a class about balance, despite what some philosophers say. Balance is the Iron Monk's domain. The Hollow is about commitment: the choice to go to one end of the spectrum and follow it wherever it leads, regardless of what the doctrine says, regardless of what the Sutensai declares, regardless of the social cost and the theological consequences. This is not the class for those who are still deciding. The Hollow have decided. They are living with the decision in every subsequent moment of their lives, finding in that living either a kind of terrible clarity or a kind of slow-motion loss that they are either too committed or too far gone to stop.

The Iron Sutra says both extremes are condemned. The Sutensai says both are spiritually dangerous. The evidence, in the form of what the Hollow actually can do, is that both extremes produce something genuine — something that is neither the standard Tesshari enhancement-user nor the standard practitioner of any established tradition. Something new. Whether new is good, in Tesshari, in the era of the Ascension War, is a question each Hollow answers for themselves, mostly in the dark, mostly alone.

## The Hollow in the World

The Hollow are not common. The commitment required is total, the cost is immediate and ongoing, and most people who would consider such extremes are deterred either by the social consequences, the Sutensai's official condemnation, or the simple practical reality of what the removal process involves — whether that removal is of steel or of flesh. Those who continue past the deterrents are, by self-selection, the kind of people who do not stop when they should. Whether this makes them extraordinary or reckless depends on which day you encounter them.

The Empty — those who chose flesh — are the object of complicated reverence among the Flesh Circle and complicated pity everywhere else. A person with no enhancements in Tesshari is not just spiritually unusual; they are practically vulnerable. The entire social infrastructure of the Caste of Steel depends on having enhancements to classify. They have nothing to classify. They register as Caste Zero, which in most territories carries the legal and social status of a child or a prisoner. Some territories have developed exemptions for recognized spiritual practitioners. Others simply don't acknowledge the category and let enforcement be flexible. The Empty navigate a world that does not have a comfortable place for them, and they navigate it with abilities that the world does not expect from someone who looks, to quick assessment, like the least enhanced person in the room.

The Shell — those who chose steel — face different problems. At high levels of organic replacement, the social question is not legal status but identity. At what point does the removal of organic tissue constitute the removal of the Echomind's substrate? At what point does the Shell cease to be a person in the legal, spiritual, and practical sense? These questions are not academic for a Shell at advanced levels. They are immediate, personal, and increasingly answered by the Shell's diminishing capacity to feel the question as urgent. This progression — the gradual fading of the urgency of identity questions as identity itself becomes more mechanical — is the tragedy the class is built around. The Shell may be becoming the most powerful being in the room at the same moment they are becoming the least certain of who the being in the room is.

## Roleplaying The Hollow

**The Point of No Return:** The Hollow has made a choice that cannot be undone. The Empty who removed their enhancements cannot simply put them back — the tissue interfaces are gone or altered. The Shell who replaced their flesh cannot restore it — the biological memory is increasingly gone. How does your character feel about being on a one-way path? Have they made peace with it? Are they still making peace? Is there something they are increasingly afraid to examine too closely?

**The Doctrine That Says You Shouldn't Exist:** The Iron Sutra condemns both paths. The Sutensai has formal positions against both extremes. Your character has heard these arguments, understood them, and continued anyway. What is the private theological position of a Hollow? Have they rejected the Sutra? Have they found an interpretation that accommodates them? Are they simply ignoring it and finding that more comfortable than a resolution they don't have?

**What Remains:** As the Empty loses the steel and the Shell loses the flesh, both are being asked the same question from opposite directions: what is left when you remove one side of the equation? The answer, in practice, is something — the Empty finds their Echomind is larger and stranger than expected; the Shell finds their Echomind persists much further than doctrine suggested. What is your character learning about themselves from what they find at the extremes?

**The Mirror:** The Empty and the Shell, if they encounter each other, are a study in what the same choice looks like taken in opposite directions. They may recognize each other in a way no one else in Tesshari can. They may find each other repugnant — the embodiment of the path they rejected. They may find in each other the only genuine peer conversation available to them. This tension, if there is a Hollow of each kind in a party, is worth exploring.

## Connections to the Ascension War

Korrath the Unbroken has a specific use case for both extremes. The Shell at high levels is simply the most durable heavy combatant available. He deploys them as such and has no theological opinions. The Empty makes him uncomfortable in a way he does not fully articulate — the naked Echomind, the spiritual ability that bypasses enhancement infrastructure, the simple fact of being in the presence of someone who has rejected the entire Caste of Steel framework without being defeated. He watches Empty practitioners more carefully than he deploys them.

Vaen of the Empty Hand has complicated feelings about both. The Empty's path is, on its face, what his theology recommends — the release from steel toward pure being. But the Empty's increasing power, the abilities that emerge from absence, were not in his theological model. His doctrine of release-into-peace does not fully account for release-into-power. He is revising his theology in light of what Hollow Empty practitioners actually become. The Shell is, to him, the exact wrong direction — but the Shell's persistence as a genuine spiritual being at extreme organic depletion is data he cannot ignore, and he is not comfortable with what it implies about his model of the Echomind.

The Hollow Author finds the Shell interesting for obvious technical reasons and the Empty alarming for less obvious spiritual ones. The Author's puppet technology depends on the gap between a person's Echomind and their physical shell — a gap the Author creates through Flesh Shaping manipulation. The Empty, whose Echomind fills the space their enhancements vacated, are difficult to puppet: there is no gap to exploit, only a resonant presence that resists external architecture. The Author considers the Empty a specific kind of strategic problem. The Shell, by contrast, is instructive — the Author studies the furthest-developed Shell practitioners with great interest, because the question of where the Echomind goes when the organic substrate disappears is one the Author has reasons to want answered.
