# Fracture Knight
## "They came back from the Iron Afterlife. Most of them. The rest stayed in my bones, and I am learning to carry the weight."

## Overview

The Ironclad Samurai who went too deep into the Iron Afterlife — this is the story told around fires in territories where the Fracture Knights are known, told with a particular kind of hushed respect, because the person telling the story usually knows someone who came back wrong, or came back carrying something, or came back and is not entirely certain they came back at all. The Iron Afterlife is a place. The living are not supposed to go there. When they do — when a resonance accident pulls a warrior through the Veil, or when an operation requires passage that leaves a spiritual signature the Afterlife does not fully release — some come back whole. Some come back less than whole. And some come back with additions.

The Fracture Knight is the warrior who returned from the Iron Afterlife carrying dead weight. Not metaphorically — the dead have attached. They are present in the Knight's Echomind as something more than memory and less than independent entity: voices, sometimes; impulses, sometimes; in moments of extreme stress or spiritual alignment, a force that acts through the Knight's body with a precision that was not there before. The Knight did not ask for this. The dead did not ask either — they were pulled by the resonant gravity of a living Echomind moving through their territory, and now the living one is home and the dead are still here, and the relationship between them is being negotiated in real time.

The Claimed Knight has one: one specific dead individual who attached, whose history and agenda and personality are present in every moment of the Knight's life. The relationship is complicated in the specific way that relationships are complicated when one party is dead and the other is trying to function in a world that killed the first. The Claimed must negotiate not only what they want but what the dead individual attached to them wants, and those agendas do not always align. The Haunted Legion has many: they went too deep, or stayed too long, or something about them attracted accumulation, and now they carry voices that have begun to blur. The Anchor has made the relationship protective: they stand between the dead and the living world, preventing the crossing that shouldn't happen, and they pay for it in the constant toll of that boundary.

All Fracture Knights fight with one foot in the spirit world. Their strikes come from angles that shouldn't be possible — the dead adjust the geometry. They phase briefly, not because they have learned the Void Walker's discipline but because the dead in them pull briefly toward home. They are simultaneously formidable warriors and walking spiritual situations, and the Sutensai does not know what to do with them, and the dead do not care what the Sutensai thinks.

## The Fracture Knight in the World

Fracture Knights are rare and recognizable to people who know what to look for: an occasional distraction in the eyes, a momentary stillness that does not match the tempo of the conversation, the habit of responding to things that were not said aloud. They are not mad — the dead they carry are not their madness. They are simply carrying on a relationship that takes place in a register other people cannot access. This has social consequences that range from "respected spiritual practitioner who happens to carry the dead" to "person whose conversations with their invisible companions make ordinary social interaction complicated."

The Sutensai's official position on Fracture Knights is complex: the attachment of Afterlife entities to living practitioners is documented, is recognized, and is formally classified as either sanctioned (if the attached dead is a recognized ancestor or approved spiritual companion) or unsanctioned (if the attached entity is not). In practice, the Sutensai cannot actually verify the character of an attached entity through standard Reading, because the entity is not separate from the Knight's Echomind — it is woven in, present as a thread in the Knight's spiritual signature. The practical response is to classify Fracture Knights as requiring periodic monitoring, which the Fracture Knights accept with varying degrees of actual compliance.

In communities that have dealt with Iron Afterlife contamination, Fracture Knights are understood as a specific resource: they can perceive Afterlife incursions, can identify when the dead are restless or bound or under external control, and can in some cases negotiate with Afterlife entities in ways that practitioners without their specific connection cannot. They are useful. They are also occasionally alarming, particularly in the moments when the dead in them take a more active interest in the situation, which is visible to anyone watching closely as a shift in affect, posture, or the way they speak.

## CARD SYSTEM

**Primary Stats:** IRON + RESONANCE
**HP:** (FRAME × 8) + 20
**Hand Size:** 5
**AP:** 3 per turn. Basic Attack free (0 AP), once per turn.

### Class Identity
The Fracture Knight is a slow build that detonates. Their hand is the smallest of any class — five cards, focused, each one powerful. Their signature mechanic is **Fracture**, a status they apply to themselves and enemies that stacks to create escalating damage. The Fracture Knight hits harder than any other class at the Tier 3 ceiling, but requires setup. Their damage ceiling is the highest single-card damage in the game. Their survivability is lower than it looks.

**Fracture (status):** Fracture can be applied to enemies or to the Fracture Knight themselves. Each Fracture stack on an enemy increases damage they take from the Fracture Knight's cards by 3. Each Fracture stack on the Fracture Knight increases the damage of their next Attack card by 4 (consumed on use — all stacks discharge into that attack). Fracture stacks to 5. Fracture on enemies persists until end of combat. Fracture on the Fracture Knight is consumed when an Attack card is played.

**Phantom Charges (resource):** Gain Phantom Charges from specific cards and at the start of combat (begin with 2). Phantom Charges fuel several mid-to-high tier cards. Maximum: 6. Reset to 0 at end of combat.

## Subclasses

### The Claimed — "Whose Agenda Are You Serving?"

One specific dead individual has attached. They are present in every moment — not controlling, but there. They have a history, a perspective, a reason for attachment, and a desire that animates their continued presence. The Claimed must live with the specific personality of a specific dead person and the weight of that person's unfinished business. Whether the Claimed's goals and the dead individual's goals align is the central tension of this subclass. Sometimes the dead person attached because they trust the Claimed to finish something. Sometimes because they want something specific. Sometimes because they are not sure why, and neither is the Claimed.

**Level 3: Define the Claimed**
Work with your DM to establish who has attached to you: their name, their history, their reason for being here, and what they want. This is a real character in your game, present in every session in a limited but genuine sense. The attached dead can communicate with you (impressions, flashes, occasional clear words), can provide information from their life experience, and can occasionally take a more active role in moments the DM designates as appropriate.

**Level 3: Their Strength**
The attached dead augments your abilities in their area of expertise. Work with your DM to determine what they were skilled in (a warrior, a diplomat, a healer, a strategist). You gain +3 to skill checks and stat-based checks related to their area of expertise. This is not your skill — it is theirs, borrowed.

**Level 6: Their Memory**
Once per long rest, you can access a specific memory from the attached dead as a 1-minute trance: you live the memory fully, gaining complete information about a specific event, location, or person from their life. This memory is fully accurate (the dead do not forget, and do not lie, at least not to the person they're attached to).

**Level 10: Full Partnership**
Your relationship with the attached dead has matured into something approaching partnership. Once per long rest, for 1 minute, you can allow them to act through you fully. During this time, you gain +3 to all checks and Attack cards in their area of expertise. When you attack, you can use their best ability modifier (Strength, Dexterity, or Intelligence, as established by their history) instead of your own, if it is higher. When this minute ends, you are yourself again, but they are present more strongly for 24 hours — their influence on your choices is more marked.

**Level 14: The Reckoning**
The attached dead's unfinished business has now fully incorporated into your story. Once per long rest, you can invoke their final purpose: for 1 minute, you are the vessel for their complete spiritual intention. All attacks deal an extra 4d8 necrotic damage. All allies within 30 feet gain +3 to resistance checks against death. Any creature directly responsible for the attached dead's unfinished business (the target of the purpose, the unresolved issue) has -3 to all resistance checks against your abilities for the duration. When this ends: the attached dead rests somewhat. They are less agitated for 7 days. They have not left — but they are, temporarily, at peace.

### The Haunted Legion — "There Are More of Them Than There Are of Me"

The Haunted Legion went too deep, or attracted accumulation, or both. They carry not one dead individual but many. The voices are not always distinct. In moments of stress, they speak simultaneously, which is difficult. In moments of spiritual significance, they organize, which is formidable. The Haunted Legion is the most powerful combat variant of the Fracture Knight and the one most at risk of losing the thread of their own identity in the accumulated weight of all the others.

**Level 3: The Chorus**
You carry multiple dead. Their number is not fixed — the DM tracks it as a narrative element, with the count changing as you encounter significant death in meaningful places. The dead you carry speak collectively but not simultaneously — they blur, overlap, and occasionally harmonize. Your Fracture Knight features that invoke the dead invoke all of them: their combined force exceeds any individual's but is harder to direct precisely.

**Level 3: Legion's Strength**
When you use Between-Strike, you add the number of distinct dead you carry (DM tracks, narrative count) to the extra damage, to a maximum of +5. The chorus concentrates in the strike.

**Level 6: Massed Presence**
Your Fractured Presence radiates more forcefully. Creatures within 30 feet (instead of 10) can sense something wrong — the accumulated weight of many dead pressing close to the world. Creatures who fail a RESONANCE resistance check (DC = your Fracture Knight save DC) when they first come within 30 feet of you are unsettled for 1 hour: -3 to RESONANCE resistance checks while unsettled.

**Level 10: Directed Voices**
Once per short rest, as an action, release a directed wave of the dead's collective presence in a 30-foot cone. All creatures in the cone must make RESONANCE resistance checks (DC = your Fracture Knight save DC) or take 4d10 psychic damage, be frightened until the end of their next turn, and be deafened for 1 round (the voices replace everything else). On a success: half damage, no other effects.

**Level 14: Legion Ascendant**
Once per long rest, for 1 minute, the dead all speak in unison. For this minute: you gain +3 to Attack cards and resistance checks, creatures that hit you with melee attacks take 2d8 psychic damage from the collective force that passes through you, and any creature reduced to 0 hit points within 30 feet of you adds their presence to the chorus temporarily — their voice joins for the duration of the combat, adding their strength to the Legion Ascendant damage. When the minute ends, the temporarily-joined voices return to the Afterlife, and you are exhausted (one level) from the volume of them.

### The Anchor — "The Thing That Stops the Crossing"

The Anchor has made a specific choice about what the Fracture Knight's relationship to the dead means: not to carry them as companions or as a burden but to serve as the line between them and the world. The Anchor holds the Veil in place. When the dead press toward the physical world — when resonant conditions or the Hollow Author's interference creates pressure for unauthorized crossings — the Anchor is the resistance. They prevent the dead from coming through. They prevent the living from being pulled through. They stand between. They pay for this in the constant sensation of being the wall between forces that are always pressing.

**Level 3: Ward the Veil**
As an action, you can establish a Veil Ward in a 30-foot radius centered on you for 10 minutes. Within this radius: no dead can cross the Veil without your permission (Afterlife entities are blocked from manifesting), no living creature can be forcibly pulled to the Iron Afterlife, and any attempt to puppet a living creature within the ward fails automatically (the Afterlife connection required for puppeting cannot be established through the ward). This does not prevent willing communication with the dead.

**Level 3: Anchor's Toll**
The weight of being the wall is real. While your Ward the Veil is active, you take 1d4 psychic damage at the start of each of your turns. This damage cannot be prevented. It is the cost of the barrier.

**Level 6: Expanded Ward**
Your Ward the Veil radius extends to 60 feet. The duration extends to 1 hour. Additionally, when an Afterlife entity attempts to cross the Veil within your ward, you are aware of the attempt and its approximate location immediately.

**Level 10: The Wall Fights Back**
When an Afterlife entity or any creature using Iron Afterlife energy (puppet technology, void walking, etc.) attempts to operate within your Ward the Veil, they take 3d8 radiant damage (Constitution save for half) as the ward resists them. This damage applies once per attempt (not per round of sustained use).

**Level 14: The Final Holding**
Once per long rest, you can establish an absolute Veil closure in a 100-foot radius for 10 minutes. Within this area: no Iron Afterlife energy of any kind can function — no Void Walker abilities, no Echo Speaker contacts, no Flesh Shaper Puppet Threads, no direct Afterlife communication. The dead remain where they are. The living remain where they are. The Author's puppets within the area are severed from their operator for the duration (they stand inert). The ward costs 5 Phantom Charges to establish and 1 Phantom Charge per round to maintain. When it ends, you gain two levels of exhaustion and the dead in you are louder than normal for 24 hours, pressing against the Veil you just reinforced. The Anchor holds the line. What it costs them to hold it is the final question the class poses.

## Roleplaying the Fracture Knight

**The Constant Presence:** The dead are with you. Not as memories — as presences. They observe through you, they comment (inwardly), they respond to situations with the perspective of someone who has seen how this kind of situation ends. How does your character relate to the voices or voice? Have they named this relationship? Do they resent it? Have they found something like comfort in it?

**Whose Business Are You Finishing?** The dead don't attach without reason. Something pulled them to you, something keeps them here. What is it? What does the dead individual, or the collective, want from the time they are spending in your Echomind? How has your own agenda been reshaped by what you now know they wanted from life?

**What You Saw in the Afterlife:** You have been to the Iron Afterlife. Not as a projection or a brief excursion — you went deep enough that something followed you back. What do you remember? What do you wish you could forget? What has it changed about how you understand death, the Echomind, and what comes after?

**The Ghost Story From the Inside:** People who look at a Fracture Knight see a ghost story. The Knight sees a relationship that is more complicated than the story implies. Who is the dead person or people? What were they like? What do they want? The Fracture Knight is not haunted in the way the word is usually meant — they are accompanied, and the distinction matters to them even if no one else fully understands it.

## Connections to the Ascension War

Korrath the Unbroken uses Fracture Knights sparingly and watches them carefully. The dead they carry have knowledge of history — they have seen previous versions of the conflict that is now called the Ascension War, and that historical perspective is occasionally inconsistent with Korrath's official narrative of events. He values the combat capability. He is less interested in the inconvenient historical witness that the dead provide.

Vaen of the Empty Hand finds Fracture Knights the most complex spiritual practitioners he has encountered in his campaign. They have been to the Iron Afterlife and returned, which his theology says is the destination of the released soul — but they returned, which his theology doesn't address. He has lengthy, personal conversations with Fracture Knights whenever he can. He is trying to understand whether the dead who attached to them are at peace, suffering, or something his framework doesn't have a word for.

The Hollow Author has a specific and urgent concern about Fracture Knights: the Between Sight ability. A Fracture Knight can see the resonant thread that connects a puppet to its operator. They can identify, at a glance, which people in a room are not fully themselves. This makes them perhaps the single most reliable field-detection tool for the Author's operations that exists. The Author has made deliberate efforts to identify and neutralize high-level Fracture Knights before they can be deployed against puppet-active operations. Several have succeeded. The ones that failed have produced intelligence about the Author's operational priorities that is among the most valuable in the war.
