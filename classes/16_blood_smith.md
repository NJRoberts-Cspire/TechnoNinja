# Blood Smith
## "You want to know if it hurts? Yes. Every time. I do it anyway. Now step back — this part is messy."

## Overview

The resonant metal in a cybernetic enhancement is supposed to be fixed. That is the point of it. The bonding process that integrates enhancement metal with organic tissue at the cellular level is designed to produce a permanent, stable connection — a system that does not shift, does not liquefy, does not renegotiate its configuration under duress. Blood Smiths have discovered that this is not actually permanent. It is merely very stable. With sufficient will, sufficient pain tolerance, and sufficient willingness to spend the next several days recovering from what that kind of effort costs a body, the resonant metal can be persuaded to move.

They discovered this, most of them, by accident. A moment of extreme stress — combat, grief, the kind of extremity that strips away everything that is not pure survival — and something in their enhancement lattice shifted. Something that should have been fixed became briefly fluid. For most people who experience this, it is a medical emergency. The tissue tears where the metal moves. The pain is reported as extraordinary. For a handful of people, in that moment, the fluid metal does something useful. It becomes, briefly, a weapon. A shield. A tool that the practitioner did not have before and will not have again until they force the same state deliberately. Those people are the first Blood Smiths, and they found each other because catastrophic enhancement accidents have a particular medical profile and word spreads.

The practice that has developed around this discovery is austere in the way that practices built around deliberate self-damage tend to be. There is no spiritual framework for it — the Iron Sutra does not discuss Blood Smithing, because the Sutensai theologians who noticed it happening did not want to give it a theological dimension that might make it feel sanctioned. There is no Wire Market product line. There are no major military programs. There is a dispersed tradition, most densely concentrated in the Ashlands where enhancement quality is poor and practitioners have more experience with improvised modification than anywhere else in Tesshari, built around practitioners who have found each other, compared notes, developed techniques, and paid for those techniques in personal biological cost that accumulates and does not fully disappear.

The median lifespan of a Blood Smith is shorter than the Tesshari average. The tissue stress of repeated liquefaction and re-solidification creates scarring that is unlike any other kind of scarring — internal, at the cellular interface between organic and metal, not visible from the outside but accumulating in ways that show up in diagnostics as a distinctive profile. Experienced physicians in the Ashlands can identify a Blood Smith's enhancement scan on sight. They do not always say so. Some of them have opinions about it. Some of them have learned that Blood Smiths do not generally want opinions — they want practical medicine for what they've done to themselves today, and they are going to do it again.

## The Blood Smith in the World

Blood Smiths are common enough in the Ashlands to have a reputation and uncommon enough everywhere else to be startling. The thing they do in combat — the deliberate bleeding, the visible reshaping of their own tissue, the weapons that appear from wounds — produces a consistent effect on first-time witnesses. Even veterans of the Ascension War, who have seen a great deal, report that Blood Smiths in combat are specifically disturbing to watch. There is a difference between violence done to the body and a person deliberately doing violence to themselves as a tactical decision, and human — or rather, cybernetic-human — observers respond to that difference instinctively.

The Caste of Steel has an awkward relationship with Blood Smithing. The practice's cavalier attitude toward enhancement integrity is technically a violation of enhancement maintenance codes in several territories. The Caste's caste-classification system assumes stable, standardized enhancements. Blood Smiths' enhancements are categorically not stable or standardized. Some territories have resolved this by classifying Blood Smiths as a special subcategory; others simply don't look too closely if the practitioner is useful. Korrath's military has active Blood Smiths and no official position on their Caste status. The pragmatism is consistent with his general approach.

Among the Shellbroken — the former puppets of the Hollow Author — Blood Smiths hold a particular significance. Flesh puppets' organic-enhancement connections were shaped by an external force. Reintegrating those connections requires, in some cases, the kind of intimate understanding of enhancement tissue that Blood Smiths have. Some Menders seek out Blood Smith expertise specifically. The Blood Smiths who work with Shellbroken report that the puppet modifications leave a distinctive texture in the enhancement lattice, and that working to restore it is some of the most technically demanding and personally difficult work they do.

## D&D 5E Class Features

**Hit Die:** d10
**Primary Ability:** Constitution (physical endurance), Strength (shaping force)
**Saving Throw Proficiencies:** Constitution, Strength
**Armor Proficiencies:** Salvage plating (light and medium), wire-mesh armor
**Weapon Proficiencies:** All weapons derived from their own body (class features), resonant blades (all configurations), pulse arms (sidearm weight), circuit lances
**Tool Proficiencies:** Calibration tools, first aid kit (repair focus)
**Skills:** Choose 2 from: Athletics, Acrobatics, Medicine, Intimidation, Survival, Perception
**Starting Equipment:** Salvage plating (medium), resonant blade (standard), calibration tools, first aid kit (5 uses), wrappings to contain bleed-off, 8 gp equivalent. Note: you begin play with visible scarring on your enhancement interfaces.

### Class Features by Level

**Level 1: Liquefaction**
As a bonus action, you can force a portion of your enhancement lattice into a temporarily fluid state. You take 1d4 necrotic damage (this damage cannot be reduced or prevented) and gain 1 Forge Charge (see below). You can liquefy up to your Constitution modifier times per short rest.

**Level 1: Forge Charges**
You have a pool of Forge Charges that power your Blood Smithing abilities. You begin each combat with 0 Forge Charges. You gain Forge Charges by using Liquefaction or through subclass features. Your maximum Forge Charges at any time equals your Constitution modifier + your proficiency bonus. Forge Charges persist until you finish a short rest, at which point your enhancement lattice re-solidifies and the charges dissipate.

**Level 1: Blood Weapon**
As a bonus action, spend 1 Forge Charge to manifest a Blood Weapon from your enhanced tissue. The weapon deals 1d8 + Strength modifier slashing or piercing damage, is treated as a resonant weapon, and uses your Strength modifier for attack and damage rolls. It lasts until you dismiss it (free action) or you drop it. If the weapon is destroyed in combat, you take 1d6 necrotic damage as the connection is severed. Blood Weapons improve with your level: at level 5, they deal 1d10 + Strength modifier damage; at level 11, 1d12 + Strength modifier; at level 17, 2d8 + Strength modifier.

**Level 2: Iron Tolerance**
You have developed an extraordinary threshold for what your body can sustain. Your maximum hit points increase by your Constitution modifier for each level you gain in this class (retroactively applied). When you take necrotic damage from your own Liquefaction, you do not lose concentration on effects if you are concentrating.

**Level 2: Blood Sense**
Your constant intimate awareness of your own enhancement lattice gives you a tactile sense for resonant metal in your environment. You can sense enhancement lattices in creatures within 15 feet (passive). As a bonus action, you can extend this sense to 30 feet for 1 round, learning the general enhancement status (stable/stressed/malfunctioning) of every creature in range.

**Level 3: Blood Smith Subclass**
You choose your path: The Weaponsmith, The Armorer, or The Sculptor. Your subclass grants features at levels 3, 6, 10, and 14.

**Level 3: Deliberate Bleed**
You have refined your Liquefaction technique. When you use Liquefaction, you can choose to take 2d4 damage instead of 1d4 to gain 2 Forge Charges instead of 1. Additionally, once per short rest, you can use Liquefaction as a free action on your turn.

**Level 4: Ability Score Improvement**

**Level 5: Extra Attack**
You can attack twice when you take the Attack action on your turn.

**Level 5: Combat Forge**
You can create Blood Weapons as part of the same bonus action as an attack — you no longer need to spend a separate action. Additionally, while holding a Blood Weapon, your unarmed strikes also deal 1d4 + Strength modifier resonant damage instead of standard unarmed damage — the fluid lattice in your fist is not entirely contained.

**Level 6: Subclass Feature**

**Level 6: Scar Tissue Resistance**
Your enhanced tissue has adapted to repeated stress. You have resistance to necrotic damage. Additionally, you have advantage on Constitution saving throws made to maintain hit points at extremity — death saves, saving throws against effects that impose maximum hit point reductions, etc.

**Level 7: Blood Armor**
As a bonus action, spend 2 Forge Charges to coat yourself in a layer of rapidly-solidified resonant metal drawn from your enhancement lattice. You gain temporary hit points equal to your Constitution modifier × 2 + your Blood Smith level. These last until the start of your next turn unless you spend an additional 1 Forge Charge per round to maintain them. While this armor is active, creatures that hit you with melee attacks take 1d4 piercing damage from exposed edges.

**Level 8: Ability Score Improvement**

**Level 8: Pain is Data**
Your relationship with personal damage has become functional rather than limiting. When you take any damage that reduces you below half your hit points for the first time in a combat, you immediately gain Forge Charges equal to your proficiency bonus. You feel it. You have learned to use what you feel.

**Level 9: Metal Storm**
As an action, spend 3 Forge Charges to release a spray of sharpened resonant metal from your enhancement lattice. Each creature in a 15-foot cone takes 4d6 piercing damage plus 2d6 resonant damage (Dexterity saving throw, DC = 8 + proficiency bonus + Constitution modifier, for half). You take 2d6 necrotic damage from the release regardless of the outcome. The metal re-integrates within rounds, at biological cost.

**Level 10: Subclass Feature**

**Level 10: Living Forge**
You can maintain Blood Weapons and Blood Armor simultaneously with no concentration requirement. Additionally, your Blood Weapons are now magically precise — they can strike ethereal or phase-shifted targets, and they ignore resistance to slashing and piercing damage.

**Level 11: Blood Link**
Once per long rest, you can use your action to forge a temporary biological link between yourself and one willing creature by touch, sharing your enhancement lattice for 1 minute. During this time: the linked creature gains resistance to bludgeoning, piercing, and slashing damage; you share their hit point losses 50/50 (damage to them is split between you); and you have an instinctive awareness of their location and status at all times during the link. When the link ends, any damage split is resolved — you both catch up on what was owed to each of you individually.

**Level 12: Ability Score Improvement**

**Level 12: Forge Mastery**
Your Forge Charge maximum increases by your proficiency bonus. When you drop to 0 hit points, instead of falling unconscious, you can spend all your remaining Forge Charges (minimum 3) to remain standing at 1 hit point for 1 round. After that round, if you have not been healed, you fall unconscious normally.

**Level 13: Total Liquefaction**
Once per short rest, as an action, you can liquefy your entire enhancement lattice simultaneously. You take 4d6 necrotic damage and gain Forge Charges equal to your Constitution modifier × 2. The total liquefaction state lasts 1 round and produces visible effects — your enhancement interfaces glow, fluid metal moves visibly beneath your skin, and your attacks this round deal an additional 2d8 resonant damage.

**Level 14: Subclass Feature**

**Level 15: Iron Body**
The repeated trauma and reconstruction of your enhancement lattice has produced a body that is genuinely extraordinary. Your Constitution maximum increases to 24. You are immune to the frightened condition. When you make a death saving throw, you succeed on a 7 or higher (instead of 10). Once per long rest, when you would be reduced to 0 hit points, you can instead take the damage as necrotic damage to a Blood Armor or Blood Weapon, destroying that structure but remaining conscious.

**Level 16: Ability Score Improvement**

**Level 17: Blood Forge Aura**
While you have 3 or more Forge Charges, you radiate a passive resonant heat that affects nearby enhancement lattices. Creatures within 10 feet with cybernetic enhancements take 1d6 resonant damage at the start of each of their turns. Allies can suppress this for themselves as a bonus action. Enemies cannot.

**Level 18: Sustained Liquefaction**
You can maintain Liquefaction indefinitely without the self-damage. While in Sustained Liquefaction, you gain 1 Forge Charge at the start of each of your turns automatically, and your Blood Weapons and Blood Armor cost 1 fewer Forge Charge to create. You can enter and exit Sustained Liquefaction as a bonus action. It does not count against your Liquefaction limit.

**Level 19: Ability Score Improvement**

**Level 20: The Bleeding Edge**
You have pushed the Blood Smith practice to its absolute limit and survived to develop the limit further. Your Forge Charge maximum is now doubled. Once per long rest, declare a Full Forge state for 1 minute: all your Blood Smith ability costs are reduced to 0 Forge Charges, your Blood Weapon deals maximum damage rather than rolling, you are immune to all damage that is not pure energy or psychic, and at the end of each round you automatically restore 1d6 hit points as your body adapts on the fly. When Full Forge ends, you gain two levels of exhaustion and one of your enhancement interfaces requires repair — it is non-functional until you complete a long rest and receive 1 hour of technical work on the affected site. The things that can be endured always have a morning after.

## Subclasses

### The Weaponsmith — "I Carry Everything I Need"

The Weaponsmith has focused entirely on offensive application. Their blood is a weapons cache. Every engagement begins with the question of what configuration is correct for what they're facing, and they answer that question by bleeding. They have developed a wider repertoire of weapon forms than any other Blood Smith path, can switch between them mid-combat, and have learned to use the wound-and-reforge cycle as a tactical rhythm: take damage, convert damage into Forge Charges, convert Forge Charges into weapons, press forward. It is a terrible feedback loop that works, and the Weaponsmith's medical profile shows that it works at a consistent biological cost.

**Level 3: Arsenal**
You can maintain up to 2 Blood Weapons simultaneously (normally you can maintain 1). Each must be a different form. You can dismiss either individually as a free action. When you attack with two Blood Weapons simultaneously, you can make an offhand attack as a bonus action without spending a Forge Charge.

**Level 3: Form Mastery**
Your Blood Weapons can take specialized forms beyond the standard blade or spike. Choose two forms at this level (and one additional at levels 7, 13, and 18):
- Spike: reach weapon, 1d8+Str piercing damage, can pin a target to a surface (Strength save DC = your Blood Smith save DC or restrained)
- Whip: 10-foot reach, 1d6+Str slashing damage, can disarm as bonus effect on hit
- Anchor: thrown weapon (20/60 feet), 1d10+Str bludgeoning damage, creates difficult terrain in a 5-foot area on impact
- Lance: two-handed, 1d12+Str piercing damage, attacks against restrained targets deal double dice
- Burst: single-use, 2d8 damage to one target, automatically converts back after use for no cost

**Level 6: Blood Fury**
When you score a critical hit with a Blood Weapon, you immediately gain 2 Forge Charges from the violence. Additionally, on a critical hit, you can choose to shatter the Blood Weapon into the target, dealing an additional 2d6 necrotic damage and imposing disadvantage on the target's next saving throw as the resonant metal disrupts their enhancement lattice.

**Level 10: Combat Alchemy**
During combat, switching your Blood Weapon's form is now a free action once per turn. Additionally, when you drop a Blood Weapon (voluntarily or forcibly), you can instantly re-manifest it in your other hand as a free action rather than spending the normal creation action.

**Level 14: The Full Arsenal**
Once per long rest, you can manifest a Blood Weapon configuration that uses your entire available Forge Charge pool. For each Forge Charge spent this way, the weapon deals an additional 1d8 damage. The resulting weapon lasts 3 rounds and then shatters, dealing half its base damage to every creature within 10 feet when it does. You take necrotic damage equal to the number of Forge Charges spent when the weapon shatters — but you'll still be standing, and your enhancement lattice will rebuild.

### The Armorer — "I Have Never Found the Hit That Breaks Me"

The Armorer is built to receive damage and continue. Their Blood Smithing practice is oriented entirely toward defensive application: the liquefied metal of their enhancement lattice is used to build reactive armor, to reinforce failing tissue, to redistribute impact. They are the most purely survivable Blood Smith path and the one with the most methodical relationship to pain. They have learned that endurance is a practice as much as a physical attribute — that the discipline of continuing to act under damage is something that can be developed, and they have developed it to a degree that is genuinely remarkable.

**Level 3: Reactive Armor**
When you take damage, you can use your reaction and spend 1 Forge Charge to reduce the damage by 1d8 + your Constitution modifier. Your lattice absorbs and redistributes the impact. You can use this reaction any number of times, as long as you have Forge Charges.

**Level 3: Iron Endurance**
You have advantage on Constitution saving throws made to resist forced movement, knockdown, or environmental hazards. Additionally, when you use your Deliberate Bleed feature, you can choose to gain the Forge Charges without taking any damage — instead, you accept one level of a minor physical impairment (reduced speed by 5 feet, disadvantage on Dexterity checks for 1 round, etc.) of your choice. Your tolerance for reconfiguration has exceeded your tolerance for pain.

**Level 6: Standing Ground**
When you would be moved against your will (pushed, pulled, teleported), you can spend 2 Forge Charges to plant your feet. You are not moved. Additionally, you cannot be knocked prone while you have at least 1 Forge Charge.

**Level 10: Living Fortress**
While you have 3 or more Forge Charges, you have resistance to bludgeoning, piercing, and slashing damage. While you have 5 or more Forge Charges, the resistance extends to all physical damage types. This does not stack with the Scar Tissue Resistance feature — it supersedes it for physical damage.

**Level 14: The Unbroken Form**
Once per long rest, when you would drop to 0 hit points, your lattice fully liquefies and re-solidifies in a combat configuration. You remain at 1 hit point, immediately gain your Forge Charge maximum in Forge Charges, and for 1 minute: you have resistance to all damage, attacks against you that deal less than 5 damage deal 0 damage after resistance is applied, and at the start of each of your turns you regain hit points equal to your Constitution modifier. When this minute ends, you fall prone and must succeed on a DC 18 Constitution saving throw or gain three levels of exhaustion. The body has limits. The Armorer has learned where they are, and how far past them they can go.

### The Sculptor — "What I Make From What I Bleed"

The Sculptor has taken Blood Smithing outward — beyond their body, beyond weapons and armor, into the creation of external structures from their blood and lattice-metal. They are battlefield artificers who build under fire: walls, bridges, distractions, constructs that serve a function and are discarded when the function is done. The cost is visible in their work — every structure is stained with the blood of its creation, and the Sculptor's enhancement scans show more lattice gaps than any other Blood Smith path, because they are constantly exporting pieces of themselves into the environment and waiting for the body to rebuild.

**Level 3: External Forge**
As an action, spend 2 Forge Charges to create a structure from solidified lattice-metal and organic material within 10 feet of you. The structure can be a wall segment (5 feet wide, 5 feet tall, 3 hit points per Blood Smith level), a platform (5 foot square, supports 500 lbs), or a bridge section (10 feet long, supports 1,000 lbs). The structure lasts 10 minutes or until destroyed. You take 1d4 necrotic damage per structure created. You can have up to your Constitution modifier structures active at once.

**Level 3: Construct Companion**
Once per short rest, spend 3 Forge Charges to create a combat construct from your lattice material. The construct has hit points equal to your Blood Smith level × 2, AC 12, and on its turn can make one attack dealing 1d8 + your Constitution modifier bludgeoning damage. It lasts 1 minute or until destroyed. You take 2d4 necrotic damage when the construct is created. The construct acts on your initiative.

**Level 6: Rapid Structure**
You can create External Forge structures as a bonus action. Additionally, your structures gain hit points equal to your proficiency bonus × 3 (in addition to the standard amount). You can spend 1 Forge Charge as a free action to repair one of your active structures for 1d8 hit points.

**Level 10: Complex Architecture**
Your External Forge structures can now include mechanical components: a structure can have a hinged section (a door), a trap component (a pressure-trigger that deals 2d6 damage and triggers on non-you creatures that step on it), or a signal relay (a Wire Craft amplification point that extends your communication range by 60 feet while active). Each additional component costs 1 extra Forge Charge and 1 additional round of work.

**Level 14: The Battlefield Rewritten**
Once per long rest, spend 8 Forge Charges to perform a Battlefield Sculpt — a full-scale reshaping of a 30-foot radius area that takes 1 full minute of work. You can: create difficult terrain from solidified lattice-metal, construct a full defensive position (four wall segments, a roof, and internal supports), create a network of trap-triggered structures, or raise a structure large enough to serve as tactical high ground. You take 3d8 necrotic damage from the creation. The result lasts for 1 hour. Within this sculpted area, allied creatures have advantage on Dexterity saving throws (they know the terrain; enemies don't). The structures built this way have your proficiency bonus × 5 hit points each.

## Roleplaying the Blood Smith

**The Accumulation:** The physical cost of Blood Smithing does not fully reset. There are scars that don't fade, gaps in the enhancement lattice that re-integrate but never quite return to their original configuration, and a diagnostic profile that every experienced Flesh Shaper reads as the record of choices made under pressure. Your character carries this record in their body. How do they feel about it? Is it the cost of the practice, neutrally accepted? Or does it mean something specific to them?

**The Visibility of Violence:** What Blood Smiths do in combat is visible, personal, and disturbing to witnesses in a way that ordinary weapon use is not. The deliberate self-harm that produces the power is not hidden. How does your character manage other people's reactions to this? Do they explain? Do they avoid explanation? Have they developed an affect around it that distances them from the reaction?

**The Practical Ethics of the Ashlands:** Blood Smithing developed in the Ashlands, where enhancement quality is poor and practitioners improvise. The ethical framework of the class is practical rather than philosophical — you do what works, you maintain your body as best you can, you accept the cost. Does your character carry this pragmatism into the rest of their life? Has the practice of treating pain as information rather than as a limit changed how they relate to other kinds of difficulty?

**The End of the Road:** Blood Smiths die younger than average. The practice accumulates biological damage at a rate that medicine can slow but not fully counter. Every Blood Smith who practices long enough has thought about where this ends. What does your character think about the end of their road? Have they made peace with it? Are they in active denial? Has the question changed how they value time?

## Connections to the Ascension War

Korrath the Unbroken has Blood Smiths in his front-line combat units, where their combination of offensive power and survivability is directly militarily useful. He does not particularly care about their lifespan or the ethical dimensions of the practice. He cares about performance metrics. His Blood Smiths receive good equipment, field-expedient medical support, and the complete absence of philosophical concern from their commanding officers. Some find this simple environment comfortable. Others find the absence of anyone asking hard questions its own kind of weight.

Vaen of the Empty Hand has a specific theological interest in Blood Smiths: they have taken the enhancement-flesh connection and made it actively mutable in a way that aligns interestingly with his theology of release — though not in the direction he expected. He has sought out Blood Smiths to discuss what liquefaction actually feels like. The answers he has received have been honest and have not always supported his theoretical framework.

The Hollow Author is not specifically interested in Blood Smiths as such. Their practice is too personal, too non-transferable, and the techniques do not scale in a way that is useful for the Author's methods. What the Author does find interesting is the Blood Smith's understanding of enhancement lattice fluidity — a purely technical interest in whether their techniques could inform the Author's own methods. Blood Smiths who have encountered the Author's practitioners report a quality of technical inquiry that is more disturbing, in some ways, than direct threat.
