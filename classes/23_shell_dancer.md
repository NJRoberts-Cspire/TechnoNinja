# Shell Dancer
## "Yes, it's broken. No, I don't need you to fix it. It's more useful this way."

## Overview

An enhancement running at failure state is a dangerous thing. The resonant metal in a malfunctioning enhancement does not simply stop — it destabilizes, it cascades, it discharges energy in ways that are erratic and unpredictable and frequently painful to whatever is nearby. The standard response to a failing enhancement is immediate shutdown, immediate medical attention, and immediate repair. The Ashlands did not always have access to immediate anything. The Ashlands had people who needed to fight today even if their enhancements were failing, and in that necessity the first Shell Dancers discovered what no one had previously thought to study: that a malfunctioning enhancement, if you learned to work with the malfunction rather than against it, was not a liability. It was a different kind of weapon.

The Shell Dancer's entire practice is built around deliberate malfunction. They run their enhancements at failure states not because they cannot afford repair but because they have discovered that the erratic energy discharge of a failing enhancement is harder to defend against than the clean output of a functioning one. The cascade patterns are unpredictable. The energy spikes are irregular. The mechanical failures that would incapacitate an ordinary practitioner are, to a Shell Dancer, the specific tools of their combat methodology. They have trained not to not break but to break productively — to control the failure, direct the cascade, and use the moment of catastrophic malfunction as a combat event in their favor.

This requires a body that can sustain repeated enhancement failure without dying. The Shell Dancer has developed this body through years of practice that is approximately equivalent to martial training conducted in conditions that would terrify standard practitioners. The neural adaptations that allow them to function through malfunctions that would incapacitate others are real, and they come from somewhere: from the repeated experience of operating under failure conditions, from the body learning to route around cascades rather than being caught by them, from the specific tolerance that only builds through exposure. Shell Dancers are not reckless. They are extremely practiced at a thing that looks like recklessness from the outside.

The Scavenger path is particularly distinctive: the Shell Dancer who can pull components from defeated enemies mid-combat and integrate them on the fly is doing something that should not be possible given the integration timelines for standard cybernetic enhancements. What they have discovered is that temporary partial integration — not the full cellular bonding of a permanent enhancement, but a loose mechanical connection held in place by the body's ambient resonant field — is sufficient for short-term use. The components do not stay. They are expelled within hours, sometimes violently. But for the duration of the fight, the Scavenger is using their enemy's hardware, and the enemy is not.

## The Shell Dancer in the World

The Ashlands produced the Shell Dancer tradition, and the Ashlands' reputation has followed it. Shell Dancers from other territories exist and are competent, but the ones who earned the tradition's most fearsome reputation came from the place where enhancements fail constantly, repair is rare, and survival is a practice rather than an assumption. An Ashlands Shell Dancer at serious capability is the product of years spent in conditions that would be considered a disaster zone everywhere else.

In military contexts, Shell Dancers occupy a specific role that commanders have learned to both value and worry about. They are extraordinarily effective in sustained engagements where the enemy has adapted to standard enhancement-user combat approaches — the erratic behavior of Shell Dancer abilities is resistant to tactical counter-adaptation, because what makes the Shell Dancer effective is precisely that their outputs cannot be predicted and planned around. They are less effective as a coordinated force element, because they operate best when they can react to the specific failure cascade of the moment rather than execute a planned sequence. They are individual weapons of enormous flexibility and limited predictability, and military commanders have mixed feelings about both qualities.

The Caste of Steel has no good category for Shell Dancers. The Caste system is built around stable, functioning enhancements — you are classified by what your enhancements can do at optimal function. A Shell Dancer at optimal function looks like an ordinary mid-tier practitioner. At full operational effectiveness, they look like a catastrophic failure who somehow keeps fighting. The Caste enforcers have, in documented cases, attempted to detain Shell Dancers for what appeared to be critical enhancement malfunction, only to discover that the Shell Dancer had not needed their assistance and had opinions about being detained. After several such incidents, the pattern has been noted, and Caste enforcers in Ashlands-adjacent territories have received updated guidance.

## CARD SYSTEM

**Primary Stats:** EDGE + FRAME
**HP:** (FRAME × 8) + 10
**Hand Size:** 7
**AP:** 3 per turn. Basic Attack free (0 AP), once per turn.

### Class Identity
The Shell Dancer has the most evasion and reaction cards of any non-technical class. Their signature mechanic is **Shell Step** — a momentary state of non-presence used as a tactical tool. Several cards trigger or interact with Shell Step, and the Shell Dancer toggles in and out of presence mid-combat as a deliberate strategy. They also build power from taking damage: **Cascade** is a resource that accumulates when the Shell Dancer is hit and converts into offensive output. They want to take hits. They want to be in the gap between alive and not, and strike from that space.

**Shell Step (mechanic):** Shell Step is entered by playing a Shell Step card or triggering a reaction card that activates it. While in Shell Step: the Shell Dancer cannot be targeted by hostile cards or Basic Attacks for 1 turn. The Shell Dancer also cannot play Attack cards while in Shell Step — only Defense, Reaction, and Mobility cards. Shell Step ends at the start of the Shell Dancer's next turn unless extended by a card. Exiting Shell Step is not optional — it ends automatically.

**Cascade (resource):** Gained when the Shell Dancer takes damage. Gain 1 Cascade per hit, +1 additional if the hit deals 10+ damage. Cascade resets to 0 at the end of combat. Several cards spend Cascade or scale with current Cascade count. Maximum Cascade: 10.

## Subclasses

### The Breaker — "The Malfunction Is My Best Attack"

The Breaker uses enhancement failures not as byproducts of combat but as primary offensive tools. They have weaponized specific malfunction types — the spark discharge of a power regulator failure, the shockwave of a structural enhancement going critical, the blinding flash of a sensory enhancement cascade — and deploy them with the same intentionality that a standard fighter deploys a sword. The difference is that a sword does the same thing every time. The Breaker's tools are never exactly the same twice.

**Level 3: Failure Cascade Attack**
When you take damage, as a reaction, you can spend 1 Malfunction Charge to immediately make one melee attack against the attacker. This attack deals an extra 2d6 damage of your enhancement type. You can use this reaction once per round.

**Level 3: Overload Strike**
Once per short rest, declare an Overload Strike before making an attack. If the attack hits, it deals maximum damage. The enhancement used for the overload enters a failure state for 1 hour, removing it from standard function but generating 2 Malfunction Charges.

**Level 6: Critical Cascade**
When you score a critical hit, the critical hit also triggers a Controlled Discharge automatically, targeting the same creature (no cost, no save — the cascade is the critical hit's companion). Both the hit and the discharge damage apply.

**Level 10: Multi-Failure**
When you trigger Tactical Failure, the radius increases to 25 feet. Additionally, you can trigger two different enhancement failures simultaneously with one Tactical Failure action, each producing its own damage type and area. Creatures caught in both areas take both damage types in full.

**Level 14: The Perfect Catastrophe**
Once per long rest, declare a Perfect Catastrophe. All of your enhancements simultaneously enter their most extreme failure state for 1 round. Every creature within 30 feet takes 6d10 damage of your enhancement type (Dexterity save for half). You take 4d10 damage of the same type (cannot be reduced). You gain 10 Malfunction Charges. For the following round, every attack you make with a melee weapon deals an extra 3d10 damage from the cascade energy still running through you. After this round, all your enhancements enter a complete shutdown for 10 minutes (they operate at minimum function — you are standard physical, no enhancement bonuses). When they restart, they restart clean.

### The Survivor — "I've Already Been Through the Thing That Would Kill You"

The Survivor has taken the Shell Dancer's fundamental approach and pushed it entirely toward endurance. They take more damage than any other class and continue. Not because they are less vulnerable — because their body has been trained through repeated exposure to failure states to simply continue. They have been at 0 hit points more times than most practitioners have been at 25%. They have developed the specific competency of a person for whom crisis is the normal operating environment.

**Level 3: Iron Constitution**
When you make a death saving throw, you succeed on any roll of 6 or higher. Once per long rest, when you would fail three death saving throws and die, you instead automatically stabilize at 1 hit point. Your body has refused to stop more times than you can count. It has not stopped learning from the practice.

**Level 3: Damage Absorption**
When you take damage of any type, reduce it by your Constitution modifier (minimum 1). This is not resistance — it is physical tolerance. You have been hit this way before. Your body knows where to route the impact.

**Level 6: Battle Hardened**
While below half your hit points, you have advantage on Constitution and Strength saving throws. Additionally, when you spend a Hit Die during a short rest, you can roll the die twice and take the higher result.

**Level 10: Endurance Beyond Reason**
Once per short rest, when you would be knocked unconscious (reduced to 0 hit points), you can continue fighting at 1 hit point for 1 additional round. During this round, you have advantage on all attack rolls and cannot be affected by conditions other than death. At the end of this round, if you have not been healed to above 0 hit points, you fall unconscious normally. The Survivor calls this the Last Round, and they have enough of them that they have started counting.

**Level 14: Unkillable**
Once per long rest, when you would die (three failed death saves, massive damage, etc.), you do not. You stabilize at 1 hit point and immediately gain Malfunction Charges equal to your Constitution modifier. Your enhancements are in critical failure state from whatever killed you (would have killed you) — they may look catastrophic. You continue. You have always continued.

### The Scavenger — "That's a Nice Enhancement. I'll Take It."

The Scavenger has developed the most technically unusual Shell Dancer ability: temporary mid-combat integration of salvaged components from defeated enemies. The integration is rough, impermanent, and occasionally painful to sustain. It works. The Scavenger approaches every combat with an eye toward what they can take from their opponents and use before the encounter ends.

**Level 3: Rapid Salvage**
When a creature with cybernetic enhancements falls within 5 feet of you (drops to 0 hit points or dies), you can use your reaction to rapidly extract one enhancement component. Make a Sleight of Hand check (DC 14). On success, you have the component available for Temporary Integration (see below). The extracted component is non-functional to the original creature (it has been physically removed).

**Level 3: Temporary Integration**
As a bonus action, you can integrate a salvaged enhancement component temporarily. Make a Constitution check (DC = 10 + number of components currently integrated). On success, you gain the use of the component's primary function for 1d4 + 1 hours. On failure, the integration is rejected — you take 1d6 necrotic damage and cannot attempt to integrate that specific component again until your next short rest. You can have a number of components integrated simultaneously equal to half your Constitution modifier (rounded down, minimum 1).

**Level 6: Improved Salvage**
You can now salvage components as a bonus action instead of a reaction. Additionally, your Temporary Integration checks are made at advantage, and the DC does not increase beyond 16 regardless of how many components you have integrated.

**Level 10: Selective Integration**
You can now choose which specific function you integrate from a multi-function enhancement component. When you extract a component, you know its full capability range and can select the most useful function. Additionally, integrated components now last for the duration of the combat plus 1 hour (the component eventually rejects, but you get the full fight out of it).

**Level 14: The Built Environment**
You have integrated so many temporary components that your body has developed a kind of baseline receptivity to foreign enhancements. Temporary Integrations last 8 hours (instead of 1d4 + 1). You can have your full Constitution modifier in simultaneously integrated components. Once per long rest, you can integrate a component you have salvaged from a significant opponent (a commander, a named character, a creature of CR 8+) and gain its full capability for 24 hours — the integration is deep enough for sustained use. When the integration ends, the component degrades completely (it was not made for you, and your body's use has been thorough).

## Roleplaying the Shell Dancer

**The Body You've Built:** Your body is not standard. Years of operating in failure states have produced physical adaptations that show up in diagnostics as anomalous. Your tolerance for pain, damage, and malfunction is genuine and hard-won. How does your character think about their own body? Is it a tool? Is it a companion that has gotten them through things it shouldn't have? Is there a specific failure state or moment of near-death that they refer back to?

**The Ashlands Legacy:** The tradition came from the worst place in Tesshari. If your character didn't come from there, they trained with practitioners who did. The Ashlands' culture of improvisation, endurance, and the specific dignity of surviving conditions that should have been survivable has shaped the Shell Dancer tradition in ways that are visible in how practitioners carry themselves. What has that legacy given your character? What has it cost them?

**The Caste Problem:** Shell Dancers are a Caste of Steel anomaly — their enhancements don't function in the ways the Caste system assumes enhancements function. Are they categorically below what they actually are? Do they use this for operational obscurity? How do they navigate the social infrastructure built around enhancement classification that they don't fit?

**What You Can Take:** The Scavenger's ability to integrate salvaged components mid-combat is powerful and transient — the components eventually go. What does it feel like to use someone else's enhancements for a few hours and then lose them? Have you ever integrated something from someone significant — someone you cared about, someone you killed, someone whose enhancement you recognized? What did you do with that?

## Connections to the Ascension War

Korrath the Unbroken has Shell Dancers in his front-line units as disruptors — not the tactically stable element that Warbanners and Ironclad provide, but the chaos element, the thing that breaks the coherence of an enemy formation. He deploys them as individual agents against specific targets and is comfortable with the lack of predictability in their methods as long as the results are good. His command structure has learned not to micromanage Shell Dancers.

Vaen of the Empty Hand has sought out Ashlands Shell Dancers specifically, because his movement includes communities from the Ashlands and because the Shell Dancer's relationship to brokenness — the finding of capability in what is supposed to be failure — resonates philosophically with elements of his theology of release. He does not fully understand it. He is interested in what they know that he doesn't. Several Shell Dancers have found his movement's community more welcoming than the standard Tesshari social structure, and they have stayed.

The Hollow Author does not engage directly with Shell Dancers. Puppet technology requires stable enhancement integration — a body that is running in deliberate failure states is an extremely poor puppet substrate. The Author's practitioners can control a Shell Dancer's body, but they cannot keep the cascade stable, and an uncontrolled cascade in a puppet is a security liability rather than a strategic asset. The Author avoids Shell Dancers as operational targets when possible. There is a certain grim comedy in the fact that the most reliable protection against being puppeted is to be constitutionally malfunctioning.
