## I. THE "SEEN" MYSTERY — Elder Voss's Arc

### Where It Begins
Session 3: Korrath's symbol is carved on the gate of Seravin after the Unmaking. Below it, in a different hand, a single word: **SEEN**. Stelvar tells players this isn't Korrath — someone followed behind his forces. The mark recurs after subsequent Unmakings across the campaign. Always in the same hand. Always in an impossibly inconspicuous location. Always placed after Korrath's forces have passed.

### Who Is Doing It
**Elder Voss** — the catatonic priest from Session 1.

Voss's catatonia resolved fully by mid-Session 2, but his spiritual equilibrium did not. Thirty years of faith, administered in a rote way that had stopped being faith and become profession, shattered in six seconds when the Singing Steel Event happened and he felt — for the first time in decades — something real. Something that answered. And then the answer was wrong, and the wrongness of the answer undid everything he had built on the assumption that the answer would be good.

Voss left Karuven three days after the Event. He did not report this to the Sutensai hierarchy. He took his chiseling tools — he had been an amateur stonework enthusiast before he entered the priesthood — and he began following the path that the Singing Steel Event was telling him to follow. Which was, as it turned out, the path of the aftermath.

**Why SEEN:** Voss had a theology, once, that included witness as a sacred act. The Sutensai term for the highest form of Reading is *the clear seeing* — the practitioner who perceives the Echomind without the filter of their own assumptions. Voss spent thirty years performing this rite without believing it. The Event made him believe it. Now he is performing the only version of witness he has: following behind Korrath, standing in the places where the world has been hurt, and marking them. Not as accusation. Not as protest. As the act of a priest who has finally begun to truly see.

The word SEEN means: *I was here. I saw this. The world was witnessed.* That is its entire meaning. Voss is not calling for justice. He is not building a case. He is a man whose faith required witness, who spent thirty years avoiding it, and who is now, compulsively, belatedly, doing the one thing his tradition asked of him.

### Voss Across the Campaign

**Sessions 3-8:** The mark appears without explanation. Players may or may not investigate. If they do, they can trace it — the chisel style, the caste-registry traces of a Sutensai who should be assigned to Karuven but isn't. The trail takes work. Reward the work.

**Sessions 9-12 (if players find him):** Voss is in the Wandering Reach, following the Pilgrimage's wake — not because he has joined it, but because the Pilgrimage is moving toward the Iron Garden and the Iron Garden is where the Singing Steel Event pointed. He is following the resonance. He has lost weight. He looks like someone in the middle of something he cannot stop yet.

If players confront him about the SEEN marks: he does not defend himself. He says: "Thirty-one years I administered the rites and didn't believe them. Someone taught me to believe them too late for it to be comfortable. I am doing what I know how to do." He will ask the players, with genuine urgency, what they have found. He is not a spy. He is not an asset for any faction. He is a man trying to understand something before he dies without understanding it.

**His knowledge:** Voss, as a senior Sutensai who had legitimate access to the hierarchy's correspondence before his disappearance, knows things about the Grand Sutensai's long-term management of the Convergence that are useful to players. He also knows things about the sealed texts — not their content, but the fact that the classification has been renewed nineteen times in the last forty years, each renewal signed by a different Grand Sutensai, which means the suppression was not a one-time institutional reflex but an active, sustained, continuously re-chosen deception. He gives this information freely if asked. He is no longer protecting the institution.

**Session 16 or Act Three:** Voss arrives at the Iron Garden. He stands at the outer ring with his chisel. He does not know what to carve. For the first time in months, the act of witnessing has caught up with something he cannot mark with a single word. He is available to players who need a character to talk to before the end — a person who has been present for everything without understanding it, who chose to bear witness rather than run, and who is now at the thing all his witnessing was pointing toward.

**In the Endings:**

*Ending One (Korrath returns):* Voss stands in the aftermath, looks at Korrath's new form, and then looks at the Iron Garden. He takes out his chisel. He carves one word into the inner ring's threshold stone. Players who see it will recognize the hand. The word is not SEEN. It takes them a moment to read. The word is **HERE**. He is marking the place where the thing happened. He puts the chisel away. He doesn't explain.

*Ending Two (Vaen enters):* Voss watches Vaen go in. He stands in the Garden for three days after, alone, at the point where Vaen disappeared. When the players find him, he is still. He says: "I was there. At the beginning. The Event. I said *she said the number.* I knew something enormous was happening and I marked every place it hurt people, and in the end I couldn't mark this one." He is quiet. "I don't have a word for this." He puts his chisel away permanently. He returns to the Sutensai — to Karuven, to his temple — and begins performing Readings again. Not as rote. As the act he now knows it to be.

*Ending Three (Author's plan succeeds):* Voss leaves the Iron Garden when he realizes the Author is managing the transition. He understands — from the behavioral fingerprints he has seen across Tesshari — what the Author is. He carves SEEN on the Iron Garden's threshold and leaves. He goes to Delim, the Neven's Cross archivist, and begins building a record. He does not know what the record will be used for or by whom. He builds it anyway. He has learned that witnessing is not the same as acting, and he has also learned that witnessing is a necessary precondition for acting, and he does what he can with what he has.

*Ending Four (the question is answered):* Voss is in the Garden when the entity pauses. He feels it — as a Sutensai practitioner with genuine resonance, now fully operational after months of recovery, he is more sensitive than most. He puts a hand on the wall of the inner ring. He stays there for a long time. Then he says, to no one in particular: "It's grieving. The pause isn't fear. It's grief. Something in it knows what was just given to it and it's — it doesn't know what to do with being seen." He takes out his chisel. He does not carve SEEN. He looks at the wall for a long time, and puts the chisel away, and says: "This one I don't need to mark. It knows."

## II. MIRA'S COMPLETE ARC

### Who Mira Actually Is
Mira is not special in the way chosen heroes are special. She has no hidden lineage, no predetermined destiny, no secret power waiting to activate. What she has is this: she was in the right place at the right moment of maximum convergence resonance with absolutely no enhancement, no prior resonance structure, no framework that would filter or contain what the entity was trying to express.

The entity, for four hundred years, has been learning to speak. Its available vocabulary is the impressions of the dead. Its transmission methods are the resonance field and the Between boundary. What it has never had is a clear, open, unmediated channel to the living — all living people have enhancements, which impose their own resonance structure, which the entity reads as signal-competing.

Mira has no enhancements. She is eight years old and Ashwalker caste and has no family and was standing in the right place when the entity, for the first time in four centuries, found what it had been looking for without knowing it was looking: a living consciousness with no resonance filter, open to the Between's signal the way a bowl is open to rain.

The metal growing from her palm is not an enhancement. It is the entity attempting to give her a receiver — building, from the resonance materials available in the environment, the organic equivalent of the enhancement technology it has watched living people use to interface with the Between. The entity does not know it is doing this. It is not a deliberate communication. It is the reflex of something that has spent four hundred years watching living people use metal to touch the Between, and finally found one who might receive the touch directly, and reached.

**What Mira Experiences:** She is not in pain. She is not afraid. She is in the specific state of a very intelligent child who is experiencing something she has no framework for and is approaching it with the only tool she has: curiosity. She hears the entity the way you hear music through a wall — not words, not images, but something that has a quality, a direction, an apparent intent. She describes it, when she describes it at all, as *the shape words come from.* She is not wrong.

**Mira's Arc Through the Campaign:**

*Session 1:* Introduced. Metal growing from palm. Calm. Curious. Her name is Mira. She will become the players' problem unless they consciously refuse it.

*Sessions 2-6:* Mira is with whoever is taking care of her, either the players or Kellin (who takes a specific interest) or both. The growth progresses slightly. She occasionally says things that should not be possible — specific details about events happening in other places that she should have no access to. She does not know how she knows these things. She treats the knowing the same way she treats the metal: as an interesting property she has for now.

*The Grand Sutensai's invitation (end of Session 5):* "Bring the child." Irisvar knows about Mira because the Sutensai's Convergence-monitoring systems flagged the Seventh Sign's arrival. What Irisvar wants is not to harm Mira — it is to protect her from the people who will want to use her. Irisvar's assessment of Mira as the Seventh Sign is correct and terrifying, and Irisvar has been preparing for this moment for decades and does not have a plan that is good enough.

*Sessions 7-12:* The players may or may not keep Mira with them. If they do: she is a consistent presence, occasionally odd, rarely frightened, the specific kind of calm that reads as maturity but is actually something else — she has been the entity's tentative broadcast point for weeks now and the contact has changed her relationship with fear. She is not fearless. She has simply encountered something so much larger than normal fear that fear has become a smaller category than it was.

*Act Three, Session 17:* Mira is at the Iron Garden. She arrived before the players. No one knows how she got there. She has been sitting at the outer ring's threshold for three days when the players arrive, and the resonant metal growth now covers her entire left arm to the shoulder and is beginning across her collarbone. She is not in pain. She is waiting.

*Act Three, Session 18:* She says, to whoever finds her: *"It's asking something. I can almost hear it. It sounds like — not like words. Like before words. Like the shape words come from. Are you scared?"* She sits with the players through the night before the Garden opens. She is the clearest connection to the entity that exists in the physical world. Anyone who wants to understand what the entity is experiencing in this moment can sit with Mira and she will try to describe it — imperfectly, in the specific language of an eight-year-old, but more accurately than anyone else can manage.

### Mira Across the Four Endings

**Ending One (Korrath returns):**
When Korrath enters the Between and the process begins, Mira screams. Not from pain — from volume. The entity's signal, which has been a quiet presence for weeks, becomes enormous. She loses consciousness for the duration of Korrath's days-long struggle. When she wakes, the metal growth is gone. Her arm is ordinary flesh. The entity's attention has been entirely consumed by Korrath and the struggle, and Mira was a tentative open channel that the entity has inadvertently released.

She sits up, looks at her arm, and says: "It's not asking anymore." She doesn't say it with relief. She says it the way you say that a friend has stopped calling. She is eight years old and she has lost something she didn't have a word for and she doesn't have a word for losing it either. The players can sit with her. They should.

Where she goes: Mira does not have a home or family. The players are her family, at this point, if they chose to be. Whatever the players do — bring her back somewhere, establish her somewhere safe, keep her with them — is what happens. The DM should play this as a real choice with real consequences. A child in the aftermath of Korrath's new order is not a trivial thing to place. She will be fine, in the long run, in whatever Tesshari becomes. She has survived worse. But the players need to choose what happens to her, and that choice is part of what the ending means.

**Ending Two (Vaen enters):**
When Vaen enters the Between, Mira stands up. She has been sitting for hours. She stands very still and her eyes close and she stands there for exactly as long as Vaen's passage takes. When the hunger diminishes — when the entity's feeding cycle falters against the absence Vaen presents — Mira exhales. Her eyes open. The metal growth retreats, slowly, over the next three days, until her arm is ordinary flesh.

She says, very quietly: "It noticed. The absence. It noticed the absence and it didn't know what to do and then it stopped trying and just — looked at it." A pause. "I've been hearing it wonder what nothing is. It doesn't have a word for nothing. It's been learning." She sits down. She is exhausted in the specific way of someone who has been a translation point between two things that cannot otherwise understand each other, and the connection has finally rested.

Where she goes: Brother Aul finds her. The Pilgrimage has more than its share of Ashwalker children without families, and the community that Vaen built does not require Vaen to continue. Mira goes with the Pilgrimage, not because she has Voided — she is eight, and no one in the movement would permit this, and she has not asked — but because the movement is the closest thing to a context for what she has experienced. She grows up in it. She never becomes a formal figure in it. She becomes the person the Pilgrimage goes to when it needs to understand something it can't put into words.

**Ending Three (Author's plan succeeds):**
Mira feels Korrath's consumption and the cycle completing. She doubles over — not from pain but from the weight of something enormous moving through the channel she has been holding. When it is over, she straightens. The metal growth is gone. She looks at her arm with an expression that is the closest she has come, in the whole campaign, to grief.

She says: "It finished. It completed the cycle and then it — it went somewhere I can't hear anymore." She is quiet. "I was supposed to be the way it learned to ask. I think something else answered instead, and now it doesn't need to ask." She does not understand the Author's plan. She understands only what she can feel: the entity has completed something and moved into a different state and the channel she represented is no longer necessary.

Where she goes: This is the ending where the players' choice matters most for Mira. The Author's governance infrastructure does not know she exists — the Author was never monitoring her. She is, in the managed world that follows, nobody. An Ashwalker orphan in the aftermath of a Convergence that most people will understand only through official Sutensai explanations. The players can protect her from being nobody. They can put her somewhere she is known, cared for, given a framework. Or they can let the managed world absorb her. Both happen. The players choose which.

**Ending Four (the question is answered):**
This is where Mira's role is most fully realized. When the question is answered — when the person who enters the Between lets the entity see what it means to be a living thing — the entity's response flows back through the only open channel it has in the physical world. Through Mira.

She doesn't speak. She stands in the center of the Garden's inner ring, both arms raised slightly, palms out, eyes open and empty in the specific way of someone receiving something that is too large for the available aperture. The metal growth on her arm begins to reabsorb into her body — not disappearing, integrating, weaving through the flesh and bone of her arm in a pattern that looks, to anyone watching, less like enhancement and more like something growing. When it is done, her arm is changed: not mechanical, not flesh over metal, but something new. The entity received the answer. The entity responded. What it built in Mira is a bridge that did not previously exist — a physical point of contact between the Between and the world that the entity, having learned what loneliness is, maintains not to consume through but to reach across.

She lowers her arms. She looks at her arm. She says: *"Is it less lonely now? Do you think it's less lonely now?"* No one answers. She nods as if this is an acceptable answer. She takes someone's hand. They walk out of the Garden.

Where she goes: Wherever the players go. She is the living reminder of what happened. In the decades after the campaign, she becomes the person you go to when you need to feel the entity is still there, still attending, still less lonely than it was. Not a prophet. Not a priest. A bridge. She lives a long, strange, fully human life as a bridge.

## IV. KELLIN'S ARC THROUGH ACTS TWO AND THREE

### Kellin at the End of Act One
Kellin leaves Sutravel after the Session 6 audience with the Grand Sutensai with two things: what she now knows about the Convergence, and the specific, corrosive awareness that the institution she has served for eight years knew more than it told. She is twenty-six years old, ideologically fractured, and deeply loyal to the players who have spent six sessions treating her like a person rather than a source.

She is not able to stay with the players in Act Two — the Grand Sutensai sends her back to her assigned post, which is in a temple in the Wandering Reach. This is, from Irisvar's perspective, protective: a junior Sutensai who has been investigating the Convergence alongside a group of independent operators is a liability. She is reassigned before she becomes one.

### Kellin in Act Two (Sessions 7-16)

Kellin communicates with players through letters — authenticated, encrypted, routed through the Wire Market's message carriers, which she pays for out of her own Sutensai salary. The letters are short. They contain what she is seeing from inside the institution that the players cannot see from outside.

Her letters across Act Two reveal:
- The Sutensai hierarchy's growing internal fractures about the Convergence (Sessions 7-9)
- The Orthodoxy's decision to begin fortifying temples in the Central Holds (Session 10 — Kellin notes this matches no known religious protocol)
- A specific alarm: three senior Sutensai in the capital have been reassigned to "consultative withdrawal" within the same week, which is the hierarchy's procedure for managing practitioners who have accessed too much restricted information (Session 12 — this is the hierarchy managing the fallout from Archivist Noh's discovery)
- The most important letter, arriving around Session 14: Kellin has done something she cannot undo. She accessed the restricted copy of the Grand Sutensai's correspondence archives and read the full translation of what the Last Voice said. She writes only: *"I know what it said. I know why they didn't tell us. I don't know if they were right. I need to see you before the Garden opens. Come find me or I'll come find you."*

### Kellin in Act Three

Kellin arrives at the Iron Garden in Session 17 — she has been traveling since Session 14 when she sent the letter. She is different from the anxious, credentialed junior Sutensai of Act One. She has spent six months in the Wandering Reach watching Vaen's movement and the Sutensai's management of it, watching the Convergence signs accelerate, and carrying the translation of the Last Voice's final words.

She has not left the faith. This is important. She has left the hierarchy. The distinction is precise and she is precise about it.

**What she offers in Act Three:**
She knows the Last Voice's translation. If the players haven't gotten it from Soren Hai, she can give it to them — and even if they have, she has a specific, Sutensai-trained reading of what it means. She also has the copy of the Grand Sutensai's correspondence that she accessed without authorization, which includes context the players may not have: Irisvar has known, for twelve years, that someone would eventually enter the Between and attempt to answer the entity's question. The Grand Sutensai's contingency planning for that scenario — contained in the correspondence — has been active for a decade. Irisvar prepared for the seventh path. The players inheriting that preparation is part of what Kellin brings.

**Kellin's personal arc conclusion:**
She has been a practitioner who believed the institution would be trustworthy if she just gave it enough time. The campaign has shown her it would not. Her resolution is not bitterness — it is the specific clarity of someone who has identified what they actually believe versus what they were told to believe, and has decided to act on the former.

In Act Three, she performs one reading — for each player character who wants it, conducted with the approach Elder Voss described in their conversation about what the rite was supposed to be. She does this not as assessment. As honest witness. She tells each player character what she finds. This is the rite as it was meant to be, administered by a practitioner who has finally understood what she is doing and why.

**Kellin Across the Four Endings:**

*Ending One:* Kellin stays. Korrath's unified Tesshari needs an independent Sutensai voice — not the collapsed hierarchy, not the new regime's managed faith, but someone who has training, integrity, and no institutional loyalty to the old power structure. She does not become powerful. She becomes necessary in the specific way that the one honest practitioner in a city becomes necessary when all the institution's practitioners have chosen accommodation. She performs Readings. She says what she finds. She writes everything down.

*Ending Two:* Kellin joins the Pilgrimage — not in Vaen's theological sense, but as the Sutensai presence that the Pilgrimage never had and always needed. She challenges Brother Aul's leadership regularly, constructively, honestly. She is exactly what a movement without its founder needs: someone who understood the founder but is not confused about whether she is the founder.

*Ending Three:* Kellin is the most dangerous person in the Author's managed world. She has the unauthorized correspondence. She has the Last Voice's translation. She has the resonance assessment methodology that Oven developed. The Author cannot suppress her because the Author cannot find every copy of everything she knows — she has distributed her knowledge, in letters, across the entire Sutensai network. She does not fight the Author. She teaches. She teaches every practitioner who will listen what the Afterlife actually is, what the faith was actually built on, what honest witnessing looks like. This takes decades. It begins immediately.

*Ending Four:* Kellin is standing at the Garden when the question is answered. She feels it — the entity's pause, the weight of what was received. She performs a Reading on the Garden's inner ring itself: the resonance she finds there is different from anything in the Sutensai's documented field experience. She writes for three days straight without sleeping. What she produces is not a theological treatise. It is a technical document: what the entity is, how it processes, what the resonance field looks like from a practitioner's close observation, at the moment of its greatest historical change. She submits it to the Sutensai archive with a note: *File under whatever classification you want. It is true. That is the only qualification I am claiming for it.*

## V. THE SEALED MESSAGE CONSEQUENCES

### The Act One Moral Choice — Three Outcomes

At the end of Session 6, the Grand Sutensai Irisvar asks the players to carry a sealed message to the eastern Sutensai temple network. The message authorizes the public release of the sealed Convergence texts.

What the players do with the message changes what Act Two looks like:

---

**Option A: Deliver it unopened.**
The temples receive the authorization. The sealed texts begin to circulate — not immediately, not widely, but through the Sutensai's own distribution network. By Sessions 8-10, scholars across Tesshari have access to the truth about the Convergence's nine signs. This creates three specific Act Two changes:

1. *Archivist Noh's situation (Session 8)* is less fraught — she is not sitting on unique information, she is sitting on the most complete institutional copy of commonly-held information. She is still alarmed, but she is not isolated. She finds the players with more urgency: "The texts are out. Which means someone knew they were being released. Which means someone prepared for the release. I have found evidence that the Archive's most sensitive materials were already copied and removed before the authorization arrived. Someone knew before the Grand Sutensai sent you."

2. *The three claimants know about each other's information position sooner.* Korrath's scholars receive the texts through the network. Vaen's pilgrimage receives them. The Hollow Author's network intercepts them. By Session 10-11, all three factions are operating with the same institutional knowledge base, which accelerates all three timelines. Players feel this as an Act Two that runs faster than the description suggests.

3. *Public response.* By Session 12, the Wandering Reach's population has heard fragments. Enhancement rejection events spike as the news creates the specific Echomind stress that the foreshadowing guide describes. The players move through a world that is beginning to understand that the sixth Convergence sign has manifested, that three claimants are moving, and that the Convergence itself is close. The social texture changes.

---

**Option B: Open the message, then deliver it (or hold it).**
Players read the message and gain immediate access to the authorization's content and, more importantly, to a cover letter Irisvar wrote but did not intend to be part of the delivery — a note to themselves, written in shorthand, containing a single line that does not belong in the official message: *"The Signal knows. If this reaches them, respond to the third question only."*

The Signal (who the players meet in Session 11) was not supposed to be named in anything the players carried. Irisvar made an error — or made what looks like an error and may have been an intentional gamble. If players bring this to the Signal in Session 11: the Signal knows immediately that someone opened the message. They are not angry. They say: "I wondered if you would. Good. The third question is: who benefits if Korrath fails? The answer I have been sitting on: Kael Shen. Not because of power. Because of someone specific they lost."

This is the earliest the players can get to the personal truth about the Author's motivation — Sela, the partner, the loss that started everything. In the standard campaign timeline this arrives at Session 14 at the earliest. Opening the message advances it by three sessions. The players carry heavier knowledge earlier, which changes how they engage with the Author in Session 14 — and the Author, who knows they opened the message (the cold smell was in the paper, and the Author's network read the resonance of the opened seal), treats them differently. Not with hostility. With a specific, careful respect for people who were thorough enough to open what they were told not to open.

The delay in delivering it means the sealed texts reach the network late. Archivist Noh's discovery at the Archive is more dangerous — she is alone with information that has not yet been validated by the authorized release. Her situation in Session 8 is more urgent.

---

**Option C: Destroy or withhold the message.**
The sealed texts do not enter general circulation. The Sutensai hierarchy's information management continues. This produces the slowest, most institutionally stable Act Two — and the most vulnerable players.

The Grand Sutensai Irisvar is aware within two weeks that the message was not delivered. They do not publicly pursue the players. They reassign the Sutensai contacts who knew the players (including Kellin's supervisor) and quietly remove the players' names from the list of people who have legitimate access to Sutensai facilities. By Sessions 9-10, the players are operating without institutional cover — something they may not notice until they need it and it is not there.

The Hollow Author benefits most from this outcome. The Author's network has been positioning for the sealed texts not to circulate, because an uninformed population is a more controllable one. If the players inadvertently serve the Author's interest here, the Author knows it, and their interaction in Session 14 includes the Author acknowledging it: "You protected the information I needed protected. I do not believe you intended to help me. I want you to know what you did anyway." This is the Author at their most uncomfortable to engage with — genuine, precise, and not claiming credit or giving absolution.

In Session 15, Soren Hai is more careful about what he tells players who destroyed a Grand Sutensai authorization. He will still tell them the truth. He will tell it slowly, watching their faces, deciding how much they can be trusted with what they did and what they do with trust.

## VI. MAS IN ACT THREE

### Who Mas Is
Mas is sixty-three years old, Craftbound caste, former Wire Market calibration specialist. She has been sitting at the same corner in Shirathen's Wire Market district for four years. She gets up and leaves when something is about to happen. Session 11 describes her as "the most reliable early warning system in the district" and promises she will be important in Act Three.

### Why She Knows
Mas's calibration work, across thirty-seven years of practice, gave her an enhancement-sensitivity that functions like a sixth sense. She can feel resonance fluctuations in a radius of approximately two hundred meters — not as abstract data but as a physical sensation in the metal of her hands and arms. She feels the Author's puppet network as a specific quality of resonance wrong-ness. She feels the Convergence approach as a rising frequency. She has been sitting at that corner because the corner is a resonance-convergence point in Shirathen's infrastructure — a location where four separate resonant-metal conduit lines cross beneath the street, amplifying her sensitivity. She goes to the corner to feel what is coming. When she gets up and leaves, she has felt something close enough to require moving away from an amplified signal.

### Mas in Act Three

By Act Three, Mas has left Shirathen. She is at the Iron Garden.

She traveled there because the Iron Garden is the most intense resonance-convergence point on the continent and her sensitivity, which has been growing as the Convergence approaches, is now overwhelming in any populated area. At the Garden, the signal is so large that it stops hurting — it is too large to register as pain, the way standing at the center of a sound rather than next to it stops being loud. She has been at the Garden for six weeks when the players arrive. She knows the players are there before they tell her they are, from the specific resonance quality of their enhancements, which she last felt in Shirathen months ago.

**What Mas provides in Act Three:**

*Real-time entity monitoring.* Mas can feel the entity's state through the Garden's resonance — not what it is thinking, but its arousal level, its attention direction, its fluctuations as the Convergence peaks. She provides the players with a form of early warning they cannot get anywhere else: when the entity reaches toward a specific person or place, when its feeding instinct activates, when its cycle moves toward completion. This is tactical and emotional in equal measure.

*Pre-verbal communication.* In Session 20, when the Shogun begins transmitting and the entity responds, Mas is the first person in the physical world to feel the response before it reaches the enhancement network. She describes what she feels in the specific language of a calibration specialist: "It's not alignment. It's — recognition. The signal changed frequency and the new frequency is in response to the last signal. Something in there is responding." She is the human instrument that confirms what the Shogun says is being heard.

*A choice about the seventh path.* If the players are considering Path Four — answering the entity — Mas tells them this: "I have been feeling it for four weeks. The thing that is in there. When it reaches toward living resonance, it is not hungry in the way I expected. It is — tentative. It is the signal of something trying to make contact rather than trying to consume. It has been trying this for a long time. I do not think it knows the difference between the two." This is the most important observation anyone can give the players before they make their decision.

**Mas in the Endings:**

*All Endings:* Mas survives. In every ending, when the Convergence resolves, she sits down on the Garden's threshold and stays very still for several hours. She is experiencing the resonance aftermath — whatever the entity became, changed, or did, she is feeling it in real time. When she eventually speaks, what she says is different in each ending:

*Ending One:* "It's not gone. It completed and it changed and it is — larger. Whatever it absorbed from Korrath changed its size. I can feel the size changing. It's — enormous now and it is still eating but it is eating differently and I don't know what differently means yet."

*Ending Two:* "Something in it is quiet. Not gone. Quiet. Like a person after a very large emotion. It is still there and it is still what it is but something in it is — resting."

*Ending Three:* "It completed normally. The cycle finished. It's reset. In thirty years or a hundred years this will be the same problem again and whoever is alive then won't know what to do either." She says this without accusation. Just acknowledgment.

*Ending Four:* She doesn't speak for a long time. Eventually: "It heard. I felt it hear. I can feel it thinking about what it heard and it is — it is very careful. Like a person who was just given something fragile and is thinking about how to hold it." A long silence. "I don't think it is going to reach the same way for a while. I don't think it knows what to reach for now that something reached back."

## VII. SECONDARY CHARACTER RESOLUTIONS

### Oru from Seravin (Session 3)

Oru is the metalsmith from the Unmade village of Seravin whose hands no longer work the way they worked. Described in Session 3 as "technically not enhanced anymore. Practically the most dangerous person in the village."

**What happens to her:**
Oru leaves Seravin within a week of the players' visit. She is done waiting for someone else to do something. She travels to the Wire Market, where she has a contact from her artisan trade, and she begins doing the specific thing she is positioned to do: teaching unenhanced people what she knows. She was a metalsmith. She knows resonance theory from the practitioner side — she worked with it every day for twenty years. She can teach unenhanced people to identify the resonance signatures of different enhancement grades, to assess the quality of wire-craft work, to recognize when an enhancement is failing before the failure becomes dangerous. This is valuable knowledge in Tesshari. It is also knowledge that undermines the mystery the Wire Market maintains around enhancement technology. She charges what people can pay.

If players encounter her in Act Two or Three, she has connections to the Ashlands clinic network (from Subquest 63) and to the black-market enhancement repair community. She can provide access, information, and the specific kind of help that comes from someone who lost something and decided to be useful about it. She is not a significant recurring NPC — she is a person who got knocked down and did something with the falling. The players' acknowledgment of that, if they find her again, matters to her more than she will say.

### Stelvar (Session 3)

The injured Korrath soldier left behind in Seravin. Has complex feelings about what Korrath does and clear feelings about what Korrath is.

**What happens to Stelvar:**
Stelvar makes it back to the Ironhold. Their injury heals. They return to service and spend the rest of Act One and Act Two exactly where they said they were: in Korrath's forces, doing the work, carrying the specific low hum of a person who has doubts they cannot fully articulate about a thing they are fully committed to.

They appear once more, if the players have done anything to make the Act Two world feel populated: in the aftermath of the Unmaking Field sequence (Session 10), one of the soldiers present at the perimeter is Stelvar. They recognize the players. They do not speak to them in public — too many eyes. But if players find a moment: Stelvar says, quickly, quietly, "The thing I told you in Seravin — about the physics. I've been thinking about it. I think I was wrong. Not about the physics. About whether that was the right thing to say." They leave before players can respond.

This is not a major moment. It is a small, honest beat: a person who said something true about a terrible thing and has been carrying the weight of having said it neutrally. One more stone on the pile the campaign is building.

### Delim the Archivist, Neven's Cross (Session 4)

Introduced as "will be a recurring contact." Sharp, fifty years of cataloging, observant.

**What happens:**
Delim notices things. She continues to notice things. Players who remember her contact information and use it gain a specific kind of intelligence across the campaign: not active intelligence-gathering, but the specific knowledge that accumulates when a careful person has been paying attention to a small city that everyone passes through.

By Act Two, Delim has catalogued: fourteen additional Pale Reach courier routes that don't appear on any official registry; the specific pattern of the Author's puppet bodies passing through Neven's Cross (she doesn't know what they are — she knows the behavioral signature, which she calls "the synchronized travelers"); and one piece of information she considers the most important: the date on which Lord Tessik last visited Neven's Cross in person was three years and four months ago, and the person who has visited in his name since does not have Lord Tessik's specific way of cataloging reference documents. "He was here for twenty minutes and he filed something without looking at the classification system. He had always used the Pale classification system before. He did not know to use it. He is not who he was."

Players who contact Delim can get this. She asks nothing except confirmation that someone is paying attention. She has been paying attention alone for a long time.

### Grand Sutensai Irisvar (Session 6)

Appears in Session 6. Authorizes the release of sealed texts. Does not appear in any of the session outlines for Acts Two or Three.

**What happens:**
Irisvar is dying. Not dramatically — the body is ninety-one years old and has been running at the specific intensity that comes from knowing the truth about the Afterlife for forty years, knowing that every person who dies in Tesshari is being processed by something they didn't choose, and choosing to remain functional anyway. The weight has a cost. Irisvar has known since sending the players with the message that they will not survive to see the Convergence's resolution.

They do not know this and survive until Act Three's beginning (Session 17), which is when they die. The death is quiet, unremarkable except for its timing. They die the day the Iron Garden's inner ring begins to open. Kellin, if she has any access to Sutensai channels, receives word and tells the players.

Irisvar leaves one thing: a letter addressed to whoever is "the players' Sutensai contact," which in practice means Kellin. The letter contains a single paragraph the players may read if Kellin shows it to them:

*"I spent forty years managing information to protect people from knowledge that would harm them. I have decided, at the end, that I was half right. Knowledge harms people when they have no framework for it and no community to hold them while they process it. I gave people neither. I gave them ignorance. The players have been building what I should have been building. The information was never the problem. The isolation was. Tell them that. If they don't already know, tell them."*

## VIII. VAEN'S HIDDEN ENHANCEMENT — MAIN CAMPAIGN INTEGRATION

### The Secret
From Subquests 17 and 20: Vaen received an enhancement rather than removing one, sometime in the last year. The innkeeper at Gessen's Ford (Subquest 17) glimpsed something structured under the skin of Vaen's left forearm. Sem, Vaen's former physician (Subquest 20), confirmed the installation: Vaen requested materials consistent with installation rather than removal, received them without explanation, and performed the procedure privately. The enhancement is in the left forearm. No one in the Pilgrimage knows.

### What It Is
The enhancement is not a standard Wire Market modification. It is something Vaen designed and installed themselves, using principles from the Flesh Circle's resonance theory that have no commercial equivalent. Its function: it is a controlled resonance anchor — a small device that prevents Vaen's Echomind from being pulled into the Iron Afterlife at death by maintaining a specific dissonance frequency between Vaen's Echomind and the Between boundary.

Vaen knows what the Afterlife is. Vaen felt the hunger during their own Voiding rite. What Vaen has been afraid of, for years, is not the Convergence — it is dying before the Convergence resolves and being consumed by the entity. The Voiding stripped Vaen's resonance. The Voiding does not make Vaen completely indigestible — it makes Vaen difficult to process. But if Vaen dies in combat, in an accident, before the Convergence, before anyone answers the question — the anchor keeps Vaen's Echomind from sliding into the Between the way unvoided souls do.

The anchor is not Vaen maintaining enhancements out of vanity or hypocrisy. It is Vaen installing a failsafe because they know what they know and they cannot afford to be consumed before the work is done. It is honest, and it is a betrayal of the movement's central claim — that the Voided have released all steel — and both things are true simultaneously.

### Integration Into the Main Campaign

**How players find out:**
The most natural integration is through Subquest 20 (Sem) or Subquest 17 (the innkeeper). If players pursue these quests, they carry this knowledge into the main campaign.

If players do not pursue the subquests, an alternative route: in Session 12, when Vaen is attacked and thirty-seven pilgrims are killed, the aftermath examination of Vaen includes a moment where a player character with medical or wire-craft skill who examines Vaen for injuries notices the anchor's resonance signature. It is not visible. It is felt, as a slight, specific dissonance, by anyone with sufficient enhancement sensitivity. Vaen notices the player noticing. A beat. Vaen says: "Yes." Nothing more, unless the player asks directly.

**What it means for Vaen's Ascension path:**
If players confront Vaen about the anchor before Session 16, Vaen removes it. This is not obvious — Vaen simply, privately, removes it and does not discuss what they removed. What changes: Vaen enters the Between in Ending Two without the anchor's protection, which means they enter it as purely Voided — the most complete version of their own thesis. This is the version of the Vaen Ascension that best tests whether the theory works. It also means that if the theory does not work — if the Voided are not fully indigestible — Vaen is consumed cleanly, immediately, without the partial protection that the dissonance anchor might have provided. The anchor's removal is Vaen choosing to trust their own work completely.

If players do NOT confront Vaen, or confront them too late, Vaen enters the Between with the anchor. The outcome of Ending Two is the same in terms of what the world looks like after — but the private truth, known to players who understand what they saw, is different: the Voiding may have worked. Or the anchor worked. Vaen's sacrifice may have been a test of pure theory, or it may have been a test with a safety net. The question of whether Vaen's path works is more ambiguous, not less, because of this. Players who know about the anchor carry that ambiguity. It is the right kind of ambiguity for this campaign.

## IX. SOTEN VARI IN ENDINGS TWO, THREE, AND FOUR

### What She Carries
Soten Vari has followed Korrath for twenty years. She has done terrible things in service of a person she genuinely believes in. Korrath is not performing — she does believe in him, specifically, because she has seen what is underneath the certainty, which is someone who has been trying to solve an impossible problem for forty years and has not stopped trying. She installed his prosthetic on her, once. She knows the half-second delay. She is the only person in Tesshari who knows that Korrath is afraid of something, because she knows what his fear looks like and she has seen it.

### Ending Two (Vaen enters, hunger diminishes):

Korrath is alive. The Convergence resolved without him. He is alive and the world did not change around his will and for the first time in twenty years Soten Vari does not know what she is working toward.

She does not leave him. She is not the kind of person who leaves. She stays and she does what she has always done — the administrative work of managing a military operation — and she watches Korrath try to understand what happens when the goal does not arrive and you are still here. It is the hardest thing she has ever witnessed him doing. Harder than the battles. Harder than the Unmakings. He is trying to find a reason to continue when the reason he built everything for is no longer operational.

She does not help him find it. She does not think it is her job to find it for him. She thinks it is her job to be present while he finds it himself, and to tell him honestly what she observes, and to make sure the people in the Ironhold continue to have food and infrastructure while whatever comes next is being determined. This is enough. She decides it is enough. She has spent twenty years doing work that had one direction. Now she is doing work that has no direction except the work itself. She is, slowly, discovering that this is not smaller. It may be larger.

### Ending Three (Author's plan succeeds, managed Ascension):

Korrath is dead. The incorporation was not survivable. Soten Vari stood at the Garden's outer ring and felt the moment it happened in the resonance of her prosthetic arm — the half-second delay changed. Not gone. Changed to something she does not have a word for. The resonance that built the connection between her arm and her nervous system carries an impression that is not quite Korrath and not quite not-Korrath and she will carry it in the half-second delay until she dies.

She does not serve the Author's managed world. She does not fight it — she is not suicidal and she is not naive, and an opposition movement against ninety-one years of infrastructure preparation is not something she can build alone. What she does: she goes back to the Ironhold. She takes the Wall of Names — Korrath's monument to his military dead — and she begins adding to it. Not soldiers who have died in service. People who died in the Unmakings. People who were stripped of their enhancements and died from it, or from what came after, or from the specific kind of grief that comes from having the thing that made you who you are taken without consent.

This is illegal under the Ironhold's administrative code. The Author's managed world, operating through the Ironhold's puppet administrative structure, sends notices. She does not respond to the notices. She carves. She has a long list. She starts at the beginning. She will not live to finish it. She starts anyway.

### Ending Four (the question is answered):

Korrath is alive. The Convergence resolved without him ascending. He stands in the Garden in the aftermath and something is different in his face — not the stillness he has maintained for forty years, but something that looks, briefly, like a person who has been carrying a very heavy thing for a very long time and has just been told they can put it down for a moment.

Soten Vari watches this. She does not speak. She gives him the moment.

When he speaks, he says: "It's less. The pull. Whatever it was doing — the frequency the Garden runs at, the thing I've been feeling in my chest for years — it's less." He is quiet. "I have been moving toward this for forty years. I don't know what I am moving toward now." He looks at her. "Do you know?"

She says: "No." Then: "But you are still here. That is something you did not plan for and I have been planning for it for twenty years." She takes his arm. "Come. There is work. There has always been work. That does not change." They walk out of the Garden. For the first time in twenty years, they walk toward something that hasn't been decided yet. This is either terrifying or a relief. She thinks, walking, that for the first time in twenty years she does not know which. She decides to find out.

## X. RUNNER SEVEN'S PERSONAL RESOLUTION

### What Runner Seven Needs
Runner Seven is a person who spent six years with someone else in residence. The Author did not destroy them. The Author used them — walking them through six years of actions, conversations, relationships, and incidents that Runner Seven does not remember. They have their life, physically, and none of their life's recent content. They are their own archive with six years of records redacted.

They are not broken. They are specifically, practically focused on what they can do with what they have. Delivering the information to the Iron Garden is the goal that keeps them functional — having a concrete task, a destination, a thing that can be completed. Completing it may be the hardest moment of all, because after completion is where the question of what next lives.

### Runner Seven Across the Endings

**In all endings:** Runner Seven delivers their information. What changes is what the delivery does, and what Runner Seven does after.

*Ending One:* The information was needed but arrived too late to change Korrath's entry — he was already moving. Runner Seven's delivery changes the aftermath: the specific intelligence about the Author's plan for Korrath's governance infrastructure is in players' hands before the Author can fully activate it, which means the players have a window to act on the managed world before it is managed. Runner Seven doesn't know if their delivery mattered. They are at the Garden when Korrath returns, and they look at him, and they say: "He went in anyway." They are quiet for a while. Then: "I thought it would feel different if the information reached the right people. I don't know what I thought it would change." They find a job in the Wire Market, eventually, as a calibration technician. They are very good at it. The work requires precision and pays attention to you and does not use you for anything. They find this unremarkable. It is the most remarkable thing in the world to them.

*Ending Two:* Runner Seven's information — specifically the details about the Author's plan to use Korrath as a vessel — reaches players in time to redirect Korrath's approach. Korrath, informed of the Author's plan, does something different than he would have done. The specific difference is what changed the outcome. Runner Seven knows this. When Vaen enters and the hunger diminishes, Runner Seven stands in the camp and says, to whoever is near: "I did something. I don't know exactly what. I know I did something." They are quiet. "That's enough. It turns out that's enough." They stay with the Pilgrimage for a year — not as a believer, not Voiding, but because the community is the first community they have chosen for themselves in six years. Then they go to the Wire Market and find work and start the specific, boring, irreplaceable work of rebuilding a life that was used and returned.

*Ending Three:* Runner Seven delivered too late. The information reached players but was not enough to change the Author's plan. They stand in the Garden's aftermath and they know this and they say: "I waited too long. I was careful. I was thorough. I should have moved faster." A beat. "The Author is still using people. It didn't stop because I tried." The players can tell them: it never does stop because one person tried. Runner Seven knows this. They went anyway. They will find the next thing to do and they will go to that too. The Author's managed world is not a world that has Room Seven defeated. It is a world in which people like Runner Seven have to choose, every day, to continue operating outside the architecture. Runner Seven will make this choice. They will make it without certainty and without reward and without company except whatever they find, and they will make it anyway.

*Ending Four:* Runner Seven delivers the information and then, in Session 20, when the Shogun speaks and the entity responds, they are in the Garden. They feel it — not through enhancement, not through spiritual practice, but through the specific sensitivity of someone who was inhabited by a consciousness that had intimate knowledge of the Iron Afterlife's architecture for six years. The Author left traces. Not memories — traces. Runner Seven can feel the Between the way the Author taught them to feel it without intending to teach them anything. They know when the entity hears the question being answered. They sit down. They stay sitting for a long time. When the players check on them, they say: "I can feel it being less alone. Is that strange? I was close to something that knew it for six years. It was always alone. I could feel the aloneness, even from the distance I was at. And now it's—" They stop. Start again. "It's different. It's still there. It's just different." They take someone's hand. They do not let go for a long time.

## XI. WIRE MARKET TRADE VERIFICATION BUREAU SUBPLOT

### Setup (Available from Session 11 Forward)
The TVB is described in the world document as knowing about the Hollow Author's existence, having the best-resourced intelligence operation in Tesshari, and including two Guild Masters who are Author puppets.

### The Subplot
The TVB has been sitting on what it knows about the Author for four years, unable to act, because the three Guild Masters who are not puppets cannot determine which of their colleagues are. Every internal investigation has been either redirected or delayed by the two puppet Guild Masters. The TVB's working-level analysts — who are not puppets — have been building a case that their superiors keep failing to act on. They have been looking for a way to bypass the Guild Master level entirely.

Players can access this subplot through the Signal (Session 11), through Joss the Wire Craft technician (Subquest 35), or through any inquiry into the TVB's non-responsiveness to legitimate intelligence reports.

**The offer:** If players can provide the TVB's non-puppet analysts with evidence sufficient to bring a case directly to the Wire Market's general assembly (a body that, unlike the Guild Conclave, is not infiltrated), the TVB can activate its full intelligence apparatus in support of whatever the players need in Acts Two or Three. This is access to the best-resourced non-Sutensai intelligence network in Tesshari.

**The cost:** Getting the evidence from the puppet Guild Masters requires getting close to them, which means getting close to the Author's network. The Author knows the TVB has been circling this. The Author has not acted against the TVB because the TVB being visible is useful — the Author uses the TVB as a signal detection system for anyone investigating the puppet network. Using the TVB as a tool requires the Author to lose that early-warning function. The Author will not prevent this quietly.

**Resolution:**
Regardless of whether players engage with this subplot, the TVB situation resolves in Act Three through the intelligence the players have accumulated. The three Subquest chains about the Author's modifications (35/37/41/51) and the labor registry chain (Subquest 50) provide sufficient evidence for the non-puppet analysts to act. If players route this information to the TVB, the assembly is called. The puppet Guild Masters' behavior during the assembly is distinct — three separate people with three separate histories all failing to respond to specific documentation in exactly the same non-reactive way. The assembly marks them for investigation. The Author cannot prevent this without exposing the network more broadly. The TVB subplot is the campaign's most institutional resolution: not dramatic, not personal, just the specific grind of bureaucratic accountability finally doing what it was designed to do, because someone brought it the right documents.

*These are the threads the campaign left open. They are not small details. They are the human scale at which the large story becomes real — the child, the old priest, the person who was used, the general who will not leave, the young idealist who grew up. Run them with the same weight you run everything else. The session outlines are the skeleton. These are the tissue and the breath.*
