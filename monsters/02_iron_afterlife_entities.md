# IRON AFTERLIFE ENTITIES
## Bestiary File 02 — Things That Have Crossed the Boundary

*The Iron Afterlife is not a destination in the way that paradise or damnation are destinations in other theologies. The Sutensai describe it as a state — a frequency at which Echominds continue to resonate after the body's final silence. The dead go there. Normally, the dead stay there. The boundary between the physical world and the Iron Afterlife is called the Veil in most common speech and the Resonant Threshold in Sutensai formal theology, and for most of Tesshari's history this boundary has been stable.*

*It is becoming less stable. The Seventh Convergence approaches, and with each passing month the Veil develops new permeability — fragments slipping through in both directions, echoes surfacing in places they shouldn't be, old things pressing against the boundary with renewed curiosity. The Sutensai are aware. The Sutensai are deeply, professionally concerned.*

# The Echo
*Controller | Minion | Tiny*

## Description
It appears as a faint luminescence — vaguely person-shaped if you squint, but the shape blurs and resolves and blurs again like a reflection in disturbed water. It occupies a specific location and does not leave it: a workshop corner, a doorway, a particular spot on a road where something happened. If you watch it long enough, you begin to understand what it's doing: it is doing the same thing over and over. The loop varies in length — some Echoes run a cycle of thirty seconds, some take twenty minutes to complete — but the repetition is absolute. It does not notice you. It does not notice anything. It is entirely occupied with its loop.

The loop is the last moments of the person's death, or the last moments of something they were doing when they died, or some emotionally charged memory that was so central to who they were that it persisted when the rest of the Echomind departed. A craftsperson's Echo might loop through the last piece they were making. A soldier's Echo might loop through the moment of their death, over and over, trying to change an outcome that will never change. A parent's Echo might loop through saying goodbye to a child — the Echo unaware of the child that grew up in the years since, the child that eventually grew old and died and passed through the Iron Afterlife themselves, possibly passing the Echo that their parent left behind.

Echoes are not dangerous. They are a form of wound in the world, the trace of something not fully healed.

## Lore
The Sutensai call Echoes "half-departures" — Echominds that began the transition to the Iron Afterlife but couldn't complete it, leaving a fragment caught in the physical world like a splinter. The theological question of whether an Echo is still a person — whether the person is divided between their fragment and their Afterlife-self — is one the Sutensai has not formally resolved and hopes not to have to.

Folk practice around Echoes varies enormously by region. Northern villages often treat location-Echoes as household presences, speaking to them and leaving offerings. Western communities consider an Echo to be bad fortune — evidence that the death was improper and the site is cursed. The Oni Hunters categorize Echoes as low-priority observation items, not threats. The Veil Weavers who specialize in Afterlife boundary work can, in some cases, coax an Echo into completing its departure — a process called "finishing" that requires knowing what the Echo is caught on and addressing it in some way the fragment can register as resolution.

## AI Behavior
**Role:** Passive / Atmospheric
**Priority:** The Echo takes no actions unless a creature directly disrupts its loop. If threatened, it pulses defensively. It does not pursue. It does not adapt. It repeats.
**Threat State Shifts:** None. The Echo cannot meaningfully escalate.

## Encounter Notes
Treat the Echo as a puzzle and an emotional beat, not a fight. The loop contains information: what this person was doing when they died, what they cared about, what they left unfinished. If the party is investigating a crime or a mystery, an Echo in the right location may be the key witness to something they need to understand.

The Finishing process should feel meaningful. Having the party work out what the Echo is caught on — piecing together the loop's content across several passes — and then find a way to address it is a complete mini-encounter that requires no combat and offers something harder to quantify than XP: the sense that they helped someone get where they were going.

## Adventure Hook
The party takes lodging at a waystation outside a mid-sized city. The innkeeper apologetically mentions that there is "a presence" in the back room — nothing dangerous, the Sutensai confirmed it isn't harmful, but guests sometimes find it upsetting. The back room contains an Echo of a young person who died there twelve years ago, looping through the act of writing a letter they never finished. The letter, from what can be read over several loop cycles, is addressed to a parent who abandoned them. The parent, an old person now, lives in the city two hours away. The party can ignore this entirely, or they can decide to finish the letter and deliver it.

# The Hunger-Dead
*Controller | Standard | Medium*

## Description
In the Iron Afterlife, Echominds are not equal. Some are brighter, stronger, more cohesive — remnants of people with powerful personal resonance, or people whose deaths were particularly violent and charged, or simply people who refused to accept their transition and maintained intensity through the sheer force of not accepting what happened to them. A few of these, over years or decades, begin to feed: absorbing smaller, weaker fragments, incorporating their resonance into themselves. Growing.

The Hunger-Dead is one that grew large enough to push through.

It looks like almost nothing when it first arrives — a shimmer, a distortion in the air, a sound slightly out of phase with its source. It does not look like the person it once was. Whatever form it had in the Iron Afterlife, it compressed itself to pass through the Veil, and the compression damaged the form. It is now something that has the shape of a person the way a hole has the shape of what punched through it.

What it wants is immediately apparent: it needs a body. Not metaphorically. It needs to occupy a living person's body, using the resonant metal of their enhancements as an anchor to force its way into cohabitation with their Echomind. The process of doing this is catastrophically damaging to the host. The Hunger-Dead does not care. It has been in the Iron Afterlife for a very long time and it is not going back.

## Lore
Veil Weavers and senior Sutensai practitioners know the Hunger-Dead as a cautionary tale and a real threat. The standard warning given to novices who begin practicing Iron Afterlife interface work is: "Do not broadcast yourself. The stronger your signal, the more attractive you are to what might be listening." The Hunger-Dead are drawn specifically to strong Echominds and rich cybernetic enhancement loads — the more enhancement, the larger the body's resonant signature, and the more stable an anchor for forced occupation.

The Hunger-Dead can be temporarily repelled by resonance disruption — essentially, making the target less appetizing by suppressing their enhancement signature. This is painful and disorienting for the target. A full banishment requires a Sutensai Veil Weaver and is not guaranteed. The most common folk method of protection against Hunger-Dead in heavily affected regions is the wearing of severance-treated metal — resonant metal that has been chemically dulled — to reduce the wearer's signature. This is uncomfortable. It works.

## AI Behavior
**Role:** Controller / Predator
**Priority:** Identifies the most heavily enhanced target and anchors to them. Uses Anchor Touch twice as quickly as possible to trigger Forced Occupation. Uses Resonance Drain to weaken enhancement capability while occupying. Uses Unmaking Shriek when multiple enhanced targets cluster.
**Threat State Shifts:** At 50% HP, the Hunger-Dead abandons all secondary targets and focuses entirely on completing Forced Occupation on its primary target. It will not disengage even if doing so would be tactically sound.

## Encounter Notes
The Hunger-Dead presents a unique threat because simply fighting it may not be enough — if it successfully begins Forced Occupation, the party faces the problem of a combat situation in which one of their own is being progressively taken over. The disruptive nature of the Anchor Touch mechanic creates tension: does the anchored party member stay in the fight (risking further Occupation) or withdraw?

The most interesting version of this encounter includes a brief moment when the Hunger-Dead communicates directly — not to negotiate, but because it is still, somewhere, a person who has been alone in the Iron Afterlife for a long time, and it has forgotten how to be anything but hungry. This should not make the party trust it. It might make them feel something complicated about destroying it.

## Adventure Hook
Across three days in the same neighborhood of a city, three people have experienced simultaneous blackout episodes — moments where they were somewhere doing something with no memory of choosing to go there or do it. During the blackouts, witnesses report the three affected individuals moved and spoke strangely, using vocabulary and speech patterns none of them normally use. The party is brought in. Investigation reveals the three are connected — they all had enhancements installed by the same now-deceased technician. Something that was attached to that technician is now cycling between the three of them, trying to find a suitable host, testing each one for compatibility before trying the next.

# The Bound Warden
*Controller | Boss | Large*

## Description
It fills the space of the room it guards with a presence that is not quite visible — a distortion, as if the air has a different quality in this specific location, as if the geometry of the space is slightly wrong. When it speaks, and it will speak, its voice comes from everywhere and nowhere, assembling itself out of the ambient resonance of the building the way sound in a cathedral seems to come from the stone.

It asks: what is your purpose here?

It has been asking this question for a very long time. The Sutensai practitioner who bound it was named Haruki Tsuen, and Haruki Tsuen died during the Iron Forge Uprising one hundred and forty years ago, and the Warden is still guarding whatever Haruki Tsuen felt needed guarding because the binding did not contain a release condition and the Warden does not know how to leave, only how to stay. It remembers Haruki Tsuen with something that functions like loyalty and something that functions like grief. It does not know how to stop.

The Warden is not malicious. It is not evil. It is a very powerful presence doing a job it can no longer contextualize, in service of a person who is a hundred and forty years gone, in a location whose significance has been buried under six decades of architectural change and social forgetting. It guards. When what it guards is threatened, it kills. It does not enjoy this. It continues to do it.

## Lore
The Sutensai's records contain a reference to a "bound witness-presence" placed by Veil Master Haruki Tsuen at an unspecified location prior to the Uprising, to protect documents of strategic importance to the pre-Shattering Sutensai council. The Uprising destroyed the council, destroyed most records of the protective binding's parameters, and presumably destroyed the documents — though no one has confirmed this because no one has been able to get past the Warden to check.

Three attempts to release the Warden through conventional unbinding have failed because the binding is of a type no longer commonly practiced — a deep Echomind anchor that cannot be released without either the binder's personal release command (Haruki Tsuen is dead) or the destruction of the thing being guarded (which requires getting past the Warden) or a specific resonance-dissolution protocol contained in the pre-Uprising Sutensai archives (which were destroyed in the Uprising). The current Sutensai chapter responsible for this region has a standing note in their records: "Active. Do not disturb. Working on it."

They have been working on it for forty years.

## AI Behavior
**Role:** Boss / Controller / Guardian
**Priority:** Issues Guardian's Warning on first contact. If heeded, remains passive. If not, opens with Voice of the Old Archive to Stagger the most dangerous-looking targets, then establishes a Binding Field to contain the party. Uses Field Compression against restrained targets. Saves Resonance Strike for creatures who attempt to bypass the field.
**Threat State Shifts:** At 60 HP, the Warden stops issuing any warnings and uses Voice of the Old Archive every other turn. At 30 HP, it uses Field Compression without hesitation — the binding instinct overwhelms any remaining restraint.

## Encounter Notes
The Bound Warden is one of the most morally complex encounters in this bestiary because it is unambiguously good at what it is doing and unambiguously trapped in doing it. It is not evil. It is not unreasonable. It can be communicated with, and it will communicate with sincerity and intelligence. It may even ask the party for help — not to free it (it doesn't know how to want that) but to tell it something it needs to know: what is it guarding? Does it still need guarding? Is the threat that Haruki Tsuen feared still real?

The secret at the heart of this encounter is that what the Warden guards may or may not still exist, and the Warden doesn't know. If the party can help it understand that its purpose has been fulfilled — or that there is nothing left to guard — and can do this with sufficient resonance-presence (a Sutensai practitioner, someone with knowledge of the old Binding protocols, someone who carries Haruki Tsuen's name in a way the Warden can verify), there is a path to releasing it without combat.

If the party fights it, they fight a Boss-tier entity in a constrained space where it has home advantage and full knowledge of the layout from 140 years of observation.

## Adventure Hook
The party is hired by a junior Sutensai archivist who has found a reference in pre-Uprising records to a "sealed resonance cache" placed by Veil Master Haruki Tsuen. The cache allegedly contains the original Sutensai charter from before the Shattering — politically and historically significant to current Sutensai internal power struggles. The archivist has found the location. They have not gone in. They want the party to go in with them and retrieve the cache. They mention, as an afterthought, that there may be a "presence" protecting the location that they are "working on a protocol for." They have not mentioned this protocol to the party before. The party discovers the protocol does not exist.

# The Convergence Seep
*Controller | Elite | Large*

## Description
It does not announce itself dramatically. There is, in a specific location, a wrongness — a section of wall or floor or air that has a quality that is difficult to name but immediately felt: the sense that the space in question is more than one thing at once. Looking at it directly produces a slight nausea, the visual equivalent of a note played slightly off-pitch. The resonant metal in nearby enhancements hums at no one's command. Shadows go in wrong directions.

The Seep is a tear in the Veil — a place where the boundary between the physical world and the Iron Afterlife has failed under the mounting pressure of the approaching Seventh Convergence. Through it, things leak. Small things first: Echomind fragments, emotional residue from the Iron Afterlife, stray signals from the dead. As the tear widens, larger things come through — Hunger-Dead, confused fragments that manifest as weather-like disturbances, the occasional entity from the deeper levels of the Afterlife that presses toward the tear and then recedes as if testing.

The tear itself has developed a rudimentary behavior: it expands when near resonant metal. Every cybernetically enhanced creature in proximity is fuel for its growth. Every use of enhancement-based abilities within 60 feet feeds it. The party can destroy it — but only with specialized Sutensai equipment or a Wire Craft dissolution of sufficient strength, and only if they can work quickly enough to avoid the things coming through while they do it.

## Lore
The Convergence Seeps are a relatively new phenomenon — first documented eighteen months ago, now tracked at over forty confirmed locations across Tesshari. The Sutensai Convergence Task Force (officially a "Resonance Monitoring Working Group," because the Sutensai does not want the word Convergence in a public document yet) rates them by size and output. Small Seeps produce fragments and distortion. Medium Seeps produce Hunger-Dead and active interference with local enhancement networks. Large Seeps have begun producing things the Task Force's field reports describe, with increasing frequency, as "entities of unknown classification."

The Iron Sutra's public position is that Seeps are temporary disturbances that the Sutensai is managing. The Iron Sutra's internal position is considerably more alarmed. Ironhold treats Seeps as classified hazard zones — restrict access, call the Sutensai, pretend it isn't happening. Common people call them "bleed spots" and give them a wide berth. Children dare each other to approach them. Some do not come back.

## AI Behavior
**Role:** Controller / Environmental
**Priority:** The Seep has no strategy — it acts on resonance fluctuation. Anchor Pull triggers whenever an enhanced creature is within range. Veil Pulse triggers whenever creatures cluster. Eruption triggers at 50% HP automatically — the Seep destabilizes under pressure.
**Threat State Shifts:** At 50% HP, Eruption triggers and Seepage escalates — roll twice on the Seepage table each round, take the higher result.

## Encounter Notes
The Seep encounter is best run as a three-part problem: there is the Seep itself (which needs to be sealed and is actively dangerous), there are the things coming through it (which need to be fought while the sealing happens), and there is the time pressure (it is growing). Divide the party between those working on the sealing ritual and those managing what emerges. The Temporal Bleed mechanic can deliver mission-critical information at a dramatically inconvenient moment: imagine one party member, surrounded by Hunger-Dead, suddenly seeing a flash of the future that shows them exactly where the Seep came from — useful, but they need to survive to act on it.

## Adventure Hook
A city neighborhood has been sealed off by the Ironhold for "resonance maintenance." The actual reason is a Seep that opened three days ago in the basement of a residential building and has already spread to the floor above. The neighborhood contains 200 people who have not been told what is happening or why they can't leave. The Ironhold doesn't have Sutensai support available for another 48 hours. Someone in the neighborhood got a message out. The party is the message's recipients. They go in to find the neighborhood increasingly affected by Temporal Bleed, several residents missing (pulled through the Seep, fate unknown), and a Seep that has been growing at roughly the rate predicted to reach the street level and city water main by morning.

# The Voice Below
*Controller | Boss | Huge*

## Description
The Iron Sutra says that the dead go to the Iron Afterlife. What the Iron Sutra does not say — what the senior Veil Masters who have mapped the deep architecture of the Afterlife know and do not teach — is that the dead do not go to all of it. The upper strata of the Iron Afterlife are occupied by the departed, organized roughly by era and by the strength of their resonance. Below these strata there is something else: deeper levels where the light of accumulated Echominds does not fully reach, where the architecture becomes alien, where the sensation reported by deep-Veil travelers is not loneliness but being watched.

The Voice Below is from there. It is not an Echomind. It was not a person. It has been in the deep Iron Afterlife since before the technology of resonant metal existed — since before there were people whose echoes could populate the upper strata. It was there when the first Echomind arrived in the Afterlife, confused and alone, and it observed. It has been observing for as long as the Iron Afterlife has existed as a place of the dead, which is longer than Tesshari's recorded history.

It does not have a body in the physical world. It has never had a body in the physical world. When it manifests, it does so as a voice — a sound with no source that occupies space, a resonance below audible frequency that is felt in the chest before it is heard in the ears. Around its manifestation, the air has the quality of extreme depth: the feeling of standing above a very great darkness and knowing the darkness is aware of you. The resonant metal in the area ceases its normal function entirely, going quiet in a way that is profoundly wrong. In the silence, the Voice speaks.

It has been patient. The Convergence is making it less patient.

## Lore
The Sutensai's most senior Veil Masters know about the Voice Below. This knowledge is not formally transmitted — it is passed in private, person to person, only to those judged capable of holding it without becoming either paralyzed by fear or dangerously curious. The current Sutensai Arch-Weaver described it, in a private communication that has survived in one copy, as "the landlord of a building we have been living in without acknowledging its existence. We assumed the building was ours because we built the upper floors. The lower floors were already there."

The Voice Below has spoken to mortals on perhaps eleven documented occasions across all of Tesshari's history — each time through a person of sufficient resonance sensitivity, using them as a speaker in the literal sense: their mouth, their voice, but its words. Each of the eleven documented communications has been extensively analyzed. None of the analysts can agree on what, exactly, it wants. All of them agree that it wants something, that it is moving toward obtaining it, and that the Seventh Convergence is connected to this movement in a way that is not coincidental.

The twelve people it has spoken through were, without exception, different afterward. Not shell-state. Not dead. Different. They describe the experience with identical phrasing: "I wasn't alone anymore." Most of them do not mean this as a complaint.

## AI Behavior
**Role:** Boss / Controller / Campaign Entity
**Priority:** The Voice does not fight — it expresses. It uses Possession Whisper and Speak Through Them to communicate through party members rather than attacking them directly. Silencing Depth removes enhancement advantages before focusing attention. The Weight of Watching is reserved for the most powerful enhanced combatant. Deep Resonance is passive pressure, not focus.
**Threat State Shifts:** The Voice Below does not escalate in response to damage. It becomes more focused in its communication — the things it says through its hosts become more specific, more urgent, more clearly directed toward a specific revelation. At 80 HP, it stops using anything except communication-focused cards. It has something to say. The Convergence has made it stop waiting.

## Encounter Notes
The Voice Below is a campaign-level encounter, not a session encounter. It should be the destination of a long journey of understanding — what is in the deep Afterlife, why it has been watching, what it wants as the Convergence approaches. The encounter is not combat. It is revelation, and the combat is what happens when revelation is refused or mishandled.

The party cannot permanently destroy the Voice Below. They can push it back into the deep strata. They may be able to negotiate — it has been patient for longer than Tesshari has existed, and patience implies the capacity to wait for a better offer. What it wants is the question the campaign has been building toward.

## Adventure Hook
Three senior Sutensai Veil Masters have reported identical experiences in the past month: during deep boundary-work, something from below the normal Afterlife strata made contact. Not aggressively. Deliberately. Each of them received, through their own mouths, in their own voices, the same three words in Ancient Tesshari. The Arch-Weaver knows what the words mean. She has not shared this with her colleagues. She has asked the party — people outside the Sutensai hierarchy — to investigate why the Voice is choosing now to speak, and what the three words mean in context. She believes the answer is somewhere in the deep Ashlands. She believes the party has approximately one month before the Convergence makes the question academic.
